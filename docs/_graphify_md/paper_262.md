# Shanley, E., Thigpen, C., Kissenberth, M., Gilliland, R. G., Thorpe, J., Nance, D., Register-Mihalik, J. K., & Tokish, J. (2019). Heads Up Football training decreases concussion rates in high school football players. Clinical Journal of Sport Medicine, 00, 1–7. https://doi.org/10.1097/JSM.0000000000000711

## TLDR
The US Football Heads Up program reduced sport-related concussion rates by 33% and shortened recovery time by 27% in high school football players over one season. Practice-specific concussion rates were nearly twice as high in untrained players, suggesting technique training is especially valuable during practice. This is the first prospective efficacy study of the HU program on SRC incidence in scholastic football.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study compared sport-related concussion (SRC) rates and recovery in 2,514 high school football players during the 2015–2016 season: 1,818 players from 14 schools receiving US Football Heads Up (HU) training versus 696 players from 10 control schools with no HU training (NHU). HU-trained coaches instructed proper blocking and tackling technique emphasizing heads-up contact. Results showed HU players had a significantly lower overall SRC rate (4.1 vs. 6.0 per 100 players; RR=1.5) and practice-specific SRC rate (RR=1.9). HU players also returned to full participation 27% faster (18.2 vs. 24.8 days).

## Methods
Prospective cohort study. 14 Greenville County high schools (n=1,818 players) received US Football HU certification (player safety coach training) before the 2015–2016 season; 10 comparison schools (n=696 players) continued standard coaching. Random fidelity audits (minimum 3 per school) confirmed ≥80% compliance with HU technique instruction and performance. Athletic trainers and sports medicine physicians documented all SRCs prospectively. Cumulative incidence, incidence rates (per 100 players and per 100,000 athlete exposures for practices), and rate ratios with 95% CIs were calculated. Time loss compared by one-way ANOVA.

## Findings
117 total SRCs observed. HU rate: 4.1 SRCs/100 players vs. NHU rate: 6.0 SRCs/100 players (RR=1.5; 95% CI 1.1–5.4). Practice SRC specific incidence rate: 2.6 vs. 4.9 per 100,000 athlete exposures (RR=1.9; 95% CI 1.1–3.2). Game SRC rate trended higher in NHU (RR=1.67; 95% CI 1.0–2.8). Time to full return: HU=18.2 days (95% CI 15.8–20.6) vs. NHU=24.8 days (95% CI 19.9–29.7), a 27% reduction. All HU schools maintained ≥80% fidelity throughout the season.

## Limitations
Assignment to training groups was not randomized; groups had unequal sample sizes. HU players were significantly heavier than NHU players. Game-level exposure by individual player was not available, limiting specific game incidence rate calculations. Position-specific data were not collected. Study conducted over a single season in one geographic region, limiting generalizability. Results require replication in larger, more controlled designs.

## Practical Implications
Implementing the US Football Heads Up training program—particularly proper blocking and tackling technique instruction—can reduce concussion rates and recovery time in high school football. At least one coach should complete HU certification and serve as a player safety coach to ensure consistent technique instruction throughout all levels of practice. Athletic trainers and sports medicine physicians should support program fidelity monitoring.

## Athlete Development Notes
Technique training focused on heads-up blocking and tackling—emphasizing breakdown position, controlled foot movement, heads-up hit position, and hip-drive shooting—reduces head contact frequency and severity in developing athletes. Integrating these principles from the earliest practice days through the full season is critical for long-term athlete safety and development.

## Return to Play Notes
HU-trained athletes returned to full participation approximately one week faster than untrained athletes following concussion, under the same standardized RTP protocol. This suggests that reduced concussion severity—potentially due to improved technique reducing impact magnitude—may facilitate faster symptom resolution and progression through return-to-play stages.
