# Sieljacks P, Knudsen L, Wernbom M, Vissing K. Body position influences arterial occlusion pressure: implications for the standardization of pressure during blood flow restricted exercise. European Journal of Applied Physiology. 2018;118(2):303–312. doi:10.1007/s00421-017-3770-2

## TLDR
When performing BFR training with lower body exercises in a seated position, do not rely on AOP values measured while the athlete is supine — seated AOP averages roughly 20% higher due to hydrostatic pressure differences, meaning a cuff set to 60% of supine AOP may only represent about 50% of true seated AOP. Wide cuffs are far safer and more predictable than narrow cuffs, which can require dangerously high pressures in athletes with large thigh circumferences. For any lower body BFR protocol, measure AOP in the position the athlete will actually exercise in, and use wide cuffs whenever possible.

## Abstract
This study investigated the effect of body position on arterial occlusion pressure (AOP) in 24 healthy subjects using Doppler ultrasound with both wide (13 cm) and narrow (6 cm) pneumatic cuffs on the thigh. AOP was measured in supine and seated positions to determine the impact of hydrostatic pressure changes and to identify predictors of AOP across positions. Results showed AOP increased significantly from supine to seated for both cuff widths, with narrow cuffs producing substantially higher and more variable AOP values exceeding 600 mmHg in some subjects. Thigh circumference was the strongest predictor of AOP in all conditions, with diastolic blood pressure explaining additional variance for the wide cuff.

## Methods
Cross-sectional study with 24 healthy subjects (14 men, 10 women; mean age 32 years). Thigh circumference, skinfold, and blood pressure were measured. AOP was determined via Doppler ultrasound (posterior tibial artery) in randomized supine and seated positions using a wide cuff (Hokanson SC12; 13 cm) and a narrow cuff (Hokanson SC5; 6 cm) placed on contralateral legs. Pressure was increased in 5 mmHg increments up to a system limit of 600 mmHg. Multiple linear regression identified predictors of AOP across body positions.

## Findings
Supine AOP was 148±19 mmHg (wide) and 348±85 mmHg (narrow); seated AOP increased to 177±20 mmHg (wide, p<0.0001, d=1.5) and 409±101 mmHg (narrow, p<0.01, d=0.7). Four subjects exceeded 600 mmHg with the narrow cuff seated. Supine-to-seated AOP correlation was strong for the wide cuff (R²=0.81) but weaker for the narrow cuff (R²=0.50). Thigh circumference was the primary predictor of AOP for both cuffs; diastolic blood pressure added predictive value with the wide cuff (combined adj. R²=0.655–0.659 for wide cuff models). Mean relative AOP change from supine to seated was approximately 20% for both cuff widths.

## Limitations
Results apply only to lower body BFR; upper body AOP is likely less influenced by body position due to proximity to the heart. AOP was measured at rest, not during exercise, and exercise-induced blood pressure increases would further elevate true AOP during exercise. Prediction equations are specific to the 12-cm and 5-cm cuffs tested. The standing position was not assessed. Clinical population applicability is unknown.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football staff implementing BFR training should measure AOP in the seated position for lower body exercises rather than relying on supine measurements. Using supine AOP overestimates the relative blood flow restriction applied during seated exercise, particularly with narrow cuffs. Wide cuffs are strongly preferred for athletes with large thigh circumferences — common in football linemen — due to unpredictably extreme pressures narrow cuffs can require. Individual AOP measurement by a trained clinician in the exercise position remains the gold standard for safe BFR prescription.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football players undergoing BFR-augmented hypertrophy work (e.g., post-practice low-load leg training), cuff pressure should be individualized and measured in the exercise position. Athletes with large thigh circumferences — offensive and defensive linemen, running backs — require higher absolute pressures and face greater risk of inadequate or excessive restriction if standardized pressures are used. Incorporating thigh circumference and diastolic blood pressure into AOP estimation using the wide-cuff prediction equations can help standardize relative restriction when direct Doppler measurement is unavailable.

## Return to Play Notes
BFR training is increasingly used in early post-surgical rehabilitation to preserve muscle mass under low mechanical load. This paper underscores that AOP must be re-measured or corrected for body position as the athlete transitions from supine (early rehab) to seated or standing exercises. Using supine AOP throughout a progressive rehab program will cause relative restriction to diminish as the athlete moves to upright positions, potentially blunting the stimulus. Wide pneumatic cuffs should be standard in clinical RTP protocols to ensure safe, predictable blood flow restriction across all stages of return to play.
