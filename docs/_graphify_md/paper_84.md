# Dairi I, Brown C, DiGregorio H, Wasfy M, Baggish A, Pitler L, Copen WA, Doyle M, Wu O, Zafonte R, Tenforde AS. A Case Report of Reversible Cognitive Decline in a Former Professional American-Style Football Player: Findings from the Football Players Health Study In-Person Assessments. Current Sports Medicine Reports. 2023;22(5):154-157.

## TLDR
Not all cognitive decline in former football players represents irreversible neurodegeneration. This case demonstrates that hydrocephalus — a treatable structural condition — can mimic frontotemporal dementia in a former professional player, and that thorough clinical evaluation including brain MRI can identify and redirect care toward curative treatment. NFL and college football medical staffs should ensure that post-career cognitive evaluations include brain imaging and systematic workup for treatable conditions (hydrocephalus, sleep apnea, depression, cardiometabolic disease) before attributing impairment to CTE or irreversible head-trauma sequelae.

## Abstract
This case report presents a 54-year-old former NFL linebacker enrolled in the Football Players Health Study (FPHS) at Harvard who had been diagnosed with early-onset frontotemporal dementia (FTD) and presented with cognitive impairment, urinary incontinence, and gait disturbance. Brain MRI obtained during the 3-day in-person research assessment revealed mild hydrocephalus rather than frontotemporal atrophy, prompting neurosurgical referral. Following ventriculoperitoneal shunt placement in February 2020, the patient experienced substantial improvement in mood and resolution of most cognitive complaints at 2-year follow-up.

## Methods
Single case report from the Football Players Health Study (FPHS) at Harvard University in-person assessment protocol. The participant was a 54-year-old former professional NFL linebacker (4 seasons) assessed in January 2020. The 3-day evaluation included telephone cognitive screening (T-MoCA-22), brain MRI, EEG, sleep study (polysomnography), cardiovascular testing (ECG, echocardiogram, peak VO2), laboratory panels (lipids, vitamins, hormones), and a structured exit interview with a study physician. Follow-up interview was conducted by study nurse in September 2022.

## Findings
T-MoCA score was 17, indicating mild cognitive impairment. Brain MRI showed mild enlargement of the third and lateral ventricles with balloon-like rounding of the frontal horns consistent with mild hydrocephalus; no frontotemporal atrophy was present. EEG showed nonspecific cortical slowing of indeterminate etiology. Sleep study indicated moderate sleep apnea. Peak VO2 was 23.4 mL/kg/min (73% predicted). Laboratory testing was notable for low HDL and low vitamin D. Following ventriculoperitoneal shunt placement, the patient reported substantial improvement in mood and near-resolution of cognitive complaints at 2-year follow-up.

## Limitations
Single case report limits generalizability. The prevalence of hydrocephalus among former NFL players has not been systematically studied. The 2-year gap between the research visit and the surgical intervention makes it difficult to attribute all improvements solely to the shunting procedure. The prior FTD diagnosis may have been influenced by the patient's football history and assumptions about head-trauma-related neurodegeneration rather than a thorough structural workup.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football medical staffs should not assume cognitive decline or behavioral changes in former players are attributable solely to CTE or irreversible neurodegeneration. Brain MRI should be obtained when evaluating players presenting with new or progressive cognitive complaints, gait disturbance, or urinary incontinence. Treatable conditions including hydrocephalus, sleep apnea, depression, and cardiometabolic disease should be systematically assessed before attributing impairment to football-related head trauma.

## Athlete Development Notes
This case underscores the importance of comprehensive post-career health monitoring for former football players. Athletic development and player wellness programs should educate current players on the range of potentially treatable conditions that can affect cognitive function after playing careers, reducing fatalism about post-career brain health and encouraging proactive medical evaluation rather than automatic acceptance of a neurodegenerative diagnosis.

## Return to Play Notes
Not directly applicable as a return-to-play study. However, the case illustrates the importance of thorough differential diagnosis when a current player presents with cognitive symptoms, gait disturbance, or urinary incontinence — conditions that might be attributed to concussion history but could reflect a treatable structural etiology such as communicating hydrocephalus. MRI brain imaging should be considered in the workup of persistent post-concussion symptoms that do not resolve on expected timelines.
