# Stemper BD, Shah AS, Pintar FA, et al. Drill-Specific Head Impacts in Collegiate Football Practice: Implications for Reducing 'Friendly Fire' Exposure. Ann Biomed Eng. 2017;45(12):2648-2662. doi: 10.1007/s10439-017-1898-y

## TLDR
Not all football practice drills carry equal concussion risk. This study found that certain full-contact drills — particularly those involving teammate-on-teammate ('friendly fire') contact — account for a disproportionate share of head impacts during practice. Coaches can meaningfully reduce cumulative head impact exposure by limiting or modifying these specific high-impact drills without eliminating overall football preparation.

## Abstract
This study quantified head impact biomechanics by specific drill type in collegiate football practice to identify which drills contribute most to cumulative head impact exposure. Head impact data were collected using helmet-mounted accelerometers during practices across a collegiate football season. Drills were categorized and head impact frequency and magnitude were compared across drill types to identify opportunities for reducing practice-based head impact exposure.

## Methods
Helmet-mounted accelerometer systems recorded head impact kinematics in collegiate football players across an entire practice season. Each impact was tagged to a specific drill type. Linear and rotational accelerations were analyzed by drill category, and cumulative exposure metrics were calculated. Drills were classified to identify those generating the most frequent and highest-magnitude impacts.

## Findings
Certain contact-based drills contributed disproportionately to cumulative head impact burden. Teammate-on-teammate contact ('friendly fire') drills generated significant impact exposure. High-magnitude impacts were concentrated in a subset of drill types, suggesting that targeted drill modification could substantially reduce overall practice head impact exposure.

## Limitations
The study was conducted at a single institution, limiting generalizability across programs with different practice schemes. Accelerometer-based systems may not perfectly replicate true head kinematics. Drills were self-reported or observed by research staff, which may introduce classification inconsistency. The study did not directly link drill-specific impacts to concussion outcomes.

## Practical Implications
Football programs can reduce cumulative head impact exposure by identifying and modifying or eliminating high-impact 'friendly fire' drills from practice schedules. Staff should audit their practice script to quantify which drills generate the most head impacts and consider substituting equivalent skill-development activities with lower contact intensity, consistent with evolving NCAA and NFL practice contact limitations.

## Athlete Development Notes
Skill development can be maintained by replacing high-contact friendly-fire drills with technique-focused or thud-level alternatives. This is particularly relevant for linemen and linebackers who absorb the highest practice impact loads. Periodizing full-contact drill exposure across the season — heaviest early, tapering mid-season — can preserve development while protecting long-term neurological health.

## Return to Play Notes
Quantifying drill-specific impact exposure provides a framework for progressive return-to-practice protocols after concussion. Players returning from head injury can be reintegrated beginning with no-contact and drill-contact activities, avoiding the high-impact friendly-fire drills identified in this study until fully cleared, reducing re-injury risk during the vulnerable post-concussion window.
