# Burke J, Geller JS, Perez JR, Naik K, Vidal AF, Baraga MG, Kaplan LD. Effect of Passing Plays on Injury Rates in the National Football League. J Strength Cond Res. 2021;35(12S):S1-S4. DOI: 10.1519/JSC.0000000000004139

## TLDR
Passing plays carry a 40% higher odds of in-game injury than running plays in the NFL, primarily driven by elevated injury risk for quarterbacks (6.9x), receivers (5.0x), and defensive backs (2.3x). Passing plays are particularly associated with concussions (1.7x) and core/trunk injuries (1.7x). As passing plays now account for ~60% of NFL plays, this trend has direct injury prevention implications. For football staff, this means play-calling tendencies and practice design should consider the injury risk profile of different play types, especially regarding high-risk positions and concussion management.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiology study analyzed data from official NFL gamebooks for all 32 teams across the 2013-2016 regular seasons to determine whether play type (pass vs. run) affected in-game injury rates. There were 2,721 in-game injuries recorded with an overall rate of 1.33 injuries per team per game. Multivariable logistic regression controlled for weather conditions and temperature.

## Methods
Retrospective descriptive epidemiology study using data extracted from official NFL gamebooks for all 32 teams across the 2013-2016 regular seasons. All 2,721 in-game injuries not occurring on special teams were included. Play type (run vs. pass), injury location (lower extremity, upper extremity, pelvis/hip, trunk/core, head/neck, concussion), and player position (QB, runner, receiver, lineman, linebacker, defensive back) were recorded. Injury location was verified through the following week's injury reports. Multivariable logistic regression controlled for weather conditions and temperature. Statistical significance set at p < 0.05.

## Findings
Passing plays comprised 55.7% of total plays. Passing plays had significantly higher odds of injury than running plays (OR 1.4, 95% CI: 1.3-1.5, p<0.0001) after controlling for weather and temperature. By injury location: concussion (OR 1.7, 95% CI: 1.2-2.4, p=0.002), core/trunk injuries (OR 1.7, 95% CI: 1.1-2.6, p=0.01), upper extremity (OR 1.4, 95% CI: 1.1-1.9, p=0.02); no effect on lower extremity (OR 1.0, p=0.70). By position: quarterbacks (OR 6.9), receivers (OR 5.0), and defensive backs (OR 2.3) had the greatest increased risk on passing plays; runners (OR 0.4) and linemen (OR 0.7) had decreased risk on passing plays.

## Limitations
The gamebook-derived injury rate (1.33/team/game) is lower than NFL-reported rates because minor injuries not requiring immediate medical attention are underreported. Study captures only in-game injuries, not practice injuries. Injury severity was not recorded in gamebooks. The study covers 2013-2016 and may not reflect more recent rule changes or evolving play trends. Player age was not recorded in gamebooks.

## Practical Implications
Football staff should recognize that as passing plays increase (from 38% in 1978 to 60% by 2018), the overall in-game injury burden increases proportionally — particularly for concussions and trunk/core injuries. Practice designs that emphasize passing routes and coverages at high intensity should be weighted against the injury risk data. Specific attention should be paid to conditioning quarterbacks, receivers, and defensive backs for the collisions common in passing situations. Core and trunk strengthening programs for wide receivers and defensive backs may help mitigate the elevated trunk/core injury rate on passing plays.

## Athlete Development Notes
Speed-position players (wide receivers, defensive backs, quarterbacks) face dramatically elevated injury risk during passing plays — the primary play type in modern football. Development programs for these players should incorporate collision preparation, core/trunk stability training, and concussion recognition education. Given that receivers face a 5x greater injury odds on passing plays, physical preparation must reflect the actual demands and risk profile of their role, not just speed and agility metrics.

## Return to Play Notes
Injury epidemiology by play type is useful for RTP decision-making. A running back (lower passing-play injury risk, OR 0.4 on pass plays) returning from injury can be more safely reintegrated in run-heavy offensive packages. Conversely, a wide receiver or defensive back cleared for RTP should be progressively reintroduced through practice exposure on passing plays before returning to full game competition, given the substantially elevated injury odds these players face during pass situations.
