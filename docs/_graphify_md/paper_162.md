# Bishay AE, Fitch SW, Iheagwaram CS, Williams KL, Naranjo C, Sills TK, Fitch RW, Pasquina PF, McAllister TW, McCrea MA, Broglio SP, Terry DP, Zuckerman SL; CARE Consortium Investigators. Does Timing of Concussion in Season Affect Incidence and Recovery in Division I Collegiate Football Players? A CARE Consortium Study. J Head Trauma Rehabil. 2026;Vol 00, No 0:1–10. https://doi.org/10.1097/HTR.0000000000001146

## TLDR
In NCAA Division I football, preseason carries the highest risk of concussion — nearly double the rate of any regular-season phase — but preseason concussions resolve fastest (median 5.7 days to SR). Concussions sustained during the early and late regular season take significantly longer to resolve (median 6.6 and 6.9 days to SR, respectively), and early-season concussions also prolong return-to-play. Football medical staff should increase concussion surveillance and prevention during preseason, while recognizing that early- and late-season concussions may require extended management windows, possibly due to academic demands, cumulative fatigue, or competitive pressures.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study from the CARE Consortium investigated whether the timing of concussion within a football season (preseason, early, middle, or late-season) is associated with concussion incidence and recovery outcomes (time to symptom resolution [SR] and return-to-play [RTP]) in 682 Division I collegiate football players across 21 institutions (2014–2020). Concussion incidence was highest during preseason (4.73 per 100 players) compared to all regular-season phases (~2.2 per 100). Early and late-season concussions had significantly longer time to SR compared to preseason (HR=0.582 and 0.634, respectively), and early-season concussions also had a lower hazard of RTP (HR=0.703).

## Methods
Prospective cohort study using the CARE Consortium multi-institution database (30 universities, 2014–2020). Final sample: 682 Division I football players across 21 institutions who sustained concussions during preseason or regular-season. Season phases were defined as preseason (August), early (Sep 1–29), middle (Sep 30–Oct 28), and late-season (Oct 29–Nov 27) on a per-team basis. Concussion incidence expressed per 100 players. Time to SR and RTP analyzed with Kaplan-Meier survival curves, log-rank tests, and Cox proportional hazards regression with covariates including age, SCAT3 TSS, LOC, amnesia, ADHD, and psychological disorders.

## Findings
Preseason incidence: 4.73/100 players (41.9% of concussions); early: 2.19, middle: 2.31, late: 2.17/100 (χ²=104.6, p<0.001). Kaplan-Meier showed significant SR differences (log-rank p=0.012): median SR was preseason 5.7 days, early 6.6, middle 4.8, late 6.9 days. Cox regression: early-season concussions had 41.8% reduced SR hazard (HR=0.582, p<0.001); late-season had 35.6% reduced SR hazard (HR=0.634, p=0.002) vs preseason. No overall RTP differences (log-rank p=0.300), but early-season concussions had 29.7% reduced RTP hazard (HR=0.703, p=0.040) vs preseason. Higher SCAT3 TSS and delayed SCAT3 testing also predicted longer SR and RTP.

## Limitations
Arbitrary season phase cutoffs limit direct comparisons with studies using different definitions. Lack of exposure data prevents precise incidence rate calculations. Observational design cannot establish causality or distinguish mechanisms (e.g., training load, cumulative head impacts, academic demands). Sample limited to Division I football; results may not generalize to other levels or sports. Concussion management protocols evolved over the 2014–2020 study period, potentially influencing reported outcomes.

## Practical Implications
Football programs should implement heightened concussion surveillance, prevention protocols, and head impact monitoring during preseason — the highest-risk period — including attention to conditioning status, practice intensity, and helmet-fit protocols. Athletic trainers should be aware that early- and late-season concussions may require longer management timelines, potentially due to academic load, cumulative fatigue, and playoff pressure. Symptom severity (SCAT3 TSS) at 24 hours post-injury remains a key prognostic indicator regardless of season phase.

## Athlete Development Notes
Preseason represents the highest concussion incidence period for collegiate football players, likely linked to elevated head impact exposure, deconditioning from the off-season, and intense competition for roster spots. Programs should prioritize preseason neuromuscular conditioning, proper tackling technique, and gradual ramp-up of contact to reduce early-season concussion burden. Player wellness and concussion literacy education at the start of each season phase is essential.

## Return to Play Notes
Timing of concussion within the season significantly affects RTP trajectory: early-season concussions showed a 30% lower hazard of RTP compared to preseason, with median RTP ranging from 10.7 to 12.7 days across phases. Medical staff should not expect uniform RTP timelines across the season; late-season concussions in particular may be complicated by cumulative fatigue and playoff stakes. Post-injury SCAT3 symptom severity is the strongest clinical predictor of prolonged RTP and should anchor all return-to-play decision-making.
