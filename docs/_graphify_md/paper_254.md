# Sim, M., Thornton, H. R., Duhig, S. J., Whiteley, R., Timmins, R. G., & Bourne, M. N. (2023). Hamstring strain injury risk factors in Australian football change over the course of the season. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 55(10), 1837–1847. https://doi.org/10.1249/MSS.0000000000003297

## TLDR
HSI risk prediction in AFL players is highly time-dependent: pre-season risk is best captured by injury history and non-modifiable factors, early in-season risk by BFlh muscle architecture, and late in-season risk is largely unpredictable by standard assessments. Practitioners should shift their risk screening strategies as the season progresses rather than applying a single static model year-round.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study followed 311 AFL players across an entire competitive season to identify how hamstring strain injury (HSI) risk factors change from pre-season through early and late in-season periods. Assessments included eccentric hamstring strength (NordBord), biceps femoris long head (BFlh) muscle architecture via ultrasound (fascicle length, pennation angle, muscle thickness), and prior HSI history. Multiple logistic regression with recursive feature elimination identified the best-fit predictors for each time epoch. Pre-season HSI risk was dominated by non-modifiable factors (prior HSI, age, games played), yielding high predictive accuracy (AUC = 0.83). Early in-season risk was predicted by modifiable BFlh architectural variables—specifically pennation angle and fascicle length—with excellent accuracy (AUC = 0.86). Late in-season risk was essentially unpredictable (AUC = 0.46), suggesting fatigue and accumulated load dominate at this stage.

## Methods
Prospective cohort design with 311 male AFL players across one full season. Baseline assessments: NordBord eccentric hamstring strength, BFlh ultrasound (fascicle length, pennation angle, muscle thickness), and injury history. Logistic regression with recursive feature elimination applied separately for pre-season, early in-season (rounds 1–11), and late in-season (rounds 12–22) HSI prediction. Model performance evaluated via AUC–ROC. Injury defined as any HSI resulting in missed training or competition.

## Findings
Pre-season model (AUC = 0.83): prior HSI, age, and total career games were the strongest predictors. Early in-season model (AUC = 0.86): BFlh pennation angle and fascicle length were the primary predictors—players with smaller fascicle length and greater pennation angle were at higher risk. Late in-season model (AUC = 0.46): no assessment combination yielded meaningful predictive accuracy. HSI incidence was highest in the late in-season phase despite the inability to predict it from standard metrics.

## Limitations
Single-season design from one AFL club limits generalizability. Ultrasound measures reflect structural status at one time point and may not capture within-season changes. Accumulated training load and fatigue metrics were not included as predictors, which may explain the late in-season prediction failure. Sample size for some sub-groups was modest.

## Practical Implications
Practitioners should use injury history and career exposure data to tier pre-season risk. Early in-season, BFlh ultrasound screening provides actionable targets—strengthening programs to increase fascicle length (e.g., Nordic hamstring exercise) should be prioritized for flagged athletes. Acknowledge that late in-season HSI risk cannot be reliably predicted and shift strategy toward workload management and monitoring instead.

## Athlete Development Notes
For American football development, this study supports periodic BFlh ultrasound as a practical tool during pre-season camps and early in the competitive season to identify athletes with architectural deficits. Integrating Nordic hamstring exercises and hip extension loading into off-season and early pre-season programming may structurally adapt the BFlh to reduce risk during the competitive window.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes returning from prior HSI should be flagged as elevated risk throughout the entire season, not just immediately post-clearance. BFlh architecture (fascicle length and pennation angle) measured via ultrasound should be assessed before RTP clearance and tracked in follow-up assessments. If BFlh fascicle length has not normalized post-injury, re-injury risk remains elevated even after clinical strength benchmarks are met.
