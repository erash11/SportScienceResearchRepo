# Crema MD, Guermazi A, Tol JL, Niu J, Hamilton B, Roemer FW. Acute hamstring injury in football players: Association between anatomical location and extent of injury—A large single-center MRI report. J Sci Med Sport. 2015;(in press).

## TLDR
In 275 professional football players, MRI demonstrated that proximal myotendinous junction injuries produce the largest edema and proximal biceps femoris injuries are most frequent, while distal semitendinosus injuries carry the largest tears—findings with direct prognostic relevance since lesion extent predicts recovery time.

## Abstract
This retrospective observational study included 275 male professional football (soccer) players with MRI-confirmed acute hamstring injuries, generating 393 lesions assessed for location and cross-sectional area of edema and tears. Lesions were classified across seven anatomical zones per muscle (proximal tendon through distal myofascial junction) in four hamstring muscles. The long head of biceps femoris (LHBF) was the most commonly affected muscle (56.5%), with proximal myotendinous junction and proximal locations most prevalent overall. Proximal myotendinous junction location was associated with significantly greater edema extent in the LHBF and semitendinosus, while distal locations in the semitendinosus had significantly larger tears.

## Methods
Retrospective review of 275 consecutive male professional football players referred to ASPETAR (Qatar) 2009–2012 with acute hamstring injury and positive MRI within 5 days; 1.5T MRI (Magnetom Espree, Siemens) using PDw fat-suppressed and T2-weighted sequences; manual segmentation of edema and tear cross-sectional area (cm²) by a blinded radiologist with 7 years of experience; inter-reader reliability assessed on random 40-player subsample; one-sample t-test with significance at p < 0.05; SAS v.9.2.

## Findings
• LHBF was most commonly affected muscle accounting for 222/393 lesions (56.5%).
• Overall, injuries most prevalent at proximal myotendinous junction (33%) and proximal locations (31.8%).
• Proximal MTJ of LHBF and ST had significantly greater mean edema area than muscle-wide mean (p = 0.0027 and p = 0.0083, respectively).
• Proximal LHBF locations had significantly larger edema than distal locations (4.09 vs. 3.23 cm²; p = 0.02).
• Distal ST locations had significantly larger tears than proximal locations (3.47 vs. 1.02 cm²; p < 0.001).
• 88.6% of lesions exhibited edema; 27.7% had measurable tears; 60.8% edema only with no tear.
• Kappa = 1.00 for edema detection; ICC = 0.98 for edema area; ICC = 1.00 for tear area (excellent reliability).

## Limitations
Only MRI-positive players included; no control group; potential initial-read bias mitigated by 2-month re-read interval; field-of-view did not cover distal tendon insertions so distal tendon lesions were excluded; retrospective design prevents causal inference.

## Practical Implications
Clinicians should document the specific anatomical location of hamstring injuries at initial MRI assessment because proximal myotendinous junction injuries carry greater edema burden (associated with longer recovery and reinjury risk) while distal semitendinosus injuries tend to have larger tears. Knowing location at diagnosis provides early, indirect prognostic information about expected recovery timeline.

## Athlete Development Notes
Applicable to American football skill positions prone to high-speed running injuries. Biceps femoris long head proximal myotendinous junction is the highest-risk site; programming should emphasise hip-dominant eccentric loading that targets this zone (RDLs, GHRs, hip extensions). Paper is from soccer/association football context.

## Return to Play Notes
Proximal myotendinous junction location is associated with greater edema extent, which prior studies link to longer recovery time and higher reinjury risk. Distal semitendinosus injuries with larger tear area also predict longer return timelines. MRI location and edema/tear cross-sectional area should inform individualised return-to-play timelines rather than relying on symptom resolution alone.
