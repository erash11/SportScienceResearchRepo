# Lamplot JD, Petit C, Lee R, Mack CD, Herzog MM, Myer GD, Hammond K. Epidemiology of Stingers in the National Football League, 2015-2019. Sports Health. 2024. doi:10.1177/19417381231223413

## TLDR
Over five NFL seasons (2015-2019), stingers occurred at a stable rate of ~138 per year and were most common in running backs and linebackers during regular-season games. The vast majority of affected players missed no additional time beyond the game of injury. While concomitant injuries were rare, concussion and cervical spine injuries did occur and must be ruled out before return to play — a critical reminder for sideline medical staff.

## Abstract
This retrospective epidemiological study analyzed all in-game stingers (transient traumatic neuropraxia of the brachial plexus or cervical nerve root) recorded in the NFL electronic health record system from 2015 to 2019. A total of 691 in-game stingers occurred over 5 seasons (mean 138.2/year). Incidence rate was 11.48 per 100,000 player-plays overall, higher during the regular season (12.26) than preseason (8.87). Running backs (18.00) and linebackers (15.87) had the highest incidence by position. The average single-season injury risk was 3.74%. Overall, 76.41% of athletes missed no additional time; mean time missed was 4.79 days. Concomitant injuries were uncommon (7.09%), most frequently shoulder injuries, cervical spine injuries, and concussions.

## Methods
Retrospective epidemiology study. All in-game stinger injuries (coded as 'neck brachial plexus stretch' or 'neck brachial plexus compression') from the NFL centralized electronic health record system were aggregated for the 2015-2019 seasons across all 32 clubs. Injury data were integrated with NFL Game Statistical and Information System (GSIS) play-participation data. Incidence rates per 100,000 player-plays were calculated using the Poisson distribution. Descriptive statistics and Poisson regression were used; significance accepted at p < 0.05.

## Findings
691 total in-game stingers over 5 seasons; average single-season risk 3.74%. Regular-season incidence (12.26/100,000 player-plays) significantly exceeded preseason (8.87, p < 0.01). Highest incidence among running backs (18.00), linebackers (15.87), and defensive linemen (14.78). 76.41% of athletes missed no additional time; mean time missed was 4.79 days. Brachial plexus compression injuries resulted in longer mean time loss (5.98 days) than stretch injuries (4.20 days). Recurrence history had no effect on time missed. Concomitant injuries occurred in 7.09%: shoulder (40%), cervical spine (31%), concussion (22%).

## Limitations
Findings may not generalize to collegiate, high school, or youth football athletes. Underreporting is likely given the injury's transient nature and players' career concerns. The number of in-game plays missed during the injury game was not available. Player age was not available, precluding age-mechanism analysis. Cervical nerve root compression as a distinct mechanism could not be isolated due to EHR coding structure. MRI and imaging data were not available. Permanent neurologic deficits could not be assessed; only time-missed data were available. Injury recurrence before NFL participation was not captured.

## Practical Implications
Medical staff should be prepared to efficiently perform concurrent concussion and cervical spine injury evaluations whenever a stinger is diagnosed, as co-occurring injuries — though uncommon — do occur. Understanding that most NFL stingers resolve without time loss can assist clinicians in managing player and coaching staff expectations. Running backs and linebackers represent the highest-risk positions and may benefit from targeted neck-strengthening and contact-technique education.

## Athlete Development Notes
Running backs and linebackers sustain stingers at more than double the rate of quarterbacks and wide receivers, attributable to high-velocity collision patterns. Position-specific training that strengthens the cervical musculature and refines contact mechanics — particularly for players who initiate or absorb high-velocity collisions — may reduce stinger incidence and recurrence over a career.

## Return to Play Notes
Return to play after a stinger requires complete resolution of symptoms including full painless cervical range of motion and full extremity strength. Concurrent concussion evaluation is mandated by NFL protocol when a stinger is diagnosed. Cervical spine injury must also be cleared before clearance. The 2018 NFL helmet-lowering rule did not appear to reduce stinger incidence, highlighting the continued need for RTP vigilance in high-contact positions.
