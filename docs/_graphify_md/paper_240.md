# Khalil, L. S., Lindsay-Rivera, K. G., Abbas, M. J., Shah, S., Tandron, M., Ferris, A., & Okoroha, K. R. (2021). Game utilization and performance following RTP from ACL reconstruction does not influence a subsequent second ACL injury in National Football League players. Arthroscopy, Sports Medicine, and Rehabilitation, 3(5), e1377–e1385.

## TLDR
How much an NFL player plays after ACL reconstruction does not predict whether they will suffer a second ACL tear. Both groups showed similar declines in game usage and performance after RTP. The one meaningful predictor of return to starting lineup was draft pick position, suggesting organizational investment—not individual workload—drives post-ACL playing time decisions. Clinicians cannot use in-game utilization as a risk stratification tool for second ACL injury.

## Abstract
This retrospective case-control study examined whether in-game utilization or performance following primary ACL reconstruction differs between NFL players who subsequently sustain a second ACL injury (retear group, n=13) and those who do not (tear group, n=32). Players who underwent ACL reconstruction between 2013 and 2017 were identified from public sources. Game utilization (games played, games started, snap counts) and season approximate value (SAV) were compared across three seasons post-RTP relative to the pre-injury baseline season. No significant differences were found between groups in utilization or performance metrics at any time point.

## Methods
Retrospective case-control study of NFL ACL injuries from 2012/2013 to 2017/2018 seasons. Players were identified via publicly available sources and classified into 'tear' (primary ACL only, n=32) and 'retear' (subsequent ipsilateral or contralateral ACL injury, n=13) groups. Game utilization statistics (games played, games started, snap counts) and season approximate value (SAV) were collected from pro-football-reference.com, ESPN.com, and NFL.com. Data were analyzed for the season prior to injury (baseline) and three seasons following RTP. Statistical comparisons used independent t-tests, Wilcoxon rank sum tests, chi-square tests, Fisher's exact tests, and repeated-measures analysis. Relative percentages used pre-injury baseline as reference.

## Findings
No significant differences in games played, games started, snap counts, or SAV between tear and retear groups across all three post-RTP seasons (P > .05 for all comparisons). Both groups demonstrated similar decreases in all utilization metrics relative to pre-injury baseline. Tear players averaged >100% of baseline for most metrics; retear players generally failed to reach 100% but differences were non-significant. Draft pick position was positively correlated with the relative percentage of games started in the first post-RTP season (r = .554, P = .017). Return to play rate was 73.3% following primary ACL reconstruction.

## Limitations
Retrospective design with data from public internet sources introduces potential for incomplete or inaccurate injury classification. Cannot be generalized to non-NFL athlete populations or other levels of play. Concomitant injuries (meniscal, cartilage), surgical technique, graft choice, and rehabilitation protocols were not standardized or controlled. Sample size was too small to independently analyze ipsilateral vs. contralateral reinjuries. No power analysis conducted. Players with pre-NFL ACL history were excluded, limiting generalizability.

## Practical Implications
Game utilization after ACL reconstruction cannot serve as a modifiable risk factor or risk screening tool for second ACL injury in NFL players. Organizational decisions about returning high-draft-pick players to starting roles appear to be independent of reinjury risk. Clinicians and performance staff should not restrict or modulate game utilization based on reinjury prevention rationale alone. Other biomechanical, neuromuscular, and psychological factors should be prioritized in return-to-sport screening.

## Athlete Development Notes
For player development after ACL reconstruction, draft position—a proxy for organizational investment and perceived talent—was the primary factor predicting return to a starting role. Development programs should focus on optimizing neuromuscular readiness and psychological readiness for RTP rather than managing game minutes as a reinjury prevention strategy, since load management did not differentiate those who re-tore from those who did not.

## Return to Play Notes
Game utilization and performance metrics following primary ACL reconstruction are not predictive of a subsequent second ACL tear. Return-to-play protocols should not use post-RTP snap counts or games started as risk stratification criteria. The similar decline in performance metrics in both tear and retear groups suggests that standard RTP decision-making should focus on objective functional and strength criteria rather than graduated game exposure as injury prevention.
