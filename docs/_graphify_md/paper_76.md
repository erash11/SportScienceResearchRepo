# Dos'Santos T, Thomas C, McBurnie A, Comfort P, Jones PA. Biomechanical Determinants of Performance and Injury Risk During Cutting: A Performance-Injury Conflict? Sports Med. 2021;51(11):2591–2608.

## TLDR
The biomechanical techniques that produce faster cutting performance — including higher approach velocity, greater braking forces, and stiffer knee strategy — directly conflict with safer cutting mechanics, meaning coaches cannot optimize both simultaneously and must carefully manage this trade-off.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study examined the biomechanical determinants of 90° cutting performance and non-contact ACL injury risk surrogates (peak knee abduction moments [KAM] and knee internal rotation moments [KIRM]) in 61 male multidirectional sport athletes. Three-dimensional motion analysis and ground reaction force (GRF) data were collected across the penultimate (PFC) and final foot contacts (FFC) during pre-planned cutting trials. Faster cutting performance was associated with greater COM velocities, greater FFC braking forces, shorter GCTs, greater knee flexion moments, and larger initial foot progression angles — all of which were also associated with greater peak KAMs and KIRMs. Stepwise regression showed these variables explained 64% of completion time variance and 43% of peak KAM variance. The findings confirm a 'performance-injury conflict' during cutting, where the mechanics enabling faster performance concurrently elevate ACL injury risk.

## Methods
Mixed, cross-sectional associative design; 61 male athletes (soccer n=43, rugby n=10, cricket n=7, field hockey n=1; mean age 20.7 ± 3.8 years) from multidirectional sports; six 90° pre-planned side-step cutting trials performed; 3D motion analysis via 10 Qualisys Oqus 7 infrared cameras (240 Hz); GRFs collected via two 600×900 mm AMTI force platforms (1200 Hz); joint moments calculated via inverse dynamics in Visual 3D; Pearson's and Spearman's correlations and stepwise multiple regression analyses performed; statistical significance set at P ≤ 0.05.

## Findings
• Faster completion times associated with greater FFC touch-down velocities (r = −0.752), greater propulsive forces, shorter GCTs, greater KFMs, and larger initial foot progression angles
• Stepwise regression: exit velocity, peak resultant propulsive force, PFC mean horizontal braking force, and initial foot progression angle explained 64.2% (adjusted 61.6%) of completion time variance
• Greater peak KAMs associated with greater COM velocities, greater peak KAAs, and greater FFC braking forces
• FFC mean vertical braking force and peak KAA explained 42.6% (adjusted 40.6%) of peak KAM variance
• Peak KIRMs largely associated with peak KAMs (r = −0.557), shorter completion times, and greater approach velocities
• Faster cutting performance and greater knee joint loads were significantly correlated, confirming the performance-injury conflict
• Reducing KAAs during cutting appeared to be a viable strategy to reduce knee loading without performance detriment

## Limitations
Male athletes only — findings may not generalize to female athletes; pre-planned 90° cuts only — findings may not apply to reactive or other angle cuts; relatively small and sport-heterogeneous sample; lab-based testing may not reflect in-game demands; no prospective injury tracking to confirm surrogate validity.

## Practical Implications
Practitioners must acknowledge the performance-injury conflict when coaching cutting technique: instructing athletes to cut more slowly to reduce ACL risk is not viable. Athletes should be progressively exposed to high-velocity cutting drills and must possess sufficient neuromuscular capacity (co-contraction, rapid force production) to tolerate the knee loads inherent in fast cutting. PFC dominant braking strategies and minimizing lateral trunk flexion are recommended as pragmatic coaching cues.

## Athlete Development Notes
Note: this study was conducted on multidirectional sport athletes (primarily soccer), not American football specifically. However, the performance-injury conflict findings are directly applicable to American football skill positions (WR, DB, RB) that frequently perform high-velocity direction changes. Developing neuromuscular resilience through strength, plyometric, and COD training is essential for safe performance development in football.

## Return to Play Notes
The performance-injury conflict framework is relevant to ACL injury RTP in American football: an athlete who has returned to baseline strength but lacks high-velocity cutting capacity may still be at elevated risk. RTP cutting assessments should evaluate both performance metrics (completion time, exit velocity) and injury risk surrogates (KAM, trunk control) to ensure readiness.
