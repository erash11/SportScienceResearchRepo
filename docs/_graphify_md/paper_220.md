# Fullagar HHK, Delaney J, Duffield R, Murray A. Factors influencing home advantage in American collegiate football. Sci Med Football. 2019;3(2):163-168.

## TLDR
Home field advantage in college football is worth about 5 points and is driven primarily by crowd size and travel distance. Travel beyond 300 miles consistently hurts away teams by 5-7 points, and westward travel across time zones is especially costly. Crowd noise may play a key role, as larger crowds disproportionately hurt visiting teams. These findings can guide travel and periodization planning for away games.

## Abstract
This study examined home advantage (HA) in NCAA Division I football and the factors contributing to it using game data from two non-consecutive seasons (2013 and 2016; 719 in-conference games; 222 teams). Outcome measures were points differential, penalty differential, and penalty yardage differential. Linear mixed models assessed the effects of game location, travel distance (quartiles), time zone direction of travel, and crowd size. Playing at home was associated with a ~5.3-point advantage (ES = 0.58). Away travel beyond 484 km produced a moderate disadvantage (~5-7 points). Westward time zone crossings yielded a ~7.5-point disadvantage. Larger crowds produced a ~6-point advantage for home teams and an ~8.7-point disadvantage for away teams per 2 SD change in crowd size.

## Methods
Retrospective analysis of in-conference games from the 2013 and 2016 NCAA Division I football seasons (n=719 games, 222 teams) using commercial statistics provider data (Sport Source Analytics). Travel distances calculated via Google Maps; time zones via the R geonames package. Linear mixed models with team as a random effect. Outcomes converted to effect sizes (ES) by dividing by between-team SD. Effects considered real if likelihood exceeded 75%. Travel categorized as Short (<483 km), Moderate (484-836 km), Long (837-1331 km), and Very Long (>1332 km).

## Findings
Home teams averaged a 5.34-point advantage (ES = 0.58, 100% likelihood). No significant penalty differential was found between home and away teams. Away travel <483 km produced no meaningful disadvantage (ES = -0.23, 56% likelihood). All distances >484 km produced moderate disadvantages (ES = -0.61 to -0.77, 99-100% likelihood). Crossing time zones westward produced a ~7.5-point disadvantage (ES = -0.82). Eastward travel showed no clear disadvantage. A 2 SD change in crowd size (~60,000 people) was worth ~11 points to home teams and ~8.7 points against away teams. Per 10,000 fans, away teams were disadvantaged by ~4 points and home teams advantaged by ~2 points.

## Limitations
Data were retrospective and from only two non-consecutive seasons, limiting causal inference and temporal generalizability. Only in-conference games at non-neutral venues were analyzed, excluding bowl games and non-conference matchups. Penalty differentials did not differ significantly, suggesting referee bias was not a strong HA factor in this dataset. Psychological factors like motivation and confidence could not be quantified. Differences in team strength across conferences and geographic regions may confound crowd-HA relationships.

## Practical Implications
Travel exceeding 300 miles should trigger enhanced logistical and recovery planning for away games. Westward cross-time-zone travel appears particularly disruptive and may warrant earlier departure, adjusted sleep schedules, or light exposure protocols. Practitioners should consider incorporating crowd noise simulation into pre-game practice for high-attendance away environments. The roughly 5-point home advantage is comparable to a successful field goal and is practically meaningful for in-season game preparation.

## Athlete Development Notes
College football players aged 18-22 may be more susceptible to travel disruption than professional athletes due to less developed pre-competition routines. Programs should systematically expose younger players to away-game preparation strategies, including managing environmental unfamiliarity and crowd noise, as part of their athletic development.

## Return to Play Notes
While not a return-to-play study, travel-related fatigue and circadian disruption may compound recovery timelines for athletes playing away games following injury. Clinicians overseeing injured athletes should account for scheduled long-distance away travel when projecting return-to-play readiness, particularly for travel involving westward time zone crossings.
