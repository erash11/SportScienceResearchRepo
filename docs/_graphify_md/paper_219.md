# Baugh CM, Gedlaman MA, Daneshvar DH, Kroshus E. Factors Influencing College Football Players' Beliefs About Incurring Football-Related Dementia. Orthop J Sports Med. 2021;9(4):23259671211001129.

## TLDR
Only about 1 in 10 college football players believes he will develop football-related dementia. Players who are linemen, have more suspected concussions, and score higher on deliberative thinking tests are more likely to hold this belief. Risk communication strategies that appeal to experiential rather than deliberative reasoning may be more effective with this population.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study examined factors influencing 296 NCAA Division I Power 5 college football players' beliefs about whether they would develop football-related dementia (CTE, Alzheimer's disease). Approximately 10% of athletes believed football-related dementia was likely in their future. Logistic regressions evaluated associations between this belief and athletic/demographic characteristics, football and general health risk postures, and cognitive reflection test scores. Skill players had lower odds than linemen of believing dementia was likely (OR 0.35; 95% CI 0.14-0.89). Each additional suspected concussion increased odds by 24% (OR 1.24; 95% CI 1.07-1.45). Higher cognitive reflection test scores were positively associated with believing dementia was likely (OR 1.57; 95% CI 1.12-2.21).

## Methods
Cross-sectional survey of 296 NCAA Division I Power 5 football players from 4 teams (spring 2017). Measured: position, years of football, suspected/diagnosed concussion history, 7-point Likert perceived risk of CTE/AD/dementia, composite acute football injury risk perceptions, chronic health risk perceptions, risk-benefit calculations, DOSPERT health subscale, and the 3-item cognitive reflection test (CRT). Logistic regressions with robust standard errors (clustered by team) evaluated associations with the binary outcome of believing football-related dementia was likely.

## Findings
66.8% of players thought football-related dementia unlikely; 22.9% were unsure; 10.3% thought it likely. Skill players had 0.35x the odds of linemen believing dementia was likely. Each additional suspected concussion raised odds by 24%. Greater perceived likelihood of future football injury and chronic health problems were positively associated with believing dementia was likely, while believing long-term benefits outweigh risks was inversely associated. Higher CRT score (OR 1.57) was positively associated with believing dementia was likely. Diagnosed concussions, years of football, race, and parental education were not significantly associated.

## Limitations
Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference. Only 4 of 65 recruited teams participated, potentially limiting generalizability, though participating and non-participating teams were similar on observable characteristics. No concussion definition was provided, allowing varying conceptualizations. Only deliberative risk perceptions were assessed; experiential and affective perceptions were not. Numeracy was not evaluated. Findings apply only to male college football players.

## Practical Implications
Sports medicine clinicians are well-positioned to facilitate individualized conversations about long-term health risks with athletes. Because athletes higher in deliberative thinking are more likely to already recognize dementia risk, risk communication strategies targeting the broader athlete population should emphasize experiential or 'gist'-based messaging rather than probabilistic statistics. Institutions have an ethical obligation to ensure athletes are appropriately informed of participation risks.

## Athlete Development Notes
Understanding athletes' risk perceptions can inform how athletic departments and sports medicine staff structure educational programming about long-term brain health. Linemen — who had lower perceived risk — may require more targeted education about cumulative head impact exposure and associated neurological risks. Concussion history tracking and counseling should include discussion of long-term neurodegenerative risk.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes who have sustained more suspected concussions are more likely to perceive long-term dementia risk, suggesting a potential opening for clinicians to engage in longitudinal risk counseling during post-concussion return-to-play encounters. Clinicians should use RTP visits not only to clear athletes for return but also to reinforce awareness of cumulative long-term brain health risks.
