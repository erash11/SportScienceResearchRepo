# Lonie, T. A., Brade, C. J., Finucane, M. E., Jacques, A., & Grisbrook, T. L. (2019). Hip adduction and abduction strength and adduction-to-abduction ratio changes across an Australian Football League season. Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport, 23(2), 130–135.

## TLDR
Hip adductor and abductor strength change meaningfully across an Australian Football season: adduction strength and the adduction-to-abduction ratio peak at mid-season before declining at post-season below pre-season values. Relying solely on pre-season hip strength testing to screen for groin injury risk or guide return-to-play criteria is insufficient—strength profiles shift considerably through the year. Regular in-season testing is needed to accurately reflect a player's current neuromuscular condition, and training load was not associated with these changes, suggesting that training volume alone does not explain the seasonal pattern.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional repeated-measures study examined changes in isometric hip adduction strength, abduction strength, and adduction-to-abduction ratio across an Australian Football (AF) League season in 38 uninjured elite players from one AFL club. Maximal isometric hip strength was measured at four time points using a hand-held dynamometer with external belt fixation: start of pre-season (T1), end of pre-season (T2), mid-season (T3), and post-season (T4). Hip adduction strength and the adduction-to-abduction ratio were significantly greater at mid-season (T3) compared to pre-season (T1), while both adduction and abduction strength were significantly weaker at post-season (T4) versus T1. No differences were found between preferred and non-preferred kicking legs in adduction or abduction, though the adduction-to-abduction ratio of the preferred leg was significantly lower than the non-preferred leg at T3 and T4.

## Methods
Cross-sectional repeated measures design. 38 elite AFL players from one club (2016 season). Maximal isometric hip adduction and abduction strength measured at T1 (start pre-season, November 2015), T2 (end pre-season, February 2016), T3 (mid-season, June 2016), and T4 (post-season, August 2016) using a hand-held dynamometer (PowerTrack II Commander, JTECH) with external belt fixation. Adduction-to-abduction ratio calculated by dividing maximal adduction by maximal abduction score. Training load quantified via session-RPE × duration. Adjusted linear mixed regression models with player characteristics (age, BM, AFL experience, games played prior to and in 2016) as covariates.

## Findings
Hip adduction strength was significantly greater at T3 vs. T1 (+22.71N, p<0.001) and significantly weaker at T4 vs. T1 (−18.6N, p=0.004). No significant difference between T1 and T2 for adduction. Hip abduction strength was significantly weaker at T4 vs. T1 (−24.67N, p<0.001); no difference between T1–T2 or T1–T3. Adduction-to-abduction ratio was significantly greater at T3 (+0.15N, p<0.001) vs. T1. No overall differences between preferred and non-preferred kicking legs in adduction (p=0.409) or abduction (p=0.602). However, at T3 and T4, the preferred leg adduction-to-abduction ratio was significantly lower than the non-preferred leg (T3: −0.14N, p=0.020; T4: −0.15N, p=0.019). Training load was not significantly associated with hip strength changes. Body mass was associated with abduction strength and the ratio.

## Limitations
Single club limits generalizability. Post-season player motivation and availability may have influenced T4 results (reduced effort anecdotally noted). Training load was not recorded during the off-season (prior to T1). The study was observational and cannot determine causation for the seasonal strength changes. No injured players were included, so findings apply only to uninjured athletes.

## Practical Implications
Pre-season hip strength testing alone cannot serve as a reliable reference point for the entire season—values change significantly by mid-season and post-season. Strength and conditioning and sports medicine staff should implement regular in-season hip strength monitoring (e.g., mid-season and post-season) to track individual player changes. The preferred kicking leg shows a relatively lower adduction-to-abduction ratio at mid-season and post-season compared to the non-preferred leg, which may reflect asymmetric loading from kicking demands and could be relevant for groin injury risk stratification during the competitive season.

## Athlete Development Notes
For developing AFL players, this study highlights that strength profiles are not static across a season and that pre-season screening captures only a single snapshot. Developmental programs should incorporate longitudinal strength monitoring to detect individual athletes whose adduction strength or adduction-to-abduction ratio deviates from expected seasonal trajectories. The groin-protective threshold of >80% adduction-to-abduction ratio may need to be evaluated relative to where a player is in their seasonal cycle, not just their pre-season value.

## Return to Play Notes
Return-to-play benchmarks for groin strain in AFL commonly use the uninjured limb as a reference, or target an adduction-to-abduction ratio of 90–100%. This study shows that hip strength symmetry and ratios fluctuate across the season in uninjured players, which has direct implications for RTP criteria. Using a pre-season value or a static normative reference may be misleading if a player's uninjured baseline has shifted. RTP assessments should ideally compare the recovering limb to in-season normative data for that athlete or to matched in-season data from uninjured teammates.
