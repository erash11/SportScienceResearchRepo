# Miller KC, Adams WM. Common body temperature sites provide invalid measures of body core temperature in hyperthermic humans wearing American football uniforms. Temperature. 2021;8(2):166-175. doi: 10.1080/23328940.2020.1829940

## TLDR
None of the four commonly used non-invasive temperature sites — oral, axillary, tympanic, or forehead strip — are valid for diagnosing or monitoring exertional heat stroke (EHS) in football players wearing full pads. Oral and axillary sites exceeded the clinical validity threshold at every one of 34 measurement time points. Axillary temperature was catastrophically inaccurate during cold-water immersion (mean bias 11.8°C), dropping nearly to water temperature while rectal temperature remained dangerously elevated. Misdiagnosis based on peripheral temperature could result in premature termination of life-saving CWI. Rectal temperature is the only valid and clinically available standard of care for EHS in football.

## Abstract
This experimental study evaluated the criterion validity of four common body temperature measurement sites — oral (T-ORL), axillary (T-AXL), aural/tympanic (T-EAR), and forehead liquid crystal monitor (T-FHD) — as surrogates for rectal temperature (T-REC) in 13 men wearing full American football uniform pads (PADS). Participants underwent four sequential phases in a controlled environmental chamber: rest, exercise-induced hyperthermia (T-REC 39.7-39.8°C), cold-water immersion (CWI, 10°C) until all sites read ≤38°C, and 15-minute post-immersion recovery. All four peripheral sites demonstrated unacceptable bias (exceeding ±0.27°C) from T-REC across testing conditions.

## Methods
Controlled laboratory experiment; 13 healthy recreationally active unacclimated men (age 22±2 y; mass 77.5±8.8 kg; height 181.3±5.7 cm) wore full American football PADS in an environmental chamber (38.7±0.8°C, 38.9±2.4% RH). Phases: 10-min rest; exercise (repeated bouts of 2-min running at 90% age-predicted max HR + 2.25-min walking at 4.8 km/h) until T-REC reached 39.7-39.8°C; CWI at 10.0±0.1°C (non-circulating tub) until all sites ≤38°C; 15-min post-immersion recovery. T-REC: rectal thermistor 15 cm past anal sphincter. T-AXL: taped skin thermistor. T-ORL: digital oral thermometer. T-EAR: digital ear thermometer. T-FHD: liquid crystal forehead monitor. Two-way repeated measures ANOVA (site x time); Bland-Altman bias analysis. Validity criterion: ≤±0.27°C bias from T-REC.

## Findings
T-ORL and T-AXL statistically differed from T-REC and exceeded ±0.27°C bias threshold at all 34 measurement time points (100%). T-FHD differed from T-REC 8 times at rest, 8 times during exercise, 0 during CWI, and twice during recovery (18/34, 53%); exceeded bias threshold 28 times (82%). T-EAR differed from T-REC 5 times at rest, 0 during exercise, 5 during CWI, once during recovery (11/34, 32%); exceeded threshold 15 times (44%). During CWI T-AXL showed mean bias of 11.8±5.5°C, severely underestimating T-REC. Overall mean bias: T-AXL 4.7±1.9°C, T-ORL 1.0±0.2°C, T-EAR 0.5±0.2°C, T-FHD -0.3±0.4°C. Limits of agreement ranged from -0.3 to 1.4°C (aural) to -6.8 to 15.9°C (axillary).

## Limitations
T-AXL measured via taped thermistor rather than a standard clinical digital axillary thermometer, potentially creating a warm microclimate; only one PADS uniform configuration tested (not shell configurations); healthy non-athlete male participants only; small convenience sample (13 completers of 15 enrolled); non-continuous recording of T-ORL, T-EAR, T-FHD during CWI due to device lead times; single testing session per participant.

## Practical Implications
Athletic trainers and physicians covering American football must use rectal temperature as the sole valid tool for diagnosing and monitoring EHS. Oral, axillary, tympanic, and forehead devices must not be used as surrogates in padded football players. Sideline emergency action plans must include rectal temperature measurement capability and must not delay or discontinue cold-water immersion based on peripheral temperature readings. Misdiagnosis of EHS as heat exhaustion based on a falsely low T-ORL or T-AXL reading could be fatal.

## Athlete Development Notes
American football players are 11 times more likely to develop exertional heat illness than athletes in all other sports combined at the high school level. PADS cover ~75% of body surface area and impair evaporative cooling, elevating core temperature more rapidly during exercise. Heat acclimatization progressions and practice policies should account for the dramatically increased thermal burden of full-equipment training. Preseason is the highest-risk period.

## Return to Play Notes
EHS cannot be confirmed or ruled out, and cold-water immersion cannot be safely terminated, using any temperature measurement other than rectal temperature in football players wearing equipment. T-AXL in particular drops nearly to water temperature within seconds of CWI while T-REC remains elevated, creating a dangerous false impression of adequate cooling. Safe RTP after EHS requires confirmed T-REC ≤38°C, complete medical evaluation, and a graduated heat re-acclimatization protocol before return to full activity.
