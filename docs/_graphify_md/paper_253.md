# Moreno-Perez V, Sotos-Martinez V, Lopez-Valenciano A, Lopez Del-Campo R, Resta R, Del Coso J. Hamstring muscle injury is preceded by a short period of higher running demands in professional football players. Biology of Sport. 2024;41(1):227–233.

## TLDR
In professional football, hamstring muscle injuries during matches are almost always (88.6% of cases) preceded by a short burst of unusually high-intensity running in the 5 minutes immediately before the injury — specifically running at speeds ≥21 km/h. The risk of injury was over 7 times higher when a player ran more than 30 m at sprint velocity in any 5-minute window compared to their own norm from recent matches. Injuries were also more common in the final 15 minutes of each half, highlighting the combined role of fatigue and acute sprint demands. For football staff, these findings support designing training programs that include short bouts of high-intensity running in controlled states of fatigue to prepare players for the specific demands that precipitate match injuries.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study examined match running patterns in the 5 and 15 minutes preceding non-contact hamstring muscle injuries in male professional football players. A total of 281 players from 7 LaLiga teams were monitored across three seasons (2016–2019) using the validated Mediacoach multi-camera tracking system. Among these, 36 players sustained 44 non-contact hamstring muscle injuries during official matches (incidence: 3.34 injuries/1000 h of match exposure; mean time loss 33±28 days). Running distances at different speed thresholds (total, 18.0–20.9 km/h, 21.0–23.9 km/h, ≥24 km/h) for the 5-min and 15-min pre-injury periods were compared to the same player's mean values from the previous 5 matches at the same match time points. In the 15-min prior to injury, running was similar to controls. However, in the 5-min prior to injury, players ran significantly more at 21.0–23.9 km/h (p<0.001; ES=0.57) and ≥24 km/h (p<0.001; ES=0.78), with 88.6% of injuries preceded by greater ≥21 km/h running distance. The odds ratio for HSI was 7.147 for players who ran >30 m at ≥21 km/h in a 5-min period (p<0.001).

## Methods
Prospective cohort study. 281 male professional LaLiga players from 7 teams monitored over three seasons (2016–2019). Non-contact hamstring muscle injuries (n=44) confirmed by MRI were recorded by club medical staff including minute of occurrence. Running data captured via Mediacoach (8-camera system, 25 Hz). Injuries analyzed for 5-min and 15-min pre-injury running at multiple speed thresholds. Control data: same player's average for the same match time periods across the preceding 5 matches (within 30 days). Statistical tests: Shapiro-Wilk normality test; χ² tests for injury distribution; two-way ANOVA (running speed × injury condition); paired t-tests for total distance and sprint distance; odds ratio with 95% CI for sprint threshold exceedance (p<0.05).

## Findings
44 non-contact hamstring injuries identified (3.34/1000 h match exposure). 18.2% were recurrent injuries. More injuries occurred in the final 15 min of the first half (p<0.05). Mean time loss: 33±28 days (range 7–117 days). In the 15-min pre-injury period: running distances at all speeds were similar to controls (p from 0.08 to 0.59). In the 5-min pre-injury period: total distance was similar (p=0.960), but distance at 21.0–23.9 km/h was significantly greater (injury vs. control; p<0.001; ES=0.57) and at ≥24 km/h was significantly greater (p<0.001; ES=0.78). Overall ≥21 km/h distance: 51.1±24.7 m (injury) vs 28.8±11.5 m (control); p<0.001; ES=1.09. 39 of 44 injuries (88.6%) preceded by greater ≥21 km/h distance. OR for injury = 7.147 (95% CI: 2.69–18.99) for players running >30 m at ≥21 km/h in a 5-min window.

## Limitations
Sample limited to male professional LaLiga players; may not generalize to female players or other sports. Training load during the week before match injuries was not quantified. Only 5-min and 15-min windows analyzed; the contribution of fatigue accumulated throughout the entire match was not fully captured. All injuries occurred during competitive matches only; training-related hamstring strains (which may have different mechanisms) were excluded. 8 of 44 injuries were recurrent, limiting sub-analysis by injury history with adequate statistical power. The study did not measure internal load, perceived fatigue, or neuromuscular fatigue state at time of injury.

## Practical Implications
Training programs should include short-duration (5-min) bouts of high-intensity running (>21 km/h) performed in a state of controlled fatigue to prepare players for the specific conditions that precede match hamstring injuries. Recovery strategies and substitution decisions should account for the elevated injury risk in the final 15 minutes of each half, when fatigue is highest. Match load monitoring should flag individual players who are executing unusually high sprint volumes in 5-minute windows during live matches, as these represent acute injury risk periods. Prevention programs should incorporate these sprint-exposure conditions in training.

## Athlete Development Notes
Training programs for developing football players should progressively introduce short bouts of maximal or near-maximal sprint running within competitive scenarios that mirror end-of-half fatigue states. Repeated sprint ability training and exposure to high eccentric demands of the posterior thigh during acceleration, braking, and high-speed running — especially performed under fatigue — will build the hamstring resilience required to withstand the acute demands that most commonly precede injury in professional matches.

## Return to Play Notes
The finding that 88.6% of match hamstring injuries are preceded by a >21 km/h sprint effort — often during states of accumulated fatigue — has direct RTP implications. Before a player is cleared for full match participation, RTP protocols should include high-intensity sprint efforts at match-realistic intensity (≥21 km/h) performed in a fatigued state (e.g., late in a training session or simulated match). Clearance based solely on straight-line sprinting at full intensity under fresh conditions may be insufficient to replicate the conditions under which reinjury is most likely.
