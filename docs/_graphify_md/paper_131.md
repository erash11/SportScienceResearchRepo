# Dello Iacono A, Datson N, Clubb J, Lacome M, Sullivan A, Shushan T. Data analytics practices and reporting strategies in senior football: insights into athlete health and performance from over 200 practitioners worldwide. Science and Medicine in Football. 2025. DOI: 10.1080/24733938.2025.2476478

## TLDR
A global survey of over 200 football practitioners reveals that data analytics in professional soccer is characterized by high heterogeneity and heavy reliance on exploratory analysis and professional community guidance, with modelling and predictive approaches still underutilized. Locomotive GPS metrics dominate training load monitoring, spreadsheets are the primary analytics tool, and scientific literature is the least-consulted evidence source across all departments. Football organizations seeking to build effective data analytics systems should prioritize standardizing metrics, investing in data infrastructure beyond spreadsheets, improving data literacy, and bridging the gap between applied practice and research evidence.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional, observational study surveyed 206 football practitioners from all six FIFA confederations to describe current data analytics practices in senior professional and semi-professional football, focusing on athlete health and performance. The survey covered personnel involved, data collected, analytical techniques employed, and reporting/dissemination strategies across performance (73%), data (18%), and medical (9%) departments. Significant heterogeneity was observed in training load and performance metrics, methodological attributes, and measurement properties across departments. Exploratory data analysis was the most common analytical approach (90%), while modelling, forecasting, and prediction were the least used (54%). Spreadsheets were cited as the primary solution for data wrangling and reporting tasks, and daily/weekly reporting to coaching, medical, and performance staff was the most common dissemination cadence.

## Methods
Cross-sectional observational study following STROBE guidelines. A convenience sample of 206 practitioners working in senior (≥18 years) professional or semi-professional football completed an online survey (Qualtrics platform) from February 23 to June 24, 2024. The survey was developed by six co-investigators, piloted in three versions, and reviewed by academic peers and industry professionals. It covered six domains: 'Who' (demographics), 'Evidence, metrics, attributes and approaches' (evidence sources, training load/performance metrics, methodological properties, analytical approaches), 'Where and content' (data storage and reporting), 'Target' (dissemination practices by stakeholder), and 'Live' (real-time data use). Respondents were grouped into performance (n=150; 73%), data (n=38; 18%), and medical (n=18; 9%) departments. Descriptive statistics (frequency, percentage, mean±SD, median, IQR) were used; subgroup analyses by department and competition tier were conducted in R (v2024.04.2).

## Findings
Of 206 respondents, 86% were male and 68% were based in European football organizations (UEFA). Performance and medical staff most frequently cited the professional industry/community as their top evidence source (63% and 67% respectively), while data staff relied more on in-house projects (57%). Scientific literature was the least-preferred evidence source across all groups. Locomotive variables (speed-zone-based distances/counts) were the most commonly used training load metric across all departments (69-72%). Field- and gym-based testing were the top two performance outcome measure categories. Exploratory data analysis was used by 90% of respondents, while modelling/forecasting/prediction was least common (54%). Most respondents (73%) used more than one storage solution; cloud-based systems dominated. Spreadsheets were the most-used tool for both data aggregation/analysis (76%) and reporting/visualization (62%). Summary tables and bar plots were the most common report contents. Daily and weekly reporting were most common across all tiers and stakeholder groups.

## Limitations
Convenience sampling limits generalizability to the broader global practitioner population. Most respondents were male (86%) and Europe-based (68%), limiting global representativeness. Only 13% of respondents worked exclusively with female players, precluding meaningful subgroup analysis for women's football practices. The survey was available only in English, potentially limiting diversity. Cross-sectional design captures a single time-point snapshot with potential social desirability bias in self-report responses. The distinction between professional and semi-professional levels was contextually dependent, limiting comparisons across competitive tiers.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football performance organizations can use these benchmarking data to evaluate their own data analytics infrastructure relative to global professional football standards. The dominance of professional industry knowledge over scientific literature as a primary guidance source highlights a translational gap; forward-thinking programs can gain competitive advantage by better integrating research evidence. The prevalence of spreadsheet-based workflows signals an opportunity to invest in more robust data management platforms. The near-universal use of exploratory data analysis, combined with limited modelling and prediction capabilities, suggests that hiring or developing data analytics expertise is a key organizational differentiator. Standardizing key metrics and reporting templates across performance, medical, and data departments can improve cross-functional efficiency and decision-making.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athlete development programs, this survey establishes that locomotive GPS metrics (speed zones, distance) and field/gym-based performance tests are the global professional standard for monitoring and evaluating players. Development programs should ensure they are collecting these benchmark metrics to enable comparisons against professional norms. The study also emphasizes the importance of regular (daily/weekly) reporting cycles to inform training programming — a practice that should be embedded in development environments. Investing in data literacy education for development coaches and sports scientists is warranted, given the identified gap between research evidence and applied practice.

## Return to Play Notes
The survey findings have indirect RTP relevance for football organizations seeking to build integrated monitoring systems that support medical decision-making. The heterogeneity in metrics and analytical approaches across medical and performance departments underscores the need for coordinated, multi-department data systems in which medical staff have access to performance monitoring data during rehabilitation progressions. The identification of daily reporting as the most common cadence in top-tier clubs supports frequent monitoring during RTP progressions. Adoption of more standardized, evidence-based metrics and analytical approaches would improve the precision and consistency of RTP readiness assessments across club departments.
