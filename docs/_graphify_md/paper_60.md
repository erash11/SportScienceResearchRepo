# Yearby T, Myszka S, Roberts WM, Woods CT, Davids K. Applying an ecological approach to practice design in American football: some case examples on best practice. Sports Coaching Review. 2022;DOI:10.1080/21640629.2022.2057698.

## TLDR
Coaches in American football should shift from rote-drill approaches to ecological, constraints-led practice designs that present athletes with 'alive' movement problems representative of game demands, fostering adaptability over automaticity.

## Abstract
This theory-practice paper outlines an ecological dynamics rationale for practice design in American football, arguing that traditional coach-centred, drill-based approaches decontextualize athlete behaviour from the game environment. The authors present two novel case examples — one with a veteran NFL defensive end and one with high school linebackers — demonstrating how a constraints-led, representative learning design (RLD) framework can be applied. The paper advocates for coaches to act as practice designers who create information-rich environments where athletes interact with authentic affordances. Key constructs include the athlete-environment mutuality, nonlinear pedagogy, and repetition without repetition.

## Methods
Theory-practice paper drawing on ecological dynamics and nonlinear pedagogy frameworks. Two qualitative case examples are presented: (1) an NFL Pro Bowl defensive end working with a movement skill acquisition coach, and (2) high school linebackers (ages 16–18) participating in representative co-design tackle drills. Constraint manipulations were systematically varied and athlete movement responses observed and described narratively.

## Findings
• Traditional American football practice relies heavily on unopposed drills, part-practice, and prescribed technique repetition that lack representative information sources
• Ecological dynamics re-frames speed as 'football speed' — functional movement within the game environment rather than isolated linear speed metrics
• A constraints-led approach (CLA) manipulates task, environmental, and organismic constraints to present athletes with 'alive' movement problems
• Case 1 (NFL DE): representative practice manipulating QB movement, down-and-distance, surface, and double-team scenarios expanded the athlete's movement repertoire and revealed new pass-rush strategies
• Case 2 (HS linebackers): open-space tackle drill starting at 20-yd lines with staggered initial positions created authentic interpersonal distance and velocity affordances for closing on ball carriers
• Both cases demonstrated that problem-solving dexterity emerged through repetition without repetition rather than fixed technique rehearsal

## Limitations
No experimental control groups or quantitative outcome measures were used; findings rely on observational, narrative case descriptions. The generalizability of NFL-level case findings to college or high school populations is limited. The paper acknowledges the need for more empirical research on ecological dynamics in American football specifically.

## Practical Implications
Strength and conditioning and position coaches should evaluate whether individual and group practice tasks contain the perceptual information athletes encounter in competition. Replacing static drills (air routes, sled work, stationary QB simulations) with co-designed, representative tasks can improve athletes' ability to detect affordances and make functional movement decisions under game-like constraints. Coaches should embrace their role as practice designers rather than technique instructors.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football athlete development at all levels (high school through NFL). The framework challenges the sport's traditional reliance on playbook-driven, coach-centred approaches and provides practical tools — representative task design, affordance emphasis, constraint manipulation — for developing football-specific movement intelligence. Particularly relevant for skill positions and pass rushers where perceptual coupling with dynamic opponents is critical.

## Return to Play Notes
Not a return-to-play study. However, the ecological dynamics framework has implications for rehab-to-performance reintegration: athletes returning from injury should be reintegrated through progressively representative movement problems rather than isolated drill work, ensuring perception-action coupling is restored before full competitive exposure.
