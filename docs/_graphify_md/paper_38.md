# Mitton JL, Meyers MC, van Woerden I, Lindsay RP, Sterling JC, Robinson SK. Adductor-related groin injury: Prevalence and etiology during NCAA football games on artificial and natural grass surfaces. PM&R. 2025;1–10.

## TLDR
Across 16 seasons of NCAA FBS football, heavyweight artificial turf was as safe as natural grass for adductor-related groin injuries, with surface type explaining minimal variance in incidence or severity; focus should shift to early detection and adductor-specific prevention programming.

## Abstract
This 16-season (2006–2021) prospective cohort study quantified the incidence and etiology of adductor-related groin injuries during NCAA Football Bowl Subdivision (FBS) games across heavyweight artificial turf and natural grass surfaces at 41 universities. Of 9360 total game injuries across 2243 documented games, 111 (1.2%) were adductor-related groin injuries: 48 (43.2%) on artificial turf and 63 (56.8%) on natural grass, with no statistically significant surface effect on any outcome. The vast majority were minor muscle strains (87%), occurring most frequently during defensive play (41%) and noncontact sprinting/running (39%). MANOVA indicated no significant difference between surfaces in injury severity, classification, category, primary type, mechanism, or situation.

## Methods
Prospective cohort; 41 FBS universities; 2006–2021 competitive seasons; injury surveillance via two-sided single-page forms completed by certified ATCs within 7 business days; artificial turf restricted to heavyweight infill systems (≥9.0 lbs/ft²); 23 player positions condensed into offense, defense, special teams and six skill positions; outcomes: injury severity (minor 0–6 days, substantial 7–21 days, severe ≥22 days), classification (acute/recurrent), category (shoe-surface contact/noncontact), primary injury type, mechanism, situation, player position, cleat design, field conditions, temperature, imaging, surgery, and turf age; injury incidence rate = (injuries ÷ games) × 10; IBM SPSS 28.0; MANOVA with Wilks' λ; effect sizes as partial η².

## Findings
• 111 adductor-related groin injuries over 16 seasons (48 artificial turf, 63 natural grass); IIR = 0.45 vs. 0.53 per 10 games; no significant surface effect (Wilks' λ p = 0.602; η² = 0.009).
• 87% minor injuries; 16.2% substantial; 3.6% severe—no significant surface difference in severity (p = 0.602).
• Primary injury type: muscle strain/spasm predominated on both surfaces (42 artificial, 55 natural grass).
• Most common mechanisms: noncontact sprinting/running (39%) and defensive play situations (41% on artificial turf).
• No significant surface effect for injury classification, category, mechanism, situation, player position, cleat design, field conditions, temperature, imaging, surgery, or turf age.
• Defensive players (especially secondary) experienced the highest counts on both surfaces.
• Molded/hybrid cleats were most common at injury, consistent with their prevalence in the game population.

## Limitations
Inability to control for inherent random variation in team-sport injury; athlete strength/conditioning and body composition status not controlled; previous injury history not fully controlled; opportunity to obtain long-term quality-of-life data was beyond scope; findings generalise only to FBS-level competition with heavyweight artificial turf; partially funded by FieldTurf (potential conflict of interest disclosed).

## Practical Implications
Playing surface type (heavyweight artificial turf vs. natural grass) should not be a primary concern when managing adductor injury risk in college football. Prevention focus should be on early detection of adductor strains, adductor strength and flexibility assessment, and implementation of structured resistance programs that progress from general lower-body development to adductor-specific training. Defensive players, particularly secondary, warrant heightened monitoring given their higher observed injury counts.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football programs at all levels. Adductor strains comprise ~1.2% of game injuries and are concentrated in high-speed, change-of-direction movements common to defensive backs and linebackers. Pre-season adductor screening (squeeze tests, Copenhagen adduction strength) and progressive adductor loading programs (Copenhagen adduction exercise, resisted lateral band walks, adductor-specific machines) should be incorporated into S&C programming to reduce strain risk and improve return-to-sport timelines.

## Return to Play Notes
Following adductor-related groin trauma, 87% of cases were minor (0–6 day time loss), suggesting most athletes can return within the same week. A structured rehabilitation progression—general lower-body resistance training in the first days post-injury advancing to adductor-specific loading in subsequent weeks—is recommended. Early detection and avoiding premature return are prioritised over surface modification as the key return-to-play considerations.
