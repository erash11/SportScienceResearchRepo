# Baker HP, Lin Y, Lee CS, Portney DA, Scott B, Athiviraham A. Concussions Increase the Odds of Lower-Extremity Injuries in National Football League Players: Four-Year Review of Publicly Available Data. Arthroscopy, Sports Medicine, and Rehabilitation. 2022;4(4):e1489-e1495. DOI: 10.1016/j.asmr.2022.05.011

## TLDR
NFL players who suffer even a single concussion have more than twice the odds of sustaining a lower-extremity injury compared to matched non-concussed peers, and this risk is similar regardless of whether they have sustained one or multiple concussions. This suggests that neuromuscular control deficits associated with concussion persist beyond clinical neurologic clearance and represent a real, ongoing injury risk. Football performance staff should implement post-concussion neuromuscular screening and targeted lower-extremity injury prevention protocols as a routine part of the return-to-play continuum.

## Abstract
This retrospective comparative prognostic study examined whether single or multiple concussions increase the odds of lower-extremity (LE) injuries in NFL players across four regular seasons (2016–2020). All active NFL players who sustained multiple concussions (MC, n=55) were identified from publicly available injury data and age- and position-matched to single concussion (SC, n=54) and no concussion (NC, n=55) cohorts. LE injuries were documented and compared using binary logistic regression and Kaplan-Meier survival analysis. Both the MC and SC cohorts had significantly greater odds of sustaining a LE injury compared to the NC cohort (OR 2.92 and 2.28, respectively), but no significant difference existed between MC and SC cohorts. Time to LE injury after RTP was significantly shorter in the SC group versus MC group within one year of concussion.

## Methods
Retrospective Level III comparative prognostic trial using publicly available NFL injury reports from the 2016–2017 through 2019–2020 regular seasons (6,784 eligible athletes). MC cohort (n=55; 39 with 2, 16 with 3 concussions) identified from public records cross-referenced across multiple sources (NFL.com, pro-football-reference.com, ESPN, CBS Sports). Age- and position-matched SC (n=54) and NC (n=55) cohorts created using MEDCALC case-control matching (1:1). Athletes with concussion in the prior season were excluded. LE injuries (hip, thigh, knee, leg, ankle, foot) were documented for the remainder of the season following concussion. Binary logistic regression calculated odds ratios; Kaplan-Meier and log-rank tests compared time-to-LE-injury in SC vs. MC cohorts censored at 90 days and 1 year.

## Findings
199 total LE injuries across 3 cohorts: 86 in MC (43%), 84 in SC (42%), 29 in NC (15%). Odds of LE injury significantly higher in MC vs. NC (OR 2.92, 95% CI 1.7–4.9, P=.001) and SC vs. NC (OR 2.28, 95% CI 1.5–3.6, P=.001). No significant difference between SC and MC cohorts (OR 1.00, 95% CI 0.7–1.3, P=.97). Time to LE injury after RTP was significantly shorter in the SC group vs. MC group within 1 year of concussion (P=.01); no significant difference in first 90 days (P=.62). MC players missed the most games due to LE injury (165/426 total missed games, 39%). Average games missed per LE injury: MC=3.0, SC=2.9, NC=1.8.

## Limitations
Publicly available injury reports are subject to underreporting and reporting errors; injury severity, imaging, and medical clearance details were unavailable. Preseason injuries and postseason play were excluded. Career concussion history prior to the study period (high school, college) was not captured. Selection bias is possible — high-exposure players are more likely to sustain both concussions and LE injuries. Study spans only 4 NFL seasons, limiting longitudinal generalizability. Subconcussive injuries not formally diagnosed were not captured.

## Practical Implications
Concussion history — even a single event — should be incorporated into LE injury risk stratification when athletes return to play. Extended neuromuscular monitoring, proprioceptive training, and gait and balance assessment after RTP from concussion may reduce secondary LE injury risk. Sports medicine and strength staff should implement injury-prevention protocols specifically targeting the post-concussion RTP window, not just at initial clearance but throughout the remainder of the competitive season.

## Athlete Development Notes
Post-concussion neuromuscular deficits in balance, proprioception, and gait appear to persist beyond clinical RTP clearance and predispose NFL athletes to ankle, knee, and hip injuries during the competitive season. Strength and conditioning programs for post-concussion athletes should incorporate balance, proprioceptive, and reactive neuromuscular training before full return to contact. These findings justify extending post-concussion monitoring and movement-quality screening throughout the entire season following concussion.

## Return to Play Notes
NFL players cleared for RTP following concussion remain at significantly elevated risk for lower-extremity injury throughout the remainder of the season and into the following year. RTP protocols should extend beyond symptom resolution and cognitive testing to include objective neuromuscular readiness criteria — such as single-leg balance, hop testing, and dynamic movement screening — before full clearance to contact practice and games. Given that time to LE injury was shorter in the SC group within 1 year, even single-concussion athletes warrant ongoing monitoring well beyond initial RTP clearance.
