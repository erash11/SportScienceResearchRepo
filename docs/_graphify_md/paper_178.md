# Gillen ZM, Burch RF, Saucier DN, Strawderman L, Luczak T, Piroli A, Petway AJ, Rath T. Effects of a Strength and Conditioning Offseason Program on Countermovement Jump Ground Reaction Forces in Division I American Football Players. J Strength Cond Res. 2023;XX(X):1–10. DOI: 10.1519/JSC.0000000000004600

## TLDR
Ten weeks of an offseason S&C program significantly improved vertical jump performance in Division I football players across multiple GRF metrics for both bilateral and unilateral jumps. Key findings: CMJ jump height increased (~5%), propulsive phase duration decreased (indicating more explosive force application), and the CMJ efficiency index improved (force at the low position closer to peak force). Single-leg jumps showed even broader improvements, including braking RFD, eccentric force, and propulsive power for both legs. This study is the first to document pre-post GRF changes from an offseason program in this population, providing a detailed framework for how S&C professionals can use force plate data to quantify program effectiveness—beyond simple jump height—and monitor eccentric and concentric adaptations that transfer to sprint, change-of-direction, and injury-resilience outcomes on the field.

## Abstract
This pre-post repeated-measures study examined the effects of a 10-week NCAA Division I football offseason strength and conditioning program on ground reaction forces (GRFs) during bilateral countermovement jumps (CMJ) and single-leg jumps (SLJ) in 16 players. Force plates quantified unweighting, braking, propulsive, and performance metrics, along with a jump efficiency index. CMJ propulsive phase duration decreased while peak braking power, peak propulsive power, mean propulsive force, and jump height increased. SLJ improvements included peak braking power, force at the low position, braking RFD, eccentric force, peak propulsive power, mean propulsive force, and jump height in both legs. Jump efficiency improved for both CMJ and SLJ.

## Methods
Pre-post repeated-measures design with 16 NCAA Division I American football players (height 187.01±3.05 cm; body mass 102.33±9.33 kg) who completed ≥90% of a 10-week offseason S&C program (resistance training, plyometrics, speed/agility, anaerobic conditioning; 4 sessions/week with a deload in week 6). Players performed 3 CMJ and 3 SLJ (each leg) attempts on a single AMTI AccuGait force plate (1 kHz) at pre- and post-testing. GRFs were processed offline in LabVIEW to calculate unweighting, braking, propulsive, and performance metrics including a jump efficiency index. Dependent samples t-tests (CMJ) and two-way repeated-measures ANOVA (leg × time for SLJ) were used; alpha=0.05.

## Findings
Body mass increased significantly (p=0.043; d=0.553). CMJ: peak braking power increased (p=0.023; d=0.545), propulsive phase duration decreased (p=0.007; d=0.691), peak propulsive power increased (p<0.001; d=1.201), mean propulsive force increased (p=0.012; d=0.630), and jump height increased (p=0.005; d=0.727). CMJ efficiency index improved (p=0.016; d=0.676; 0.07→0.04). SLJ (both legs): peak braking power (p=0.044; η²=0.245), force at low position (p<0.001; η²=0.662), braking RFD (p<0.001; η²=0.587), eccentric force (p=0.030; η²=0.276), peak propulsive power (p=0.001; η²=0.555), mean propulsive force (p=0.026; η²=0.288), and jump height (p=0.002; η²=0.469) all increased. SLJ efficiency index improved (p=0.001; η²=0.552). No significant leg × time interactions were found.

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=16) limits position-specific analyses. No control group without S&C training. Jump efficiency index is relatively understudied; reliability data are not yet established and individual responses were variable (5 subjects did not improve CMJ efficiency). Sprint and change-of-direction performance were not concurrently assessed to confirm transfer of jump improvements. Body mass increased, which may confound absolute force interpretations for some metrics. The specific contribution of individual program components (resistance training vs. plyometrics vs. conditioning) cannot be isolated.

## Practical Implications
This study validates the use of bilateral and unilateral force plate CMJ assessments as sensitive tools for monitoring offseason S&C program effectiveness in collegiate football players. Strength and conditioning coaches should track GRF metrics—particularly braking phase variables, propulsive power, and the efficiency index—not just jump height, as these provide mechanistic insight into eccentric and concentric adaptations. SLJ assessments appear especially sensitive to training-induced braking and eccentric force changes and can also inform injury risk monitoring. A 10-week offseason block is sufficient to produce meaningful, statistically significant improvements across multiple GRF metrics, supporting its use as a standard assessment window.

## Athlete Development Notes
The improvements in eccentric (braking) and concentric (propulsive) GRF metrics after 10 weeks reflect enhanced stretch-shortening cycle efficiency, which is foundational for sprint acceleration, cutting, and jumping performance in football. The jump efficiency index—measuring how close force at the low position is to peak force—improved from 7% to 4% difference for the CMJ, suggesting athletes better compressed the 'spring' of the countermovement. For linemen and skill positions alike, tracking these changes across offseason blocks can guide individualized programming decisions and help coaches identify athletes with 'energy leakage' during the eccentric phase that may limit explosive performance.

## Return to Play Notes
The SLJ has been identified in the literature as a potential indicator of knee injury risk and return-to-sport status after ACL reconstruction (Kotsifaki et al., 2022, cited within). This study demonstrates that SLJ GRF metrics improve meaningfully after an offseason program, establishing training-response benchmarks relevant to return-to-play progressions. For athletes recovering from knee injuries—the most common injury type in American football—regular force plate SLJ assessments can help sports medicine staff objectively track recovery of eccentric and propulsive force capabilities and determine readiness for return to full practice and competition.
