# Gabler LF, Patton DA, Reynier KA, Barnett IJ, Miles AM, Dau NZ, Clugston JR, Cobian DG, Harmon KG, Kontos AP, Lynall RC, Mihalik JP, Moran RN, Terry DP, Mayer T, Solomon GS, Sills AK, Arbogast KB, Crandall JR. Distribution of position-specific head impact severities among professional and Division I collegiate American football athletes during games. BMJ Open Sport & Exercise Medicine. 2025;11:e002365. doi:10.1136/bmjsem-2024-002365

## TLDR
This landmark study provides the first rigorous comparison of head impact severity between NFL and Division I collegiate football players using instrumented mouthpieces—the gold standard measurement method. The key finding: head impact severity distributions are not significantly different between the two levels of play across all position groups and kinematic metrics (p≥0.320). Median peak linear accelerations were nearly identical: ~17 g for linemen, ~20–21 g for hybrid and speed positions in both leagues. This means D-I collegiate impact data can serve as a reliable proxy for NFL data, enabling combined datasets to accelerate position-specific helmet design and safety testing. For performance and medical staff, this reinforces that the biomechanical demands on players' heads are consistent from college to the pros.

## Abstract
This study compared head impact severity distributions between NFL and NCAA Division I football players using instrumented mouthpieces (IMs) capable of measuring six degrees-of-freedom head kinematics during games. A total of 243 players (99 NFL from 12 clubs, 144 NCAA D-I from 8 Power Five teams) were instrumented across the 2019–2022 seasons. Head impact severity distributions across multiple kinematic metrics and three position groupings (linemen, hybrid, speed) were compared between leagues using permutation-based Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests with false discovery rate correction.

## Methods
Prospective observational cohort study. 99 NFL players from 12 clubs (2019–2022) and 144 NCAA D-I players from 8 Power Five teams (2021–2022) wore custom-fit IMs with triaxial linear (±400 g) and angular (±20 krad/s²) accelerometers. Events triggered at >10 g linear acceleration. Kinematic data postprocessed with zero-phase 300 Hz filter, integrated for velocity, and transformed to head coordinate system. Head impacts classified using a machine learning model validated against video (precision 98.3%, recall 96.4%) combined with ambient light sensor to confirm IM was worn. NFL data synchronized to Next Gen Stats player tracking; NCAA to Catapult. Top 10% severity impacts reviewed manually. Distributions compared using permutation-based KS tests with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction across 18 comparisons. Position groupings: linemen (OL/DL), hybrid (TE/LB/RB), speed (WR/DB).

## Findings
11,038 head impacts >10 g from 1,208 player-games across 286 player-seasons analyzed (NFL: 5,207 impacts from 547 player-games; NCAA: 5,831 from 661 player-games). No significant between-league differences in any kinematic metric for any position grouping after multiple comparison correction (p≥0.320 for all). Median PLA for linemen: 17.2 g (NFL) vs. 17.0 g (NCAA); hybrid: 20.7 g vs. 20.0 g; speed: 21.0 g vs. 20.8 g. Median differences across all metrics were within 13% of NFL median values. Individual player variability within positions was substantial (highest vs. lowest median PAA among linemen: 1.7x in NFL, 1.5x in NCAA), exceeding between-league differences.

## Limitations
Voluntary participation may introduce sampling bias, particularly for hybrid and speed positions which were underrepresented in the NFL. NFL players were not incentivized, potentially affecting compliance. NCAA data limited to Power Five teams and may not generalize to all collegiate programs. Study only evaluated game severity, not frequency of head impacts or practice environments. No head injury outcome data collected, limiting direct clinical inference. Video verification of impacts was not universally available for NCAA data.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football programs can now be confident that head impact severity data are transferable across levels of play. This supports use of a combined NFL-NCAA dataset for developing and evaluating position-specific helmets and protective equipment standards. Performance and medical staff should recognize that position-specific helmet recommendations developed from collegiate data are directly applicable to NFL players. The high within-position variability also suggests that individual player technique evaluations—not just position-level assessments—may be warranted for athletes with unusually high-severity impact profiles.

## Athlete Development Notes
The similarity of head impact severity across NFL and D-I levels confirms that the biomechanical demands placed on athletes' heads are established early in competitive careers. Athlete development programs should begin implementing head impact mitigation strategies (technique coaching, position-specific helmet selection, contact limitation in practice) at the collegiate level with confidence that these interventions will translate to professional environments.

## Return to Play Notes
Although this study did not examine concussion outcomes directly, its findings establish position-specific impact severity baselines that are relevant to RTP decision-making. Speed positions (WR/DB) demonstrate the highest median impact severity (~21 g PLA), while linemen experience the highest total frequency at lower per-impact magnitudes. This profile should inform individualized RTP protocols and exposure management during staged return-to-contact progressions following concussion.
