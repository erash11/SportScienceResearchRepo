# Franettovich Smith MM, Bonacci J, Mendis MD, Christie C, Rotstein A, Hides JA. Gluteus medius activation during running is a risk factor for season hamstring injuries in elite footballers. J Sci Med Sport. 2016. doi:10.1016/j.jsams.2016.07.004

## TLDR
Elite AFL players who went on to suffer a hamstring injury during the season showed significantly higher gluteus medius (GMED) activation during running — but not walking — compared to uninjured players. This suggests overactivity of the GMED during high-speed running may reflect compensatory lumbo-pelvic control deficits, creating conditions favorable for hamstring strain. Practitioners should assess GMED function during running-specific tasks and consider neuromuscular control of the hip-pelvis region as a modifiable target in hamstring injury prevention programs.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study investigated whether size and activation of the gluteal muscles (GMED and GMAX) were risk factors for hamstring injury in 26 elite male Australian Football League (AFL) players. Bilateral GMED and GMAX muscle volume was measured via MRI and electromyographic (EMG) recordings were obtained during treadmill gait at 6, 12, and 15 km/h at the start of the season. Nine players (35%) sustained a hamstring injury during the season. Higher GMED muscle activity during running at 12 and 15 km/h was significantly associated with in-season hamstring injury (p=0.03, effect sizes 1.1–1.5). No significant differences were found for GMED volume, GMAX volume, or GMAX activation. ROC analyses identified average GMED activity cut-offs of 12.0% MVC at 12 km/h (AUC=0.791, sensitivity=77.8%, specificity=76.5%) and 13.4% MVC at 15 km/h (AUC=0.824, sensitivity=77.8%, specificity=82.4%) as best predictors of season hamstring injury.

## Methods
Prospective cohort study of 26 elite male AFL players from one professional club. Pre-season bilateral GMED and GMAX muscle volumes were measured via 3 Tesla MRI (T2-weighted axial sequences). Surface EMG was recorded using bipolar electrodes (Noraxon Telemyo DTS) during treadmill running at 6, 12, and 15 km/h; EMG was normalized to maximal voluntary isometric contraction (%MVC). Ten consecutive strides per speed were analyzed for peak and average muscle activation. Injury incidence was tracked prospectively via club medical records. Linear mixed models, independent t-tests, and ROC analyses were used for statistical analysis.

## Findings
Nine of 26 players (34.6%) sustained an in-season hamstring injury. Players who were injured showed significantly higher average and peak GMED activation at 12 km/h (mean difference: 3.3% MVC average, 26.1 N peak) and 15 km/h (mean difference: 4.0% MVC average, 27.2 N peak) with effect sizes of 1.1–1.5 (large). No differences were found at walking speed (6 km/h). GMAX activation and muscle volume (GMED and GMAX) did not differ significantly between groups. ROC cut-offs identified average GMED activity above ~12–13% MVC during running as predictive of hamstring injury with 77–78% sensitivity.

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=26) from a single AFL club limits generalizability to female athletes, other sports, and skill levels. Only GMED and GMAX were assessed; other lumbo-pelvic muscles and hamstring activation itself were not measured. Hip strength and lower limb kinematics were not evaluated, limiting mechanistic interpretation. No baseline hamstring activation data were collected. The study cannot determine whether high GMED activation is a cause or consequence of underlying neuromuscular deficits.

## Practical Implications
Measuring GMED activation during running (not walking) may serve as a useful screening tool for hamstring injury risk in team sports athletes. Athletes demonstrating higher GMED activation during high-speed running warrant closer attention in injury prevention programming. Neuromuscular control of the lumbo-pelvic region — specifically GMED — should be incorporated into hamstring injury prevention and rehabilitation strategies alongside traditional hamstring-focused work.

## Athlete Development Notes
Assessment of GMED function during running-speed-specific tasks should be considered in pre-season athlete screening for football players. Lumbo-pelvic stability training targeting GMED neuromuscular control may help reduce hamstring injury risk in developing and elite players who demonstrate compensatory gluteal overactivity during high-speed running.

## Return to Play Notes
Players returning from hamstring injury should be assessed for GMED activation asymmetries during sport-specific running speeds before return-to-play clearance is granted. Rehabilitation protocols should address lumbo-pelvic neuromuscular control in addition to traditional hamstring strength and flexibility criteria, with running-specific testing used to confirm normalized GMED recruitment patterns.
