# Langan SP, Martin DG, Thomas L, Good A, Funk JR, Barker R, Deaton S, Guevel S, Crandall JR, Sills A, Myers BS, Arbogast KB, Casa DJ. Effect of newly required American football headgear on thermoregulation during exercise in the heat. Sports Engineering. 2025;28:19. DOI: 10.1007/s12283-025-00498-4

## TLDR
The NFL's required Guardian Cap NXT helmet cover does not meaningfully worsen core body temperature, heart rate, or sweat rate during intense exercise in the heat compared to standard helmets alone. The manikin showed a small (2.3%) reduction in heat loss with the GC NXT, but this was dwarfed by differences between helmet models. In humans, no objective thermoregulatory differences were detected across helmet conditions, though players subjectively felt cooler with white GC NXTs. For NFL/college staff, this means the mandated GC NXT should not increase exertional heat illness risk, but white caps may provide a modest comfort benefit over black caps in hot weather.

## Abstract
This study evaluated whether the NFL-mandated Guardian Cap NXT (GC NXT) soft-shell helmet cover accentuates hyperthermia during exercise heat stress using both a sweating thermal manikin and 12 male human volunteers (>91 kg) exercising in a chamber at 40°C, 40% humidity with ~800 W/m² simulated solar radiation. The manikin assessed heat loss and radiant heat absorption across four helmet models with and without the GC NXT; the human study measured rectal temperature, skin temperature, heart rate, sweat rate, and subjective measures across five 90-minute exercise trials.

## Methods
Two-phase study. Phase 1: Sweating head manikin at the Textile Protection and Comfort Center (NC State University) tested 4 helmet models (Riddell SpeedFlex, Schutt F7 UR2, Schutt Air XP Pro Q11 LTD, VICIS ZERO2-R) with and without GC NXT for thermal resistance, evaporative resistance, and radiant heat absorption. Phase 2: Randomized crossover design with 12 males (age 18-35, >91 kg) completing 5 exercise trials in an environmental chamber (40°C, 40% RH, ~800 W/m² solar radiation) wearing different helmet/GC NXT combinations. Exercise protocol: 15-min repeating bouts (5-min walk, 5-min jog, 5 x 20-s sprint/40-s rest) for 90 min or until Tr reached 40°C. Outcome measures: rectal temperature (Tr), mean skin temperature, helmet interior temperature, heart rate, sweat rate, RPE, thermal sensation, fatigue.

## Findings
Manikin: GC NXT reduced calculated heat loss by 4.85 W/m² (2.3%) across all helmets (p=0.0283), but effect size was small (Hedges' g*=0.13) compared to helmet model variation. GC NXT reduced radiant heat absorption vs. bare helmet (p<0.02). Human study: No significant differences in Tr (p=0.731), mean skin temperature, heart rate (average p=0.673; peak p=0.359), sweat rate (p=0.180), or body mass loss (p=0.441) between any headwear conditions. Interior helmet temperature was 5-10°C lower with GC NXT vs. bare helmet (p<0.001). At 75 min, thermal sensation was lower with white GC NXT vs. control (p=0.007) and vs. black GC NXT (p=0.011).

## Limitations
Human study participants were not blinded to cap color, introducing potential placebo effects on subjective measures. Only male participants >91 kg were tested, limiting generalizability to smaller players, females, or youth. The study was conducted under laboratory-simulated solar conditions which may not perfectly replicate outdoor environments. Conflict of interest: authors affiliated with Biocore LLC (GC NXT developer) were involved in testing, though separate laboratories conducted manikin and human studies.

## Practical Implications
Football staff and medical personnel can be reassured that the mandated Guardian Cap NXT does not significantly increase exertional heat illness risk during intense exercise in hot conditions. Heat illness prevention protocols used with standard helmets should be equally effective with the GC NXT. Choosing white over black GC NXTs may reduce subjective thermal discomfort at the end of long practices. Helmet model choice has a much greater effect on thermoregulation than GC NXT presence — this should be considered when selecting equipment for hot-weather training environments.

## Athlete Development Notes
The GC NXT's lack of thermoregulatory impact means it can be worn during summer training camps and hot-weather practices without additional heat illness precautions beyond standard protocol. Player welfare decisions about heat illness should focus on environmental conditions, workload, hydration, and acclimatization status rather than GC NXT use. Education for players and staff should clarify that wearing the cap is not a meaningful heat risk, reducing potential non-compliance.

## Return to Play Notes
Players returning from exertional heat illness (EHI) who are required to wear a GC NXT can do so without concern that the cap will significantly elevate thermoregulatory stress above baseline. The interior helmet temperature reduction (5-10°C lower with GC NXT) may even be marginally beneficial in hot conditions. Standard EHI return-to-sport protocols are appropriate for players wearing any of the tested helmet/GC NXT combinations.
