# Walter, K. D., Witmer, B., Huber, D. L., Alber, S., & Slobounov, S. M. (2022). Head acceleration event exposure and cognitive and functional outcomes: A comparison of multiple football seasons. Research in Sports Medicine, 31(4), 440–453. https://doi.org/10.1080/15438627.2022.2090249

## TLDR
In NCAA football players monitored across three seasons, the number of head acceleration events measured by helmet sensors decreased over time but did not significantly predict changes in cognitive or functional performance assessed by VR testing. Aggregate impact count alone may be an insufficient indicator of neurocognitive risk—the biomechanical characteristics and locations of impacts likely matter more than raw count.

## Abstract
This prospective study followed NCAA Division I FBS football athletes at Penn State across three seasons (2015, 2017, 2019) to examine the relationship between head acceleration event (HAE) exposure, measured via helmet-mounted accelerometers, and cognitive and functional outcomes assessed via virtual reality (VR) testing. Incidence rates of HAEs declined across the three seasons. VR-based cognitive change scores (pre- to post-season) were not significantly predicted by HAE incidence rates in any season, suggesting that the volume of sub-concussive impacts alone does not reliably predict short-term cognitive function changes over a season.

## Methods
Prospective multi-season cohort at Penn State University (NCAA FBS). Athletes wore Riddell HITS or equivalent helmet-mounted accelerometers throughout practice and games in the 2015, 2017, and 2019 seasons. VR-based cognitive and functional assessments administered pre- and post-season. HAE incidence rates (events per athlete-exposure) calculated for each season. Linear regression used to test association between HAE incidence rates and VR change scores. Seasons compared descriptively for HAE trends.

## Findings
HAE incidence rates declined across the three seasons (2015 > 2017 > 2019), consistent with rule changes and practice contact restrictions. Pre-to-post-season VR cognitive change scores did not significantly correlate with HAE incidence rates in any of the three seasons. No season showed a meaningful relationship between how many head impacts an athlete sustained and how their cognitive scores changed over the season.

## Limitations
VR testing sensitivity for detecting sub-concussive cognitive change is not fully validated. Accelerometer-based HAE metrics capture linear and rotational head acceleration but do not directly measure brain tissue strain. Athletes with diagnosed concussions during the season may have been excluded, which could attenuate observed effects. Relatively small per-season sample sizes may have limited statistical power.

## Practical Implications
Programs should not rely solely on reducing total impact count as a proxy for protecting neurocognitive health. Complement impact monitoring with biomechanical quality indicators (location, direction, magnitude distribution) and individualized cognitive tracking. Practice contact restrictions reduce exposure but may not eliminate risk if high-magnitude events persist. Cognitive baselines and serial monitoring remain important components of athlete brain health programs.

## Athlete Development Notes
For American football programs, this study reinforces that reducing practice contact exposure (as implemented under NCAA and NFL protocols) has successfully decreased HAE incidence. However, the absence of a significant HAE-to-cognition relationship suggests developmental programs should focus on technique quality—particularly blocking and tackling mechanics that reduce high-magnitude head impacts—rather than relying on impact count reduction alone.

## Return to Play Notes
From a concussion RTP perspective, cumulative sub-concussive impact burden does not appear to independently predict short-term cognitive function changes in this population. Clinical concussion assessment and symptom resolution remain the primary gatekeepers for RTP decisions. Serial VR or neurocognitive testing can provide a more sensitive window into subtle changes that aggregate impact counts miss.
