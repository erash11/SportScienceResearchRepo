# Ashley, C. D., Lopez, R. M., & Tritsch, A. J. (2023). Football practices in hot environments impact subsequent days' hydration. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research. https://doi.org/10.1519/JSC.0000000000004600

## TLDR
Back-to-back and twice-daily football practices in hot conditions produce cumulative hydration deficits measurable via urine color carryover. Neither urine color nor urine-specific gravity alone reliably detects ≥2% body mass loss in this population, though urine color effectively tracks USG. Athletes must actively replenish fluids during and between sessions—especially during 2-a-day preseason structures—to maintain euhydration and reduce exertional heat illness risk.

## Abstract
This study examined the accuracy of urinary hydration measures and carryover effects of consecutive outdoor football practices in hot conditions (mean WBGT = 31.3 ± 1.8°C) in 31 male high school football players (age 16 ± 1 years) across 7 outdoor practices over 2 preseason weeks. Urine color (Ucol) and urine-specific gravity (USG) were compared against percent body mass loss (%BML) using ROC analysis. Neither Ucol nor USG was a valid standalone predictor of ≥2% BML (AUC = 0.55 and 0.49; sensitivity/specificity both ~60%/46%). However, Ucol accurately identified USG > 1.020 (AUC = 0.85). Significant carryover effects of Ucol were found between practices with less than 24 hours recovery, including 2-a-day sessions. %BML was significantly greater after 2-a-day practices.

## Methods
Observational field study in 31 male high school football players during the first 2 weeks of preseason (7 outdoor practices analyzed; mean WBGT 31.3 ± 1.8°C). Pre- and post-practice urine samples were collected for Ucol (8-color Armstrong scale) and USG (refractometry). Seminude body mass was measured pre and post each practice to calculate %BML. ROC curve analysis assessed Ucol and USG sensitivity/specificity for detecting ≥2% BML. Paired-samples t-tests assessed postpractice to next prepractice carryover. Repeated-measures ANOVA assessed carryover trends across all practices.

## Findings
ROC analysis: AUC for Ucol to detect ≥2% BML = 0.55 (p = 0.53), USG AUC = 0.49 (p = 0.90); neither was significant. Sensitivity for both = 60%; specificity = 48.8% (Ucol) and 44% (USG). Ucol significantly detected USG > 1.020 (AUC = 0.85, sensitivity 82.5%, specificity 67.9%). Significant Ucol carryover effects between practices with <24 hours recovery (practices 2→3, 6→7AM, 7AM→7PM, 10→11; p ≤ 0.004; effect sizes 0.65–1.10). Significant USG carryover only from practice 2 to 3 (p = 0.011, ES = 0.51). No significant body mass carryover. %BML significantly greater after 2-a-day practices (7PM practice: 1.48 ± 0.75%; practice 10: 1.32 ± 0.72%) versus early practices and baseline (~0.59–0.82%).

## Limitations
Convenience sample from one high school varsity team limits generalizability. Small sample size (n = 31) may have reduced power to detect smaller carryover effects. Mixed indoor/outdoor practice schedule meant only 7 of 11 practices were analyzed. %BML rarely exceeded 2% in this sample, limiting the utility of testing urinary measures against that threshold. Individual variations in dietary and fluid intake between practices could not be fully controlled.

## Practical Implications
Athletic trainers and strength coaches managing preseason American football in hot environments should track urine color trends across consecutive practice days rather than relying on a single snapshot. Fluid replenishment protocols must be reinforced especially after 2-a-day practices and when less than 24 hours separates sessions. Urine color can serve as a practical proxy for USG in field settings without refractometer access. Cumulative hydration deficits across a preseason week are a tangible exertional heat illness risk factor that requires proactive management.

## Athlete Development Notes
High school and developing football players practicing in hot environments accumulate hydration deficits across back-to-back sessions. Educating young athletes on consistent fluid intake routines—before, during, and after each practice—is a key component of heat illness prevention during preseason development periods. Equipment additions (pads, helmets) added progressively across preseason further increase sweat loss and compound hydration challenges.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes returning to full-contact or full-load practices after a heat-related illness or in hot preseason environments should be monitored with serial urinary hydration markers (Ucol, USG) and body mass checks across consecutive days. Residual hypohydration from previous practices can impair thermoregulation, increasing risk during RTP progressions in hot conditions; ensuring euhydration before each session is a prerequisite for safe return.
