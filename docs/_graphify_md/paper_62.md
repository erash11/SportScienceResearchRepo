# Mühlenfeld N, Steendahl IB, Berthold DP, Meyer T, Hauser T, Wagner N, Sander AL, Marzi I, Kaltenbach B, Yel I, Vogl T, Eichler K. Assessment of muscle volume using magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) in football players after hamstring injuries. European Journal of Sport Science. 2021;DOI:10.1080/17461391.2021.1942226.

## TLDR
Recreational football players sustaining moderate hamstring tears lose between 2–7% of hamstring and quadriceps muscle volume within six weeks, with reduced training activity of approximately 111 min/week sufficient to preserve 99% of remaining muscle mass from weeks 3 to 6.

## Abstract
This prospective study examined muscle volume changes in 20 male recreational association football (soccer) players following type 3a or 3b hamstring injuries, using serial 3-Tesla MRI at baseline, 3 weeks, and 6 weeks post-injury. The injured hamstrings lost 6.5% of muscle volume at 3 weeks and 6.5% at 6 weeks, while healthy hamstrings lost 2.1–2.2%. The quadriceps in the injured leg lost 3.5–3.8% and in the healthy leg 3.9–4.5%. Muscle volume loss correlated strongly with training activity levels in the healthy quadriceps and moderately in the healthy hamstrings, but not significantly in the injured hamstrings. Hamstring-to-quadriceps volume ratios remained stable throughout rehabilitation.

## Methods
Prospective observational study. Twenty male recreational football players (mean age 25±4 years) with MRI-confirmed type 3a (n=12) or 3b (n=8) hamstring injuries were enrolled between January 2018 and March 2019. 3-Tesla MRI performed at injury (MRI₀), 3 weeks (MRI₃), and 6 weeks (MRI₆). Muscle volumes of hamstring group and quadriceps femoris were calculated bilaterally via manual transversal T2-sequence segmentation. Rehabilitation included rest phase (weeks 1–3) followed by individualized activity resumption. Players documented rehabilitation minutes per week (weeks 4–6). One-way ANOVA with Bonferroni correction and Pearson correlation used for analysis. Note: this study involved soccer players, not American football players.

## Findings
• Injured hamstrings lost mean 6.7% muscle volume at 3 weeks (including edema resorption artifact) and 6.5% at 6 weeks
• Healthy hamstrings lost 2.2% at 3 weeks and 2.1% at 6 weeks
• Quadriceps in injured leg lost 3.5% at 3 weeks and 3.8% at 6 weeks; healthy leg lost 3.9% and 4.5%
• Volume loss in quadriceps (both legs) correlated very strongly with training activity (r=0.96 and 0.70, p<0.001)
• Volume loss in injured hamstrings did NOT significantly correlate with training activity (r=0.39, p=0.091)
• Hamstring-to-quadriceps volume ratios remained stable (~0.52) across all time points
• Mean rehabilitation activity of 91–134 min/week (weeks 4–6) was sufficient to preserve 99% of remaining muscle volume
• Only 2 of 20 players had hamstring-to-quadriceps ratios above 0.61 (threshold associated with injury risk)

## Limitations
Only male recreational soccer players included, limiting generalizability to elite or American football athletes. Edema in first 3 weeks confounds injured hamstring volume measurements. Quadriceps and hamstring strength were not assessed. Rehabilitation was not standardized for activity type, frequency, intensity, or volume. The study cannot establish causal relationships between specific rehabilitation activities and muscle volume preservation.

## Practical Implications
Rehabilitation staff should expect 2–7% muscle volume loss in the proximal thigh within 6 weeks of a moderate hamstring injury. Maintaining even light training activity (approximately 111 min/week) from weeks 3–6 appears sufficient to preserve the vast majority of remaining quadriceps muscle volume. The lack of a significant correlation between activity and injured hamstring volume recovery suggests other factors (pain, edema, protective unloading) govern early hamstring recovery and should be monitored independently.

## Athlete Development Notes
While this study examined recreational soccer players (not American football), the findings on hamstring muscle volume loss timelines and hamstring-to-quadriceps volume ratios are directly applicable to American football athlete monitoring. Most American football players demonstrated low baseline hamstring-to-quadriceps ratios (<0.61), suggesting a widespread opportunity for quadriceps and hamstring hypertrophy programming to reduce injury risk, particularly during offseason strength training blocks.

## Return to Play Notes
Most recreational football players return to sport approximately 3 weeks post-hamstring injury; after 6 weeks, some structural damage may still be visible on MRI. Muscle volume loss of 2–7% in the proximal thigh warrants attention during RTP progressions, as strength deficits may persist beyond symptom resolution. Volume ratios and activity-correlated volume recovery provide quantifiable benchmarks for MRI-guided RTP criteria. Low hamstring-to-quadriceps volume ratios (<0.61) at RTP may indicate elevated reinjury risk.
