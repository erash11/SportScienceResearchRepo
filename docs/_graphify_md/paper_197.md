# Pietsch S, Green B, Schache AG, Pizzari T. Epidemiology of quadriceps muscle strain injuries in elite male Australian football players. Scand J Med Sci Sports. 2023;00:1–11. DOI: 10.1111/sms.14542

## TLDR
In AFL players, quadriceps strains are predominantly rectus femoris injuries caused by kicking, affecting the dominant leg, and most often occurring in training. They carry a mean RTP of about 25 days, with kicking-mechanism and rectus femoris injuries taking the longest. Re-injuries exclusively involve the rectus femoris, primarily from kicking, and occur early in recovery—emphasizing the need for careful RTP criteria and targeted rehabilitation.

## Abstract
This study described the epidemiology of quadriceps muscle strain injuries (QMSIs) in AFL players using data from the Soft Tissue Injury Registry of the AFL (STRAFL) across seven consecutive seasons (2014–2020) from all 18 AFL teams. A total of 164 QMSIs from 122 players were reported (134 index; 30 re-injuries). Almost all QMSIs (91.3%) involved the rectus femoris. Half (48.4%) were caused by kicking and most commonly affected the dominant kicking leg (72%). The majority occurred during training (64.6%). Mean return to play (RTP) was 25.4 days (95% CI 22.6–28.2). Rectus femoris injuries took approximately 14 days longer to RTP than vastii injuries (p=0.001). QMSIs with a kicking mechanism had the longest RTP of all injury mechanisms. All 30 re-injuries involved the rectus femoris and most occurred within 6 months of the preceding injury (70%).

## Methods
Retrospective analysis of QMSI data reported to the STRAFL across seven seasons (2014–2020) from all 18 AFL teams. Injuries were classified as index or re-injury. Player demographics, injury circumstances (activity, mechanism, timing), MRI reports (primary muscle), and recovery milestones were extracted. Descriptive frequency distributions presented. Recovery outcomes compared across injury type, primary muscle, and mechanism using Mann–Whitney U-tests; Kruskal–Wallis tests for multi-group comparisons. Effect sizes calculated (r = z/√n). Significance level p<0.05.

## Findings
164 QMSIs from 122 players over 7 seasons (23.4 QMSIs/year; 1.5 per club per year). 134 index injuries (81.7%), 30 re-injuries (18.3%). 91.3% involved rectus femoris on MRI. 48.4% from kicking; 31% running-related. 72% affected dominant leg. 64.6% occurred at training. Mean RTP: 25.4 days (95% CI 22.6–28.2). Rectus femoris injuries took ~14 days longer RTP than vastii injuries (p=0.001). Kicking-mechanism injuries had longest RTP (H=8.185, p=0.02). All re-injuries involved rectus femoris; 63% from kicking; 70% occurred within 6 months of preceding injury. Competitive season injury frequency highest May–July (middle of season). Re-injury cumulative incidence: 53% within 2 months.

## Limitations
STRAFL captures only injured players, so incidence rates relative to total exposure cannot be calculated. Voluntary reporting may have resulted in underrepresentation of injuries, particularly pre-season. MRI reports were unavailable for 15.9% of cases, limiting primary muscle analysis in those instances. Data were recorded only for elite male AFL players, limiting generalizability to other populations or female athletes.

## Practical Implications
Given that rectus femoris injuries from kicking dominate QMSIs in AFL and carry the longest recovery times, rehabilitation programs should specifically address eccentric strength and load tolerance of the rectus femoris through kicking-specific movement patterns. Strict RTP criteria—particularly for kicking loads—are warranted, as 53% of re-injuries occurred within 2 months. Training environments should be carefully managed given that 64.6% of QMSIs occur at training, and kicking workloads during training likely exceed match loads.

## Athlete Development Notes
Kicking skill development is central to AFL, but the rectus femoris undergoes large rapid length changes during the kick, predisposing it to strain. Athletes should develop adequate eccentric quad strength, hip flexor control, and progressive kicking load exposure across their development. Identifying dominant-leg asymmetry and prior QMSI history should be part of routine screening, as these factors elevate re-injury risk.

## Return to Play Notes
Mean RTP was 25.4 days overall, but rectus femoris injuries averaged approximately 14 days longer than vastii injuries. Kicking-mechanism injuries had the longest RTP. Re-injury risk is highest within the first 2 months (53% cumulative incidence), indicating that full kicking load tolerance—not just pain-free walking or sprint speed—should be confirmed before RTP. A graduated progression through running, sprinting, and sport-specific kicking workloads is recommended.
