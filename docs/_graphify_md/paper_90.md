# Massidda M, Flore L, Cugia P, Piras F, Scorcu M, Kikuchi N, Cieszczyk P, Maciejewska-Skrendo A, Tocco F, Calo CM. Association Between Total Genotype Score and Muscle Injuries in Top-Level Football Players: A Pilot Study. Sports Medicine - Open. 2024;10:22. https://doi.org/10.1186/s40798-024-00682-z

## TLDR
A combined polygenic score (TGS) from four SNPs—ACE I/D, ACTN3 c.1729C>T, COL5A1 C>T, and MCT1 c.1470A>T—strongly predicted muscle injury incidence in elite Italian Serie A football players over a 10-year period. Players with TGS below 56.2 a.u. had 3.5x higher odds of injury, and the ACE DD, ACTN3 CC, COL5A1 CC, and MCT1 TT genotypes each conferred individual protection. This framework supports the future use of genetic risk profiling to individualize training load management and injury prevention strategies, with the long-term goal of adapting training volume, intensity, specificity, recovery, and density based on an athlete's genetic muscle injury risk profile.

## Abstract
This pilot study investigated the combined effect of four gene polymorphisms—ACE I/D (rs4341), ACTN3 c.1729C>T (rs1815739), COL5A1 C>T (rs2722), and MCT1 c.1470A>T (rs1049434)—on muscle injury incidence in 64 elite Italian male professional football players (Serie A) followed for 10 years (2009–2019). A total genotype score (TGS) on a 0–100 arbitrary unit scale was calculated using the Williams and Folland polygenic method. Mean TGS was 63.7±13.0 a.u. in non-injured vs. 42.5±12.5 a.u. in injured players (p<0.001), and a TGS cut-off of 56.2 a.u. was identified with OR=3.5 (95%CI 1.8–6.8) for sustaining an injury.

## Methods
Longitudinal cohort study of 64 male professional football players (age 23.1±5.5 years; height 179.3±7.3 cm; weight 73.0±7.9 kg) from one Italian Serie A club. Muscle injury data were prospectively collected over 10 years (2009–2019) per UEFA consensus guidelines. Buccal swabs provided genomic DNA; PCR genotyping for four SNPs. Genotype scores of 2 (protective), 1 (heterozygous), or 0 (worst) were assigned per polymorphism and summed to compute TGS (0–100 scale). Injury severity was classified as minor (4–7 days), moderate (8–28 days), or major (>28 days). ROC curve analysis assessed TGS discriminatory accuracy for injury incidence and severity. Linear regression evaluated TGS as a continuous predictor. Analyses were conducted in SPSS v21.0 and Genepop v4.0.3; Bonferroni corrections applied.

## Findings
ACE I/D genotype frequency differed significantly between injured and non-injured players (p=0.001); MCT1 c.1470A>T (p=0.005) and ACTN3 c.1729C>T (p=0.016) also differed significantly. COL5A1 trended toward significance (p=0.079). Protective genotypes (ACE DD, ACTN3 CC, MCT1 TT) were associated with significantly lower injury incidence. Mean TGS: 63.7±13.0 a.u. (non-injured) vs. 42.5±12.5 a.u. (injured; p<0.001). ROC AUC=0.873 (95%CI 0.788–0.958; p<0.001; sensitivity=0.857, specificity=0.241). TGS threshold of 56.2 a.u. yielded OR=3.5 (95%CI 1.8–6.8; p<0.001) for injury. TGS was a significant predictor of injury incidence (R²=0.29, p<0.001) but not injury severity (R²=0.02; AUC=0.636, p=0.189). Mean injury incidence per 1,000 h: 2.28±2.80 for TGS>56.2 a.u. vs. 0.12±0.28 for TGS<56.2 a.u.

## Limitations
Small sample (n=64) from a single Italian club limits statistical power and generalizability to other populations or ethnic groups. Results are restricted to male Italian Caucasian players. Only four polymorphisms were analyzed; additional relevant SNPs (AMPD1, CKM, MLCK) shown to influence injury in non-Italian populations were not included. Injury incidence may be influenced by training type, playing surface, player age, and competition level. Conclusions need replication in larger independent cohorts before clinical translation.

## Practical Implications
Genetic profiling using a TGS derived from ACE, ACTN3, COL5A1, and MCT1 polymorphisms may help identify professional football players at elevated muscle injury risk. Coaches and medical staff could use this information to individualize training load parameters—volume, intensity, specificity, recovery, and density—for genetically at-risk athletes. Players with TGS below approximately 56 a.u. warrant enhanced monitoring and potentially modified training loads, particularly during high-volume or high-intensity phases.

## Athlete Development Notes
Polygenic risk scoring for muscle injuries has direct applications in American football player development. Players with unfavorable ACE, ACTN3, COL5A1, and MCT1 genotype combinations may benefit from earlier integration of injury prevention programs targeting the hamstrings and connective tissues. Genetic profiling could guide position-specific training load prescriptions and long-term athlete development pathways for NFL and collegiate football players.

## Return to Play Notes
Although TGS did not predict injury severity, it significantly predicted injury incidence, suggesting that players with lower TGS returning from muscle injury face elevated re-injury risk. Incorporating genetic profiling into RTP frameworks could support more conservative return timelines and enhanced monitoring for athletes with unfavorable polygenic profiles. Players with ACE II, ACTN3 TT, or MCT1 AA genotypes may warrant extended rehabilitation and reduced loading during the return-to-play continuum.
