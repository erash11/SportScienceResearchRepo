# Cho E, Granger J, Theall B, Lemoine N, Calvert D, Marucci J, Mullenix S, O'Neal H, Jacome T, Irving BA, Johannsen NM, Carmichael O, Spielmann G. Blood and MRI biomarkers of mild traumatic brain injury in non-concussed collegiate football players. Sci Rep. 2024;14:665.

## TLDR
Repeated sub-concussive head impacts during an NCAA football season elevated several blood-based brain injury biomarkers in linemen, but these elevations were not accompanied by detectable changes in brain structure or cognitive function, highlighting the limited diagnostic utility of individual biomarkers for mTBI.

## Abstract
This prospective observational study assessed blood biomarkers of mild traumatic brain injury (mTBI) — S100B, GFAP, BDNF, NFL, and NSE — and structural and functional MRI outcomes across three timepoints (pre-camp, pre-season, post-season) in 48 non-concussed NCAA Division I football players (32 linemen, 16 non-linemen) at LSU. S100B, GFAP, and BDNF concentrations were significantly elevated post-season compared to pre-camp in linemen, while white matter hyperintensities increased during pre-season training camp in linemen. No clinically relevant changes in brain anatomy or function were detected, and no players were diagnosed with concussion during the study. The findings indicate that sub-concussive repeated head impacts detectable in blood biomarkers do not necessarily translate to structural brain changes, but warrant continued monitoring.

## Methods
Prospective observational design; 48 NCAA Division I male football players (32 linemen, 16 non-linemen); three study visits: pre-camp, post-pre-season training camp, post-competitive season; fasted serum blood samples analyzed via ELISA for S100B, GFAP, BDNF, NFL, NSE; structural MRI (sMRI) and functional MRI (fMRI) performed on a sub-sample of 17 players (11 linemen, 6 non-linemen) on a 3T GE Discovery scanner at Pennington Biomedical Research Center; cognitive tasks included Stroop Task and Attention Network Test (ANT); statistical analyses via two-way RM ANOVA in JMPro 16 (SAS); significance set at P < 0.05.

## Findings
• S100B significantly elevated in linemen at post-season vs pre-camp (107.93 ± 88.96 pg/ml vs 51.01 ± 39.97 pg/ml; P < 0.01)
• GFAP increased from pre-camp to post-season in linemen (P < 0.46 overall, but directional increase observed)
• BDNF higher in linemen at post-season vs pre-camp (29.56 ± 16.17 ng/ml vs 21.12 ± 8.43 ng/ml; P < 0.05)
• NFL decreased during pre-season training camp in linemen (P < 0.01) then increased during competitive season (P < 0.01)
• NSE did not change significantly throughout the season in either group
• White matter hyperintensities increased in linemen during pre-season training camp vs pre-camp (P < 0.05), then returned to pre-camp levels at post-season (P < 0.01)
• No significant changes in gray matter, white matter, or CSF volumes observed
• No association found between biomarker changes and fMRI or sMRI outcomes when analyzed independently by timepoint
• Higher circulating S100B associated with increased total white matter volume (r² = 0.076, P < 0.05)
• Forced fumbles was not relevant here — this finding belongs to paper 75

## Limitations
Unable to record head impact magnitude and number, limiting ability to correlate impact dose with biomarker response; no non-contact sport control group; MRI sub-sample too small for full generalization; blood biomarkers measured 24–48 hours post-practice/game, not immediately post-impact; single institution study.

## Practical Implications
Blood biomarkers of mTBI are detectable in non-concussed football players, particularly linemen, across a competitive season. However, current biomarkers (S100B, GFAP, BDNF, NFL, NSE) lack sufficient sensitivity and specificity to serve as standalone clinical diagnostic tools for mTBI. Sports medicine staff should not rely on any single biomarker for concussion detection and should continue developing multi-modal assessment protocols.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly studied in NCAA Division I American football players. Linemen, due to their repetitive high-frequency low-moderate head impact exposure, showed the greatest biomarker changes. Pre-season training camp appears to be a period of elevated neurological stress. Long-term monitoring of brain health across an athlete's career should be a priority for collegiate football programs.

## Return to Play Notes
The study underscores that clinically asymptomatic athletes (no diagnosed concussion) can still exhibit elevated brain injury biomarkers throughout a season. This has implications for RTP protocols: biomarker elevation alone may not indicate clinical concussion, but the accumulation of sub-concussive impacts across a career warrants cautious RTP frameworks, especially for linemen with repeated high-frequency head exposure.
