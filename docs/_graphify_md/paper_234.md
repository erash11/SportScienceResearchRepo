# Wusk, Z., & Rowson, S. (2022). Football Shoulder Pad Design and Its Effect on Head Kinematics in Shoulder-to-Helmet Impacts. Annals of Biomedical Engineering. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10439-022-03063-3

## TLDR
Adding foam padding to football shoulder pads meaningfully reduces the head kinematics generated during shoulder-to-helmet impacts. A 31% drop in peak linear acceleration and a 28% drop in rotational acceleration were observed at realistic impact speeds, suggesting that shoulder pad design—an area currently without performance standards—is a viable lever for reducing concussion risk in football. Impact response corridors developed in this study can guide future higher-speed testing to evaluate padding performance at concussive impact levels.

## Abstract
Shoulder-to-helmet (STH) impacts account for approximately 20% of concussions in football, yet no published research had examined football shoulder pad design in relation to STH impacts. This study compared an unmodified Douglas SW24 flat pad (control) to a modified version with added Vinyl Nitrile 600 closed-cell foam at the top flap, interior arch, and shoulder resting point. Twenty participants with varsity-level football experience each performed six impacts against a helmeted Hybrid III dummy equipped with a NOCSAE instrumented head, yielding 111 analyzable trials. The modified shoulder pad produced statistically significant reductions in peak linear acceleration (31%), peak rotational acceleration (28%), and peak rotational velocity (10%), while increasing impact duration by 40%. Impact response corridors were developed for both conditions to support future controlled lab replication of STH impacts.

## Methods
Prospective lab study; 20 male participants with varsity football experience each delivered 6 impacts (3 per pad condition) to the upper-side of a helmeted Hybrid III 50th-percentile dummy suspended at 40 degrees. Participants used a 5-m runway before striking. Head kinematics captured at 20,000 Hz via three linear accelerometers and three angular rate sensors (NOCSAE head). High-speed cameras (960 fps) measured impact speed and contact location. Nine impacts removed due to off-target contact. Paired one-sided t-tests (alpha = 0.05) compared conditions; impact response corridors built using a modified Maltese method.

## Findings
Modified (foam-added) shoulder pad vs. control: peak linear acceleration reduced 31% (29.4 g to 20.3 g, p<0.001); peak rotational acceleration reduced 28% (1960 to 1396 rad/s², p<0.001); peak rotational velocity reduced 10% (22.8 to 20.4 rad/s, p<0.001); impact duration increased 40% (24.8 to 34.8 ms, p<0.001). Average impact speed was consistent across conditions (5.8 m/s). Control pad rotational velocities approached published concussive thresholds (~22 rad/s).

## Limitations
Impact speeds (mean 5.8 m/s) were below concussive levels reported in NFL studies (~9.3 m/s), limiting generalizability to high-severity impacts. Hybrid III dummy biofidelity for lateral STH impacts is uncertain. Variability in participant striking technique, though mitigated by paired analysis design. Ordered/learning effects not fully controlled. No existing shoulder pad performance standards for comparison.

## Practical Implications
Shoulder pad design is an underexplored, actionable target for concussion risk reduction in football. Adding closed-cell foam at the top flap, arch, and resting point of existing pads is a low-cost modification that meaningfully reduces head kinematics in shoulder-to-helmet impacts. Programs should advocate for shoulder pad performance standards similar to those for helmets. Future higher-speed testing is needed before definitive concussion-risk claims can be made.

## Athlete Development Notes
For developing players at all levels, shoulder pad design may contribute to long-term head safety. Programs should consider padding quality in shoulder pad selection criteria, not just fit or position-specific coverage. Coaches and equipment managers should be aware that STH impacts—now comprising ~22% of NFL concussions—are an increasing share of head contact events as helmet-to-helmet rules tighten.

## Return to Play Notes
Not directly applicable to RTP decision-making. However, the study underscores that modifiable equipment factors (shoulder pad design) contribute to concussion exposure, which has downstream implications for cumulative head impact burden and RTP thresholds. Players with concussion history may benefit from upgraded shoulder pad padding as a secondary prevention measure.
