# Mihalik JP, Chandran A, Powell JR, Roby PR, Guskiewicz KM, Stemper BD, Shah AS, Rowson S, Duma S, Harezlak J, Riggen L, Broglio SP, McAllister TW, McCrea M, and CARE Consortium Investigators. Do Head Injury Biomechanics Predict Concussion Clinical Recovery in College American Football Players? Annals of Biomedical Engineering. 2020. doi:10.1007/s10439-020-02658-y

## TLDR
Head impact biomechanics—including the magnitude of the injury-causing impact itself—do not reliably predict how fast a concussed college football player will recover clinically. The one exception is season-long repetitive head impact exposure (RHIE): athletes with greater cumulative head impact exposure across the season had 22–25% longer symptom resolution times and, counterintuitively, 20–28% shorter return-to-participation times (possibly reflecting institutional variation in RTP management). This means athletic trainers and team physicians should not use the 'size' of a concussive hit to predict recovery or set return timelines. Clinical factors—concussion history (86% longer RTP), playing position (offensive players resolve symptoms ~49% faster than defensive players), and impact location—carry more predictive weight than biomechanics alone.

## Abstract
This multi-site NCAA-DoD CARE Consortium study examined whether head injury biomechanics—specifically injury-causing linear and rotational acceleration, season repetitive head impact exposure (RHIE), and injury day RHIE—predicted clinical recovery from concussion in 45 Division I college football players sustaining 51 incident concussions. Clinical outcomes included symptom resolution time (SRT), return-to-participation (RTP) time, and trajectories of symptom severity, balance (BESS), and mental status (SAC). Head impacts were measured using the HIT System; multivariable negative binomial regression models were employed.

## Methods
Multi-site prospective study within the NCAA-DoD CARE Consortium. 941 Division I football players from 4 civilian universities and 2 military service academies monitored during the study period; 45 athletes sustaining 51 incident concussions were analyzed. Head impacts measured using the HIT System (Riddell; 6 spring-loaded accelerometers measuring linear and rotational acceleration; triggered at ≥9.6 g). Concussion defined as a change in brain function following head trauma identified by team physicians and athletic trainers. Clinical measures collected at 5 time points: pre-injury baseline, time of injury (<6 h), 24–48 h post-injury, cleared for RTP, and unrestricted RTP. Symptom severity via SCAT3 (22-item, 0–132), balance via BESS (0–60), mental status via SAC (0–30). Season RHIE and injury day RHIE derived from cumulative impact data. Multivariable negative binomial regression models; adjusted for race/ethnicity, concussion history, medical history, BMI, event type, impact location.

## Findings
Median injurious linear acceleration: 66.7 g (IQR 48.2 g); rotational: 2963.0 rad/s² (IQR 2759.2 rad/s²). Median SRT: 6.1 days (IQR 5.8, n=45); median RTP: 12.3 days (IQR 7.8, n=36). Season RHIE predicted 22% longer SRT (Ratio 1.22, 95% CI [1.09, 1.36]) and 28% shorter RTP (Ratio 0.72, 95% CI [0.56, 0.93]) in multivariable models. No other biomechanical predictor (linear acceleration, rotational acceleration, injury day RHIE) significantly predicted SRT or RTP after covariate adjustment. Concussion history predicted 86% longer RTP (Ratio 1.86, 95% CI [1.05, 3.28]). Offensive players had SRTs 49% shorter than defensive players (Ratio 0.51, 95% CI [0.29, 0.92]). Side impacts predicted 54% shorter RTP vs. front impacts. Balance recovery varied by rotational acceleration for similar athletes but was not affected by linear acceleration, season RHIE, or injury day RHIE.

## Limitations
Small sample size (51 concussions from 45 athletes) limited multivariable model scope. HIT System is subject to known measurement error (~20% RMS). 52% of injuries were not immediately reported to clinical staff, potentially affecting impact identification. No computerized neurocognitive testing was included. Study limited to elite Division I athletes; findings cannot be generalized to other levels or sports. No formal power analysis performed. Participant retention across 5 time points was a pragmatic challenge in the multi-site design.

## Practical Implications
Clinical staff should not use the biomechanical characteristics of a concussive impact to predict individual recovery or set RTP timelines—impact magnitude alone is insufficient. Athletes with a prior concussion history should receive more conservative management given their 86% longer RTP duration. Season-long head impact exposure (cumulative load) carries more prognostic relevance than single-event biomechanics, reinforcing the value of monitoring cumulative impact metrics throughout the season. Offensive vs. defensive position differences in SRT may reflect clinical management differences and warrant investigation at individual programs.

## Athlete Development Notes
These findings underscore that subconcussive cumulative impact exposure across a season—more than any single injurious hit—is predictive of extended recovery timelines. Athlete development and sports science staff should track season RHIE for high-exposure athletes and use practice contact limitations to keep cumulative loads within safer ranges. Position-specific approaches to contact management are warranted given that defensive players showed significantly longer symptom resolution times.

## Return to Play Notes
Head injury biomechanics do not predict RTP time from concussion in college football players. Median RTP was 12.3 days. Greater season RHIE was associated with shorter RTP—possibly reflecting more aggressive clinical management decisions for athletes with low-exposure histories. Prior concussion history is the strongest clinical predictor of extended RTP (86% longer). Programs should base RTP decisions on standardized symptom resolution protocols rather than on injury-causing impact metrics, and should proactively flag athletes with concussion histories for extended monitoring.
