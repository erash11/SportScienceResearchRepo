# Card RK, Harrington JM, Reyes NP, Huish EG Jr, Pettegrew J. Does Playing Surface Affect the Risk of Anterior Cruciate Ligament Injuries in the National Football League? A Look at the 2017-2021 NFL seasons. The Physician and Sportsmedicine. 2023. doi:10.1080/00913847.2023.2178817

## TLDR
Non-natural playing surfaces (synthetic turf and GrassMaster combined) carry a 21.1% higher ACL injury rate compared to natural grass in the NFL over five seasons (2017–2021), but this difference does not reach statistical significance (OR=1.239, 95% CI 0.900–1.704, p=0.189). GrassMaster hybrid surfaces showed the highest injury rate (0.134/game), followed by turf (0.115) and grass (0.097). The study is the first to include GrassMaster in NFL surface analysis. Despite the trend being consistent with prior literature, the lack of statistical significance—likely due to limited sample size and reliance on publicly available data capturing only ~68% of true NFL ACL injuries—means definitive conclusions cannot be drawn. ## Abstract
This epidemiological study examined the prevalence and incidence of ACL injuries across natural grass, synthetic turf, and GrassMaster (hybrid) surfaces in the NFL during the 2017/18 through 2021/22 seasons using publicly available data. A total of 173 ACL ruptures with identifiable playing surfaces were recorded across 1,603 games: 73 on grass, 85 on turf, and 15 on GrassMaster. ACL injury rates were 0.097 per game on grass, 0.115 on turf, and 0.134 on GrassMaster, yielding a non-natural surface injury rate of 0.118. The injury rate ratio for non-natural vs. natural grass surfaces was 1.211 (21.1% higher risk), and the odds ratio was 1.239 (95% CI 0.900–1.704, p=0.189), which did not reach statistical significance. ## Methods
Retrospective epidemiological study using publicly available data (NFL team press releases, game reports, published injury reports) for NFL preseason, regular season, and playoff games from the 2017/18 through 2021/22 seasons. ACL injuries were included if they involved confirmed rupture or tear during a game where the playing surface could be identified; practice injuries and OTA injuries were excluded. Playing surfaces classified as: natural grass (Kentucky Bluegrass, Bermuda, or blended grass), synthetic turf (100% synthetic, multiple manufacturers), or GrassMaster (Tarkett Sports hybrid surface with synthetic fibers embedded in natural grass sub-base). ACL injuries recorded by field type (73 grass, 85 turf, 15 GrassMaster). Total games: 1,603 (752 grass, 739 turf, 112 GrassMaster). Injury rate defined as ACL ruptures per game. Injury rate ratios and odds ratios with 95% confidence intervals calculated; statistical analysis performed by a senior author (EH).

## Findings
173 total ACL ruptures on identifiable surfaces across 1,603 games. Grass: 73 injuries (42.1%), 752 games; Turf: 85 injuries (49.1%), 739 games; GrassMaster: 15 injuries (8.7%), 112 games. ACL injury rates per game: Grass 0.097, Turf 0.115, GrassMaster 0.134, Non-natural combined 0.118. Injury rate ratio turf vs. grass: 1.185 (18.5% increased risk on turf). Injury rate ratio non-natural vs. grass: 1.211 (21.1% increased risk). OR for turf vs. grass: 1.209 (95% CI 0.869–1.683, p=0.261). OR for non-natural vs. grass: 1.239 (95% CI 0.900–1.704, p=0.189). No statistically significant difference in ACL injury risk between natural and non-natural surfaces. The trend is consistent with prior studies showing elevated ACL injury rates on synthetic surfaces.

## Limitations
Reliance on publicly available data, which captures approximately 68% of true NFL ACL injuries reported to NFL medical staff (per Inclan et al., 2022), may underestimate the true incidence and potentially introduce bias. Practice injuries were excluded due to inability to identify playing surface, even though many teams use both natural and synthetic practice surfaces. Only the game surface (not practice surface) was attributable to each injury. Study is limited to preseason, regular season, and playoff games. Sample size may be insufficient to detect statistically significant differences; the authors note that collaboration with the NFL to access internal medical records would substantially strengthen future analyses. The GrassMaster surface was used by only two NFL teams (Philadelphia Eagles and Green Bay Packers), limiting its representativeness.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football medical and performance staffs should be aware that a consistent—though not statistically significant—trend of 18–21% higher ACL injury rates exists on non-natural playing surfaces compared to grass in the NFL. GrassMaster hybrid surfaces showed the highest per-game injury rate in this dataset. Teams playing home games on synthetic surfaces should consider this trend when designing ACL prevention programs and load management strategies around surface type. Advocacy for NFL-level access to internal injury surveillance data is needed to generate statistically powered studies. Until more definitive data is available, surface type should be considered one factor among many in individualized ACL risk profiling.

## Athlete Development Notes
Youth through college football programs should consider playing surface as a contextual risk factor in ACL injury prevention programming. Athletes regularly training or competing on synthetic turf may benefit from surface-specific neuromuscular training that addresses the higher traction and altered lower extremity mechanics associated with artificial surfaces. Footwear optimization (cleat type and length matched to surface) is a modifiable variable that may reduce traction-related ACL injury risk. Programs with access to both surface types should consider assigning highest-intensity ACL-risk activities (cutting drills, reactive agility) to natural grass when feasible.

## Return to Play Notes
Surface type should be considered in return-to-play planning for NFL players recovering from ACL reconstruction. Given the trend toward higher ACL injury incidence on synthetic turf and GrassMaster, a conservative approach to first full-contact return on these surfaces is warranted. Progressive surface reintroduction—beginning with natural grass and advancing to synthetic turf—may be appropriate for athletes with a history of ACL injury, particularly when returning to competition at venues with non-natural surfaces. The approximately 10.75-month average RTP timeline for NFL ACL injuries should factor in surface type and the athlete's neuromuscular readiness for cutting and planting mechanics on high-traction artificial turf.
