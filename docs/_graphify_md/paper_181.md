# Costello JP II, Wagner JD, Dahl VA, Cohen JL, Reuter AM, Kaplan LD. Effects of COVID-19 on Rate of Injury and Position-Specific Injury During the 2020 National Football League Season. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research. 2023. 10.1519/JSC.0000000000004610

## TLDR
The elimination of the 2020 NFL preseason and COVID-19-related restrictions on in-person training resulted in a 30.8% overall increase in regular-season injury risk compared to the prior 5-season average, with the largest spikes in soft tissue injuries early in the season (Week 1 RR=7.33). This study strongly supports the principle that proper preseason conditioning and gradual workload progression are essential injury prevention strategies. For football staff, the findings reinforce that virtual-only offseason programs are insufficient substitutes for structured in-person training, and that specific positions (cornerbacks, safeties, running backs, guards, defensive tackles) carry the highest risk when traditional preseason preparation is disrupted.

## Abstract
This retrospective epidemiological study compared NFL injury rates during the 2020 COVID-affected season against a 5-season baseline (2015-2019). A total of 5,597 injuries were identified across all seasons. Relative to prior seasons, the 2020 regular season showed significantly elevated overall injury risk, particularly during the first two thirds of the season, as well as increased postseason injury risk. Specific increases in hamstring, groin/hip, and abdominal/core injuries were found, with position-specific spikes in cornerbacks (hamstring), guards (hip/groin), safeties (Achilles), running backs (quadriceps and hamstring), and defensive tackles (arm injuries).

## Methods
Retrospective review of all NFL injuries from the 2015-2020 seasons using publicly available data from NFL.com, Pro-Football-Reference.com, ProsportsTransactions.com, and ESPN.com. An injury was defined as any event causing a player to miss the next game or be listed as do-not-return in elimination games. Athlete exposure (AE) was calculated assuming 3 live practices and 1 game per week per team. Relative risk (RR) with 95% CI was calculated comparing 2020 season injury rates to the 2015-2019 mean. Statistical analyses used SPSS v28, including two-tailed t-tests, chi-square tests, and one-way ANOVA.

## Findings
Total injuries: 993 in 2020 vs. 879-962 per season from 2015-2019. Regular season overall RR=1.308 (p=0.0005). Week 1 RR=7.33 (p=0.0024). W1-6 RR=1.964 (p=0.0304); W7-12 RR=1.8909 (p=0.0208); W13-17 no significant change. Postseason overall RR=1.1444 (p<0.0001). Anatomic increases: abdominal/core RR=1.248 (p=0.0011), groin/hip RR=2.534 (p=0.0019), hamstring RR=3.644 (p=0.0143). Position-specific increases: cornerback overall RR=3.99 (p<0.0001), guard overall RR=1.1913 (p=0.0136). Specific anatomic-position combinations with significant increases included CB hamstring, G hip/groin, S Achilles, RB hamstring, RB quadriceps, and DT arm. No change in proportion of game vs. practice injuries.

## Limitations
Retrospective design relying on publicly available data with potential for misreporting. No access to electronic medical records limited injury detail. Player- and team-specific variability in offseason training routines during COVID-19 could not be controlled. Roster size rule changes and COVID-related schedule disturbances introduced variability in AE calculations. Causal attribution to preseason elimination vs. other COVID-related factors cannot be definitively established.

## Practical Implications
The dramatic injury spike in Week 1 and across the first half of the 2020 season provides strong evidence that canceling preseason games and replacing in-person preparation with virtual training creates unacceptable injury risk for NFL players. Performance and sports medicine staff should resist pressure to eliminate or substantially reduce preseason physical preparation and should ensure that any offseason disruption is followed by a structured return-to-full-load protocol before the regular season begins. Position groups most at risk for soft tissue injuries following disrupted preparation include cornerbacks (hamstring), safeties (Achilles), running backs (hamstring and quadriceps), and guards (hip/groin).

## Athlete Development Notes
The findings underscore the critical role of sport-specific preseason conditioning in injury prevention. Hamstring, hip/groin, Achilles, and quadriceps injuries — all of which increased in 2020 — are well-documented sequelae of deconditioning in explosive, sprint-dependent athletes. Strength and conditioning programs for football must include progressive eccentric hamstring loading, hip mobility work, and Achilles tendon load tolerance training as core preseason elements, particularly after any period of reduced training.

## Return to Play Notes
The study is directly relevant to RTP protocols following extended absences (e.g., injury, suspension, holdout). An athlete returning to full NFL practice after weeks of limited training mirrors the physiological conditions of the 2020 preseason-less return: elevated soft tissue injury risk, especially for hamstrings, hip flexors, Achilles, and quadriceps. RTP programs should include a phased workload ramp-up over multiple weeks, position-specific movement preparation, and close monitoring of soft tissue injury symptoms, particularly during the first 6-12 weeks of return.
