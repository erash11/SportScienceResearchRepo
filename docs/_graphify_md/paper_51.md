# Novak AR, Impellizzeri FM, Trivedi A, Coutts AJ, McCall A. Analysis of the worst-case scenarios in an elite football team: Towards a better understanding and application. J Sports Sci. 2021;39(20):2323-2334.

## TLDR
Note: This paper studies soccer (English Premier League). The WCS as defined by rolling time windows is highly variable and context-dependent, making its use as a training benchmark conceptually questionable. Coaching and sport science staff should treat WCS as a complex composite construct rather than a simple threshold to replicate in training.

## Abstract
This study investigated variability in the worst-case scenario (WCS) and proposed a framework to improve its definition and guide further investigation in professional football (soccer). Optical tracking data from 26 male players across 38 English Premier League matches were analyzed for total distance, high-speed running (>5.5 m/s), and sprinting (>7.0 m/s) using a 3-minute rolling window. Linear mixed effects models examined contextual factors hypothesized to associate with WCS values, including player position, match half, time of occurrence, starter vs. substitute status, and prior epoch load. Unexplained variance remained high, with intra-individual CVs ranging from 4.6–76.4% across metrics.

## Methods
Retrospective cross-sectional study; optical tracking data (TRACAB Gen4, 25 FPS) from 26 male EPL players (age 28±4 y) across 38 matches of the 2018/19 season; WCS defined as peak 3-minute total distance (WCS-TD), high-speed running >5.5 m/s (WCS-HSR), and sprinting >7.0 m/s (WCS-SP) via rolling window; linear mixed effects models with position, match half, time-in-half, starter/substitute, uncoupled total output, pre-epoch measures, and minutes played as fixed effects; player ID and game ID as random effects; AIC model comparison; marginal and conditional R² reported.

## Findings
• High intra-individual variability: CV = 4.6–8.2% for WCS-TD, 15.6–37.8% for WCS-HSR, 21.1–76.4% for WCS-SP
• Marginal R² ranged from 0.28–0.40, leaving 47–60% of variance unexplained
• Position affected WCS-TD: Central Midfielders covered the most total distance (mean 500 m); Centre Backs covered the least
• Starters produced higher WCS-TD values than substitutes
• WCS-TD occurred earlier in match half; WCS-SP occurred later
• WCS-HSR associated with fewer minutes played and higher uncoupled total output
• No significant positional differences found for WCS-SP
• WCS for different metrics did not occur concurrently in the same match for individual players

## Limitations
Only 21 players included after filtering goalkeepers and those with <5 match appearances; data from a single EPL team over one season limits generalizability; analysis restricted to external load metrics (distance, speed) without internal load measures; some players observed <5 times, making individual WCS estimates unreliable; no causal inference possible from cross-sectional design.

## Practical Implications
Practitioners should refrain from using the WCS as a simple benchmark for training prescription, as its high variability and multi-factorial nature make it an unstable target. Training drills designed to replicate a single metric's WCS may not expose athletes to the concurrent demands that produce a true WCS. The WCS should be understood as a composite construct requiring both external and internal load context to be meaningful.

## Athlete Development Notes
Note: This paper is based on soccer (English Premier League). The conceptual critique of WCS applies broadly to any team sport, including American football: peak locomotor demands measured by a single external load metric (e.g., top-speed distance in a 3-minute window) do not capture the full physiological stress an athlete encounters during the most demanding game situations. American football practitioners using GPS/tracking data should interpret rolling-window peak demands with similar caution.

## Return to Play Notes
The concept of targeting an athlete's individual WCS as a return-to-play benchmark is explicitly challenged by this study. Given the high intra-individual variability (CV up to 76% for sprinting WCS), an athlete could theoretically achieve their prior WCS value by chance rather than through genuine readiness. RTP criteria based on WCS should be supplemented with internal load monitoring and contextual match data to avoid clearing athletes prematurely.
