# Lord C, Blazevich AJ, Drinkwater EJ, Ma'ayah F. Greater loss of horizontal force after a repeated-sprint test in footballers with a previous hamstring injury. J Sci Med Sport. 2018. doi:10.1016/j.jsams.2018.06.008

## TLDR
Footballers with a previous hamstring injury show a significantly greater fatigue-induced decline in horizontal ground reaction force (FH) during a repeated-sprint test compared to the non-injured leg or legs of uninjured players. A 13% drop in FH across 10 sprints in the injured leg — versus only 3% in uninjured legs — suggests persistent neuromuscular deficits that impair sprint endurance even after return to play. Repeated-sprint testing of FH asymmetry is a practical, football-specific screening tool with good discriminative ability (AUC=0.846) for identifying players with residual hamstring dysfunction.

## Abstract
This study quantified changes in running kinetics and kinematics during a repeated-sprint test (RST) in football players with and without a previous unilateral hamstring injury, and examined the sensitivity and specificity with which these variables could identify previous hamstring injury. Forty semi-professional Western Australia State League footballers — 20 with previous unilateral hamstring injury and 20 without — completed a 10 x 6-second RST on a non-motorised treadmill dynamometer (Woodway Curve). Horizontal force (FH), vertical force (FV), contact time, and flight time were compared between previously injured and uninjured legs. Mean FH in the previously injured (kicking) leg decreased by 13% (p=0.001; ES=1.69) across the RST, compared to only 3% in uninjured legs. This between-leg difference in FH correctly identified 77% of previously injured legs (77% sensitivity, 85% specificity; AUC=0.846).

## Methods
Cross-sectional study of 40 semi-professional footballers: 20 with previous unilateral hamstring injury to the kicking leg (injured group, IG) and 20 uninjured controls (UG). Players completed two familiarisation sessions followed by a 10 x 6-second RST on a Woodway Curve non-motorised treadmill with 24-second active recovery jogs between sprints. FH (propulsive), FV, contact time, and flight time were recorded at 200 Hz per leg across all sprints. Data were analysed using repeated-measures ANOVA. Binary logistic regression and ROC curve analyses assessed sensitivity and specificity of FH change for identifying previously injured legs.

## Findings
The kicking (injured) leg in the IG showed a 13% decrease in mean FH from sprint 1 to sprint 10 (p=0.001; ES=1.69), significantly greater than the 3% decline in the kicking leg of the UG (p=0.058; ES=0.28). Between-leg FH asymmetry increased substantially over the course of the RST in the IG but not in the UG. No significant between-group differences were observed for FV, contact time, or flight time. Binary logistic regression showed that the FH difference from RST1 to RST10 explained 39.8% of variance (Nagelkerke R²) and correctly classified 77% of previously injured legs with 85% specificity. ROC analysis identified an optimal FH cut-off of 11.5 N (AUC=0.846, p<0.001, 95% CI: 0.721–0.970).

## Limitations
Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality or determine fatigability prior to injury. All injured players had sustained injury to the kicking leg, limiting generalizability to non-kicking leg injuries. Players had varying rehabilitation histories and recovery times post-injury. The study cannot confirm whether FH deficits preceded injury or arose from it. The RST commenced from a jogging (rather than stationary) start, which may have reduced absolute FH magnitudes compared to other protocols.

## Practical Implications
The repeated-sprint test using a non-motorised treadmill provides a practical, football-specific assessment of horizontal force fatigue that can identify persistent hamstring deficits after injury with 77% sensitivity and 85% specificity. Rehabilitation and return-to-play programs should target the reduction of inter-limb FH asymmetry, particularly under repeated high-intensity sprint conditions. Players cleared for return to play may still harbour meaningful hamstring deficits detectable only under fatigue, supporting the use of fatiguing sprint protocols as a final-stage RTP assessment tool.

## Athlete Development Notes
Non-motorised treadmill repeated-sprint testing can be integrated into pre-season and in-season athletic screening to detect inter-limb horizontal force asymmetries in football players. Athletes showing greater fatigue-related FH asymmetry may benefit from targeted eccentric hamstring and hip extensor development to improve sprint endurance and reduce re-injury risk during the late stages of high-intensity match play.

## Return to Play Notes
A repeated-sprint test monitoring horizontal force production per leg is recommended as a sport-specific component of hamstring RTP assessment. Players who demonstrate a >11.5 N between-leg decline in FH across 10 sprints may not be ready for full return despite passing traditional strength tests. Rehabilitation should emphasise asymmetry reduction under fatigue, specifically targeting the eccentric knee flexor and hip extensor functions that underpin horizontal force production during sprinting.
