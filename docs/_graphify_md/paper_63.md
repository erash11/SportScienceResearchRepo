# Wodka-Natkaniec E, Zyznawska J. Assessment of static foot posture as an indicator of biomechanical adaptation in American football players. Acta of Bioengineering and Biomechanics. 2025;27(3):DOI:10.37190/abb/213617.

## TLDR
American football players demonstrate more frequent foot pronation than non-athletes despite mostly normal total FPI-6 scores, suggesting sport-specific biomechanical adaptations that may warrant individualized footwear assessment and pronation monitoring as a preventive strategy.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional observational study assessed static foot posture using the Foot Posture Index-6 (FPI-6) in 70 male American football players (Kraków Football Kings Club, Poland) compared to 35 non-athletic male controls. Athletes had a significantly higher BMI (mean 27 kg/m²) than controls (mean 22.47 kg/m²). Both groups showed predominantly neutral foot posture overall, but FPI-6 component analysis revealed significantly greater pronation tendencies in football players across all sub-scores except FPI-2 of the left foot. Pronation was approximately three times more frequent in the right foot and four times more frequent in the left foot in athletes versus controls. No significant relationship was found between FPI-6, BMI, age, or training experience within the athlete group.

## Methods
Cross-sectional observational study. Sports group (SG): 70 male American football players (mean age 22.6±4.1 years, mean BMI 27 kg/m², mean height 184±7.1 cm). Control group (CG): 35 male non-athlete university students (mean age 21.5±3.3 years). FPI-6 assessed bilaterally by a single trained physiotherapist using standardized standing position. Chi-squared test for FPI-6 categorical comparisons; Mann-Whitney U-test for BMI comparison. Relationships between FPI-6 and BMI, age, and training experience evaluated within each group. Ethics approval obtained (nr 1072.6120.129.2018).

## Findings
• BMI was significantly higher in football athletes (mean 27 kg/m²) vs. controls (22.47 kg/m²; p<0.01)
• Both groups showed predominantly neutral foot posture on total FPI-6 (84.3% left, 81.4% right in SG vs. 85.7% left, 80% right in CG)
• Significant between-group differences in FPI-6 component scores (p<0.01) except FPI-2 left foot
• Pronation was ~3× more frequent in the right foot (15.7% vs. 5.7%) and ~4× more frequent in the left foot (11.4% vs. 2.9%) in athletes vs. controls
• Supination occurred more often in the control group than in athletes
• No significant relationship found between FPI-6 and BMI, age, or training experience within the athlete group (all p>0.05)
• In the non-athlete CG, a significant relationship between left foot FPI-6 and BMI was found (p=0.02)

## Limitations
Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference. American football is a relatively young sport in Poland, so most players had limited training experience (<6 years), potentially attenuating training-duration effects. Only static foot posture assessed — dynamic foot function and kinematic analysis not included. No assessment of injury history or lower limb strength. Single-site study with a relatively small sample; generalizability to more experienced or American-based players is uncertain.

## Practical Implications
Given the higher prevalence of foot pronation in American football athletes, individualized footwear assessment — including anti-pronation options — may be beneficial. Monitoring FPI-6 as part of routine screening can identify athletes at risk for lower limb injuries related to biomechanical foot adaptations. BMI management and body composition monitoring are important given the significant BMI difference between athletes and non-athletes and its potential effect on foot loading.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football. The study is conducted specifically in American football players and highlights a sport-specific tendency toward foot pronation, likely driven by high body mass, cleat use, abrupt directional changes, and repeated ground reaction forces. Foot posture screening using FPI-6 could be integrated into pre-participation physical exams for American football players. Position-specific footwear recommendations may be warranted given differences in movement patterns and body weight across positions.

## Return to Play Notes
Not a return-to-play study. However, findings are relevant to RTP considerations following lower limb injuries. Pronated foot posture in football athletes may contribute to ankle sprains, knee valgus, and Achilles/peroneal tendon stress. Athletes returning from lower limb injuries should have foot posture evaluated as part of a comprehensive biomechanical screen to address any pronation-related contributing factors before full sport resumption. Individualized footwear and orthotic interventions may reduce reinjury risk.
