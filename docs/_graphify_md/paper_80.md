# Sisk M, Medawar N, McClure M, Cooke B, Cannon R, Kufner D, D'Almeida S, Jardaly A, Asif I, Momaya A, Ponce B. Cardiovascular Disease in Retired NFL Players: A Systematic Review. The Physician and Sportsmedicine. 2024. DOI: 10.1080/00913847.2024.2315929.

## TLDR
Despite high rates of overweight and obesity (90%), retired NFL players show comparable or more favorable cardiovascular and metabolic profiles than community controls, though cardiovascular disease remains their leading cause of death. Tailored preventive cardiovascular care strategies are needed for former players, particularly former linemen who show significantly higher rates of metabolic syndrome.

## Abstract
This systematic review synthesized available literature on cardiovascular health outcomes in former NFL players using PRISMA guidelines and searches of PubMed, Scopus, and Embase. Sixteen studies met inclusion criteria, covering weight gain/obesity, mortality, obstructive sleep apnea, and cardiovascular disease risk factors. Though evidence was discordant across studies, former NFL players appeared to possess more favorable metabolic profiles and decreased mortality compared to community controls. Approximately 90% of former players were overweight or obese, and cardiovascular/cardiometabolic disease was identified as the leading cause of death in this population. Further research is necessary to establish the full impact of NFL play on cardiovascular health.

## Methods
Systematic review following PRISMA 27-item checklist. Searches conducted in PubMed, Embase, and Scopus (completed September 2021). Inclusion criteria: retired NFL players, cardiovascular-related outcomes, English-language, full-length publications. Exclusion: active players, case reports/series, commentaries, non-English studies. Risk of bias assessed independently by two reviewers using the Newcastle-Ottawa Quality Assessment Scale. Studies organized into four categories: weight gain/obesity, mortality, obstructive sleep apnea, and cardiovascular disease. Sixteen studies (n=122 to 3506 participants) met final inclusion criteria.

## Findings
• 90% of former NFL players had overweight-range BMI (>24.9 kg/m²).
• Cardiovascular/cardiometabolic disease was the leading cause of death in both NFL-exposed cohorts and controls.
• Former linemen had 59.8% prevalence of metabolic syndrome vs. 30% for former non-linemen and 24% for US adult males.
• Compared to community controls matched for age, ethnicity, and BMI, former players had significantly lower prevalence of smoking, diabetes (p<0.01), sedentary lifestyle (p<0.05), and metabolic syndrome (p<0.05), but higher impaired fasting glucose (p<0.01).
• Former NFL players had a significantly higher risk of sleep apnea (27% vs. 11.5% of controls; p=0.002).
• BMI was associated with a 31% increase in coronary artery calcium prevalence (OR 1.31, 95% CI 1.11–1.56).
• Overall mortality was lower in former NFL players compared to US population controls.
• Weight gain from high school to college was significantly associated with cardiovascular disease (OR 1.08), chronic pain (OR 1.09), and sleep apnea (OR 1.13).

## Limitations
Evidence was discordant across studies due to heterogeneous study designs, control group selection, and data sources. Eleven of 16 studies used the same NFL Player Care Foundation/Living Heart Foundation Screening Program dataset, raising concern for overlap. Most studies are cross-sectional. BMI may be an inadequate measure for this population due to high muscle mass; percent body fat may be more appropriate. Selection bias may affect self-reported survey studies.

## Practical Implications
Former linemen are a particularly high-risk group for metabolic syndrome and warrant proactive cardiovascular screening and lifestyle intervention programs post-retirement. Weight management strategies during playing career may reduce long-term cardiovascular risk. Obstructive sleep apnea screening should be standard for retired players, especially linemen. Despite general cardiovascular risk, former players show protective factors (less smoking, more physical activity history) that can be leveraged in preventive care.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to NFL football. Progressive weight gain from high school to professional play is linked to cardiovascular disease, sleep apnea, and chronic pain in later life. Monitoring body composition trajectories from recruiting through career transition and emphasizing healthy weight management as part of long-term athlete development may reduce post-career cardiometabolic risk, particularly for linemen.

## Return to Play Notes
Not a return-to-play study. However, cardiovascular screening findings may be relevant during RTP evaluations after cardiac events in current players. The high prevalence of metabolic syndrome risk factors in linemen, even during their active careers, suggests cardiovascular assessment should be integrated into pre-participation and return-to-play protocols.
