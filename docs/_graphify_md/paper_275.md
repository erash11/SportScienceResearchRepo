# O'Connor C, Renaghan E, Bennett A, Ferrer L, Damodaran R, Geraghty P, Feigenbaum LA. Hip adduction and abduction strength values in NCAA Division I American football players: strength values and a comparison across position groups. Phys Ther Sport. 2025; doi:10.1016/j.ptsp.2025.01.004.

## TLDR
NCAA Division I American football players demonstrate lower hip adduction and abduction relative strength — and markedly lower adduction:abduction ratios — compared to soccer, Gaelic football, and rugby union athletes. Lineman-position players show the lowest relative hip strength of all position groups despite similar absolute values, suggesting their large body mass masks meaningful strength deficits relative to sport demands. These findings establish a first reference dataset and highlight a potential groin injury risk that warrants targeted hip strengthening, particularly for linemen.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study is the first to report maximal isometric hip adduction squeeze and abduction press strength values in NCAA Division I American football players, and to compare strength across position groups. Eighty-five male players from an ACC program were tested using the ForceFrame Strength Testing System in two positions: 0° hip-flexion long-lever and 0° hip-flexion short-lever. Absolute, relative (N/kg), and normalised (Nm/kg) strength values were reported. Players were grouped as lineman (offensive/defensive line), mid-position (tight end, running back, linebacker), or skill position (quarterback, wide receiver, defensive back, punter, kicker, snapper). ANOVA revealed significant differences in relative hip adduction and abduction strength between position groups (medium-to-large effect size, n²=0.116–0.284), with skill and mid-position players demonstrating significantly greater relative strength than linemen. Overall hip adduction:abduction ratios ranged from 0.68–0.88 (long-lever) and 1.05–1.09 (short-lever), notably lower than other field-sport cohorts.

## Methods
Cross-sectional study. 85 male NCAA Division I football players (mean age 20.0±1.79 years; mass 108.3±23.69 kg; height 188.7±6.57 cm) from one ACC program. Testing conducted at the university training facility at the start of summer pre-season. ForceFrame Strength Testing System (VALD Performance) used for maximum isometric hip adduction squeeze and abduction press in two positions: 0° hip-flexion long-lever (malleoli against force pads) and 0° hip-flexion short-lever (femoral condyles against force pads). Two maximal 5-second trials per test position; peak value used. Absolute (N), relative (N/kg), and normalised (Nm/kg) values reported bilaterally and unilaterally. Players grouped into lineman, mid-position, and skill-position categories. ANOVA with Bonferroni post-hoc compared position groups; pain during testing recorded on a 0–10 NPRS.

## Findings
ANOVA revealed significant differences (p≤0.05) in relative hip adduction and abduction strength between position groups with medium-to-large effect sizes (n²=0.116–0.284). Skill and mid-position players had significantly greater relative hip adduction squeeze and abduction press strength than linemen across both testing positions (differences of 0.37–1.01 N/kg in adduction, 0.37–1.01 N/kg in abduction). No significant differences were found between skill and mid-position groups. Absolute strength values were not significantly different between position groups. Hip adduction:abduction ratios ranged from 0.68–0.88 in the long-lever position and 1.05–1.09 in the short-lever position — lower than comparable field-sport cohorts. The lineman group showed an adduction:abduction ratio of 0.68±0.21 in the long-lever position, falling below the 0.80 threshold associated with elevated groin injury risk.

## Limitations
Data collected from a single NCAA Division I program; results may reflect that specific programme's strength and conditioning practices rather than the broader population. Testing conducted at the start of summer pre-season; values may change as the season progresses. The convenience sample limits generalisability across Division I programs. Players with prior hip/groin injury were included to reflect the true playing population, potentially influencing strength values.

## Practical Implications
This study provides the first hip strength reference values for American football players using a gold-standard ForceFrame testing protocol, enabling practitioners to benchmark individual athletes. The notably low hip adduction:abduction ratios — especially in linemen (0.68) — suggest a population-wide risk for hip/groin injury that may be underappreciated. Routine hip strength screening and targeted hip adductor and abductor strengthening programmes, particularly for lineman-position players, are warranted to reduce groin injury risk and improve player availability.

## Athlete Development Notes
Position-specific differences in relative hip strength should inform training programme design. Linemen, who have the lowest relative adduction and abduction strength despite their mass, may benefit most from targeted hip adductor/abductor strengthening incorporated into their strength and conditioning. Skill and mid-position athletes, who perform more high-speed running and change-of-direction, appear to develop greater relative hip strength naturally through those movement demands, but should still be individually screened against normative references.

## Return to Play Notes
The low hip adduction:abduction ratios observed in this American football cohort — particularly the 0.68 ratio in linemen, below the 0.80 injury-risk threshold — suggest that return-to-play criteria used in other field sports (e.g., achieving a ratio ≥0.90) may be relevant targets for post-groin-injury clearance in American football. Position-specific normative data from this study can be used to set individualized RTP benchmarks for players recovering from hip/groin injuries.
