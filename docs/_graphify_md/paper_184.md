# Horgan BG, West NP, Tee N, Halson SL, Drinkwater EJ, Chapman DW, Haff GG. Effect of repeated post-resistance exercise cold or hot water immersion on in-season inflammatory responses in academy rugby players: a randomised controlled cross-over design. European Journal of Applied Physiology. 2024. doi:10.1007/s00421-024-05424-3

## TLDR
Both CWI and HWI can be used post-resistance exercise during the competitive season in team sport athletes without compromising adaptive inflammatory responses. CWI produced small reductions in muscle damage (CK) and increased heat shock protein HSP-72, suggesting a thermoregulatory adaptive response. HWI produced moderate reductions in CK and the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-1ra, while increasing anabolic growth factors IGF-1 and PDGF-BB. Practitioners can prescribe either modality during in-season phases, with HWI providing an anabolic signalling advantage and CWI supporting thermoregulatory adaptation.

## Abstract
This randomised controlled cross-over study investigated the effects of repeated post-resistance exercise cold water immersion (CWI, 15°C×15 min) versus hot water immersion (HWI, 39°C×15 min) versus a control (CON, static stretching) on systemic inflammatory and hormone responses in 18 male academy Super Rugby players across a 12-week in-season programme. Blood measures were collected fasted and in the morning at baseline and every four weeks. Linear mixed-effects models were used to analyse treatment, time, and interaction effects across the biomarkers.

## Methods
Randomised controlled cross-over design; 18 male academy Super Rugby players (age 19.9±1.5 y, mass 98.3±10.7 kg); 12-week programme divided into three 4-week blocks (CON, CWI at 15°C, HWI at 39°C), each 15 min whole-body post-resistance exercise twice weekly; fasted morning blood samples at baseline and end of each 4-week block for CK, myoglobin, IL-1ra, IL-1b, IL-4, IL-6, IL-8, IL-10, IL-17a, TNF-α, IGF-1, IGFBP-3, PDGF-BB, HSP-72, testosterone, and cortisol; linear mixed-effects models for analysis.

## Findings
CWI significantly reduced CK compared to CON (p=0.025, g=0.05) and HWI reduced CK compared to CON (p<0.001, g=0.62). HWI decreased IL-1ra compared to CON (p=0.013, g=0.59). HWI increased growth factors IGF-1 and PDGF-BB compared to CON and CWI (p<0.001–0.026, g=0.06–0.17). CWI increased HSP-72 compared to HWI (p=0.004, g=0.46). No significant differences in testosterone, cortisol, or pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1b, IL-6, IL-8, IL-17a, TNF-α) were observed between interventions.

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=18) and a single time-point blood measurement at the end of each 4-week block may not capture the full kinetics of the biomarkers. Dietary standardisation was not fully controlled, which may influence inflammatory and hormonal responses. Subjects could not be blinded to temperature conditions. Pro-inflammatory cytokine responses were influenced by percent body fat as a covariate, limiting direct comparisons.

## Practical Implications
Both CWI and HWI are safe and viable post-resistance exercise recovery strategies during the competitive season in highly trained athletes. HWI may be favoured when anabolic adaptation is a priority (via IGF-1 and PDGF-BB elevation), while CWI may be preferred when thermoregulatory stress adaptation (HSP-72) is desired. Practitioners should consider individualising water immersion temperature based on athlete goals and responses across a competitive block.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football players in training blocks requiring simultaneous strength development and recovery, HWI post-resistance exercise may support anabolic signalling pathways (IGF-1, PDGF-BB) that underpin muscular adaptation, while still reducing muscle damage markers. CWI remains effective for blunting exercise-induced muscle damage (CK) without substantially impairing adaptive inflammatory responses, making both viable tools in periodised recovery plans for developing athletes.

## Return to Play Notes
During return-to-play from soft tissue injury, water immersion strategies can be strategically periodised. CWI may be appropriate in early stages to attenuate muscle damage and inflammation, while HWI may support the anabolic and growth factor environment needed for tissue remodelling and muscular reconditioning in later rehabilitation stages. Neither modality significantly alters testosterone or cortisol, suggesting minimal hormonal disruption during RTP programmes.
