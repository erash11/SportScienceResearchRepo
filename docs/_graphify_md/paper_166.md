# Pfaller AY, Brooks MA, Hetzel S, McGuine TA. Effect of a New Rule Limiting Full Contact Practice on the Incidence of Sport-Related Concussion in High School Football Players. Am J Sports Med. 2019;47(10):2294-2299. doi: 10.1177/0363546519860120

## TLDR
Limiting full-contact practice time by rule reduced high school football practice concussions by 57%, with no corresponding increase in game-time concussions. This is strong evidence that targeted contact restrictions during practice are effective for concussion reduction without compromising game-readiness or technique. The data also show that overall concussion rates trended down (1.58 to 1.28 per 1000 AEs), though the overall change was not statistically significant, likely due to sample size. For football programs at any level, reducing unnecessary full-contact repetitions in practice is one of the most evidence-backed interventions available.

## Abstract
This cohort study examined whether a Wisconsin Interscholastic Athletic Association (WIAA) rule change limiting full-contact practice duration reduced sport-related concussion (SRC) rates in high school football. Data from 2081 players in pre-rule seasons (2012-2013) were compared to 945 players in the post-rule 2014 season. Athletic trainers tracked athlete exposures and concussion incidence across practice and competition settings.

## Methods
Prospective cohort study across Wisconsin high schools: 34 schools in 2012, 18 in 2013 (pre-rule), and 26 schools in 2014 (post-rule). Athletic trainers classified practice sessions as full-contact, drill-contact, or no-contact, and recorded all athlete exposures (AEs) and SRC diagnoses. SRC defined per American Academy of Neurology guidelines. Chi-square tests compared concussion rates; Wilcoxon rank sum tests compared days lost.

## Findings
Practice SRC rate fell 57% after the rule change: from 0.76 per 1000 AEs (2012-2013) to 0.33 per 1000 AEs in 2014 (P=0.003). Game concussion rate was unchanged (5.81 vs 5.74 per 1000 AEs, P=0.999). Overall concussion rate trended down from 1.58 to 1.28 per 1000 AEs but was not statistically significant (P=0.139). Days lost to SRC were not significantly different (13 vs 14 days, P=0.967). No difference in concussion rates by prior football experience, suggesting less-experienced players were not harmed by reduced contact practice.

## Limitations
Head impact magnitude was not directly measured, so volume reduction in total head impacts is inferred. Classification of practice contact type by athletic trainers may introduce bias. Study was underpowered to detect significant overall concussion rate change. Other injury types were not tracked. Adherence to the new rule was assumed but not directly verified. Time-series design is susceptible to secular trends including changing concussion awareness and clinical practice.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football programs should use this evidence to support limiting unnecessary full-contact repetitions in practice, consistent with existing NFLPA collective bargaining agreement restrictions on padded practices. The argument that full contact in practice is required to prevent injury during games is not supported by this data — game concussion rates did not rise after contact was restricted. Programs should audit their practice scripts to classify contact levels and minimize full-contact time to what is strictly necessary for competitive preparation.

## Athlete Development Notes
Players at all experience levels — including those with less prior football experience — showed no disadvantage from reduced full-contact practice, suggesting that technical skill development is preserved with drill-contact and thud-level activities. Programs should develop practice structures that achieve necessary positional skill development within no-contact and drill-contact frameworks as much as possible, reserving full-contact for specific installation and game-simulation periods.

## Return to Play Notes
The study indirectly informs RTP protocols by demonstrating that concussed players who miss practice but return to game competition do not face elevated concussion risk when the practice environment has reduced contact levels. Graduated RTP progressions that begin with no-contact and drill-contact activities are consistent with the practice structure that reduced overall concussion incidence in this study.
