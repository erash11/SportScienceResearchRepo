# Bergstein VE, Agarwal AR, Solon LF, Mikula JD, Best MJ, LaPorte DM. Epidemiologic Trends in hand injuries in the National Football League from 2009-2010 to 2019-2020. Phys Sportsmed. 2023. DOI: 10.1080/00913847.2023.2286942.

## TLDR
NFL hand injuries declined 65.6% over 11 seasons (2009–2019), a trend that coincides with the implementation of 39 new player safety rules—16 of which directly involve the hands. Quarterbacks are disproportionately affected across all hand injury subtypes due to the demands of throwing and the vulnerability during tackles. Wrist injuries are the most severe hand injuries by all severity metrics. These findings support position-specific injury prevention protocols and continued advocacy for hand-protective rule changes in the NFL.

## Abstract
This retrospective study characterized the prevalence, anatomic distribution, and severity of hand injuries (wrist, metacarpus, finger, thumb) in NFL players over 11 seasons (2009–2010 to 2019–2020) using the publicly available Pro Football Reference database. Of 6,127 players, 847 (13.8%) sustained at least one hand injury, totaling 825 injuries. Hand injuries decreased in prevalence by 65.6% over the study period, coinciding with increased NFL player safety rule implementation. Quarterbacks had the highest prevalence and severity of hand injuries across all anatomic locations. Wrist injuries were the most severe, with the lowest return-to-play rate (93.3%), most players on injured reserve (28.6%), and most games missed (mean 2.5).

## Methods
Retrospective review of the publicly available Pro Football Reference database for all 32 NFL teams from the 2009–2010 through 2019–2020 seasons. Hand injuries were subclassified as finger, hand (metacarpus), thumb, or wrist. Players excluded if any injury not related to the hand was the reason for games missed. Variables analyzed included injury anatomic location, player position, games missed (mean and SD), percentage of players placed on injured reserve (IR), and percentage returning to play (RTP). Data analyzed using Microsoft Excel.

## Findings
825 total hand injuries were identified across 726 players (11.85%). Injury distribution: wrist 23.75%, metacarpus 34.90%, finger 18.18%, thumb 23.15%. Overall RTP rate was 97.40%; mean games missed was 1.70 (SD 3.31); 14.77% placed on IR. Wrist injuries had the lowest RTP (93.33%), highest mean games missed (2.48, SD 1.96), and highest IR rate (28.57%). Finger injuries had the lowest mean games missed (0.72) and lowest IR rate (4.61%). Quarterbacks had the highest prevalence of hand injuries at all anatomic locations (26.67% overall). Hand injuries declined 65.6% from 2009 to 2019, with the steepest decline after 2012 when most new hand-relevant safety rules took effect.

## Limitations
Data sourced from a public database that classifies injuries only by general anatomic location, precluding specific injury type identification (e.g., fracture vs. ligament tear). Injuries classified as 'other' receive no anatomic designation, creating potential underrepresentation. Underreporting of hand injuries is likely given financial disincentives for NFL players to disclose injuries. Some position groups had very small sample sizes, limiting generalizability of position-specific findings. Study did not explore associations or risk factors contributing to hand injury occurrence.

## Practical Implications
Medical and coaching staff should implement position-specific hand injury prevention protocols, with special emphasis on quarterbacks who face the highest prevalence and severity. Wrist injuries warrant heightened clinical attention given their association with prolonged absence and IR placement. The correlation between rule changes and declining hand injury prevalence supports advocacy for continued adoption of player safety rules involving hand and blocking mechanics.

## Athlete Development Notes
For skill players—especially quarterbacks and wide receivers—hand and wrist injury prevention should be integrated into sport-specific training and protective equipment protocols. The high RTP rate for most hand injuries (97.4%) is reassuring for development staff, but wrist injuries may require modified training plans and extended timelines. Position-specific screening at the combine and preseason may help identify at-risk athletes for targeted prehabilitation.

## Return to Play Notes
Overall RTP rate across all hand injuries was 97.4%, with an average of 1.7 games missed. Wrist injuries had the lowest RTP rate (93.3%) and highest IR placement (28.6%), suggesting they carry the greatest risk of season-ending outcomes. Finger injuries had the fastest recovery (mean 0.72 games missed). Return-to-play planning should account for position demands: quarterbacks, who rely heavily on hand function for throwing mechanics, may require longer functional recovery timelines even after apparent clinical healing.
