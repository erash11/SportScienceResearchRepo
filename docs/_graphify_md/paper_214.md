# Barreira P, Vaz JR, Ferreira R, Araújo JP, Tavares F. External training loads and soft-tissue injury occurrence during congested vs noncongested periods in football. Int J Sports Physiol Perform. 2024; Ahead of Print. doi:10.1123/ijspp.2024-0058

## TLDR
Across two full professional football seasons, congested fixture schedules (matches ≤96 hours apart) did not significantly increase soft-tissue injury rates or burden compared to noncongested periods. Higher match exposure during congestion elevated weekly match load, while noncongested periods allowed greater training load accumulation. The finding challenges the assumption that congested schedules directly elevate injury risk and suggests that adequate training during regular periods may provide a protective conditioning effect. Relative speed metrics V80 and V90 are recommended as individualized training planning tools.

## Abstract
This study examined the influence of congested (matches within ≤96 hours) versus noncongested fixture periods on soft-tissue injury incidence and external load across two full professional football seasons (2021–22 and 2022–23) in a Portuguese Liga I team. Thirty-three outfield players were tracked using 10-Hz GPS (STATSports APEX). External load metrics included HSR (>19.8 km/h), sprint distance (>25 km/h), relative velocity zones above 80% (V80) and 90% (V90) of individual maximum speed, and acceleration/deceleration metrics (AD2, AD3). Soft-tissue injuries were classified using IOC/UEFA consensus definitions. A total of 65 medical-attention and 39 time-loss injuries occurred over the two seasons, producing 704 layoff days and 113 official matches missed. Soft-tissue injury rates were 3.9 and 3.2 per 1000 hours for congested and noncongested periods respectively, with no statistically significant difference between periods. Total weekly external load was significantly higher during noncongested periods for all metrics except AD3; training load was higher in noncongested weeks while match load was higher during congested weeks.

## Methods
Prospective observational cohort study. N=33 professional outfield players from Sporting CP (Portuguese Liga I), seasons 2021–22 and 2022–23. Congested periods defined as official matches ≤96 hours apart (15 periods, 225 days, 71 games). External load collected via 10-Hz GPS (STATSports APEX): HSR, sprint distance, V80, V90, AD2 (meters accelerating/decelerating >2 m/s²), AD3 (count of accelerations/decelerations >3 m/s²). Injury data collected per IOC/UEFA football-specific consensus. Statistical comparisons via paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test (Jamovi v1.6); significance at P<.05.

## Findings
Soft-tissue injury rates were 3.9 (congested) vs 3.2 (noncongested) per 1000 hours (P=.781, ES=0.074); injury burden was 71.8 vs 60.5 days/1000 hours (P=.668). Total weekly HSR (1643 vs 1968 m, P<.001), sprint (339 vs 404 m, P<.001), V80 (101 vs 140 m, P<.001), V90 (12.2 vs 19.0 m, P<.001), and AD2 (5137 vs 6389 m, P<.001) were significantly higher during noncongested periods; AD3 did not differ (613 vs 629, P=.689). Match load was significantly higher during congested weeks; training load was significantly higher during noncongested weeks. One player with 5 injuries in 2022–23 disproportionately inflated the injury burden data.

## Limitations
Single-team sample from one club limits generalizability. No position-specific or consecutive match participation analysis was performed. International duty periods excluded. Post-COVID 5-substitute rule and individual injury history were not formally modeled. Outlier player substantially affected injury rate and burden totals. Retrospective injury classification may have inherent bias.

## Practical Implications
Congested and noncongested schedules produce similar soft-tissue injury rates, indicating that match congestion alone does not drive injury risk when training and recovery are managed appropriately. Staff should use noncongested periods to build chronic load across all metrics — particularly V80, V90, and AD2 — to prepare players for the demands of congested stretches. Squad rotation and individualized speed-zone targets (V80/V90) should be incorporated into periodization planning to maintain readiness while limiting injury exposure during fixture pileups.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athlete development, this study reinforces the importance of systematically accumulating high-intensity running load (especially above 80–90% of individual maximum velocity) during non-congested training windows. These relative speed zones are linked to musculoskeletal adaptation in hamstrings and lower limb muscles. Developing tolerance to these exposures during pre-season and regular-season training blocks can buffer players against the increased match load that accompanies congested schedules.

## Return to Play Notes
No direct RTP protocol is described. However, findings suggest that players returning from soft-tissue injuries should be progressively reintroduced to V80/V90 and AD2 load targets derived from match demands before being cleared for congested match schedules. The lack of injury rate difference between congested and noncongested periods implies that match load per se is manageable if chronic load preparation is adequate, which has implications for setting RTP readiness thresholds for soft-tissue injuries.
