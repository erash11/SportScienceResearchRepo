# Kang L, Okolo M, Cleary EJ, Lu Y, Iyer S, Levy BA, Hevesi M, Okoroha KR, Krych AJ. High Rates of Return to American Football After Primary Hip Arthroscopy for Femoroacetabular Impingement: A 2-Year Minimum Follow-up. Orthop J Sports Med. 2026;14(1):23259671251397513. DOI: 10.1177/23259671251397513

## TLDR
Amateur American football players — most of them high school or college athletes — achieve an 88% return-to-sport rate after hip arthroscopy for FAI when nonmedical reasons for not returning (graduation, changing priorities) are excluded. The overall return rate including nonmedical factors was only 61%, highlighting that life circumstances significantly affect football participation at these levels. Outcomes are excellent and durable at 7+ years. Linemen represent over half the surgical population, likely due to the extreme hip loading demands of their position. No factors — including BMI, age, position, or alpha angle — significantly predicted RTS.

## Abstract
This retrospective case series from Mayo Clinic examined return-to-sport (RTS) rates and patient-reported outcomes in 38 active American football players (49 hips) who underwent primary hip arthroscopy with labral repair for femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) between 2010 and 2023. The cohort was predominantly high school (74%) and college (21%) athletes with a mean age of 18.1 years; 51% were linemen. At a mean 7.6-year follow-up, 88% of players achieved RTS (excluding those who did not return for nonmedical reasons). Significant improvements were observed in all patient-reported outcome measures (mHHS, HOS-ADL, HOS-Sport), all exceeding the minimal clinically important difference. The reoperation rate was 8%.

## Methods
Retrospective analysis of a prospectively maintained institutional database (Mayo Clinic) from 2010–2023. Inclusion: active football players with primary hip arthroscopy for FAI with labral repair and intent to return to sport. Exclusion: follow-up <2 years, concomitant PAO, insufficient documentation, or opted out. 49 hips in 38 male players included. Data collected: demographics, radiographic parameters (lateral center-edge angle, Tonnis angle, alpha angle), surgical details, complications, reoperations. Minimum 2-year follow-up via REDCap and telephone interview for updated PROMs (mHHS, HOS-ADL, HOS-Sport, Tegner, VAS pain, satisfaction) and RTS status. RTS defined as participation in ≥1 competitive game at the same or lower level. MCID thresholds applied (mHHS 9.2, HOS-ADL 9.7, HOS-Sport 14.3). Logistic regression for RTS predictors. Statistical analysis in R.

## Findings
Mean age 18.1±3.0 yrs; 51% linemen; 74% high school, 21% college. Bilateral involvement in 29%. Mean alpha angle 59.8±12.1°. Labral repair in 100% of cases; femoroplasty in 100%; acetabuloplasty in 86%; capsular repair in 98%. RTS (excluding nonmedical): 23/26 (88%); all returned at same or higher level. Overall RTS including nonmedical non-returners: 61%. Mean follow-up 7.6±3.9 yrs: mHHS 90.2±12.6, HOS-ADL 94.5±6.8, HOS-Sport 88.7±17.0; satisfaction 8.7±1.7/10. Significant pre-to-post improvements in all PROMs (p≤.002), all exceeding MCID. 4 hips (8%) reoperated at mean 4.9 yrs. No perioperative complications. No statistically significant differences in outcomes between those who did and did not return. Logistic regression: no significant predictors of RTS (BMI, age, level of play, alpha angle, position).

## Limitations
PROMs collected from only 27/38 athletes (71%), introducing potential response bias. 12 athletes who did not return for nonmedical reasons may bias results. Small sample limits regression model interpretation. Procedures performed by multiple surgeons over 13 years with evolving techniques. Case series design; Level 4 evidence. Study population predominantly high school and college athletes, limiting direct applicability to professional American football.

## Practical Implications
Football players undergoing hip arthroscopy for FAI can be counseled that they have an 88% chance of returning to play when medical factors are the primary consideration. Nonmedical factors (graduation, changing priorities) are important to address in shared decision-making, particularly for high school and college athletes. Outcomes are durable at 7+ years with high satisfaction. Rehabilitation protocols with clear milestones (jogging/plyometrics by 12 weeks, 75% LSI at 12 weeks, 90% LSI at 16 weeks) supported full return.

## Athlete Development Notes
Linemen are disproportionately represented in football FAI surgical populations (51%) due to their crouched stance requiring maximal hip flexion and asymmetric loading. Higher BMI in linemen may increase compressive and shear forces on the hip. Screening programs for FAI symptoms should target linemen specifically. Early identification and management may prevent symptom progression and surgical necessity.

## Return to Play Notes
88% RTS rate in amateur American football players after primary hip arthroscopy for FAI is comparable to NFL studies (84–93%). Standard rehabilitation protocol: partial weight-bearing and passive ROM for first 2 weeks, progressive weight-bearing and functional exercises thereafter, jogging and plyometrics by 12 weeks, with LSI targets of 75% at 12 weeks and 90% at 16 weeks before full RTS. No significant predictors of failure to return were identified, meaning individualized counseling regarding nonmedical factors is important alongside medical management.
