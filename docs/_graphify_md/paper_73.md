# Kilwein TM, Curry KA, Sutcliffe J, Manzler CA. Beyond Averages: Uncovering Within-Person Links Between Sleep and Performance in Division I Collegiate Football Players. Research Quarterly for Exercise and Sport. 2026. DOI: 10.1080/02701367.2025.2608371

## TLDR
On a day-to-day basis, Division I football players who fall asleep faster and more efficiently perform better at next-day training (higher max acceleration), and players who train less intensely that day fall asleep faster and more efficiently that night—revealing a reciprocal, within-person sleep-performance relationship that individual averages alone would miss.

## Abstract
This study examined daily within-person associations between objectively measured sleep and athletic performance in 65 Division I NCAA collegiate football players (ages 17–23) over a three-week fall training camp period. Sleep was measured using Oura Ring wearables (efficiency, latency, total sleep time) and performance was measured using Catapult technology (maximum acceleration, maximum velocity, explosive movements). Multilevel Bayesian models revealed no significant between-person effects, but within-person analyses showed that nights with longer sleep onset latency or lower sleep efficiency predicted reduced next-day maximum acceleration, and that days with lower maximum acceleration predicted shorter sleep latency and higher sleep efficiency that night.

## Methods
Retrospective analysis of de-identified data from routine program monitoring at a single NCAA Division I football program (Vanderbilt Athletics) during fall training camp (July 30 – August 20, 2024). 65 male players aged 17–23 (M=19.88, SD=1.41) wore Oura Ring (sleep) and Catapult (performance) devices. Sleep variables: total sleep time, sleep onset latency, sleep efficiency. Performance variables: maximum acceleration (m/s²), maximum velocity (mph), explosive efforts. Data analyzed using multilevel Bayesian models in Mplus 8.11 with 50,000 iterations, two chains, thinning interval of 10. Within-person (daily fluctuation) and between-person (average differences) effects modeled separately. 99% Bayesian credible intervals used as sensitivity check for multiple comparisons.

## Findings
- No significant between-person associations between sleep and performance variables
- Within-person: longer sleep onset latency on a given night predicted lower next-day max acceleration (estimate=-0.007; 95% BCI -0.013 to -0.003)
- Within-person: lower sleep efficiency on a given night predicted lower next-day max acceleration (estimate=0.005; 95% BCI 0.001 to 0.010)
- Within-person: lower max acceleration on a given day predicted shorter sleep onset latency that night (estimate=6.869; 95% BCI 3.998 to 9.269)
- Within-person: lower max acceleration on a given day predicted higher sleep efficiency that night (estimate=-5.289; 95% BCI -10.170 to -1.027; less robust at 99% BCI)
- No within-person or between-person effects found for total sleep time or max velocity or explosive efforts
- Within-person variance dominated: 87–95% of variance in sleep variables and 51–94% in performance variables occurred within individuals across days

## Limitations
Data from a single team, sport, and gender limits generalizability. Retrospective observational design precludes causal inference. Athletes were aware their data were being monitored, introducing performance bias. Three-week assessment window cannot capture long-term sleep-performance relationships. Unmeasured confounders (illness, nutrition, psychological stress, noise) may have influenced both sleep and performance. Oura Ring underestimates sleep efficiency and REM sleep relative to polysomnography. Small sample size (n=65) and single site.

## Practical Implications
Athletic departments should prioritize monitoring individualized sleep quality metrics (latency and efficiency) rather than focusing solely on total sleep duration for football players. Nightly deviations from an athlete's own average sleep latency and efficiency—not just absolute values—are more informative for predicting next-day performance. Sport science staff should establish individual baselines and flag concerning within-person deviations to trigger recovery interventions. Integrating sleep assessments into preparticipation routines and conducting regular time-demands surveys can help identify and address sleep disruptors.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly relevant to American football player development. Data collected during fall training camp—the most physically demanding period of the collegiate football calendar—demonstrate that daily sleep quality drives next-day neuromuscular performance (max acceleration) in college football players. Embedding individualized, wearable-based sleep monitoring into training camp and season programming can provide actionable data for athletic development staff to optimize readiness and performance.

## Return to Play Notes
The bidirectional relationship between sleep quality and next-day explosive neuromuscular output has direct relevance for return-to-play decision-making. Athletes returning from injury who exhibit poor sleep efficiency or elevated sleep latency may have impaired max acceleration capacity the following day, independent of their physical readiness status. Incorporating objective sleep monitoring (e.g., Oura Ring) into RTP protocols could provide an additional physiological readiness indicator and help identify athletes at risk for re-injury due to neuromuscular fatigue compounded by sleep disruption.
