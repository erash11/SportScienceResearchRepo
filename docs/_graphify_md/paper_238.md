# Palermi, S., Vittadini, F., Pedret, C., Vecchiato, M., Della Villa, F., Corsini, A., Jokela, A., & Lempainen, L. (2025). From the Field to the Screen: A Scoping Review of Video Analysis as a Tool for Understanding Thigh Muscle-Tendon Injuries in Football. Clinical Journal of Sport Medicine. https://doi.org/10.1097/JSM.0000000000001377

## TLDR
Video analysis of professional football injuries consistently shows that severe thigh muscle-tendon injuries are primarily noncontact or indirect-contact events driven by eccentric loading during high-speed running, kicking, and sudden directional changes. Hamstring injuries predominantly occur during sprinting (acceleration/deceleration), rectus femoris injuries during kicking and sprinting, and adductor injuries during complex closed and open kinetic chain movements. Prevention strategies should target eccentric strength in these positions and movement patterns, not simply limit high-speed running exposure—as many injuries occur during short accelerations and unexpected situations, not necessarily at maximal sprint speed.

## Abstract
Thigh muscle-tendon injuries are among the most prevalent and costly injuries in professional football, with hamstrings, adductors, quadriceps (rectus femoris), and calf muscles collectively affected. This scoping review synthesized data from 10 studies published 2010-2025 that used video analysis to investigate thigh muscle-tendon injury mechanisms in football players. Studies were quality-appraised using the QA-SIVAS scale. The review identified noncontact and indirect contact as the predominant mechanisms for severe injuries. Common injury scenarios included sprinting-induced eccentric hamstring strains, kicking-related rectus femoris injuries, and closed kinetic chain adductor longus injuries. Video analysis is positioned as an essential tool for understanding injury biomechanics, informing prevention, and guiding rehabilitation—with AI-enhanced video analysis identified as a critical future direction.

## Methods
Scoping review methodology; comprehensive search of PubMed, Scopus, Cochrane, Embase, SportDiscuss, and Google Scholar from inception to February 2025, supplemented by AI-assisted preliminary mapping via ResearchRabbit. Inclusion criteria: video analysis as a primary method, thigh muscle-tendon injuries in professional football, acute injuries only. Ten studies met inclusion criteria. Quality assessed with the 18-item QA-SIVAS scale. Data extracted: objectives, number of injuries analyzed, muscle groups, and main injury mechanism findings.

## Findings
All 10 included studies found that severe thigh muscle-tendon injuries are predominantly noncontact (54-83% across studies). Hamstring: sprint-related (acceleration/deceleration) and stretch-related (CKC) mechanisms; biceps femoris long head most affected; injuries occur at high horizontal speeds but also during short accelerations <10m (86% involved running per Vermeulen et al.). Rectus femoris: kicking is the dominant mechanism (80% per Jokela et al.); complete ruptures common with kicking/sprinting. Adductor longus: change of direction (35%), kicking (29%), reaching (24%), jumping (12%); predominantly CKC movements with hip extension, abduction, external rotation. Gender differences noted: women have higher proportion of noncontact injuries during running and kicking than men. Eccentric muscle activation during rapid loading is consistently implicated across all injury types.

## Limitations
Evidence classified as Level 3 (Oxford Centre for Evidence-Based Medicine)—observational studies preclude causal inference. Methodological variability across studies (QA-SIVAS scores varied; no control groups in any study). Findings specific to professional football; limited applicability to amateur or youth levels. Only 10 studies included, restricting breadth of conclusions. Reliance on peer-reviewed journals may introduce publication bias. Practical implementation of video-based injury prevention in real-world settings faces cost and infrastructure barriers.

## Practical Implications
Prevention programs should emphasize eccentric strengthening of hamstrings (Nordic curls, Romanian deadlifts), hip adductors, and quadriceps rather than solely limiting high-speed running volumes—as injuries commonly occur at submaximal speeds and during unexpected situational demands. Kicking mechanics and load management during kicking-intensive training phases are particularly relevant for rectus femoris injury prevention. Video analysis of training and match footage should be incorporated into injury surveillance programs to identify individual movement patterns associated with elevated injury risk. Return-to-play protocols should assess movement quality in the specific kinematic patterns (sprinting, kicking, CKC reaching) associated with each injury type.

## Athlete Development Notes
Developing football athletes should progressively build eccentric strength capacity in hamstrings, adductors, and quadriceps from early in their career to withstand the high eccentric demands of sprinting and kicking. Position-specific loading patterns should inform training design: wide receivers and midfielders face greater hamstring sprint demands; forwards and attacking midfielders face higher rectus femoris kicking loads. Video-based movement screening can identify athletes whose running and kicking mechanics place excessive eccentric demand on specific muscle groups.

## Return to Play Notes
Video analysis findings directly inform RTP criteria for thigh muscle-tendon injuries. Functional RTP assessments should include sport-specific movement tests corresponding to the injury mechanism: maximal sprint at progressive speeds for hamstring injuries, kicking mechanics assessment for rectus femoris injuries, and CKC loading tests (change of direction, lunging) for adductor injuries. The review supports monitoring movement quality—not just pain absence or strength ratios—as a criterion for progressive RTP in professional football.
