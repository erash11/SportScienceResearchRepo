# Ballmann CG, Rogers RR. American Football Headgear Impairs Visuomotor Drill Performance in Division I NCAA Football Athletes. Journal of Functional Morphology and Kinesiology. 2024;9:169.

## TLDR
Any American football helmet with a facemask — regardless of how reinforced it is or what type of visor is attached — significantly slows reactive visuomotor performance in Division I NCAA athletes, suggesting the helmet-facemask combination itself is the primary source of impairment rather than the specific facemask or visor type.

## Abstract
This study investigated whether American football facemask reinforcement level and visor/eye shield type impair dynamic visuomotor drill performance in Division I NCAA football athletes. Sixteen male athletes (age 20.4±1.3 years) completed two counterbalanced repeated-measures experiments using a FITLIGHT reactive agility drill. Experiment 1 (EXP1) compared baseline (no helmet) against three facemask reinforcement levels (light, medium, heavy). Experiment 2 (EXP2) compared baseline against three visor conditions (clear, smoke-tinted, mirrored). All helmeted and all visor conditions produced significantly slower response times than baseline, with the impairment consistent across central, mid, and outer sensor positions and upper and lower sensor levels. No significant differences in response time were found between facemask reinforcement levels or between visor tint conditions.

## Methods
Randomized, counterbalanced repeated-measures design with two experiments in Division I NCAA football players (n=16; offense=8, defense=6, special teams=2). EXP1: four conditions — baseline/no helmet (BL), helmet + light facemask (HL), helmet + medium facemask (HM), helmet + heavy facemask (HH) using a Schutt Vengeance Pro helmet. EXP2: four conditions — BL, helmet + clear visor (HCV; ~90% VLT), helmet + smoke visor (HSV; ~48% VLT), helmet + mirrored visor (HMV; ~28% VLT). Dynamic visuomotor test: FITLIGHT system with 10 LED sensors on 5 poles in a semi-circle (1 m radius); participants stepped to deactivate each illuminated sensor as fast as possible; 20 total responses per attempt, 3 attempts per condition with 3-min rest. Primary outcome: average response time (ms) from the single fastest attempt. Statistical analysis: 1×4 repeated-measures ANOVA with Bonferroni-Holm post-hoc; effect sizes as partial η².

## Findings
- EXP1: All helmeted conditions (HL, HM, HH) produced significantly slower overall response times than BL (all p<0.001; η²=0.091); no significant differences between facemask reinforcement levels (p>0.05).
- EXP1 by sensor position: BL significantly faster than all helmeted conditions at outer, mid, and central positions; response time slower at central vs. outer and mid positions.
- EXP1 by sensor level: BL faster than all helmeted conditions at upper and lower levels (p<0.001); upper sensors responded to faster than lower sensors (p=0.041).
- EXP2: All visor conditions (HCV, HSV, HMV) significantly slower than BL (all p<0.001; η²=0.321); no significant differences between visor types (p>0.05).
- EXP2 by sensor position: BL faster than all visor conditions; interaction between condition and position was significant (p<0.001; η²=0.037).
- EXP2 by sensor level: BL faster than all visor conditions at both upper and lower levels (all p<0.001; η²=0.461).

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=16) limits statistical power to detect differences between helmet conditions. Experiment conducted indoors under standardized lighting, which does not replicate outdoor game-day conditions where tinted visors may provide visibility benefits. Participants were unaware of hypotheses, but performance validity testing was not employed. Study does not examine head or neck kinematics that may partially explain compensation mechanisms. All male Division I athletes — results may not generalize to other levels or female athletes.

## Practical Implications
All American football headgear configurations impair reactive visuomotor performance relative to no helmet, regardless of facemask or visor type. This suggests that training drills requiring rapid visual-motor response should be conducted in full game-day headgear to habituate athletes to these impairments. Performance and safety staff should consider the visual field and reaction time constraints imposed by headgear when designing catching, route-running, and tackling drills. Coaches advocating for reduced facemask reinforcement may not achieve meaningful reaction time benefits under dynamic movement conditions.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football at all levels. Findings indicate that the helmet itself — not the specific facemask or visor — is the primary limiter of dynamic visuomotor performance. For athlete development, this means players should train reaction-based skills while wearing full game equipment to build appropriate visual-motor adaptations. Visor selection (tint level) does not appear to add meaningful impairment beyond the facemask combination under standardized indoor conditions.

## Return to Play Notes
Relevant to return-to-play protocols following concussion or ocular injury. Because all helmet configurations impair dynamic reactive visuomotor performance, progressive return-to-sport testing that includes full equipment conditions is important to accurately assess readiness. Athletes may appear capable in no-helmet baseline testing but still show functional performance deficits in game equipment, which should be accounted for in RTP decision-making.
