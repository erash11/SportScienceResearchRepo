# Grosicki GJ, Langan SP, Bagley JR, Galpin AJ, Garner D, Hampton-Marcell JT, Allen JM, Robinson AT. Gut check: Unveiling the influence of acute exercise on the gut microbiota. Exp Physiol. 2023;1–15. doi:10.1113/EP091446

## TLDR
A single bout of exercise causes modest, variable changes in gut microbial community structure but can meaningfully alter the metabolic output of gut microbes — including short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), bile acids, and amino acid metabolites — in ways that may benefit host health and athletic performance. The chronic gut health benefits of regular training are likely built upon these acute transient responses. Future research should prioritise rigorous experimental controls (diet, non-exercise controls, multiple sample time points) and larger, more diverse populations to clarify which specific exercise parameters drive the most beneficial microbiome adaptations.

## Abstract
This comprehensive narrative review summarised 11 human studies (n=191; 147 males, 54 females) examining gut microbiota and faecal metabolite responses to acute and very short-term (<3 weeks) exercise. Literature was identified from PubMed and Web of Science (up to July 2023) using terms combining 'gut microbiome' AND 'acute exercise' OR 'short-term exercise'. The review evaluated evidence on changes in alpha-diversity, beta-diversity, individual microbial taxa and metabolic pathways, and faecal metabolites following single exercise bouts ranging from cycling and running to ultra-endurance events. Key conclusions are that acute aerobic exercise has: (i) limited effects on intra- and inter-individual gut microbial diversity; (ii) variable effects on specific microbial taxa likely driven by exercise intensity, duration, and interindividual differences; and (iii) numerous effects on microbial metabolic activity with potential implications for host health and performance.

## Methods
Narrative review with systematic literature search conducted in PubMed and Web of Science for all studies published in English combining 'gut microbiome' AND 'acute exercise' OR 'short-term exercise' up to July 2023. Eleven eligible human studies were identified and characterised across: study design, population characteristics (n=191), exercise stimulus, dietary control, non-exercising controls, microbiome measurement approach (16S rRNA amplicon sequencing or shotgun metagenomics), alpha- and beta-diversity findings, individual taxa changes, metabolic pathway shifts, and faecal metabolite changes. Evidence was synthesised narratively and methodological gaps and future research priorities were identified.

## Findings
Of 11 studies, 9 reported alpha-diversity findings — 5 found no change, 3 found increases (via Shannon Index) primarily after extreme exercise (ultra-marathon, transoceanic rowing, military ski), and 1 found a decrease. Six beta-diversity analyses showed mostly no significant change, with 2 reporting changes after extreme exercise. Nine of 11 studies reported changes in specific microbial taxa, with the most consistent finding being increased relative abundance of Veillonella (especially Veillonella atypica) following intense endurance exercise in highly trained athletes — a species that converts lactate to propionate. Four studies on faecal metabolites all reported substantial pre–post differences, including changes in amino acids, bile acids, carbohydrate metabolites, fatty acids, and nucleic acid-related compounds. Extreme exercise was the most potent driver of acute microbiome changes.

## Limitations
Only 11 studies with fewer than 200 total participants were identified, limiting statistical power and generalisability. Only 1 of 11 studies included a non-exercising control group. Dietary control was largely absent or not standardised. Most studies used 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing (lower resolution than shotgun metagenomics). Sample collection timing varied widely and was often imprecise relative to exercise. Substantial inter-individual variability in microbiome responses makes group-level findings difficult to apply individually. Only aerobic exercise modalities were studied; resistance exercise responses are essentially unknown.

## Practical Implications
Sports practitioners should understand that acute exercise modestly impacts gut microbial diversity but can substantially alter microbial metabolite production, which may influence energy availability, immune function, and recovery. Designing training sessions to support gut health — including adequate hydration, avoiding extreme single-bout overload, and timing exercise relative to meals — may help optimise microbiome-mediated health benefits. Probiotic or dietary interventions timed around acute exercise bouts represent a promising area for future evidence-based performance nutrition strategies.

## Athlete Development Notes
The finding that extreme endurance exercise transiently increases Veillonella abundance (converting lactate to propionate) suggests that training-induced gut microbiome adaptations may contribute to improved lactate clearance and energy metabolism in developing elite athletes. Progressive training load management — avoiding sudden spikes to extreme exercise — may help foster adaptive rather than disruptive acute microbiome responses as athletes develop their aerobic base.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes returning from injury who resume training may experience acute changes in gut microbiota driven by the sudden reintroduction of exercise stress. While direct RTP implications are currently limited by sparse evidence, ensuring adequate dietary fibre intake, hydration, and gradual training load progression during the return-to-play continuum may support a gut microbiome environment conducive to tissue repair, immune modulation, and performance restoration.
