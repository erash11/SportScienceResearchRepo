# Chung AS, Makovicka JL, Hassebrock JD, Patel KA, Tummala SV, Deckey DG, Hydrick TC, Rubel NC, Chhabra A. Epidemiology of cervical injuries in NCAA football players. Spine. 2019;44(12):848–854. DOI: 10.1097/BRS.0000000000003008

## TLDR
Cervical spine injuries in NCAA football players are mostly minor—stingers dominate, and nearly two-thirds of athletes return to play within 24 hours. Serious injuries such as fractures and disc injuries are rare but carry long return-to-play timelines. Linebackers and defensive linemen are highest-risk positions and should be targeted with neck strengthening, tackling technique, and protective equipment interventions.

## Abstract
A descriptive epidemiology study using the NCAA Injury Surveillance Program (ISP) database for the 2009–2010 to 2013–2014 academic years. An estimated 7,496 cervical spine injuries were identified across all NCAA football divisions. The overall injury rate was 2.91 per 10,000 athlete-exposures (AEs). Stingers (brachial plexus injuries) were the most common injury type (1.87/10,000 AEs), followed by cervical strains (0.80/10,000 AEs). Injuries were approximately nine times more likely during competition than practice. The regular season had the highest injury rate (3.30/10,000 AEs). Division I athletes had the highest rates. Linebackers (20.3%) and defensive linemen (18.2%) sustained the most injuries. Contact mechanisms accounted for 90.8% of injuries. The majority of players (64.4%) returned to play within 24 hours; only 2.8% were out more than 21 days. No players required surgery.

## Methods
Descriptive epidemiology study using the NCAA-ISP database (2009–2010 through 2013–2014). Poststratification sample weights based on sport, division, and academic year were applied to each injury and AE. Injury rates were reported per 10,000 AEs overall and by event type (practice vs. competition) and time of season (preseason, regular season, postseason). Injury rate ratios (IRRs) with 95% CIs were calculated; CIs not containing 1.0 considered significant. Participation restriction time reported as intervals (<24 h, 1–6 d, 7–21 d, >21 d).

## Findings
Estimated 7,496 cervical spine injuries over 5 academic years at a rate of 2.91/10,000 AEs. Stingers were most common (1.87/10,000 AEs); cervical strains second (0.80/10,000 AEs). Competition injury rate (14.82/10,000 AEs) was 9.1x higher than practice rate (1.62/10,000 AEs). Regular season rate (3.30) exceeded preseason (2.27; IRR 0.69) and postseason (1.27; IRR 0.39). Division I had highest rates. Linebackers accounted for 20.3% and defensive ends/tackles 18.2% of injuries. Contact mechanism: 90.8%. New injuries: 85.6%. 64.4% returned within 24 hours; 2.8% out >21 days. Cervical disc injuries associated with longest absences (41.6% out 7–21 d; 29.2% out >21 d). No surgeries required.

## Limitations
Retrospective data reliant on athletic trainer documentation accuracy. NCAA-ISP is a voluntary convenience sample, introducing potential selection bias and limiting generalizability to all NCAA programs. Use of weighted estimates may have biased statistical significance. More severe injuries were rare, limiting power for those subgroup analyses.

## Practical Implications
Prevention efforts should be concentrated during the regular season when cervical spine injuries are most likely, and should focus on linebackers and defensive linemen given their elevated risk. Promotion of safe tackling technique, appropriate helmet fit and design, and preventive rehabilitation targeting neck strength are key strategies. While most injuries are transient, cervical disc injuries can result in extended absences and require conservative management timelines.

## Athlete Development Notes
Cervical spine injuries are an occupational hazard of high-contact football positions. Youth and collegiate players in linebacker and defensive line roles should be taught proper tackling and blocking mechanics early in development to minimize axial loading and hyperextension mechanisms. Neck strengthening programs integrated into regular training may reduce injury severity and recurrence.

## Return to Play Notes
The majority of cervical spine injuries (64.4%) resolved within 24 hours, and no players required surgical intervention during this study period. Cervical disc injuries carried the longest absences (>21 days in 29.2% of cases). RTP decision-making should be individualized, with more severe pathologies (fractures, disc injuries, spinal stenosis) requiring comprehensive medical clearance protocols before return.
