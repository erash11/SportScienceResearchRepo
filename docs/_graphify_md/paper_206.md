# Fanchini M, Steendahl IB, Impellizzeri FM, Pruna R, Dupont G, Coutts AJ, Meyer T, McCall A. Exercise-Based Strategies to Prevent Muscle Injury in Elite Footballers: A Systematic Review and Best Evidence Synthesis. Sports Medicine. 2020;50:1467–1480.

## TLDR
Despite widespread use of eccentric and proprioceptive exercises in professional football, this systematic review finds no high-quality scientific evidence supporting their effectiveness for preventing muscle injuries in elite players. The only exercise with a low-risk-of-bias RCT behind it is the Copenhagen Adduction Exercise for groin injuries. Practitioners should treat current evidence with caution and prioritize well-designed future trials. In the absence of strong RCT evidence, consensus-based expert recommendations may be the most practical guide.

## Abstract
This systematic review (PROSPERO CRD42017077705) evaluated the scientific evidence for exercise-based muscle injury prevention strategies in adult elite football (soccer). Searches of PubMed, Web of Science, Cochrane Library, and SPORTDiscuss identified 15 studies meeting inclusion criteria: 3 systematic reviews, 5 RCTs, and 7 NRCTs in elite adult players (>16 years). Risk of bias was assessed using ROBIS, Cochrane RoB, and ROBINS-I tools. The two low-risk-of-bias systematic reviews (McCall et al. 2015; Rogan et al. 2013) found insufficient evidence to support eccentric, balance, or static stretching for muscle injury prevention. Five RCTs supported eccentric exercise and proprioception for injury reduction, though all were at high or unclear risk of bias. Seven NRCTs supported eccentric and multi-component approaches but showed critical risk of bias. Only one RCT (Haroy et al. 2018) was low risk of bias and supported the Copenhagen Adduction Exercise for reducing groin problems. Overall, limited scientific evidence was found to support exercise-based muscle injury prevention in elite footballers.

## Methods
Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines, registered in PROSPERO (CRD42017077705). Databases searched: PubMed (MEDLINE), Web of Science, Cochrane Library, SPORTDiscuss (EBSCO) through June 2018 (updated November 2019). Inclusion criteria: elite adult (>16 yr) soccer/football players; interventions targeting lower-limb muscle injury prevention through exercise; outcomes of injury count, incidence, or severity. Risk of bias assessed with ROBIS (systematic reviews), Cochrane RoB tool (RCTs), and ROBINS-I (NRCTs). Evidence levels graded using OCEBM 2011 criteria.

## Findings
15 studies included (3 systematic reviews, 5 RCTs, 1 cluster RCT, 7 NRCTs). The two low-risk-of-bias systematic reviews found insufficient evidence to support eccentric exercise, balance training, and static stretching for muscle injury prevention. RCTs showed contradictory results; only one (Haroy et al. 2018, Copenhagen Adduction Exercise) was low risk of bias and found a significant reduction in groin problems (11.7% vs 21.3% with substantial groin problems in EXP vs CON). All NRCTs showed critical overall risk of bias. No clear, high-quality evidence base exists for any specific exercise-based strategy to prevent muscle injury in elite footballers.

## Limitations
Only English-language studies were included, potentially missing relevant non-English literature. No meta-analysis was performed due to heterogeneity and high risk of bias across studies. Elite team status was defined as top-three league of a country, introducing heterogeneity. Co-interventions (training load, fitness level, flexibility) were not controlled in any of the NRCTs. Compliance and adherence to interventions were either unreported or low in many studies. Country-level heterogeneity may limit generalizability across leagues.

## Practical Implications
Practitioners cannot confidently recommend any specific exercise-based muscle injury prevention strategy based on current high-quality evidence. The Copenhagen Adduction Exercise is the only intervention with low-risk-of-bias RCT support, specifically for groin problems. In the absence of RCT evidence, consensus-based recommendations from expert groups offer the most practical guidance. Coaches and medical staff should document and monitor the effect of any prevention program they implement.

## Athlete Development Notes
Long-term athlete development programs that incorporate muscle injury prevention should acknowledge the limited evidence base and apply eccentric and multi-component programs with appropriate monitoring and individualization. The Copenhagen Adduction Exercise can be recommended for groin health in developing footballers. Emphasis should be placed on load management, strength balance, and movement quality rather than assuming any single exercise protocol guarantees injury reduction.

## Return to Play Notes
Exercise-based prevention programs examined in this review are primarily aimed at reducing first-time injuries, not re-injury specifically in the RTP context. However, the limited evidence for Nordic Hamstring exercises and multi-component programs (balance, eccentric, proprioception) suggests that incorporating these into RTP rehabilitation protocols may provide some protective benefit. The critical risk of bias in most studies means RTP practitioners should supplement exercise-based strategies with individualized load management and clinical monitoring.
