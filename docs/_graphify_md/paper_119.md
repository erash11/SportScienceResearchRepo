# Bailey AM, McMurry TL, Cormier JM, Funk JR, Crandall JR, Mack CD, Myers BS, Arbogast KB. Comparison of Laboratory and On-Field Performance of American Football Helmets. Ann Biomed Eng. 2020. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10439-020-02627-5

## TLDR
Laboratory helmet performance scores positively correlate with on-field concussion rates in the NFL (r² = 0.61, p < 0.001), meaning helmets that perform worse in lab testing are associated with higher concussion rates on the field. Helmets prohibited by the NFL/NFLPA based on poor lab performance had 24% higher odds of concussion (OR 1.24; 95% CI 1.04-1.47; p = 0.017) compared to non-prohibited models. Football equipment staff should use annual NFL/NFLPA helmet ratings as the primary guide for equipment procurement and ensure all players are in approved, high-performing helmets. Kickoff play types carry the highest concussion probability, and prior concussion history is a strong independent predictor of future concussion risk.

## Abstract
This study assessed the relationship between laboratory performance scores and on-field concussion rates for 31 football helmet models worn by NFL players during the 2015-2019 seasons. Linear impactor tests at six impact locations and three velocities were used to compute a Helmet Performance Score (HPS) based on head injury criterion (HIC) and diffuse axonal multi-axis evaluation (DAMAGE) metrics. On-field concussion data were collected across over 5.4 million player-plays, and logistic regression related HPS to helmet-specific concussion rates while controlling for position, play type, season year, season type, and prior concussion history. Helmets prohibited by the NFL/NFLPA based on poor laboratory performance had significantly higher on-field concussion risk.

## Methods
Cross-sectional observational study. Two samples each of 31 helmet models worn by NFL players (2015-2019) were tested using a pneumatic ram and sliding table at 18 test conditions (3 speeds x 6 locations). Six-degree-of-freedom head kinematics computed HARM (0.0148 x HIC + 15.6 x DAMAGE) per condition; a weighted composite HPS was calculated per helmet. NFL Game Statistics and Information System and Helmet Tracker RFID data identified helmet use across 5,460,388 player-plays. Concussion diagnoses came from the NFL electronic medical record. Logistic mixed regression with random player effects estimated concussion probability, controlling for position, play type, season year, season type, and prior concussion count. Helmets were also grouped as prohibited or non-prohibited per the 2020 NFL/NFLPA helmet poster.

## Findings
HPS for the 31 helmets ranged from 0.874 to 1.163 (mean 1.03, SD 0.07); 24 of 31 helmets were within one SD of the mean. A positive correlation between HPS and on-field concussion rate was found for helmets with >50,000 player-plays (r² = 0.61, p < 0.001). On-field concussion rates ranged from 1.23 to 3.11 per 10,000 player-plays. Prohibited helmets had a raw concussion rate 1.62x higher than non-prohibited helmets and significantly higher adjusted odds (OR 1.24; 95% CI 1.04-1.47; p = 0.017). Returned kickoffs had the highest concussion probability; tight ends and wide receivers had the highest positional risk. Concussion probability increased significantly with each additional prior concussion (p < 0.001). Concussion probability was significantly lower in 2018 and 2019 relative to 2015.

## Limitations
Laboratory testing used only helmet-to-helmet impact conditions accounting for approximately 36% of NFL concussions, not helmet-to-ground or helmet-to-shoulder impacts. Only one helmet size per model was tested; padding thickness varies with size within a model. Player position served as a surrogate for impact frequency and severity rather than direct head impact sensor data. Findings are specific to professional-level football and may not apply to youth, high school, or collegiate play.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football equipment staff should consult annual NFL/NFLPA helmet laboratory testing results and ensure all players use models not on the prohibited list. Equipment upgrade programs targeting high-risk positions (tight ends, wide receivers, cornerbacks) offer the greatest potential for reducing concussion incidence. Kickoff rule changes already implemented in 2016 and 2018 appear to have contributed to declining concussion rates. Prior concussion history must be carefully tracked as an independent risk factor when making return-to-play and medical clearance decisions.

## Athlete Development Notes
Equipment selection is a foundational element of player development programs. Integrating up-to-date helmet performance ratings into equipment purchasing decisions protects athlete longevity and developmental continuity. Educating players from high school through the NFL about helmet selection may reduce cumulative head impact burden across a career and support sustained physical development.

## Return to Play Notes
Prior concussion count was the strongest modifiable predictor of future concussion risk, underscoring the importance of thorough concussion documentation and conservative return-to-play decision-making. Players returning from concussion who wear lower-performing helmet models should be considered for equipment upgrades. Preseason play had the highest odds ratio for concussion relative to the regular season, warranting heightened RTP protocol vigilance during preseason camp.
