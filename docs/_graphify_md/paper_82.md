# Khalil LS, Jildeh TR, Abbas MJ, McIntosh MJ, Sokoli A, Cominos N, Okoroha KR. Career Longevity and Performance Following Shoulder Instability in National Football League Athletes. Arthroscopy: The Journal of Arthroscopic and Related Surgery. 2021. doi: 10.1016/j.arthro.2020.12.225.

## TLDR
NFL players who undergo surgical stabilization following shoulder instability return to play with similar workload and performance as nonoperatively managed players but experience greater career longevity, fewer recurrent instability events, and longer time between instability events. Nonoperative management offers faster initial RTP but is associated with higher recurrence risk and shorter careers.

## Abstract
This retrospective case-control study investigated career longevity, game utilization, and performance of NFL athletes following glenohumeral instability events treated operatively versus nonoperatively from 2000 to 2019. Ninety-seven NFL players sustained a first-time shoulder instability event; 91 (93.8%) returned to play. Final analysis included 58 operatively managed and 33 nonoperatively managed players. Operatively managed players played significantly more seasons following RTP (4.1±2.7 vs. 2.8±2.5; P=0.015) and experienced less recurrent instability (27% vs. 50%; P=0.035) with longer intervals before recurrence (105.7±100.1 vs. 24.7±40.6 weeks; P<0.001). No significant differences existed in games played, games started, snap counts, or performance (SAV) between cohorts.

## Methods
Retrospective case-control study. NFL players with first shoulder instability events from September 2000 to February 2019 identified via public internet sources (team websites, injury reports, sports news). Inclusion required prior NFL experience, RTP after injury, and no prior ipsilateral upper extremity surgery. Players categorized as operative or nonoperative based on final treatment at end of index season. Outcomes: seasons played post-RTP, games played/started, offensive/defensive snap count percentage (OSCP/DSCP), season approximate value (SAV), recurrent instability rate, and interval to recurrence. SAV collected from Pro Football Reference. Statistical analysis: t-tests, Wilcoxon rank sum, chi-square, Fisher's exact, repeated measures ANOVA; significance p<0.05.

## Findings
• 97 NFL players with first shoulder instability identified; 91 (93.8%) returned to play.
• Quarterbacks significantly more likely to undergo immediate surgery vs. other positions (P=0.023).
• Operative group played significantly more seasons post-RTP (4.1±2.7 vs. 2.8±2.5; P=0.015).
• Time to RTP significantly longer for operative group (36.62±10.32 vs. 5.43±12.33 weeks; P<0.05).
• Recurrent instability significantly lower in operative group (27% vs. 50%; P=0.035).
• Interval to recurrent instability significantly longer in operative group (105.7±100.1 vs. 24.7±40.6 weeks; P<0.001).
• No significant differences in games played, games started, OSCP, DSCP, or SAV between operative and nonoperative cohorts.
• Performance metrics (SAV) did not significantly change before vs. after injury for either cohort.

## Limitations
Retrospective design with reliance on publicly available data may introduce reporting inaccuracies. Players who did not return to play were excluded, potentially introducing selection bias toward more successful outcomes. Treatment group classification relied on news reports and absence of in-game data rather than surgical records. Limited ability to account for rehabilitation quality, team medical staff variation, or player motivation. Level of evidence: III.

## Practical Implications
For NFL athletes with glenohumeral instability, surgical stabilization provides meaningful career longevity benefits and significantly reduces recurrent instability without compromising performance upon return. Medical staff should counsel players that operative management involves a longer initial recovery (~9 months vs. ~1 week nonoperative) but substantially reduces the likelihood of reinjury (27% vs. 50%) and supports a longer career. Quarterbacks appear particularly likely to elect surgery, likely reflecting the high performance demands of the position.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to NFL football. Shoulder instability prevalence in NFL players is reported at 10–14%. Defensive players represent the largest proportion requiring surgical management. Position-specific shoulder stability demands should inform strength and conditioning programming, particularly for defensive players and quarterbacks. Pre-participation screening for shoulder laxity and prior instability may help identify players at elevated risk requiring prophylactic programming.

## Return to Play Notes
Directly relevant to RTP decision-making. Nonoperative management allows faster initial RTP (~5 weeks) but carries 50% recurrent instability risk and shorter careers (2.8 vs. 4.1 post-injury seasons). Surgical stabilization requires ~37-week RTP timeline but reduces recurrence to 27% and extends career longevity. Staff should use shared decision-making weighing the team's season timeline against the player's career investment. Performance outcomes (snap count %, SAV) are equivalent regardless of management choice, supporting that full return to prior performance level is achievable via both pathways.
