# Brophy RH, Crain J, Baker J, Silverman R, Wojtys EM, Stollberg B, Mack C, Herzog M. Anterior Cruciate Ligament Tear Location in National Football League Athletes Based on Magnetic Resonance Imaging. HSS Journal: The Musculoskeletal Journal of Hospital for Special Surgery. 2025;doi:10.1177/15563316251396136.

## TLDR
In NFL players, 74% of ACL tears are mid-substance (type III) — a far higher proportion than the general population — suggesting that most NFL athletes are not candidates for ACL preservation techniques and reconstruction remains the standard of care.

## Abstract
This retrospective cross-sectional study reviewed MRI and video data from 191 NFL players who sustained acute ACL tears during the 2015–2019 seasons. MRIs were independently reviewed by two musculoskeletal radiologists to classify tears by location (type I–V). The distribution was 3% type I (proximal avulsion), 20% type II (proximal), 74% type III (mid-substance), 2% type IV (distal), and less than 1% type V (distal avulsion). Injury mechanism analysis from a subset of 73 players (2018–2019) showed no statistically significant difference in tear location distribution by contact type.

## Methods
Retrospective cross-sectional study approved by the Mount Sinai IRB and NFL-NFLPA Medical Research Approval Process. From a 2023 dataset of 314 ACL tears (2015–2019 NFL seasons), 294 had MRI uploaded to the NFL imaging system (Infinitt); 274 MRIs were identified. After excluding 17 players with prior ACLR, 191 players (61%) were analyzed. Two fellowship-trained musculoskeletal radiologists (11 and 20 years experience) independently classified tears as type I–V per a published scheme. Inter-rater reliability assessed with kappa statistics. For 73 players (2018–2019), MRI tear location was linked to video-determined or EHR-reported injury mechanism. Fisher's exact chi-squared tests compared tear location distributions by mechanism.

## Findings
• Type I (proximal avulsion): 3% (n=6)
• Type II (proximal/mid): 20% (n=39)
• Type III (mid-substance): 74% (n=141)
• Type IV (mid/distal): 2% (n=4)
• Type V (distal avulsion): <1% (n=1)
• Inter-rater kappa=0.37 (minimal agreement); all discrepancies within ±1 type
• Mechanism analysis (n=73): noncontact 47%, indirect contact 32%, direct contact 21%
• No significant difference in tear location distribution by injury mechanism (p=0.14)
• Noncontact: 71% type III, 29% type II; indirect contact: 78% type III, 13% type II; direct contact: 73% type III, 13% type II
• Higher proportion of type III and lower proportion of type I tears vs. prior general population studies (type I previously 4–26%)

## Limitations
MRIs were performed at multiple centers with varying protocols, reducing standardization and contributing to low inter-rater reliability. True tear location was not surgically confirmed. The tear location classification system showed only minimal inter-rater agreement (kappa=0.37), raising questions about classification accuracy in acute settings. Mechanism analysis was limited to 73 players due to the 2018 EHR reporting change. Stratification by age, BMI, or position was not performed.

## Practical Implications
The overwhelming predominance of mid-substance (type III) ACL tears in NFL athletes indicates that ACL preservation techniques (e.g., primary repair), which are most applicable to proximal type I tears, are unlikely to be appropriate for the vast majority of NFL players. This data supports ACL reconstruction as the standard surgical treatment for this population and should inform pre-operative counseling and surgical planning for team physicians.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football (NFL). The high-energy nature of NFL play likely drives the predominance of mid-substance tears. Understanding tear location distribution helps staff set realistic surgical expectations, counsel athletes on reconstruction vs. repair candidacy, and design post-surgical rehabilitation timelines. The low prevalence of type I tears further limits applicability of newer preservation approaches in this population.

## Return to Play Notes
The dominance of mid-substance (type III) tears in NFL athletes reinforces that reconstruction — not preservation — is the appropriate surgical pathway, with corresponding RTP timelines of 9+ months. Very few NFL ACL patients (only ~3% with type I tears) would be candidates for repair-based protocols that might offer accelerated return timelines. This has direct implications for season-ending injury planning and multi-year contract and performance projections.
