# Johnson DA, Cable TG, Funnell MP, Peden DL, Thorley J, Ferreira de Cunha M, Reynolds KM, Harris L, Wood M, Chavez-O'Reilly T, Carrington J, Bailey SJ, Clifford T, Heaney LM, James LJ. Effects of Cannabidiol Ingestion on Thermoregulatory and Inflammatory Responses to Treadmill Exercise in the Heat in Recreationally Active Males. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise. 2024. 10.1249/MSS.0000000000003568

## TLDR
A single 298 mg dose of CBD taken roughly 105 minutes before exercise did not reduce core temperature or improve thermoregulation during 1 hour of treadmill running in the heat, but it did modestly blunt post-exercise immune activation markers (CD14 and IL-6). For sports performance staff considering CBD use during summer training or heat exposure, current evidence does not support it as a thermoprotective strategy, though its marginal anti-inflammatory effects warrant continued investigation under more severe heat stress conditions relevant to NFL training camp environments.

## Abstract
This double-blind, randomized crossover study investigated whether acute CBD ingestion (298 mg) could attenuate thermoregulatory and inflammatory responses to 1 hour of treadmill exercise at 60-65% VO2peak in 32°C, 50% relative humidity in 13 recreationally active males. Core temperature, skin temperature, heart rate, sweat loss, plasma intestinal damage marker (I-FABP), monocyte activation (CD14), and inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, IL-8, TNF-alpha) were assessed at multiple timepoints. CBD did not affect thermoregulation but produced minor immunosuppressive effects, reducing CD14 and peak IL-6 responses post-exercise.

## Methods
Randomized, double-blind, counterbalanced crossover design. Thirteen recreationally active males (age 25±5 y; VO2peak 50.4±3.2 mL/kg/min; body mass 79.5±8.9 kg). Participants ingested 298 mg CBD isolate or placebo (hemp oil capsules) 105 minutes before 1 hour treadmill exercise at 60-65% VO2peak in an environmental chamber at 32°C and 50% relative humidity. Blood samples collected at baseline, pre-exercise, 20 min post-exercise, and 90 min post-exercise for I-FABP, CD14, IL-6, IL-8, and TNF-alpha. Core temperature (rectal), skin temperature, heart rate, RPE, sweat loss, and plasma osmolality also measured. At least 1 week washout between conditions.

## Findings
Core temperature increased similarly in both conditions (delta 1.69±0.48°C CBD vs 1.79±0.53°C placebo; p>0.05). No condition differences in heart rate, skin temperature, sweat loss, RPE, or plasma osmolality. Plasma CD14 was 1776 (463-3090) pg/mL lower in CBD vs placebo 90 min post-exercise (p=0.049). Peak IL-6 concentration was 0.8 (-1.1, -0.3) pg/mL lower in CBD (p=0.050); IL-6 AUC difference was -113 (-172, 27) pg/mL·270min (p=0.054). No significant differences in IL-8 or TNF-alpha AUC between conditions. Plasma CBD Cmax was 190±59 ng/mL occurring 90 min post-exercise.

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=13), all male, recreationally active (not well-trained athletes), limiting generalizability to elite populations. Exercise intensity and duration were modest, producing limited gastrointestinal damage (I-FABP did not exceed baseline). A trial-order effect was detected for I-FABP AUC and IL-6 AUC (when outliers excluded), suggesting the washout period may have been insufficient. CBD absorption was highly variable and peak plasma CBD occurred after exercise cessation, potentially limiting effects during exercise. Findings may not translate to females or more prolonged/severe heat stress.

## Practical Implications
CBD does not appear to confer thermoprotective benefits during moderate-intensity exercise in the heat for recreationally active males, so it cannot currently be recommended as a strategy to reduce heat illness risk during summer football camp. However, its mild attenuation of post-exercise monocyte activation (CD14) and IL-6 may be relevant in contexts of high inflammatory load. Staff should be aware that CBD pharmacokinetics are highly variable, that peak plasma concentrations arrive well after ingestion, and that current consumer-recommended doses (10-150 mg/day in most nations) are substantially lower than the 298 mg dose used here.

## Athlete Development Notes
The finding that CBD did not impair any physiological or perceptual response to exercise in the heat — and produced minor anti-inflammatory effects — suggests it is unlikely to negatively affect training adaptation or performance in athletes who choose to use it recreationally. However, evidence for a meaningful benefit to athletic development is currently insufficient. Athletes curious about CBD should be counseled on the high variability of absorption and the lack of evidence for thermoregulatory benefit.

## Return to Play Notes
The marginal reduction in post-exercise CD14 and IL-6 with CBD raises the hypothesis that it could assist in managing systemic inflammation during return-to-play from soft tissue injuries. However, the effect sizes observed here were small and occurred under only modest inflammatory stress. Clinical application to RTP is premature without further evidence. Anti-doping status should always be confirmed as CBD product purity varies and contamination with prohibited cannabinoids is a documented risk.
