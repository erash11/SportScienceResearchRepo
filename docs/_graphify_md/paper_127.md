# Maynard JR, Nadwodny JP, Haak IS, DeMatas KF, Rosario-Concepcion RA, Seemann L, Pujalte GGA. Correlation of King-Devick Test and Helmet Impact Exposures Over a Youth Football Season. Sports Health. 2025. DOI: 10.1177/19417381241309956

## TLDR
A single youth football season of subconcussive head impacts did not measurably worsen King-Devick test performance — most players actually improved their KDT times from preseason to postseason, likely due to school-year cognitive development rather than football-specific effects. No correlation was found between cumulative head impact force and KDT change. For football staff working with youth athletes, KDT remains a valuable sideline concussion screening tool, but this study cautions against using it as a season-long marker of cumulative subconcussive damage. Proper tackling technique and contact-limiting practice guidelines remain the most effective prevention strategies at the youth level.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study examined whether cumulative helmet impact exposures (HIE) over a 10-week, 10-game youth football season correlated with changes in King-Devick test (KDT) performance in 58 players aged 9–13. Players wore Riddell SpeedFlex helmets with InSite Impact Response System accelerometers recording impacts ≥15g. Head impacts were tallied and scored by force magnitude. A total of 2,013 HIEs were recorded. Median total cumulative force score was 24 (range 5–476). Postseason KDT times improved overall by a median of −4.8 seconds compared to preseason, and no significant correlation was found between change in KDT time and total cumulative force score (r=−0.06). Four players were clinically diagnosed with concussion during the season.

## Methods
Prospective cohort study (Level 3). 68 youth football players (ages 9–13, Junior Development League, Mayo Clinic IRB-approved) recruited July 2019; 58 completed both pre- and postseason evaluation. Riddell SpeedFlex helmets with InSite Impact Response System accelerometers (≥15g threshold) recorded HIE during all practices (2/week, 26 total) and games (10 games) over 10 weeks. HIE scored 1 (low: 15–28g), 2 (medium: 29–63g), 3 (high: >63g); cumulative force score calculated per player. Pre- and postseason tablet-based KDT administered (faster of 2 baseline attempts recorded). Suspected concussions evaluated by fellowship-trained CAQ sports medicine physician using SCAT5 on the sideline with postinjury KDT. Pearson correlation coefficient used for primary analysis; Wilcoxon rank sum test for secondary concussion subgroup analysis.

## Findings
2,013 total HIEs: 1,696 during games (84%), 317 during practices (16%). Majority were low-force (1,424 low, 263 medium, 9 high during games). Median total cumulative force score: 24 (range 5–476). Median preseason KDT: 54.7 seconds; median postseason KDT: 49.2 seconds; overall improvement of −4.8 seconds (−7.6, −1.1). No significant correlation between HIE and KDT change (r=−0.06). Players with cumulative force score ≤24 improved −2.9 sec; those >24 improved −5.4 sec (P=0.19 between groups). 6 players had concerning head impacts; 4 were clinically diagnosed with concussion. All 6 showed postseason KDT improvement (median −3.7 sec). Most impacts occurred on the rear of the helmet during games. 4 confirmed concussions over 10 weeks (6.9% concussion rate).

## Limitations
InSite 15g threshold underestimates total HIE — approximately 50% of impacts captured at a 10g threshold are missed. Player position could not be tracked as the JDL rotates positions weekly. Preseason KDT was conducted during summer break (summer learning slide effect on reading/cognitive function), while postseason testing occurred during the school year — academic re-engagement likely contributed to improved postseason scores independent of football. Effort and motivation during testing were uncontrolled. KDT is a single neurocognitive domain tool; subtle effects may not be captured. Small concussed subgroup (n=4) limits secondary analyses.

## Practical Implications
Youth football staff should use the King-Devick test as a rapid, objective sideline concussion screening tool during games and practices. Helmet accelerometer technology provides useful data on impact location and frequency that can guide coaching adjustments to tackling technique and contact time in practice. Implementing USA Football Heads Up Football coaching certification and limiting full-contact practice time (per Pop Warner and JDL guidelines) appears to produce a low overall head impact burden in youth players and is associated with a low clinical concussion rate.

## Athlete Development Notes
Youth football players in this study exhibited naturally improving KDT performance over a season, suggesting cognitive development during the school year outpaces subconcussive effects of a 10-week limited-contact season. Coaches and athletic trainers working with youth athletes should prioritize proper shoulder-tackling technique to reduce rear-of-helmet impacts, which were the most common HIE location. Annual or per-season KDT baseline testing is recommended given rapid neurocognitive development in athletes under age 14.

## Return to Play Notes
KDT is a validated sideline concussion tool that, when compared to an individual baseline, can increase concussion detection probability approximately 5-fold. For youth football, postinjury KDT slowing relative to personal baseline should trigger removal from play and formal evaluation with SCAT5 or equivalent. This study showed variable KDT responses among the 4 clinically diagnosed concussion cases, reinforcing that KDT must always be interpreted alongside clinical symptoms and physical examination — not used as a standalone diagnostic tool for RTP decisions.
