# Bourgon V, Duval FG, Forest G. Circadian variation in coaches' decision-making in the National Football League's evening games. Chronobiology International. 2024. DOI: 10.1080/07420528.2024.2393880

## TLDR
NFL coaches traveling westward for evening games call significantly more aggressive (risky) fourth-down plays than coaches traveling eastward or within the same time zone — an effect that disappears entirely for daytime games. This is the first study to show that circadian misalignment extends beyond player physical performance to coaching decision-making. The effect scales with westward longitudinal distance and only emerges in games starting after approximately 16:24h. Sleep deprivation and circadian disruption from westward travel may compromise in-game cognitive judgment, and protocols to mitigate this (sleep interventions, phase-shifting) should extend to coaching staff.

## Abstract
This study examined whether circadian misalignment from westward travel affects NFL coaches' fourth-down decision-making across 20 seasons (2000–2020, n=5360 games). Factorial ANCOVA and moderation analyses tested whether travel direction and longitudinal distance interacted with game time to predict offensive play-calling on fourth down. Coaches on westward-traveling teams called significantly more offensive fourth-down plays (14.90%) during evening games compared to same-time-zone (12.20%) and eastward-traveling (11.35%) teams, an effect absent in daytime games, suggesting circadian misalignment impairs coaching cognition.

## Methods
Retrospective analysis of 5360 regular season NFL visiting team games from 2000–2020 (pre-COVID), excluding international games and playoff games. Data extracted from profootballreference.com and nfl.com. Variables included travel direction (westward, same time zone, eastward), longitudinal degrees traveled (−51° to +51°), time of game (day <18:00h vs. evening ≥18:00h, and as continuous variable), year of season, and percentage of offensive plays on fourth down. A factorial ANCOVA (Time of game × Direction of travel) with year as covariate was conducted, followed by post hoc ANOVAs and Tukey t-tests for day (n=4410) and evening (n=950) games separately. A moderation analysis tested whether time of game moderated the relationship between longitudinal degrees traveled and fourth-down play calling.

## Findings
Significant interaction between Time of game and Direction of travel (F[2,5358]=5.39, p=0.005). In evening games, westward-traveling teams had 14.90% offensive fourth-down plays vs. 12.20% (same time zone, p=0.03) and 11.35% (eastward, p=0.007). No significant direction effect in daytime games (p=0.62). Moderation analysis: significant longitudinal degrees × time of game interaction (F[1,5355]=9.51, p=0.002). Westward longitudinal degrees significantly predicted greater fourth-down aggression only for games starting at 16:24h or later. The explained variance was modest (0.2%) but statistically significant and meaningful in the context of elite competition.

## Limitations
No data on coaches' individual sleep patterns or chronotypes. Game situational variables (score, time remaining, field position, opponent strength) were unavailable and could not be controlled. Fourth-down decisions represent only one of many coaching decisions per game and may be influenced by cumulative in-game context. The study was exploratory with a modest effect size. Causal inference is limited by the retrospective observational design.

## Practical Implications
Sleep and circadian health protocols should extend beyond players to include all coaching and support staff. Teams traveling westward for evening games should implement pre-travel phase-shifting strategies (morning bright light, evening melatonin) for coaches as well as players. NFL schedulers should consider offering earlier kickoff times to teams traveling west across multiple time zones. Organizations should monitor in-game decision metrics in relation to travel schedule as part of performance management.

## Athlete Development Notes
This research broadens the scope of circadian science in football to include coaching staff cognitive performance. For player development, it underscores the importance of preparing players for complex decision-making demands (quarterbacks, defensive captains) under conditions of circadian disruption, particularly when preparing for westward road trips with evening kickoffs.

## Return to Play Notes
From a return-to-play perspective, this study highlights that cognitive readiness — not just physical readiness — must be considered when clearing athletes after travel-heavy schedules. Medical and performance staff should monitor decision-making quality and cognitive load tolerance in players returning from injury during periods of circadian disruption, particularly in westward-travel evening game scenarios.
