# Lauck BJ, Sinnott AM, Kiefer AW, Padua DA, Powell JR, Sledge HR, Mihalik JP. Association Between Head Impact Biomechanics and Physical Load in College Football. Annals of Biomedical Engineering. 2022.

## TLDR
Physical load burden—as measured by GPS-based player load—is a significant predictor of head impact exposure in college football, suggesting that tracking physical load may serve as a practical proxy for anticipating repetitive head impact risk both within sessions and across a full season.

## Abstract
This prospective study examined whether an association exists between repetitive head impact exposure (RHIE) and physical load metrics in college football across the 2017–2021 seasons (excluding 2020). Forty-one Division I players were instrumented with both the Head Impact Telemetry (HIT) System (Riddell helmets) to measure linear and rotational head accelerations and Catapult Vector GPS units embedded in halters to quantify player load. Generalized linear mixed models and linear regression models were used to analyze in-session and season-long associations while controlling for position group (BIGS, BIG SKILL, SKILL) and session type (practice vs. game). Player load was significantly associated with RHIE both within individual sessions and across an entire season, with season-long player load explaining 31% of the variability in cumulative RHIE (R²=0.31; p<0.0001). Game sessions produced significantly greater player load and RHIE compared to practice sessions, while position group did not significantly affect RHIE.

## Methods
Prospective cohort study; 41 male Division I college football players across four seasons (2017–2019, 2021); Head Impact Telemetry (HIT) System in Riddell helmets for head impact biomechanics; Catapult Vector GPS monitoring system in halters for player load; session RHIE computed via Stemper et al. risk-weighted exposure (RWE) formula; generalized linear mixed models (PROC MIXED) for in-session and season-long associations; linear regression (PROC REG) for secondary analyses; position groups: BIGS (OL/DL), BIG SKILL (LB/TE), SKILL (DB/RB/WR); outliers ≥3 SD removed; QBs, kickers, and punters excluded; 2020 excluded due to COVID-19.

## Findings
• Player load was significantly associated with in-session RHIE (p<0.001)
• Season-long player load predicted season-long RHIE (R²=0.31; F=18.79; p<0.0001)
• Game player load and RHIE were both significantly greater than practice (p<0.001)
• Position group significantly affected in-session player load (p=0.025; SKILL > BIGS) but not RHIE (p=0.343)
• Session type significantly affected both player load and RHIE (p<0.0001), with games > practices
• 41 players contributed 2,330 unique sessions with 27,349 total head impacts recorded
• Cumulative RHIE: Game=1.1±1.3; Practice=1.4±2.0; Combined=2.5±2.8

## Limitations
Single college football program limits generalizability; HIT System detects ~70% of on-field head impacts; Catapult can underestimate loads by up to 15%; participants' playing status may have changed during the study; site-specific coaching strategies may influence findings; HIT System only compatible with select Riddell helmet models; QBs and specialists excluded; 2020 season excluded.

## Practical Implications
Physical load tracking via GPS (already pervasive across college athletics) may serve as a real-time proxy for monitoring anticipated head impact burden. Sports medicine providers and S&C staff can use player load data to inform practice planning and scheduling to reduce cumulative head impact exposure. This approach is particularly actionable during preseason and regular season management for athletes returning from concussion or musculoskeletal injury.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American college football. SKILL position players (DBs, WRs, RBs) generate higher player loads than BIGS (OL/DL), while linemen tend to sustain higher head impact frequency—these position-specific differences should inform individualized load management strategies. Monitoring GPS-derived player load across preseason and regular season can help coaching and medical staff calibrate practice intensity to manage cumulative head impact risk.

## Return to Play Notes
Physical load data may provide clinicians with a practical metric for gauging head impact re-exposure risk when returning athletes to full participation after concussion or musculoskeletal injury. Authors note findings apply directly to medical providers monitoring athletes returning to sport, as physical load burden can inform anticipated RHIE during progressive RTP protocols.
