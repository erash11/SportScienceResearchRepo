# McGinniss A, Guinand LA, Vosbikian M, Ahmed I. Distal Biceps Ruptures in National Football League Players: Return to Play and Performance Analysis. Journal of Shoulder and Elbow Surgery. 2021. doi:10.1016/j.jse.2020.11.010

## TLDR
Distal biceps ruptures surgically repaired in NFL players carry a high return-to-play rate (~94%), with an average time to return of approximately 325 days (about one year). Post-surgical performance scores across most positions are statistically comparable to pre-injury levels and to matched controls, meaning coaches and medical staff can counsel athletes that this injury, while prolonged, does not broadly diminish long-term performance. Offensive linemen are the most commonly affected position and show a modest but statistically significant reduction in games played per season post-surgery compared to controls, though their career length is not affected.

## Abstract
This retrospective case-control study examined distal biceps ruptures in NFL players from the 2000–2016 seasons, focusing on return-to-play (RTP) rates and post-surgical performance. Thirty-five NFL players with surgically treated distal biceps ruptures were identified through publicly available sources and matched to a control group by position, age, experience, and performance score. Pre- and post-injury performance statistics and career length were compared between cohort and control groups using paired-samples Student t-tests.

## Methods
Retrospective case-control study of NFL players from seasons 2000–2016 with surgically treated distal biceps ruptures identified via publicly available media, injury reports, and team websites confirmed by two independent searches. Thirty-five players were included. Each was matched to a control by position (CB, DE, DT, LB, TE, RB, S, OL), age (±1 year), experience, and performance score. Pre- vs. post-injury and cohort vs. control comparisons used paired-samples and independent-samples t-tests (p<0.05). Performance scores used a previously published standardized scoring system. Career length and games per season were also analyzed.

## Findings
35 NFL players identified; 33 (94%) returned to sport at an average of 324.7 ± 112 days (351.4 ± 123.9 days per the abstract). Offensive linemen made up 35% (n=12) of the cohort; two OL (6%) failed to return. Average age at injury was 28.5 ± 3.5 years. Defensive tackles showed a significant drop in pre- vs. post-surgery performance scores (2.9 ± 0.7 vs. 1.7 ± 0.3, p=0.02), but no significant difference was found when DT cohort was compared to controls post-surgery. Offensive linemen played significantly fewer games per season post-surgery vs. controls (11.1 ± 3.4 vs. 13.6 ± 2.1, p=0.04). Career length post-surgery did not differ between OL and controls (p=0.79). No other positions showed significant differences in games played, career length, or performance scores.

## Limitations
Small cohort size due to rarity of the injury may have underpowered the study. Reliance on publicly available data introduces selection, reporting, and observer bias; surgical technique (1- vs. 2-incision) and specific operative details were not accessible from the NFL directly. No performance scoring system existed for offensive linemen, limiting analysis for that position group. Findings may not generalize beyond elite NFL athletes.

## Practical Implications
NFL medical and performance staff should counsel players that surgical repair of distal biceps ruptures results in high RTP rates (~94%) but a prolonged absence of roughly one year. Performance upon return is generally preserved across positions. Offensive linemen may play fewer games per season post-surgery, likely due to the biomechanical demands of their position placing repetitive eccentric load on the biceps. Long-tenured players (8–9+ seasons) appear more susceptible, which may inform load management strategies for veteran linemen.

## Athlete Development Notes
Offensive and defensive linemen are the most at-risk positions for distal biceps ruptures due to the high eccentric biceps loading inherent in blocking and hand-fighting. Strength and conditioning staff should monitor cumulative upper-extremity strain in veteran linemen and prioritize eccentric biceps strength work and forearm supination/flexion loading tolerance as preventive training strategies.

## Return to Play Notes
Average time to RTP after surgical distal biceps repair in NFL players is approximately 324–352 days (nearly a full calendar year). A 94% RTP rate supports aggressive surgical intervention. Return decisions should be guided by position-specific demands; offensive linemen may require extended monitoring given their reduced games-per-season post-surgery. Post-surgical performance is broadly comparable to pre-injury levels, supporting optimistic return-to-competition counseling.
