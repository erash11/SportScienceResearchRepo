# Ribeiro AS, Avelar A, Kassiano W, Nunes JP, Schoenfeld BJ, Aguiar AF, Trindade MCC, Silva AM, Sardinha LB, Cyrino ES. Creatine Supplementation Does Not Influence the Ratio Between Intracellular Water and Skeletal Muscle Mass in Resistance-Trained Men. International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism. 2020;30(6):405-411. DOI: 10.1123/ijsnem.2020-0080

## TLDR
Creatine supplementation combined with resistance training produces greater increases in skeletal muscle mass and intracellular water than training alone, but the ratio between these two variables remains stable — meaning the additional lean mass reflects genuine hypertrophy with proportional intracellular hydration, not simply water retention. For football staff, this confirms that DXA-measured lean mass gains in creatine-using athletes accurately represent true muscle tissue adaptation. Creatine can be confidently recommended during off-season or pre-season hypertrophy training blocks for resistance-trained athletes seeking to accelerate muscle mass accretion.

## Abstract
This randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial investigated the effects of 8 weeks of creatine (Cr) supplementation combined with resistance training (RT) on skeletal muscle mass (SMM), total body water (TBW), intracellular water (ICW), and extracellular water (ECW) in 27 resistance-trained men. Both groups improved in SMM, TBW, and ICW, with significantly greater gains in the Cr group, yet the SMM/ICW ratio did not change in either group despite these differences. The ECW/SMM ratio decreased only in the Cr group, and a positive correlation was found between percentage changes in SMM and ICW (r=0.71, p<0.001). These results suggest that Cr-induced muscle mass gains occur with proportional intracellular hydration rather than artifactual water retention.

## Methods
Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial over 30 weeks in 27 resistance-trained men (Cr: n=14, age 21.8±4.1 yr; Placebo: n=13, age 21.7±4.2 yr). A 16-week preconditioning RT phase standardized training history before an 8-week supplementation intervention. Creatine protocol: 5-day loading at 20 g/day followed by 51-day maintenance at 3 g/day; placebo used maltodextrin at identical doses. SMM was estimated from DXA-derived appendicular lean soft tissue (GE Lunar Prodigy) using the Kim et al. (2004) predictive equation. TBW, ICW, and ECW measured via spectral bioelectrical impedance (Xitron Hydra 4200). RT in the intervention phase consisted of two split routines four times per week using a pyramid loading scheme (12/10/8/6 RM). Dietary intake was monitored during the first and last weeks of the intervention.

## Findings
Creatine group showed significantly greater increases than placebo in body mass (Cr: +3.0% vs. Pla: +0.7%), SMM (Cr: +7.1%, ES=0.71 vs. Pla: +2.8%, ES=0.28), TBW (Cr: +7.0%, ES=0.68 vs. Pla: +1.7%, ES=0.16), and ICW (Cr: +9.2%, ES=0.81 vs. Pla: +1.6%, ES=0.14). ECW increased similarly in both groups (~1%). The ICW/SMM ratio did not change in either group (p>0.05). The ECW/SMM ratio decreased only in the Cr group (p<0.05). A significant positive correlation was found between percentage changes in SMM and ICW (r=0.71, p<0.001).

## Limitations
Dietary intake was monitored only in the first and last weeks rather than throughout the full intervention, limiting verification of nutritional confounders. The supplementation phase was relatively short (8 weeks), so long-term responses remain unknown. DXA scans were performed at least 4 hours post-food/fluid intake rather than after an overnight fast, which may introduce some measurement error in lean soft tissue estimates. Muscle biopsies were not performed to confirm intramuscular creatine storage or characterize subfractional hypertrophy mechanisms. Findings are specific to resistance-trained young men and may not generalize to women, older adults, or other populations.

## Practical Implications
For NFL and college football staff, these findings confirm that creatine monohydrate supplementation is an effective, evidence-based ergogenic aid for resistance-trained athletes seeking to maximize muscle mass during structured strength training phases. The stable ICW/SMM ratio indicates that DXA-measured lean mass changes accurately reflect true hypertrophy rather than artifactual water retention, supporting the validity of DXA body composition monitoring in creatine-using athletes. A loading protocol (20 g/day for 5 days) followed by maintenance (3 g/day) is an effective administration strategy. The reduced ECW/SMM ratio in the creatine group may suggest improved muscle quality as an additional benefit.

## Athlete Development Notes
Football athletes in developmental programs undertaking structured resistance training can benefit from creatine supplementation to enhance muscle hypertrophy adaptations beyond what training alone produces. Given that resistance-trained individuals experience attenuated responses to training stimuli compared to novices, creatine provides a practical, safe ergogenic strategy to overcome hypertrophic plateaus. DXA-based lean mass gains can be interpreted as genuine muscle tissue development in creatine-supplemented athletes, making body composition monitoring a valid tool for tracking developmental progress in players using this supplement.

## Return to Play Notes
While this study was not designed as a return-to-play investigation, the findings have indirect RTP relevance: creatine supplementation may accelerate muscle mass recovery following injury-related deconditioning when combined with progressive resistance training. The proportional relationship between ICW and SMM gains confirms that creatine-enhanced hypertrophy reflects genuine contractile tissue rather than edematous swelling, which is important when using body composition monitoring to guide RTP readiness decisions. The decreased ECW/SMM ratio in the creatine group may also suggest improved muscle quality relevant to structural rehabilitation outcomes.
