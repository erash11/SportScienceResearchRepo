# Marín, K., & Castellano, J. (2023). High-speed running distance and frequency in football training: When and how are they stimulated in a microcycle? International Journal of Sports Science & Coaching, 18(4), 1132–1141.

## TLDR
Professional football teams follow an inverted-U weekly load pattern: high-speed running is concentrated in the mid-week acquisition sessions (MD-3 and MD-4), tapers before match day (with MD-2 as the lowest stimulus day), and is kept low immediately post-match. Total accumulated HSR volume does not increase proportionally when more days are available between matches, but longer microcycles push high-speed work further from the upcoming match (e.g., MD-5 in 7-day weeks). Staff should program sprint and high-speed running deliberately in mid-week to optimize adaptation while preserving freshness for competition.

## Abstract
This study described the distribution of distance and frequency of high-speed running (HSR >21 km/h), very high-speed running (VHSR >24 km/h), and sprint (SPR >27 km/h) in professional football training across microcycles (Mi) of 4, 5, 6, and 7 days between matches. GPS data were collected from 23 La Liga players across 28 microcycles during the 2018–19 season. Central training days (MD-3, MD-4) accumulated the greatest high-speed locomotive demands regardless of microcycle duration, while MD-2 consistently showed the least stimulation. Longer microcycles (Mi7) had significantly higher HSR values on MD-4 and MD-5 compared to shorter microcycles.

## Methods
Descriptive, observational design. Twenty-three male professional players from one La Liga club were monitored via 10 Hz Catapult GPS units across 28 competitive microcycles (2018–19 season). Microcycles were classified by days between matches (Mi4, Mi5, Mi6, Mi7). HSR (>21 km/h), VHSR (>24 km/h), and SPR (>27 km/h) were quantified by total distance (m) and frequency (occurrences) per session. One-way ANOVA with Bonferroni/Dunnett's T3 post hoc tests; effect sizes reported as Cohen's d. Mixed linear models used for MD-day comparisons.

## Findings
No significant differences in total accumulated HSR, VHSR, or SPR volume across microcycle durations (Mi4–Mi7). Regardless of microcycle length, central days (MD-4, MD-3) received significantly greater locomotive demands than post-match (MD-7, MD-6) and pre-match (MD-2, MD-1) sessions, with MD-2 requiring the least stimulation (p<0.05). In 7-day microcycles, MD-4 and MD-5 showed significantly higher HSR/VHSR/SPR than shorter microcycles. MD-3 was consistently the day with the greatest stimulation across all microcycle lengths. Substantial intra-session sprint variability was noted, attributable to position, fitness status, and competition participation.

## Limitations
Single team and single coaching philosophy limits generalizability. No internal load metrics (e.g., heart rate, RPE) were included to contextualize physiological response. Testing/readiness and match performance data were not integrated. Large SD in sprint outputs reflects high inter-individual variability.

## Practical Implications
High-speed running (HSR, VHSR, SPR) should be programmed primarily on mid-week days (MD-3 and MD-4), distanced from both the preceding and upcoming match to allow recovery and avoid compromising match-day readiness. When more days are available between matches, push high-speed work earlier (MD-5) rather than simply adding more total volume. MD-2 should consistently be the lowest-stimulus session. Coaches should adjust the timing of sprint stimuli based on microcycle length, not just overall volume.

## Athlete Development Notes
For developing football athletes, this study supports the principle of horizontal alternation within the weekly microcycle: recovery sessions post-match, acquisition sessions mid-week (with sprint and high-speed running emphasis), and tapering pre-match. Building tolerance to high-speed running across the week at appropriate distances and frequencies may serve a protective function against injury and support long-term speed development.

## Return to Play Notes
The study underscores that MD-2 is the most protected session in professional programming. For athletes returning from injury, this session structure provides a logical integration framework: return players to mid-week sessions with graduated HSR exposure (beginning at MD-3 or MD-4 level intensities), progressing as they demonstrate tolerance, before full participation in match-day activities.
