# Mitchell A, Holding C, Greig M. Factors influencing optimum countermovement jump performance and movement strategy in Championship professional football players: implications for player profiling. Res Sports Med. 2020. DOI: 10.1080/15438627.2020.1860049.

## TLDR
In professional soccer/football players, jump height alone is an insufficient profiling metric because athletes can achieve acceptable jump heights through compensatory strategies that mask eccentric deficits. The flight time:contraction time ratio reveals true movement quality, with the best performers characterized by a shallower countermovement and greater concentric rate of force development. Practitioners should prioritize movement strategy metrics over gross outcome measures when assessing readiness and return to play.

## Abstract
This study examined single-leg countermovement jump (CMJ) performance and movement strategy in 36 professional outfield players from an English Championship football club. Players were retrospectively classified as Best (n=10) or Worst (n=10) performers based on CMJ jump height and flight time:contraction time (FT:CT) ratio. Movement strategy was quantified through force-time history metrics differentiating eccentric and concentric phases. Best performers demonstrated greater rate of force development in both phases (P ≤ 0.033), with concentric impulse the strongest predictor of jump height. FT:CT ratio differentiated best performers as using a shallower countermovement (P = 0.002), with concentric RFD the strongest predictor of good FT:CT ratio. Jump height alone can mask ineffectual eccentric mechanics; movement strategy via time ratio is advocated as the key profiling indicator.

## Methods
Thirty-six full-time professional outfield players from a Championship football club (age 23.3 ± 5.6 yrs, weight 78.1 ± 1.9 kg) performed 10 single-leg CMJs on a dual PASPORT force plate system (NMP ForceDecks) 5 days post-match, +5 days from a scheduled rest day. Mean scores across 5 dominant limb trials were calculated. Players ranked on jump height and FT:CT ratio independently; top 10 vs. bottom 10 formed Best and Worst groups. Force-time variables included peak force, impulse, RFD, phase duration, force at zero velocity, and CMJ depth for eccentric and concentric phases. Univariate general linear models and linear regression analyses were used.

## Findings
Best performers had significantly higher jump height (36.24 ± 1.53 cm vs. 20.15 ± 1.56 cm; P < 0.001, η²=0.968) and higher FT:CT ratio (1.04 ± 0.17 vs. 0.35 ± 0.09; P < 0.001, η²=0.880). When classified by jump height, Best performers showed significantly higher EccRFD (P=0.006), concentric impulse, PkForce, and RFD (P ≤ 0.034). When classified by FT:CT ratio, Best performers had greater eccentric PkForce and RFD (P ≤ 0.002), shorter eccentric phase duration, shallower CMJ depth (P=0.002), and greater concentric PkForce and RFD (P < 0.001). Concentric RFD was the strongest predictor of FT:CT ratio (r=0.88 in Best performers). Worst performers showed strong associations with greater eccentric phase metrics and deeper countermovement, suggesting compensatory strategy.

## Limitations
Study limited to outfield male players at one professional club; findings may not generalize to other populations, female athletes, or other sports. Only the single-leg CMJ was assessed; double-leg CMJ findings may differ. The group stratification compared the top and bottom 10 of 36 players, meaning the 'worst' performers are still professional footballers. Jump height calculated from flight time is susceptible to body position changes at landing. Future work should examine unloading and braking sub-phases separately and consider single-subject longitudinal approaches.

## Practical Implications
Practitioners without force platforms can approximate movement quality using a countermovement:rocket jump height ratio as a proxy for stretch-shortening cycle efficacy. Profiling should incorporate FT:CT ratio alongside jump height to avoid misclassifying athletes with compensatory eccentric strategies as adequately prepared. Training interventions should target concentric rate of force development while ensuring eccentric phase quality is not neglected.

## Athlete Development Notes
Single-leg CMJ profiling with force-time analysis provides individualized neuromuscular data to guide personalized training prescription for football athletes. Athletes with poor FT:CT ratios showing deep countermovements and extended eccentric phases may benefit from targeted SSC and reactive strength training. Regular single-leg CMJ assessment is logistically feasible and sensitive enough to detect training adaptations and fatigue-related changes in movement strategy.

## Return to Play Notes
Jump height returning to pre-injury levels during rehabilitation may mask persistent neuromuscular deficits if movement strategy is not assessed. The FT:CT ratio is sensitive to previous injury and can identify eccentric strength deficiencies that are a risk factor for hamstring strains, ACL injuries, and ankle injuries — all common in football. Return-to-play clearance should include FT:CT ratio criteria alongside gross performance outcome thresholds.
