# Eichner ER. Fatal Exertional Heat Stroke in Football: The Coaches Are the Culprits. Curr Sports Med Rep. 2019;18(7):251-252.

## TLDR
Fatal exertional heat stroke in football is entirely preventable, yet since 2000 it has killed over 40 high school and at least 10 college players. Nearly every death occurs not during game play but during coach-mandated conditioning drills. Large linemen forced to run high-volume sprints on early training days in heat are at extreme risk because their bodies generate heat far faster than they can dissipate it. Immediate cold-water immersion is life-saving; the key is early recognition (fuzzy thinking, erratic behavior) before physical collapse occurs.

## Abstract
This commentary reviews fatal exertional heat stroke (EHS) cases in college football from 2014–2018, arguing that reckless coach-mandated conditioning drills—particularly high-intensity sprints for large linemen in the heat on early training days—are the primary cause. Four college football player deaths and two severe near-fatal cases are described in detail, with core temperatures at hospital ranging from 105°F to 108°F. The author advocates for ice-water immersion as the immediate treatment, with a 100% survival rate documented at the Falmouth Road Race over 18 years. The article concludes that fatal EHS in football is preventable and that coaches bear primary responsibility for creating the conditions that lead to these deaths.

## Methods
Narrative commentary and case review. The author describes six detailed case vignettes of college football players who suffered EHS during conditioning drills (2013–2018), summarizes epidemiological data on EHS deaths in high school and college football since 2000, and references evidence on cold-water immersion efficacy from the Falmouth Road Race (274 cases over 18 years, 100% survival).

## Findings
Since 2000, EHS has killed more than 40 high school football players and at least 10 college football players, almost all during conditioning rather than game play. Four college football players died from EHS in the five years preceding publication (2014–2018). Core temperatures at hospital ranged from 106°F to 108°F in fatal cases. Two survivors experienced severe multi-organ failure (liver transplant, rhabdomyolysis with CK ~1,000,000 IU/L, prolonged ICU stays). Immediate cold-water immersion at the Falmouth Road Race achieved a 100% survival rate across 274 EHS cases. Cooling is effective even with full football gear in place.

## Limitations
This is an opinion/commentary piece, not a systematic study. Case data are drawn from media reports and published case series rather than a controlled data set. No incidence rates are formally calculated. The causal attribution to coaches is the author's interpretation rather than a formal epidemiological finding.

## Practical Implications
Coaches must eliminate or radically modify high-volume sprint conditioning sessions for large linemen, especially on day 1 of preseason in the heat. Staff should 'bird-dog the big guys,' watching for earliest EHS signs (confusion, odd behavior) and act before collapse. Ice-water immersion tubs must be immediately accessible at all practices. Coaches and athletic trainers must be trained to recognize EHS early and act without delay, as >90-minute delays to hospital treatment resulted in death.

## Athlete Development Notes
Large-bodied linemen are at disproportionate EHS risk because their high mass generates heat rapidly while their surface-area-to-mass ratio limits heat dissipation. Conditioning programs for these athletes must be designed around this physiological reality. Volume and intensity of early-preseason sprinting must be appropriate for body size, fitness level, heat acclimatization status, and ambient conditions. Progressive acclimatization protocols should replace single high-intensity conditioning sessions at the start of camp.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes who survive EHS face prolonged recovery from multi-organ damage, rhabdomyolysis, renal and liver injury, and neurological sequelae. Return to sport after severe EHS requires formal heat tolerance testing before clearance, as demonstrated in one case where a survivor was deemed medically unfit to return to college football despite recovery of fitness. Staff should be aware that even surviving athletes may not be cleared for full football participation.
