## Limitations
Generalizability limited to one D1 football team at a single institution in a warm, humid climate. External factors potentially influencing ANS (medications, caffeine, sleep quality, mental stress) were not measured. Female athletes and other competitive levels not represented. Core temperature measurement via non-invasive armband infrared sensor, though validated, is not equivalent to gold-standard invasive measures for all athletes.

## Practical Implications
Coaches and sports medicine staff should monitor both core temperature and training duration during sessions conducted in hot/humid conditions (especially summer training camps and preseason). When core temperatures are â‰¥38Â°C, training bouts should be limited to 30â€“45 minutes with prolonged cooling breaks. Consecutive training days following elevated core temperature exposure compound ANS dysfunction and increase exertional heat illness risk. Athletes training in thermoneutral environments showed no significant ANS deterioration, confirming the value of climate-controlled facilities or cooling strategies.

## Athlete Development Notes
Summer camp and preseason training represent the highest heat-exposure risk period for collegiate football athletes. Athletes in detraining periods entering summer camps may experience accelerated core temperature rises due to reduced heat acclimatization. Development staff should incorporate heat acclimatization protocols, monitor core temperature via wearables, and structure practice schedules to allow adequate cooling. Long-term cumulative ANS dysfunction from repeated hyperthermic exposures may impair performance adaptation and increase injury risk over a season.

## Return to Play Notes
For athletes returning to activity after illness, injury, or extended break, core temperature monitoring is particularly important as fitness reductions impair thermoregulation. Elevated ANS dysfunction 24 hours post-training can mask readiness for return-to-play assessments that rely on resting HR or HRV. Practitioners should consider scheduling RTP evaluations on days following thermoneutral training or after confirmed normal ANS recovery to avoid false negatives in readiness assessment.
