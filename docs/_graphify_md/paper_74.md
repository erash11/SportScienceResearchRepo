# Krystofiak J, Asselstine S, Toto D, Rybak K, Zhao Y, Misuraco A, McCune D. Biceps Femoris Long Head Fascicle Length Not Associated With Hamstring Injury Risk in NCAA Football Athletes. Clin J Sport Med. 2024;00(00):1–3.

## TLDR
BFlh fascicle length measured by ultrasound does not predict hamstring injury risk in American football athletes, suggesting that risk screening tools validated in soccer may not transfer across sports.

## Abstract
This retrospective cohort study examined whether biceps femoris long head (BFlh) fascicle length (FL) measured via ultrasound was associated with hamstring strain injury (HSI) risk in 129 NCAA Division I collegiate football athletes. Ultrasound measurements were taken on the nondominant leg and injury data were reviewed retrospectively from the 2022 and 2023 preseasons and seasons. Logistic regression found no significant relationship between BFlh FL and HSI risk (OR = 1.01; 95% CI, 0.92–1.10; P = 0.86). Incidentally, BMI ≥30 kg/m² was found to be a protective factor against HSI (OR = 0.27; 95% CI, 0.08–0.84; P = 0.03). The previously established cutoff of FL < 10.56 cm performed poorly in this population, yielding a 44.96% misclassification rate and a sensitivity of only 0.18.

## Methods
Retrospective cohort design; 129 NCAA Division I football athletes; BFlh FL assessed via B-mode ultrasound on the nondominant leg using a previously validated protocol; injury records from 2022 and 2023 preseasons and seasons reviewed; logistic regression with post hoc power analysis; previously established cutoff of 10.56 cm evaluated via sensitivity, specificity, and F1 score.

## Findings
• BFlh FL was not significantly associated with HSI risk (OR = 1.01; 95% CI, 0.92–1.10; P = 0.86)
• Mean BFlh FL was 13.09 cm (±4.60) in injured and 12.91 cm (±4.88) in uninjured athletes
• 27 of 129 athletes sustained HSI over the study period
• The 10.56 cm cutoff had 44.96% misclassification rate, sensitivity of 0.18, specificity of 0.78, and F-score of 0.24 in this population
• BMI ≥30 kg/m² was a significant protective factor (OR = 0.27; 95% CI, 0.08–0.84; P = 0.03)
• Defensive backs had the highest HSI incidence among positions (36.36%)

## Limitations
Single-institution study with small sample size; low overall HSI incidence; no advanced imaging for injury confirmation; no control for prior HSI history; between-limb FL differences not assessed; wide spectrum of position roles and body types not fully accounted for.

## Practical Implications
BFlh ultrasound FL screening should not be used as a standalone HSI risk screening tool in American football. Risk screening protocols validated in soccer or Australian rules football may not generalize to American football populations. BMI and position-specific factors may be more relevant screening variables in this sport.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly studied in NCAA Division I American football athletes. Highlights that body composition (BMI) and position-specific training demands influence HSI risk more than BFlh FL. Defensive backs showed the highest HSI incidence, suggesting position-specific injury prevention programming may be warranted.

## Return to Play Notes
The lack of association between BFlh FL and HSI risk suggests that FL measurement alone should not guide return-to-play decisions following hamstring strain in American football. Criteria-based RTP protocols should incorporate multiple factors beyond muscle architecture.
