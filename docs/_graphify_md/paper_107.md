# Dow B, Doucet D, Vemu SM, Boddapati V, Marco RAW, Hirase T. Characterizing neck injuries in the national football league: a descriptive epidemiology study. BMC Musculoskelet Disord. 2023;24:702. doi:10.1186/s12891-023-06830-y

## TLDR
Neck injuries occur at an incidence of 23.5 per 10,000 team plays in the NFL, predominantly affecting defensive backs and linebackers. The vast majority (87.7%) are non-season-ending with a mean return of 9 days, but 12% lead to season- or career-ending consequences. Four primary mechanisms are identified, with head-down tackling associated with the highest rate of serious outcomes despite not reaching statistical significance. For football staff, this study supports ongoing emphasis on proper tackling technique education and pre-participation cervical spine screening for high-risk defensive positions.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiological study characterized neck injuries in NFL athletes using the league's publicly available injured reserve list from the 2016 through 2021 seasons, identifying 464 players (mean age 26.8±3.2 years) with neck injuries over the 6-year period. Defensive players accounted for 61.4% of injuries (p<0.001), with defensive backs the most common position (111 players, 23.9%). The overall incidence was 23.5 per 10,000 team plays. Of all injuries, 87.7% were non-season-ending (mean RTS 9.2 days), 7.8% were season-ending (mean RTS 378.6 days), and 4.5% were career-ending. Video review of 38 available injuries identified four main mechanisms: head-to-head contact (39.5%), head-down tackling (28.9%), extremity-to-head contact (18.4%), and head-to-ground contact (13.2%).

## Methods
Retrospective descriptive epidemiological study using the publicly available NFL injured reserve list from 2016 through 2021. Players designated with neck injuries were included; those with non-neck designations were excluded. Demographics (age, position, injury/return dates) were collected. Injuries were classified as non-season-ending, season-ending (did not return same season), or career-ending (no documented return). Incidence rates calculated per 10,000 team plays using NFL total team plays for all 32 teams. Slow-motion game footage reviewed for MOI classification when available (38 of 464 injuries). Chi-square or Fisher's exact tests for categorical data; t-test or Mann-Whitney U for continuous data (SPSS v25.0; p<0.05).

## Findings
464 players with neck injuries over 6 seasons (mean age 26.8±3.2 years). Defensive players: 61.4% vs offensive 38.6% (p<0.001). Defensive back was the most common position (111 players, 23.9%; incidence 5.6/10,000 team plays). Non-season-ending injuries: 407 (87.7%), mean RTS 9.2±11.3 days. Season-ending injuries: 36 (7.8%), mean RTS 378.6±162.0 days. Career-ending injuries: 21 (4.5%). Overall incidence: 23.5/10,000 team plays. Season-ending incidence: 1.82/10,000; career-ending: 1.06/10,000. MOI from 38 video reviews: head-to-head contact 39.5%, head-down tackling 28.9%, extremity-to-head 18.4%, head-to-ground 13.2%. Head-down tackling associated with >50% season- or career-ending injuries. No significant differences in age, position, or MOI between severity categories (p>0.05).

## Limitations
The NFL IR list uses a generalized 'neck injury' designation without specifying diagnosis, imaging findings, or injury type. Practice versus competition occurrence cannot be distinguished from available data. Players with concurrent injuries make it difficult to attribute time lost solely to neck injury. Season-ending RTS dates may be overestimated as players may have healed during the off-season. MOI analysis limited to 38 of 464 injuries (8.2%) with adequate footage, creating selection bias in mechanism characterization.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football medical staffs should emphasize proper tackling technique—particularly eliminating head-down tackling—as the highest-risk mechanism for serious cervical injury. Defensive players, especially defensive backs and linebackers, represent the highest-risk population and should be prioritized for neck strengthening protocols and technique review. The finding that non-tackle direct head impacts (extremity-to-head, head-to-ground) also contribute meaningfully to cervical spine injuries suggests that rules protecting defenseless players and consistent penalty enforcement remain critical injury prevention tools.

## Athlete Development Notes
Youth and high school football programs should prioritize Heads Up Football-style tackling technique to reduce head-down contact patterns that carry the greatest risk for catastrophic cervical injury. At the collegiate and professional levels, position-specific neck strengthening protocols—particularly for defensive backs and linebackers—are warranted given their elevated injury incidence. Pre-participation cervical spine screening, including assessment of subaxial space available for the cord, may help identify athletes at heightened risk for severe injury. Defensive players have reduced subaxial cervical space compared to offensive players due to repetitive cervical loading from blocking and tackling.

## Return to Play Notes
The majority of NFL neck injuries (87.7%) are non-season-ending with a mean return of 9.2 days. Season-ending injuries (7.8%) require a mean of 378.6 days, and 4.5% are career-ending. RTP after cervical spine injury requires neurological clearance, full cervical ROM and strength restoration, and confirmation that the underlying structural pathology is stable or resolved. Head-down tackling-related injuries have the highest likelihood of serious outcomes and require the most conservative RTP approach. Pre-return cervical stability and proprioception testing should be incorporated into RTP protocols for season-ending neck injuries.
