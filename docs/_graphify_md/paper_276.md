# Koyama T, Saita Y, Tateishi T, Tanita T, Ukita H, Shima T, Itaya N, Yaguchi H, Yamamoto N, Mori Y, Aizawa T, Nagao M. Hip internal rotation restriction and heel raise mechanics as risk factors for Jones fractures in youth football players. Asia Pac J Sports Med Arthrosc Rehabil Technol. 2025;42:15-20.

## TLDR
Youth football players with restricted hip internal rotation (<30°) who also bear weight on the lateral side of the foot during a single heel raise face approximately four times the risk of Jones fracture compared to unaffected peers. Restricted HIR induces femoral/tibial external rotation and foot supination, increasing lateral metatarsal loading. Screening these two simple clinical measures can identify high-risk athletes for targeted preventive interventions before a complete fracture develops.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study investigated the prevalence and risk factors for Jones fractures (stress fractures of the proximal metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction of the fifth metatarsal) among 232 male youth football players from 20 top-level high school teams in Japan during the 2024 tournament. Medical examinations combined ultrasound and portable X-ray imaging with physical testing including hip internal rotation (HIR) range of motion, single heel raise (SHR) weight-bearing position, ankle lunge test, straight leg raise, single-leg sit-to-stand, toe grip strength, and hip abductor strength. Eighteen players (7.8%) tested positive on ultrasound and seven (3.0%) had confirmed fractures on X-ray; 11 had a fracture history. Restricted HIR of <30° was significantly associated with Jones fracture risk (Crude OR 3.74, p=0.01). Combined restricted HIR <30° with lateral weight-bearing during SHR further elevated risk (Crude OR 4.02, p=0.007), and this combination remained significant after adjustment for height, weight, and dominant foot (Adjusted OR 3.87, p=0.009).

## Methods
Cross-sectional medical examination study. 232 male high school football players (mean age 16±1 years) from 20 top-level Japanese teams examined during a 2024 tournament. Fracture identification: ultrasound (hump sign or beak sign) confirmed by ≥2 physicians; positive cases confirmed by portable dental X-ray device; fractures classified as true fractures. Physical examinations by 32 physical therapists included: HIR range of motion (prone active rotation measured by digital inclinometer), single heel raise weight-bearing position (neutral vs. lateral), ankle lunge test (cm), straight leg raise angle, single-leg sit-to-stand time, toe grip strength, and hip abductor strength. Questionnaire collected height, weight, dominant foot, and fracture history. Statistical analysis: Wilcoxon signed-rank test (within-injury-group bilateral comparison), Mann-Whitney U test (injury vs. control groups), ROC curve for HIR cutoff, univariate and multivariate logistic regression adjusted for height, weight, and dominant foot. IRB approval: Juntendo University (M16-0167).

## Findings
18 of 232 players (7.8%) positive on ultrasound; 7 (3.0%) confirmed true fractures on X-ray; 11 had fracture history. Injury group: 16 limbs with fractures or fracture history; control group: 422 limbs. HIR was significantly lower in the injury group vs. controls (27.0° vs. 35.0°, p=0.02). Lateral weight-bearing during SHR trended higher in the injury group (75% vs. 53%, p=0.08). HIR <30° associated with Crude OR 3.74 (p=0.01), Adjusted OR 3.77 (p=0.01). Lateral weight-bearing alone: Crude OR 2.65 (p=0.096). Combined HIR <30° and lateral SHR weight-bearing: Crude OR 4.02 (p=0.007), Adjusted OR 3.87 (p=0.009). ROC AUC for HIR=0.68; cutoff 30° yielded sensitivity 62.5%, specificity 65.7%. SL-STS time was significantly longer on the injured vs. contralateral limb (p=0.008).

## Limitations
Small sample of confirmed fractures (n=16 injury limbs) may have reduced statistical power and contributed to the modest ROC AUC. Cross-sectional design from a single tournament prevents causal inference. Timing of physical examination varied (pre- vs. post-match), introducing possible fatigue effects. Ultrasound sensitivity is uncertain; negative findings assessed by only one examiner. Multiple PTs performed examinations, introducing potential inter-rater variability despite use of measurement instruments.

## Practical Implications
A simple two-test screen — active HIR range of motion in prone and weight-bearing position during a single heel raise — can identify youth football players at elevated Jones fracture risk. Players with HIR <30° and lateral SHR weight-bearing are approximately four times more likely to sustain a Jones fracture. Early screening followed by targeted hip flexibility work (e.g., caudal mobilisation with movement) and peroneal/intrinsic foot muscle strengthening may reduce fracture incidence. Early ultrasound detection of incomplete fractures allows treatment while maintaining training participation.

## Athlete Development Notes
Jones fractures are particularly prevalent among high school and college-aged football players and carry a high refracture rate (up to 67% with conservative management). Youth players should be screened for restricted HIR — potentially linked to cam morphology that becomes increasingly prevalent between ages 12–19 — and corrective flexibility and foot strengthening programmes should be implemented early. Playing on artificial turf and shoe design are additional modifiable extrinsic risk factors to address in youth football settings.

## Return to Play Notes
Surgical treatment for Jones fractures in athletes yields return-to-play times of 8–10 weeks with a 5.6% refracture rate, substantially better than conservative management (18.5% refracture rate). Preventing complete fracture progression through early detection (ultrasound/extracorporeal shock wave therapy while training continues) is preferable. Players cleared to return should demonstrate corrected lateral weight-bearing mechanics during heel raise and normalised HIR before full return to high-intensity football activities to minimise refracture risk.
