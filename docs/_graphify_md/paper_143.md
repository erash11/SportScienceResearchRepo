# Frank DJ, King MJ, Dennard CD, Macnamara BN. Discriminant Function Analysis Reveals Which Combination of Measures from the NFL Scouting Combine Predict NFL Performance. Journal of Expertise. 2023;6(2):130-163.

## TLDR
This is the largest and most methodologically rigorous analysis of NFL Scouting Combine predictive validity to date. The key insight for scouts and general managers: at most positions it is the right combination of physical traits—not any single measure—that predicts success. For defensive positions especially, DFA identified novel multivariate patterns that were missed by previous univariate approaches. Speed remains king at nearly every position, but big, fast edge rushers who can also broad jump (horizontal explosiveness) outperform those who are just fast or just big. For football staffs using Combine data in evaluation, this work provides a position-by-position roadmap of which trait combinations actually predict starter frequency and elite honors—versus which ones scouts simply appear to value during drafting.

## Abstract
This study applied discriminant function analysis (DFA) to 20 years of NFL Scouting Combine data (2000–2018) across 10 position groups to identify the best combination of physical attributes predicting draft status, starter frequency (games started, upper vs. lower quartile in first 5 years), and elite honors (multiple All-Pro/Pro Bowl selections). Using 8 Combine measures—including a novel arm-length-adjusted bench press work variable—the study found that combinations of traits predicted defensive success more consistently than offensive success, with 6 of 10 DFA models for defense finding multivariate predictors compared to 1 of 10 for offense. Speed (40-yard dash) emerged as a universal factor, while size variables (weight, height) and explosiveness measures (vertical, broad jump) contributed in position-specific combinations.

## Methods
Secondary data analysis; Combine participants from 2000–2018 sourced from pro-football-reference.com; 10 position groups analyzed: QB, RB, WR, TE, OL, DL, edge rushers, LB, CB, S; predictors: 40-yard dash, 20-yard shuttle, 3-cone drill, height, weight, vertical jump, broad jump, and bench press work (arm-length-adjusted reps × force × distance in foot-pounds); success metrics: draft status, games started quartile (upper vs. lower, first 5 years), and multiple career honors (≥2 All-Pro or Pro Bowl selections); stepwise discriminant function analysis (DFA); multivariate outliers removed via Mahalanobis distance (p<0.001); predictors Winsorized at ±2.99 SD; 74% data completeness threshold for predictor inclusion; alpha=0.05 two-tailed.

## Findings
DFA successfully predicted draft status for 8 of 10 position groups (5–18% of between-group variance explained). For offensive positions, single measures typically drove models: RB speed (40-yard dash) predicted draft status, starter frequency, and elite honors; WR speed + vertical jump predicted draft; OL vertical jump predicted starter frequency; OL bench press work predicted elite status. For defensive positions, multivariate combinations were more common: edge rushers—speed, vertical, and weight predicted draft; weight and broad jump predicted starter frequency; speed and weight predicted elite honors. DFA models for starters correctly classified 61–70% of players at positions where models were significant. No Combine measures significantly predicted QB elite honors or WR elite honors.

## Limitations
Combine data explained only 5–18% of variance in most significant models; college performance and competition level—known predictors—were excluded to allow uniform analysis. Missing data required dropping participants, especially for 3-cone and 20-yard shuttle. Pre-draft position classifications may not match NFL positions played. Outcome variables (games started, honors) are sensitive to team success and do not capture position-specific contributions like blocking grades. No cognitive or personality measures included despite their likely relevance to NFL success. Arm length estimates used for bench press work calculation are approximations.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football scouting and coaching staffs should use Combine data as a combination-based supplement to film evaluation, not as a single-metric filter. At skill positions, scouts appear to be mostly using the right variables, but edge rushers who are big and explosive in horizontal direction (broad jump) are undervalued relative to those who combine size with vertical explosiveness. Defensive tackle speed and linebacker weight are overlooked predictors of performance. Scouts should be cautious about over-weighting vertical jump for edge rushers and cornerback strength, as these did not predict performance after controlling for other variables.

## Athlete Development Notes
For NFL draft preparation and college player development, this study identifies which physical attribute combinations most reliably translate to professional success by position. Edge rushers should develop both speed and horizontal explosiveness (broad jump) not just vertical power. Linebackers benefit from combining speed with size. For offensive linemen, vertical jumping ability (lower body power) predicts starter frequency while upper body strength (bench press work) predicts elite performance—suggesting strength and power training should be balanced, not siloed, in OL development programs.

## Return to Play Notes
While not a RTP study, the Combine attribute profiles identified here have relevance for post-injury return decisions. Athletes returning from lower extremity injuries can be assessed against the position-specific athleticism benchmarks identified as performance-predictive—particularly broad jump (horizontal explosiveness) for edge rushers and vertical jump for offensive linemen and wide receivers—to determine whether they have regained the physical capabilities associated with successful NFL performance prior to full return.
