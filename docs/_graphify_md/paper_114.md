# Han Z, Sun P, Chen T, Dong Y. Club-based vs. hospital-guided rehabilitation after ACL reconstruction in football participants: a two-year prospective cohort study. Front Sports Act Living. 2025;7:1641750. doi: 10.3389/fspor.2025.1641750

## TLDR
Football players recovering from ACL reconstruction achieve meaningfully better outcomes with club-based sport-specific rehabilitation compared to standard hospital-based care. At two years, club-based athletes scored significantly higher on activity scales (Tegner: 8.5 vs. 7.2) and psychological readiness (ACL-RSI: 73.3 vs. 54.3), returned to recreational matches ~6 weeks earlier (12.4 vs. 18.3 weeks), and returned to official competition ~7.5 weeks earlier (19.0 vs. 26.5 weeks) — all without increasing re-injury risk. Competitive RTS rate was 90% vs. 65%. Football programs should integrate high-intensity sport-specific rehabilitation (agility, cutting, simulated contact) directly into post-ACL recovery protocols.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study compared two-year clinical outcomes of club-based versus hospital-guided rehabilitation in 40 football participants following primary ACL reconstruction. The club-based group (n=20) received intensive sport-specific training at a football base, while the hospital-guided group (n=20) underwent standardized function-oriented rehabilitation at a sports medicine center. Outcomes included subjective functional scores (Tegner, Marx, IKDC, Lysholm, ACL-RSI), objective knee function, return-to-sport timing, and re-injury rates over a two-year follow-up.

## Methods
Prospective cohort design; 40 football players and enthusiasts aged 18-40 who underwent primary ACL reconstruction at Huashan Hospital, Fudan University (2018-2021). Hospital group: standardized physical therapy at the Sports Medicine Center emphasizing ROM, strength, pain control. Club group: sport-specific program at Dalian Football Base (Coach Peng Sun) including agility, explosive power, change-of-direction maneuvers, and match simulation with twice-daily sessions. Assessment: preoperative baseline and 2-year postoperative follow-up. Instruments: Tegner Activity Score, Marx Activity Rating Scale, IKDC, Lysholm, ACL-RSI, joint effusion, Lachman/anterior drawer/lever tests, ROM. Return-to-sport timing recorded. Statistical analysis: independent t-tests or Mann-Whitney U for continuous variables; chi-square for categorical variables.

## Findings
Club-based group showed significantly higher Tegner score (8.5±1.4 vs. 7.2±1.0, p=0.006), Marx score (12.7±2.8 vs. 10.6±2.7, p=0.019), and ACL-RSI (73.3±21.4 vs. 54.3±18.7, p=0.003). No significant differences in IKDC (90.0 vs. 91.5) or Lysholm (93.2 vs. 90.2) scores. Competitive RTS rate: 90% (club) vs. 65% (hospital). Return to recreational match: 12.4 vs. 18.3 weeks (p=0.042); return to official match: 19.0 vs. 26.5 weeks (p=0.002). No significant differences in ROM, joint stability, joint effusion, or re-injury rate. One reinjury per group occurred.

## Limitations
Non-randomized prospective cohort with residual confounding despite baseline matching; professional players more prevalent in club group (55% vs. 30%, non-significant) potentially introducing motivation/fitness bias; both artificial ligament and autologous tendon grafts included in same analysis; club-based rehab requires substantially higher time, resource, and athlete commitment limiting accessibility; only two assessment time points (baseline and 2 years) with no intermediate follow-up; small sample size (n=20 per group).

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football programs should embed sport-specific rehabilitation within their own facilities alongside sports medicine oversight rather than relying exclusively on clinic-based care. Club-based rehab significantly accelerates return to competitive play and improves psychological readiness without increasing re-injury risk. Programs should include agility drills, explosive training, change-of-direction work, and simulated contact scenarios beginning in mid-phase rehabilitation (4-6 months post-surgery).

## Athlete Development Notes
Football athletes post-ACL reconstruction benefit from early integration of football-specific movement patterns — including 5-10-5 shuttles, slalom runs, targeted passing drills, and small-sided game simulations — within the rehabilitation timeline. This approach preserves and develops neuromuscular adaptations specific to football demands and accelerates return to full competitive readiness compared to generic clinical rehabilitation.

## Return to Play Notes
Club-based rehabilitation enables significantly faster return to play: recreational match participation at ~12 weeks and official competition at ~19 weeks post-ACL reconstruction versus 18 and 26.5 weeks with hospital-guided care. Competitive RTS rate at 2 years was 90% vs. 65%. ACL-RSI psychological readiness was markedly superior in the club group, a well-established predictor of successful RTS. Re-injury rates were equivalent (one per group), supporting the safety of the accelerated sport-specific approach.
