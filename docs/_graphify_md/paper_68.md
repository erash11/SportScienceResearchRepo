# Liu Z, Dudley JA, Diekfuss JA, Ahmed N, Edmondson AD, Cecil KM, Yuan W, Zuleger TM, Slutsky-Ganesh AB, Barber Foss KD, Myer GD, Fleischer CC. Associations Between Brain Metabolites Measured With MR Spectroscopy and Head Impacts in High School American Football Athletes. Journal of Magnetic Resonance Imaging. 2024.

## TLDR
Repeated subconcussive head impacts during a high school football season produce measurable neurochemical changes in the brain as detected by MRS, with tCho/tCr in the primary motor cortex showing a significant positive association with mean g-force of impacts in concussed players, suggesting that MRS metabolite changes—particularly involving membrane turnover markers—may be sensitive indicators of cumulative head impact burden even without diagnosed concussion.

## Abstract
This prospective controlled trial used proton MR spectroscopy (¹H MRS) to examine changes in brain metabolites—total N-acetylaspartate (tNAA), total choline (tCho), myo-inositol (myol), and glutamate+glutamine (Glx)—in the left primary motor cortex (M1) and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) of 215 male high school American football players (ages 13–18) from seven Cincinnati-area schools during the 2018 season. Participants were divided into a jugular vein compression (JVC) collar group (n=114) and noncollar group (n=101). Head impacts were tracked via accelerometers affixed to the mastoid process; MRS was acquired pre- and postseason, with an additional postconcussion scan for the 24 athletes who sustained a concussion. In non-concussed participants, significant decreases in tCho/tCr in ACC and increases in Glx/tCr in ACC were observed from pre- to postseason. The relationship between ΔtCho/tCr and mean g-force of impacts differed significantly between participants with and without concussion at g-force thresholds above 80 g, and use of a JVC collar was associated with distinct myol/tCr trends in the ACC at higher g-force thresholds.

## Methods
Prospective controlled trial; n=284 recruited, 215 included in final analysis (mean age 15.9±1.0 years); seven greater Cincinnati area high schools; 2018 competitive season (August–November); JVC collar group (n=114) wore Q-collar (Q30 Innovations) during all practices and games; head impact tracking via CSx System accelerometer affixed to left mastoid; impacts >10 g triggered 45 ms recording windows; MRS acquired on 3 Tesla Philips MR scanners pre- and postseason with postconcussion scan where applicable; ¹H point resolved spectroscopy (PRESS) in M1 and ACC; metabolites: tNAA, tCho, myol, Glx normalized to tCr; MATLAB LCModel v6.3-I for spectral fitting; Wilcoxon signed-rank, Friedman's, and Mann–Whitney U tests for group comparisons; univariate linear regressions for metabolite–g-force relationships; FDR-corrected P-values; significance set at P≤0.05.

## Findings
• In non-concussed participants: significant decrease in tCho/tCr in ACC (pre: 0.233±1.40×10⁻³; post: 0.227±1.47×10⁻⁷; FDR-corrected) and increase in Glx/tCr in ACC from pre- to postseason
• Relationship between ΔtCho/tCr and mean g-force of impacts differed significantly between concussed and non-concussed participants at g-force thresholds >80 g to >140 g in both M1 and ACC
• In concussed athletes: positive associations between ΔtCho/tCr in M1 and mean g-force >100 g (β=3.6×10⁻³; P=9.3×10⁻³) and >110 g (β=2.9×10⁻³; P=3.3×10⁻²)
• In the ACC, ΔtCho/tCr positively associated with mean g-force at thresholds >80 g to >140 g in concussed participants
• JVC collar associated with negative associations between ΔmyoI/tCr in ACC and mean g-force >110 g (β=−1.1×10⁻³; P=3.9×10⁻²) and >120 g (β=−1.1×10⁻³; P=2.0×10⁻²)
• No significant tNAA/tCr changes as a function of time, concussion, or collar use
• Median impacts >20 g per player: 296±20; 24 participants sustained a diagnosed concussion

## Limitations
Male-only sample from a single metropolitan area; relatively small concussion subgroup (n=24) with limited statistical power for concussion-specific analyses; accelerometer affixed to mastoid rather than helmet—may not precisely reflect head kinematics; single MRS voxel per region limits spatial coverage; metabolite changes are indirect markers of neurological change; no long-term follow-up beyond the competitive season; JVC collar assignment was not fully randomized across all teams.

## Practical Implications
MRS-based brain metabolite markers—particularly tCho/tCr as an index of membrane turnover—show promise as objective, noninvasive biomarkers of cumulative head impact burden in adolescent athletes. The finding that neurochemical changes occur even without diagnosed concussion underscores the importance of subconcussive impact monitoring. The JVC collar's association with attenuated myoI/tCr changes at high g-force thresholds suggests potential neuroprotective benefit warranting further investigation.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American high school football. Even without a diagnosed concussion, high school football players sustain neurochemical brain changes over a season proportional to the mean g-force of their head impacts. This finding reinforces the need for practice structure modifications that reduce high-magnitude head impacts (>80–110 g) to minimize cumulative neurobiological burden during adolescent development, a period of heightened brain vulnerability.

## Return to Play Notes
The neurochemical differences observed between concussed and non-concussed athletes in the relationship between tCho/tCr and head impact magnitude suggest that MRS biomarkers may eventually inform RTP decision-making by providing objective evidence of neurochemical recovery. Clinicians should be aware that postconcussion neurochemical trajectories differ from those of subconcussive impact accumulation, and that high-magnitude head impacts post-RTP may carry distinct neurobiological risk.
