# Weber AE, Bolia IK, Korber S, Mayfield CK, Lindsay A, Rosen J, McMannes S, Romano R, Tibone JE, Gamradt SC. Five-Year Surveillance of Vitamin D Levels in NCAA Division I Football Players: Risk Factors for Failed Supplementation. Orthop J Sports Med. 2021;9(1):2325967120975100.

## TLDR
Nearly half of NCAA Division I football players begin each season with insufficient vitamin D levels, but a structured annual screening and supplementation program can cut that prevalence by more than half over five years. Black athletes have consistently lower vitamin D levels and higher rates of supplementation failure. Vitamin D3 alone is significantly more effective than combined D3/D2 therapy for restoring sufficient levels. Annual testing at the start of preseason, with D3-only supplementation for those found insufficient, is the evidence-supported approach for this population.

## Abstract
This study reports results of a 5-year vitamin D surveillance program in 272 NCAA Division I football players at USC between 2012 and 2017. Serum 25(OH)D levels were measured annually at the start of each season; athletes with insufficiency (<32 ng/mL) received vitamin D3 alone (50,000 IU/week) or combined D3/D2. The prevalence of vitamin D insufficiency decreased significantly from 55.5% in 2012–2013 to 30.7% in 2016–2017 (P=.033), and mean 25(OH)D rose from 36.3 to 40.5 ng/mL (P<.001). Black athletes had the lowest mean vitamin D levels; quarterbacks had the highest. Among athletes with insufficiency who completed 12 months of supplementation, vitamin D3 alone had a significantly higher conversion rate (48.1%) than combined D3/D2 (22.2%; P=.034).

## Methods
Prospective cohort study; Level of evidence 3. Serum 25(OH)D was measured annually at the start of each season (June–August) from 2012 to 2017 in all athletes on the USC football roster (n=272 total analyzed). Insufficiency was defined as <32 ng/mL. Insufficient athletes received vitamin D3 (50,000 IU/week) or combined D3 (2,000 IU/day) + D2 (50,000 IU/week) per the treating physician's preference. Program efficacy was assessed by comparing insufficiency prevalence in the first two vs. last two years. Subgroup analysis of athletes with complete 12-month supplementation data evaluated factors associated with failed supplementation (failure to convert to ≥32 ng/mL) using Fisher exact and chi-square tests for categorical variables including age, BMI, race, position, and supplement type.

## Findings
Prevalence of vitamin D insufficiency decreased from 55.5% (2012–2013) to 30.7% (2016–2017; P=.033). Mean 25(OH)D rose from 36.3 ng/mL (2012) to 40.5 ng/mL (2017; P<.001), though the increase was nonlinear. By the final year, returning athletes had 16.2% insufficiency vs. 55.6% among incoming athletes (P<.001). White/non-Hispanic athletes had the highest mean 25(OH)D (43.47 ng/mL); Black athletes had the lowest (31.02 ng/mL; P<.001). Quarterbacks had the highest positional mean (47.48 ng/mL); running backs had the lowest (30.7 ng/mL; P<.001). In the supplementation subgroup (n=82), conversion success: D3 alone 48.1% vs. D3/D2 combined 22.2% (P=.034). No significant differences in age, BMI, race, or position between successful and failed converters.

## Limitations
Athlete compliance with supplementation relied on nutritionist administration but could not be fully guaranteed. Two supplementation regimens were used based on physician preference rather than randomization, introducing selection bias. Sun exposure at time of testing was not recorded, which could significantly affect baseline levels. Players who left the team after initial testing were not tracked. Prior supplementation history was not recorded. Subgroup analysis for failed supplementation was limited to athletes with complete data (n=82), reducing statistical power for race and position subanalyses. No clinical outcomes (injury rates, performance) were measured.

## Practical Implications
Football programs should implement annual preseason vitamin D screening for all athletes. Vitamin D3 monotherapy (50,000 IU/week) should be the preferred supplementation protocol over combined D3/D2 regimens based on superior conversion rates. Black athletes warrant close monitoring and potentially more aggressive supplementation given consistently lower baseline levels and higher rates of supplementation failure. Incoming freshmen and transfers are at particularly high risk of insufficiency and should be prioritized for early testing and treatment. Compliance infrastructure (e.g., nutritionist-administered supplementation) improves program effectiveness.

## Athlete Development Notes
Vitamin D insufficiency is highly prevalent in NCAA Division I football players, particularly among Black athletes, running backs, and incoming athletes. Given associations between vitamin D insufficiency and stress fractures, muscle strains, hormonal imbalance, and immune function, maintaining adequate levels is an important component of athlete health management. Programs should build annual screening and supplementation into standard preseason medical workflows, recognizing that new athletes arriving each year will reset the insufficiency prevalence without ongoing surveillance.

## Return to Play Notes
Low vitamin D levels have been associated in other studies with lower extremity muscle strains and core muscle injuries in NFL players. While this study did not measure injury outcomes directly, optimizing vitamin D status through supplementation may contribute to injury prevention and support recovery. Athletes presenting with recurrent lower extremity or core muscle injuries should have vitamin D status assessed as part of their workup. Ensuring sufficient 25(OH)D (≥32 ng/mL) before return to full training is a reasonable clinical consideration.
