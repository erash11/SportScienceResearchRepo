# Muench JR, Sinson GP, Glaeser B, Shah AS, Rowson S, Duma S, Mihalik J, Brooks A, Cameron K, Giza C, Goldman J, McGinty G, Riggen LD, Pasquina PF, Broglio SP, McAllister T, McCrea MA, Harezlak J, Stemper BD. Change in Head Impact Exposure Following Return-to-Sport in Concussed Football Athletes. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise. 2025. doi: 10.1249/MSS.0000000000003833.

## TLDR
Most concussed college football athletes naturally reduce their head impact exposure upon returning to sport within the same season — 75% immediately reduced impacts and 40% never returned to pre-injury exposure levels — with longer recovery periods associated with larger post-RTS impact reductions. These findings suggest a natural conservative shift in playing behavior after concussion that may offer some neurological protection during the vulnerable post-injury window.

## Abstract
This study examined how head impact exposure (HIE) changed during return-to-sport (RTS) following sport-related concussion in 52 concussed NCAA Division I college football athletes from six programs (2015–2019 seasons) using the helmet-based Head Impact Telemetry (HIT) System. Daily volume of head impacts after concussion was compared to pre-injury levels and to team- and position-matched non-concussed controls. Seventy-five percent of concussed athletes reduced HIE in their immediate RTS period compared to pre-injury levels, and over 40% never reached their pre-injury HIE at any point during the remainder of the concussion season. Concussed athletes had significantly lower HIE post-RTS compared to matched controls, and longer recovery duration was associated with larger reductions in HIE after RTS.

## Methods
Subset of NCAA-DoD CARE Consortium data from six NCAA Division I FBS programs (Air Force Academy, West Point, UCLA, UNC Chapel Hill, University of Wisconsin, Virginia Tech). Fifty-two concussed college football athletes with recorded contact days both before and after concussion within the same season were included; only first concussion analyzed. Each athlete equipped with helmet-based HIT System measuring head accelerations (>10 g threshold). Head impact data collected across all practice, scrimmage, and competition activities (2015–2019 seasons). Three time periods analyzed: Pre-Injury, First 7 Days Post-RTS, and >7 Days Post-RTS. Daily HIE compared to pre-injury baseline and to team- and position-matched non-concussed controls. Regression analysis used to examine association between recovery duration and change in HIE. Preseason and regular season segments analyzed separately.

## Findings
• 75% of concussed athletes reduced HIE in their immediate RTS period compared to pre-injury levels.
• Over 40% of concussed athletes did not reach their pre-injury HIE level at any point during the remainder of the concussion season.
• Concussed athletes had significantly lower HIE post-RTS compared to team- and position-matched non-concussed controls over the same time period.
• Longer post-concussion recovery times were significantly associated with larger reductions in HIE after RTS.
• The reduction in HIE was most pronounced in the immediate days following RTS (First 7 Days Post-RTS window).
• Findings are consistent with the broader shift toward more conservative post-concussion management seen across multiple sports and playing levels.

## Limitations
Study limited to NCAA Division I FBS programs, reducing generalizability to non-elite or youth populations. Only athletes with recorded contact days both before and after concussion within the same season included, which may exclude more severely concussed athletes. HIE measured only via HIT System accelerometers (linear acceleration); rotational components not fully captured. Psychological factors (fear of reinjury, playing style changes) were not directly measured. Only first concussion per athlete included, limiting examination of repeat concussion effects.

## Practical Implications
Concussed athletes naturally reduce head impact exposure upon RTS, which aligns with conservative post-concussion management guidelines and may contribute to neurological recovery. Medical staff should be aware that while most athletes self-reduce their impact exposure post-RTS, over 40% still do not return to pre-injury baseline — a finding that may warrant ongoing HIE monitoring even after clinical clearance. Longer recovery periods appear to reinforce more conservative playing behavior, suggesting that appropriate recovery time has biomechanical as well as clinical benefits.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American college and professional football. The finding that athletes naturally reduce head impact exposure after RTS suggests a behavioral adaptation that may reduce re-concussion risk during the vulnerable post-injury window. Athlete development staff and sports scientists should track HIE in the immediate post-RTS period, particularly in the first 7 days, to identify athletes who are not reducing impact exposure as expected and may need additional guidance or load modification.

## Return to Play Notes
Directly relevant to RTP protocols. The study provides objective biomechanical data showing that most concussed football athletes reduce HIE upon RTS within the same season, supporting the neurobiological rationale for gradual RTS protocols. The association between longer recovery and greater HIE reduction post-RTS suggests that premature RTP may not only increase re-injury risk clinically but also biomechanically (less HIE reduction). Monitoring head impact exposure in the first 7 days post-RTS may serve as a useful objective metric to complement standard neurocognitive and symptom-based RTP criteria.
