# Romero-Morales C, Berzosa-Rojo A, Di Luca-Calabrese D, Vázquez-González S, Abuín-Porras V, Jaén-Crespo G, García-Sanz F, Pareja-Galeano H. Comparative analysis of patellar tendon, achilles tendon and plantar fascia structure in indoor and outdoor football players: a novel cross-sectional pilot study. Sci Rep. 2024;14:3930. doi: 10.1038/s41598-024-54403-3

## TLDR
Outdoor soccer and indoor futsal players show virtually identical patellar tendon, Achilles tendon, and plantar fascia thickness by ultrasound, suggesting the load differences between these two football modalities are not sufficient to drive tendon structural adaptation. The only significant difference was a reduction in left Hoffa's fat pad thickness in outdoor vs. indoor players (1.28 vs. 1.38 cm, p=0.026). For performance and medical staff, tendon thickness alone is not a reliable marker of differential training load between playing surfaces. Clinical evaluation combining symptoms, functional testing, and imaging together is more informative than structural tendon imaging in isolation.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional pilot study used ultrasound imaging (USI) to compare patellar tendon (PT), Achilles tendon (AT), plantar fascia (PF), and Hoffa's fat pad thickness between 15 outdoor soccer players and 15 indoor soccer (futsal) players aged 18-30. All players trained three sessions per week plus one match and had at least 8 years of registered playing experience. The study aimed to determine whether differences in playing surface, field size, and movement demands between outdoor and indoor football modalities produce measurable tendon structural adaptations detectable via USI.

## Methods
Cross-sectional pilot study (March-May 2023) at Universidad Europea de Madrid, following STROBE criteria. 30 registered male football players aged 18-30: outdoor soccer (n=15) and indoor futsal (n=15). Both groups completed 3 training sessions/week (90 min each) plus 1 match/weekend; all had ≥8 years playing experience. USI performed by a single trained therapist using GE LogiQ P7 (4-14 MHz linear transducer, L612 RS). Measurements: PT longitudinal and transverse thickness (30° knee flexion), Hoffa's fat pad thickness, AT longitudinal and transverse thickness (4 cm from calcaneus, prone), PF thickness (medial calcaneal tubercle, prone) — all bilateral. Statistics: Shapiro-Wilk, Student t-test or Mann-Whitney U, Levene's test, stepwise linear regression. SPSS v22.0.

## Findings
No significant differences in PT-L (p=0.639 R, p=0.795 L), PT-T (p=0.847 R, p=0.629 L), AT-L (p=0.699 R, p=0.560 L), AT-T (p=0.979 R, p=0.653 L), or PF (p=0.929 R, p=0.926 L) between outdoor and indoor groups. PT, AT, and PF showed only 0.01 cm mean difference between groups. Left Hoffa's fat pad was significantly lower in outdoor vs. indoor players (1.28±0.13 vs. 1.38±0.10 cm; p=0.026); right side was non-significant (1.34±0.11 vs. 1.36±0.06 cm; p=0.545). Stepwise linear regression showed left Hoffa's fat pad difference was significant (p=0.048; R²=0.137); dominant leg did not predict the difference. Groups differed in age (outdoor 22.5±1.2 vs. indoor 24.3±2.4 years; p=0.021) but not height, weight, or BMI.

## Limitations
Small pilot sample (n=15 per group) limiting statistical power; only healthy participants (no tendinopathy cases); dynamic USI not performed; left vs. right sides compared rather than dominant vs. non-dominant limb; exact years of sport practice not recorded; minor competition calendar differences between groups; age differed significantly between groups (potential confounder); Doppler and elastography not included; results should be interpreted with caution pending larger studies.

## Practical Implications
Ultrasound-based tendon thickness is not a useful tool for differentiating training load exposure between football players across surface types. Hoffa's fat pad monitoring via USI may have value as an adjunct knee health screening tool given its sensitivity to load differences. Football medical staff should rely on comprehensive clinical evaluation — combining symptoms, functional testing, and imaging — rather than structural tendon thickness alone when assessing lower extremity injury risk or making load management decisions.

## Athlete Development Notes
The absence of tendon structural differences between outdoor and indoor football players suggests that standard football training volumes across both modalities do not generate sufficient load to drive PT, AT, or PF structural adaptation. Targeted high-load training interventions (e.g., heavy slow resistance training, plyometric progressions) above and beyond normal match and session volumes are likely necessary to produce tendon structural changes that could confer injury protection in football athletes.

## Return to Play Notes
This study does not directly address return-to-play. However, the finding that Hoffa's fat pad was the only structure showing between-group differences is clinically relevant: infrapatellar fat pad pathology can cause anterior knee pain and restrict return to activity. USI assessment of Hoffa's fat pad should be incorporated into anterior knee pain evaluations in football athletes as part of the RTP process, complementing clinical examination and functional testing.
