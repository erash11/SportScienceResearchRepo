# DeLang MD, Ishøi L, Thornton K, Thorborg K. High previous-week training load amplifies hip adduction squeeze strength reductions in male academy football players with performance-limiting groin pain. Phys Ther Sport. 2026;78:101902. DOI: 10.1016/j.ptsp.2026.101902

## TLDR
When a football player has groin pain, higher training loads make their hip adduction squeeze strength measurably worse. Pain-free players, by contrast, tolerate high training loads with no meaningful change in adduction strength. This finding means that monitoring training load in the context of serial groin strength screening provides additive information — a player showing strength deficits who also had a high-load week is at compounded risk and may need load modification as part of their management.

## Abstract
This longitudinal observational study examined whether preceding seven-day football training load and previous seven-day groin pain influence hip adduction squeeze strength in 19 male academy football players (age 16.5±0.9 years) from a West African under-18 academy. Players were monitored twice weekly over seven weeks with serial hip adduction squeeze strength testing (GroinMate device), subjective groin pain reporting, and GPS-derived training load. Linear mixed-effects models stratified by groin pain presence showed that training load significantly influenced squeeze strength only when groin pain was present (estimate −0.13 N/kg, 95% CI [−0.20, −0.07], p<0.001), while pain-free players showed no training load effect (estimate −0.01, p=0.634).

## Methods
Longitudinal observational study with twice-weekly repeated measures over 7 weeks in-season (summer 2023). 19 male academy players (U-18, West Africa). Monitoring protocol (Monday and Thursday, prior to training): 1) Verbal subjective groin pain NRS (0–10) for the preceding 7 days, operationalized as 'performance-limiting'; 2) Hip adduction squeeze strength via GroinMate (CCAthletics, Denmark) in long-lever Copenhagen 5-second squeeze test position, one maximal isometric trial, normalized to body mass (N/kg); 3) GPS training load (Catapult) compiled as 7-day cumulative summaries — variables: total distance, high-speed running distance (>21 km/h), sprint distance (>25 km/h), accelerations + decelerations (>2.5 m/s²), duration. PCA reduced multicollinear GPS variables into a single composite load score (PC1: 81% variance, eigenvalue 4.06). Linear mixed-effects models (lme4, R 4.2.3) with training load and random player intercepts, stratified by pain presence.

## Findings
17 of 19 players reported groin pain during the study; 119 of 266 observations (44.7%) were pain-associated. No time-loss groin injuries occurred. In the pain group: training load estimate = −0.13 N/kg (95% CI [−0.20, −0.07], p<0.001) per 1 SD increase in training load. In the no-pain group: estimate = −0.01 N/kg (95% CI [−0.06, 0.03], p=0.634). Pain-free players maintained adduction strength regardless of training load. The interaction between pain and training load was positive, supporting model stratification.

## Limitations
Small sample (n=19) from a single elite West African academy limits generalizability. Detection bias possible due to structured monitoring capturing subthreshold pain. No time-loss injuries captured during the study period. Training load not stratified by playing position. Individual cut-off points or predictive algorithms for at-risk players not yet feasible. Age range of 15–18 introduces maturational variability not accounted for in the analysis. No consideration of preceding 24-h fatigue on monitoring days.

## Practical Implications
Serial monitoring of hip adduction squeeze strength combined with training load data provides richer early-detection information than either measure alone. When a player reports groin pain, practitioners should consider modifying training load as a primary intervention — not just exercise prescription — to break the cycle of strength degradation. A combined pain-load flag in a monitoring system could prioritize early intervention. Reducing football exposure is a legitimate and evidence-based pain modulation strategy.

## Athlete Development Notes
Supports integrating GPS-based load monitoring with serial groin strength screening in academy-level football programs. Players with groin pain experiencing high training loads are at compounded risk of further strength deficits and eventual time-loss injury. Academy programs should build load modification protocols specifically for non-time-loss groin pain cases to prevent escalation.

## Return to Play Notes
Directly informs the management of players returning from or managing groin pain in active training environments. Elevated training load amplifies strength deficits in symptomatic players, suggesting that load reduction — not just rehabilitation exercise — should be a component of RTP protocols for groin conditions. A player's training load history in the week preceding an RTP test should be considered when interpreting adduction strength scores.
