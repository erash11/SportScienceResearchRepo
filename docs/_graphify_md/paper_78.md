# Murray A, Fullagar HHK, Delaney JA, Sampson J. Bradford Factor and seasonal injury risk in Division I-A collegiate American footballers. Sci Med Football. 2018;2(3):173–176.

## TLDR
A high Bradford Factor (>7) during preseason — reflecting frequent short absences — is associated with elevated in-season injury risk in NCAA Division I-A football players, suggesting BF could be a practical 'flagging' tool for at-risk athletes alongside traditional training load monitoring.

## Abstract
This retrospective study investigated whether preseason training participation percentage and the Bradford Factor (BF) — a human resources absenteeism index calculated as (number of absences)² × total days of absence — could predict in-season injury risk in NCAA Division I-A American collegiate football. Training load (via GPS/IMU player load) and injury data from two consecutive seasons were collected for 70 players. A total of 184 injuries were recorded, with 106 resulting in time loss. A 1 SD increase in accumulated training minutes over 7 days increased injury likelihood in the following week by 35%. A high preseason BF (>7) was associated with increased in-season injury risk compared to athletes with a low preseason BF, while preseason completion rate itself was not associated with a substantial reduction in in-season injury risk.

## Methods
Retrospective design using two consecutive NCAA Division I-A football seasons (Season 1 n=44, Season 2 n=48; 22 athletes participated in both); 70 players total; training load monitored via inertial measurement units (Optimeye S5; Catapult Innovations) — PlayerLoad™ calculated per session; injury surveillance conducted by certified athletic trainers and licensed medical staff; BF calculated as rolling total from season start; multilevel logistic regression with random effects fitted for each variable; effect sizes calculated as ORs per 1 SD increase in training load; R's lme4 package used for analysis; P < 0.05 for significance.

## Findings
• 184 total injuries across two seasons; 106 resulted in time loss (15.6 ± 5.4 time-loss injuries per 1000 h)
• ~32% of injuries occurred during preseason (25% of the season)
• A 1 SD increase in accumulated training minutes over 7 days increased injury likelihood by 35% in the following week (OR = 1.35; 95% CI 1.03–1.78)
• Preseason completion rate was not significantly associated with time-loss injury risk (OR = 0.98; 95% CI 0.86–1.11)
• High preseason BF (>7) was associated with increased in-season injury risk vs low BF
• Mean preseason BF differed meaningfully between groups: Low <85% completion = 23.7, Med 85–91% = 6.6, High >91% = 3.7
• In-season mean BF: Low = 84.1, Med = 79.1, High = 26.7
• Athletes completing >91% of preseason sessions had the lowest BF and lowest injury risk profile

## Limitations
Retrospective design; single team from one institution; relatively small sample size; external validity to other programs uncertain; BF is a blunt instrument that does not distinguish injury type or cause; NCAA preseason structural restrictions (limited length and session number) may limit generalizability.

## Practical Implications
The Bradford Factor offers a simple, objective, and practically meaningful measure to monitor training consistency and flag at-risk athletes — analogous to session RPE in its ease of use. A preseason BF > 7 should prompt practitioners to investigate the reason for absences and consider individualized load management. BF is best used alongside other load monitoring metrics, not in isolation.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly studied in NCAA Division I-A American collegiate football players. The first two weeks of preseason — which carry the highest training loads — represent the greatest injury risk window. Ensuring high preseason participation and minimizing short-term absences (which drive BF upward) is a modifiable factor that coaching and sports medicine staff can address to optimize player availability throughout the season.

## Return to Play Notes
The Bradford Factor's tracking of absence frequency is relevant to RTP in that athletes who accumulate repeated short training absences (including those related to minor injury management) during preseason carry elevated in-season injury risk. RTP decisions should consider re-integration volume and the athlete's accumulated absence pattern, not just clinical clearance status.
