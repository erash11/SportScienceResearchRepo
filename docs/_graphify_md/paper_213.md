# Wiig H, Raastad T, Luteberget LS, Ims I, Spencer M. External Load Variables Affect Recovery Markers up to 72 h After Semiprofessional Football Matches. Frontiers in Physiology. 2019;10:689.

## TLDR
High-intensity running (HIR) was the strongest predictor of muscle damage indicators (CK and myoglobin) up to 72 hours post-match, while PlayerLoad™ and total distance best predicted sprint performance recovery. CMJ recovery could not be reliably predicted by any external load variable. Despite substantial mean effects of external load on recovery markers, individual player recovery could not be predicted due to large inter-individual variability. Practitioners should monitor multiple external load variables together rather than relying on a single metric.

## Abstract
This study investigated how external load variables derived from player tracking devices affected recovery markers up to 72 hours after football matches in 75 semiprofessional male players from six Norwegian second division teams. Three experimental matches were played in an indoor stadium. External load variables — playing duration, total distance, PlayerLoad™, high-intensity running (HIR, >19.8 km/h), and high-intensity events (HIE, accelerations/decelerations/COD >2.5 m/s²) — were collected via combined IMU/LPS tracking. Recovery markers included creatine kinase (CK), myoglobin (MYO), countermovement jump (CMJ), 30-m sprint (SP30), and Yo-Yo Intermittent Recovery Level 1 (YOYO) at 1, 24, 48, and 72 h post-match. Effects were modeled as the difference between a typical high-load and a typical low-load match (2 SDs of external load).

## Methods
Cross-sectional within-subject design with 75 outfield male semiprofessional players (mean age 20.4±4.6 yr, mass 72.7±7.2 kg) from six Norwegian second division teams. Three experimental matches (FIFA-standard 90 min indoor, 15±1°C) played 14–23 days after the season. Players wore Catapult OptimEye S5 (IMU) and ClearSky T5 (LPS) tracking devices. Blood samples drawn at −1, 1, 24, 48, and 72 h for CK and MYO. CMJ (force platform), SP30 (timing gates), and YOYO assessed at corresponding time points (n=24–59 per test). Log-transformed recovery markers analyzed via linear mixed model (SAS MIXED procedure) with effects rescaled to 2 SDs of each external load variable. Effects interpreted using magnitude-based inferences with 90% CIs.

## Findings
Matches induced most likely substantial increases in CK (ES=0.92–1.20 at 1–24 h), myoglobin (ES=3.80 at 1 h), and CMJ decreases (ES=−0.75 at 1 h) persisting through 72 h. HIR had the strongest, most consistent effect on CK (ES=0.60–1.08, very likely to most likely substantial, all time points). Duration, total distance, and HIE had likely substantial effects on CK at 1, 24, and 72 h. All five variables had likely substantial effects on MYO at 1 h. CMJ was largely unpredicted by external load (trivial or unclear across variables). PlayerLoad™ and total distance showed likely substantial negative effects on SP30 at 72 h (ES=0.46–0.56). HIE and PlayerLoad™ showed likely substantially positive effects on YOYO at 72 h (ES=0.47–0.56), possibly reflecting a fitness-conditioning interaction. Wide individual prediction intervals prevented reliable individual player recovery prediction from external load variables alone.

## Limitations
One match per player design limited modeling to between-player effects; within-player analysis with multiple matches would reduce uncertainty. Matches were nonofficial and played 2–3 weeks post-season with reduced training load, potentially underrepresenting typical in-season recovery demands. Control of physical activity, nutrition, and sleep between measurements was limited to pre-study instructions. Study may have been underpowered at later time points due to participant dropout and inherent measurement unreliability.

## Practical Implications
HIR distance is the most important external load variable for monitoring post-match muscle damage recovery (CK, MYO); practitioners should ensure this metric is captured alongside total distance. PlayerLoad™ and total distance are better indicators of sprint performance recovery. Tracking multiple external load variables simultaneously provides a more complete picture of recovery status than any single metric. Despite significant group-level effects, external load alone cannot reliably predict when individual players will be recovered by 72 h — direct wellness or performance testing remains essential for individual load management decisions.

## Athlete Development Notes
For player development in football, this study confirms that high-intensity running is the primary driver of post-match muscle damage, providing a physiological rationale for progressive exposure to high-speed running in developing players. Understanding the dose-response relationship between match load variables and recovery kinetics helps coaches structure training loads during the 72-hour post-match period to manage cumulative fatigue across a competitive season.

## Return to Play Notes
In return-to-play contexts following match-related muscle damage or fatigue, high-intensity running distance (HIR) is the most predictive external load metric for CK and myoglobin elevation and should be carefully managed in early reintegration phases. Sprint performance (30-m sprint) remains impaired at 72 h post-match, particularly in high-total-distance and high-PlayerLoad™ matches, meaning athletes in the 3-day post-match window may not have fully recovered sprint capacity. Individual variability in recovery is substantial, underscoring the need for direct performance or wellness testing rather than time-only RTP criteria.
