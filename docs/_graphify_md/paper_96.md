# Goldschmied N, Ratkovich T, Raphaeli M. Brief report: Exploring the icing the kicker strategy in the NFL. Journal of Applied Sport Psychology. 2024. doi:10.1080/10413200.2024.2437166

## TLDR
Opposing coaches in the NFL should use timeouts to ice the kicker in game-deciding field goal situations: data from 25 seasons (1999–2024) confirm that iced kickers convert at a meaningfully lower rate (74.1%) than non-iced kickers (79.8%), and this effect holds regardless of home/away, indoor/outdoor, or regular season vs. playoffs. Kick distance remains the strongest predictor of success. The psychological mechanism likely involves disruption of pre-performance routines and negative rumination rather than simple physical fatigue. Kicker development programs should simulate icing conditions in practice — including unexpected timeout calls at the moment of pre-kick routine initiation — to build resilience under this specific stressor.

## Abstract
This archival study examined the effectiveness of the 'icing the kicker' strategy across 25 NFL seasons (1999–2024) using a generalized estimating equation (GEE) model applied to 1,290 pressure field goal attempts (within the final 2 minutes, score within 3 points, including overtime). Icing — a timeout called by the opposing coach immediately before a decisive field goal attempt — was found to significantly reduce kicking success (74.1% when iced vs. 79.8% otherwise). A secondary analysis of 106 nullified kicks requiring a re-kick did not find a statistically significant deterioration on the second attempt, failing to support the 'twice-taxed' hypothesis for why icing works.

## Methods
Archival data from 25 NFL seasons (1999–2024) obtained via the nflverse GitHub repository. Pressure kicks were defined as field goal attempts within 2 minutes of the end of regulation when the kicking team trailed by 3 or fewer points (or was tied), plus all overtime attempts (n=1,290 after excluding 42 blocked kicks; 935 games, 128 unique kickers). A generalized estimating equation (GEE) model with binary logistic regression accounted for non-independence of repeated observations per kicker. Predictor variables included timeout condition (iced/none/own team), kick distance, arena (indoor/outdoor), season type (regular/playoff), and location (home/away). A separate chi-square analysis of 106 nullified re-kicks tested the 'twice-taxed' hypothesis.

## Findings
End-of-game pressure kick success rate was significantly lower than non-pressure situations (77.76% vs. 84.32%; t(24)=6.425, p<0.001; d=1.285, large effect). Non-pressure kicking improved over time (r=0.82, p<0.001) but pressure kicking showed no improvement trend across 25 seasons (r=−0.013, p=0.95). GEE Model 1 showed icing significantly predicted reduced scoring (B=0.162, p=0.021); Model 2 treating icing as dichotomous also showed a significant negative effect (B=−0.274, p=0.049). Kick distance was the only other significant predictor (B=0.102, p<0.001). Home/away, indoor/outdoor, and regular/playoff distinctions were all non-significant. Secondary nullified-kick analysis: 78.3% success on second attempts vs. 84.32% baseline; non-significant (χ²=2.52, p=0.112, small effect).

## Limitations
Archival design limits causal inference about psychological mechanisms. Blocked kicks were excluded (42 total), attributing failure to the kicker rather than teammates. The nullified-kick subsample was relatively small (n=106), occurred at varying distances, and was drawn from non-critical game situations rather than true pressure contexts, limiting the validity of the 'twice-taxed' test. Self-report and observational data on pre-performance routines were unavailable.

## Practical Implications
NFL coaches should preserve and use timeouts to ice the kicker in game-deciding field goal situations — 25 seasons of data confirm iced kickers convert at a meaningfully lower rate (74.1% vs. 79.8%). Special teams coaches should incorporate simulated icing conditions in practice, including unexpected timeout stoppages at the moment of pre-kick routine initiation, to build psychological resilience. Kickers may benefit from quiet eye technique, robust pre-performance routines, and systematic exposure to anxiety and self-focus conditions in training to counter the disruptive effect of icing.

## Athlete Development Notes
Kicker development programs at the college and NFL level should include systematic exposure to icing conditions — simulating opponent-called timeouts during critical practice moments — to build tolerance and maintain consistent pre-performance routines under pressure. The persistent failure of end-of-game kicking to improve over 25 seasons (unlike non-pressure kicking) indicates a specific, unaddressed performance gap under stress that sport psychology integration can target.

## Return to Play Notes
Not directly applicable to return-to-play protocols. However, the broader finding that well-practiced motor skills degrade under unexpected psychological disruption is relevant to athletes returning from injury, who may be particularly vulnerable to performance anxiety disrupting automated movement patterns. Structured mental performance work addressing pre-performance routine robustness under unexpected conditions may benefit athletes in the late-stage RTP process.
