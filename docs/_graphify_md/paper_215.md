# Dalen-Lorentsen T, Ranvik A, Bjørneboe J, Clarsen B, Andersen TE. Facilitators and barriers for implementation of a load management intervention in football. BMJ Open Sport Exerc Med. 2021;7:e001046. doi:10.1136/bmjsem-2021-001046

## TLDR
Even when players and coaches believe load management can prevent injuries and enhance performance, real-world compliance remains a challenge. The most important facilitators are scientific evidence of effectiveness, positive mutual attitudes between coaches and players, and time-efficient protocols. Key barriers include time constraints and the lack of convincing proof that load management works in football. Practitioners implementing load management programs should focus on brief, intuitive tools; build coach and player buy-in simultaneously; and communicate both injury prevention and performance benefits to maximize adoption.

## Abstract
This study investigated the barriers and facilitators to implementing a load management intervention among elite youth football players and coaches in Norway. Conducted as a survey appended to a cluster-randomized controlled trial (RCT) involving 25 teams and 482 players, 250 players (51%) and 17 coaches (68%) completed the questionnaire. The survey was structured around the RE-AIM (Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation, Maintenance) framework. Key facilitators identified by both players and coaches included scientific evidence of injury prevention benefit and performance enhancement, and a mutually positive attitude between players and coaches toward the intervention. Time efficiency was identified as a critical practical requirement. Despite belief in the value of load management, players showed reluctance to invest more than 10–20 minutes per week unless performance benefits were demonstrated. All coaches valued load management for both prevention and performance but were constrained by time demands. These findings contextualize why a well-designed load management RCT (using the Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio) failed to reduce health problems — compliance was 63% — and provide guidance for future implementation.

## Methods
Cross-sectional survey conducted in November 2018 following a cluster-RCT of elite Norwegian youth footballers (both sexes). Questionnaires were based on the RE-AIM framework and adapted from prior implementation studies in handball and football. Four questionnaire versions were developed (intervention/control × player/coach). Online distribution via Briteback software; non-respondents received reminders. 23 of 25 teams participated; 250 players and 17 coaches returned usable responses. Descriptive statistics used for analysis; no between-group statistical comparisons were made.

## Findings
88% of players rated scientific evidence of performance improvement as a key facilitator; 84% rated injury/illness mitigation as important; 86% rated positive coach attitude as important. 100% of coaches rated scientific evidence of prevention effectiveness as most important; 88% rated player motivation as important. 93% of intervention-group players spent >10 min/week on load monitoring. 63% of coaches used the protocol as intended (minimum 1 hour/week). 45% of players and 50% of coaches planned to continue load management the following season. Only 28% of players were willing to spend >10 min/week on load management if only injury prevention was the benefit, but 70% were willing if performance enhancement was also a goal. Coaches and players both perceived footballers as being at high risk of injury and overuse injury, and nearly all believed load management could reduce injury.

## Limitations
Survey-based methodology limits depth of insight; a qualitative approach would have added nuance. Terms such as 'scientific evidence' and 'performance' may have been interpreted differently across respondents. Low response rate (51% of players) introduces selection bias — those who responded were likely more positively disposed to the intervention. Low number of coaches (17) limits robustness of coach-specific findings. Questionnaire did not fully capture the maintenance dimension of RE-AIM. Compliance in the intervention group was suboptimal, suggesting questionnaire fatigue from daily load logging.

## Practical Implications
Load management programs in team football environments must be designed with two non-negotiable properties: they must be time-efficient (under 10 minutes per session for players) and they must be framed around both performance enhancement and injury prevention to maximize player motivation. Coach buy-in is essential — coaches who are enthusiastic create a spiral of positive compliance among players. Clubs and federations should mandate or formally endorse load monitoring programs to increase institutional-level adoption. Intuitive digital tools with minimal data entry friction will improve real-world adherence.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athlete development environments, this study highlights that young elite players will engage with load monitoring when they perceive direct performance benefits — not merely injury prevention. Development programs should embed load tracking into athlete education, position it as a performance optimization tool, and ensure coaches are trained advocates. Establishing load management habits early in an athlete's career improves long-term health outcomes and professional development trajectory.

## Return to Play Notes
While not an RTP-specific study, the findings are directly relevant to return-to-play load management protocols. Players returning from injury are precisely the subgroup for whom load management is most critical. The barriers identified here — time burden, skepticism about effectiveness, and lack of coach engagement — are equally applicable in rehabilitation settings. RTP programs using load monitoring apps or wearable-based ACWR tracking will achieve higher compliance if the data is shared transparently with both the athlete and the coaching/medical team and if the monitoring is framed as readiness optimization rather than restriction.
