# Messer DJ, Williams MD, Bourne MN, Opar DA, Timmins RG, Shield AJ. Anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction increases the risk of hamstring strain injury across football codes in Australia. Sports Medicine. 2021;doi:10.1007/s40279-021-01567-x.

## TLDR
In male football athletes across three codes, a prior ACL reconstruction nearly doubles the risk of future hamstring strain injury, and high eccentric knee flexor strength may be partially protective — reinforcing the importance of hamstring strengthening programs after ACLR.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study of 531 male athletes from Australian Football League (AFL), elite soccer (A-League), and rugby examined whether prior ACL reconstruction (ACLR) and recent hamstring strain injury (HSI) predict future HSI risk and eccentric knee flexor strength deficits. Athletes underwent preseason Nordic hamstring eccentric knee flexor strength testing and had injury histories collected. A history of ACLR was associated with 2.2 times greater odds of subsequent HSI, and a recent HSI (within 12 months) was associated with 3.1 times greater odds. Only athletes with combined ACLR and recent HSI history showed significantly weaker eccentric knee flexor strength and greater between-limb imbalances compared to uninjured athletes.

## Methods
Secondary analysis of data from three previously published prospective cohort studies (2013–2015) involving 531 male athletes: 206 AFL players, 147 elite A-League soccer players, and 178 rugby players. Preseason eccentric knee flexor strength was measured bilaterally using the Nordic hamstring exercise (NHE) with custom load cells. Injury history (lifetime ACLR, HSI in prior 12 months) was documented. The primary outcome was prospective HSI during the subsequent competitive season. Univariate and multivariable logistic regression and decision tree induction (DTI) assessed risk factors. ANCOVA compared eccentric strength and between-limb imbalance across injury history groups.

## Findings
• 74 of 531 athletes sustained at least one prospective HSI
• Univariate: prior ACLR OR=2.2 (95% CI 1.1–4.4; p=0.029); prior HSI OR=3.1 (95% CI 1.8–5.4; p<0.001)
• Multivariable: prior HSI remained the only significant predictor (OR=2.9; 95% CI 1.3–5.1; p<0.001); ACLR not significant when eccentric strength controlled (OR=1.8; p=0.130)
• Only athletes with combined ACLR + recent HSI showed significantly weaker eccentric strength and greater between-limb imbalance vs. uninjured
• Decision tree: recent HSI (G²=15.2) and prior ACLR (G²=14.8) were top predictors; eccentric strength contributed protective effect (G²=4.8) for ACLR athletes
• No athletes with scaled Nordic strength >33 N·kg⁻⁰·⁵⁴ and prior ACLR + recent HSI sustained a prospective HSI (n=16)
• Graft type information was unavailable for all athletes

## Limitations
Graft type for ACLR was unknown for most athletes, limiting analysis of semitendinosus vs. patellar tendon graft effects on outcomes. The relatively small number of athletes with prior ACLR (n=53) limited power to detect small-to-moderate effect sizes. Previous hamstring injury history was based on athlete recall for only the prior 12 months, potentially misclassifying older injuries. Decision tree model could not be validated with a hold-out dataset. Results may not generalize to non-elite or female athletes.

## Practical Implications
ACLR should be recognized as an independent risk factor for future hamstring strain injury, even after return to full sport. Post-ACLR rehabilitation programs should include targeted progressive eccentric hamstring strengthening (e.g., Nordic hamstring curls) as a secondary prevention measure. Athletes with both a prior ACLR and a recent HSI represent the highest-risk group and warrant heightened monitoring and strength-oriented rehabilitation.

## Athlete Development Notes
NOTE: This study was conducted in Australian football (AFL), elite soccer (A-League), and rugby — not American football. However, the findings are broadly applicable to American football athletes who have undergone ACLR. The increased hamstring strain risk post-ACLR is relevant across football codes and should inform post-ACLR return-to-sport programming, including eccentric hamstring load monitoring and preseason strength benchmarks.

## Return to Play Notes
ACLR significantly increases the risk of hamstring strain injury in the subsequent competitive season (OR 2.2 univariate), which can complicate and extend the overall RTP timeline. Athletes returning from ACLR who also have a recent HSI are at the highest risk and require more comprehensive RTP criteria. High eccentric knee flexor strength may reduce this risk and should be a target criterion before ACLR athletes are fully cleared for unrestricted participation.
