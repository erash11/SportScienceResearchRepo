# Esteve E, Rathleff MS, Hölmich P, Casals M, Clausen MB, Vicens-Bordas J, Pizzari T, Thorborg K. Groin problems from pre- to in-season: a prospective study on 386 male Spanish footballers. Res Sports Med. 2020. doi:10.1080/15438627.2020.1860044

## TLDR
Groin problems are nearly twice as prevalent during football pre-season compared to the in-season, primarily because of a higher rate of non-time-loss groin pain. The spike in groin problems coincided with the transition from off-season to structured football training, emphasising that rapid load increases at the start of pre-season are a key risk period. Preventive measures — particularly hip adductor strengthening programmes — should be implemented proactively before and during pre-season rather than waiting for in-season injury surveillance to identify affected players.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study investigated weekly prevalence of groin problems over a 3-week football pre-season compared to a 39-week competitive in-season in 17 amateur male Spanish football teams (386 players, tiers IV–VI). Players completed weekly groin-pain surveys and the HAGOS Sport/Recreation subscale; football staff registered time-loss groin injuries and team exposure. The average weekly prevalence of groin problems was 1.8 times higher (95% CI 1.6–2.0) during pre-season (21%) than in-season (12%). The elevated pre-season prevalence was driven by a higher rate of groin problems without time loss (19% vs. 10%; PR=1.8). Time-loss groin injury incidence and injury burden did not differ significantly between periods. HAGOS Sport/Rec scores were slightly but significantly lower pre-season among players reporting groin problems without time loss.

## Methods
Prospective cohort study extending previously published in-season data (Esteve et al., 2020) with a 3-week pre-season monitoring period. A convenience sample of 17 amateur Spanish football teams (tiers IV–VI; 386 players) completed weekly online surveys asking about groin pain in the preceding week and the HAGOS Sport/Recreation subscale. Football staff registered time-loss groin injuries and team exposure in hours. Weekly groin problem prevalence was calculated for players with and without time loss. Prevalence ratios (PR) and prevalence differences (PD), incidence rate ratios (IRR), and linear mixed models for HAGOS scores were computed. Alpha level set at 5%.

## Findings
Average weekly prevalence of all groin problems was 21% pre-season vs. 12% in-season (PR=1.8; 95% CI 1.6–2.0; PD=9.0%). Groin problems without time loss were substantially higher pre-season (19% vs. 10%; PR=1.8; 95% CI 1.6–2.1). Time-loss groin injury prevalence (2.0% vs. 1.3%; PR=1.5; 95% CI 1.0–2.4) and time-loss injury incidence (1.2 vs. 0.9 per 1000 h) and burden were not significantly different. The highest weekly prevalence (28%) occurred in week 1 of pre-season. HAGOS Sport/Rec scores were marginally but significantly lower in players with non-time-loss groin problems during pre-season (mean difference: 3.1 points; 95% CI 0.3–6.0; P=0.03).

## Limitations
Team-level exposure registration led to slight underestimation of individual injury rates. Survey response rate during pre-season (75%) was lower than the in-season rate (93%). Only 3 weeks of pre-season data were captured despite teams having varying pre-season lengths (4–5 weeks). Findings may not generalise to extended pre-season periods or higher professional football levels. No controlled prevention strategies were initiated, so natural prevalence changes cannot be attributed to specific interventions.

## Practical Implications
Pre-season groin pain should be considered a major injury management concern, not only in-season. Teams should conduct brief pre-season groin pain screening (two simple questions about past-season groin pain and its duration) to detect at-risk players early. Implementation of hip adductor strengthening programmes (e.g., the Copenhagen Adductor Programme) before and during pre-season is recommended to reduce the prevalence of groin problems through the subsequent in-season. Gradual progression of training loads from off-season to pre-season is critical.

## Athlete Development Notes
Off-season should be used as an opportunity to build hip adductor capacity and address residual groin problems before pre-season loads resume. Players presenting with groin symptoms at the start of pre-season should be flagged using HAGOS questionnaires or the five-second squeeze test and managed proactively with adductor loading protocols to prevent escalation into time-loss injuries during the competitive season.

## Return to Play Notes
Players returning from groin or adductor injuries should be monitored closely during the high-load pre-season transition period, as this phase carries the highest weekly prevalence of groin problems. Specific return-to-training load thresholds and monitoring tools — including hip adductor strength tests (>15% reduction alert) and HAGOS scores (<70/100) — should be used to guide safe progression back to full training and match participation.
