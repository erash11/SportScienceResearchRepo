# Aiello F, Di Claudio C, Fanchini M, Impellizzeri FM, McCall A, Sharp C, Brown SJ. Do non-contact injuries occur during high-speed running in elite football? Preliminary results from a new GPS and video-based method. Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport. 2023. 10.1016/j.jsams.2023.07.007

## TLDR
This study provides the first GPS-plus-video method to quantify the exact running speed when non-contact injuries occur in elite soccer. The key finding is that nearly all hamstring injuries happened while players ran faster than 25 km/h — above 80% of their individual maximal speed. This means practitioners should not limit high-speed running exposure as an injury prevention strategy; instead, they should systematically prepare players to tolerate and decelerate from maximal sprint velocities. The method itself is a practical template for any club with GPS and video infrastructure to conduct standardised injury circumstance analysis.

## Abstract
This retrospective descriptive study evaluated the feasibility of combining GPS and video data to estimate the speed and acceleration at the time of non-contact injuries in elite football players. Data from 46 male elite Serie A players over three seasons were analysed, with training and matches video-recorded and external load measured via Catapult Vector S7 GPS. A total of 34 non-contact injuries were analysed using the Football Injury Inciting Circumstances Classification System (FIICCS), with inciting circumstances coded for speed, acceleration, and activity type.

## Methods
Retrospective descriptive study. 46 male elite players (mean age 26.6 ± 4.3 years, height 185 ± 6.5 cm, weight 80.8 ± 6.3 kg) from one Serie A football club over three seasons. External load collected via Catapult Vector S7 GPS (10 Hz). Training and matches video-recorded via fixed AXIS P1448-LE camera system and TV broadcasts. Injuries diagnosed by team physician. Inciting circumstances classified using FIICCS. Speed and average acceleration computed manually from GPS data for short-duration activities. Descriptive statistics reported as median and IQR.

## Findings
34 non-contact injuries were analysed (34 of 68 total had complete data). Of 17 hamstring injuries, 16 occurred during running with a median peak speed of 29.28 km/h (IQR: 26.61–31.13 km/h), corresponding to 87.55% of maximal speed (IQR: 78.5–89.75%). All 17 hamstring running injuries occurred at peak speeds >25 km/h. Players were accelerating <2 m/s² in 8 cases and >2 m/s² in 3 cases (median acceleration 1.74 m/s²); decelerating >2 m/s² in 4 cases and <2 m/s² in 1 case. Both quadriceps injuries occurred during kicking. Adductor injuries occurred during acceleration, deceleration, and kicking. Most injuries (82%) occurred during matches; 97% occurred in players who had played from the start of the session.

## Limitations
Retrospective design prevented systematic player interviews immediately post-injury, requiring assumption that injuries occurred at peak speed. Acceleration data for short activity periods had to be calculated manually (Δv/Δt) as the software could not extract it automatically. 37% (30/80) of originally reported non-contact injuries could not be analysed due to missing GPS or video data. Reliability of the FIICCS classification system was not formally evaluated. Data from a single club only, limiting generalisability.

## Practical Implications
Limiting players' high-speed running exposure is unlikely to reduce hamstring injury risk since most injuries occur at speeds >25 km/h and above 80% of individual maximal speed. NFL and college football staff should ensure players regularly experience and are conditioned for maximal sprint speeds and high-speed deceleration in training. Combining GPS and video to classify injury inciting circumstances is feasible and provides actionable data for designing injury prevention and rehabilitation protocols. Training programmes should prepare players for decelerating from sprint speeds, not just accelerating to them.

## Athlete Development Notes
Football players at all development levels should be progressively exposed to high-speed running (>25 km/h) and sprint-to-deceleration sequences in training. The finding that 97% of injuries occurred in players who started the session suggests fatigue management and late-game conditioning are important athlete development targets. Periodised speed development that includes maximal-velocity work and high-intensity deceleration loading is indicated.

## Return to Play Notes
Return-to-play progressions after hamstring, adductor, or quadriceps injury should include running exposures that reach and exceed 80% of the athlete's individual maximal speed before full clearance, as this is the zone in which most non-contact injuries occur. Video-GPS analysis of the original injury inciting circumstance can guide task-specific RTP criteria. Deceleration capacity from sprint speed should be specifically tested before return to full training.
