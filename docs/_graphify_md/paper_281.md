# Rowell, A. E., Aughey, R. J., Hopkins, W. G., Stewart, A. M., & Cormack, S. J. (2017). Identification of sensitive measures of recovery after external load from football match play. International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, 12(7), 969–976.

## TLDR
FT:CT — the ratio of flight time to contraction time during a countermovement jump — is the most sensitive CMJ variable for monitoring recovery after football match play, outperforming jump height by detecting neuromuscular fatigue that persists beyond what jump height reveals. Players accumulating more than 500 PlayerLoad units show meaningfully reduced CMJ performance for at least 42 hours post-match, suggesting practitioners should wait at least 42 hours before prescribing intensive training. Cortisol spikes 55–165% immediately post-match across all load levels, but the hormonal response shows substantial individual variability, limiting the utility of routine salivary hormone monitoring as a team-level recovery tool.

## Abstract
This study investigated the sensitivity of countermovement-jump (CMJ) performance measures and salivary hormones (testosterone and cortisol) to levels of accelerometer-derived PlayerLoad during elite A League football match play. Eighteen elite male football players were measured before (27 h, 1 h) and after (0.5, 18, 42, 66, 90 h) three consecutive preseason matches. Match load was divided into tertiles: low (<500 au), medium (500–1000 au), and high (>1000 au). CMJ variables and hormonal concentrations were analyzed using a linear mixed model; sensitivity was quantified via t-statistics and magnitude-based inferences. The ratio of flight time to contraction time (FT:CT) was identified as more sensitive to match load than jump height alone, with reductions persisting to later post-match time points.

## Methods
Eighteen elite male A League football players (age 23.3±4.1 y) from one club were tested across three consecutive preseason matches. CMJ was performed on a force plate (Fitness Technology) with hands on hips; variables included jump height, FT:CT, peak velocity, peak/mean power, peak force, and contraction time. Saliva samples (2 mL passive drool, frozen at −80°C) were analyzed for cortisol (μg/dL) and testosterone (pg/mL) via ELISA. Accelerometer-derived PlayerLoad (100-Hz) quantified match load divided into three tertiles. Data analyzed with SAS linear mixed model (Proc Mixed); changes expressed as % ± 90% CI and standardized effects (ES). Nonclinical magnitude-based inference framework applied.

## Findings
Jump height was reduced in medium load at 0.5 h (10%±7%) and 18 h (7%±4%) post-match, and in high load at 0.5 h (16%±8%) and 18 h (9%±5%). FT:CT showed a 12%±7% reduction in high load at 0.5 h post, with reductions in medium and high load at 18 h, and FT:CT reductions persisting to 42 and 66 h post in some groups. Jump height changes were unclear at 42 h across all loads. Cortisol increased 55–165% across all load tertiles at 0.5 h post with substantial individual variability thereafter. Testosterone increased 17–20% across all loads at 0.5 h. T:C ratio was reduced (large/very-large effects) at 0.5 h post for all loads, suggesting an acute catabolic state, with unclear changes at 18 h and small-to-moderate increases at 42 h.

## Limitations
Small sample (n=18) from a single club limits generalizability. Testing was conducted during the preseason, which may not reflect in-season physiological state. Arbitrary division of PlayerLoad into tertiles may not fully discriminate individual responses; players within the same tertile could differ by ~500 au. Activity profile (speed distribution, number of sprints vs. continuous low-intensity work) within a given PlayerLoad was not controlled. Circadian variation in cortisol and testosterone could not be fully eliminated due to match timing constraints. Low-load group trained at 18 h post-match, potentially confounding recovery measures at later time points.

## Practical Implications
FT:CT from a CMJ is the most useful metric for monitoring recovery status after football match play and should be assessed for at least 42 hours post-match to guide training prescription. Players accumulating PlayerLoad >500 au show impaired neuromuscular function for at least 42 hours, indicating that high-intensity training or heavy loading sessions should be avoided within this window. Routine salivary hormone monitoring at the team level has limited utility due to high individual variability and the cost of analysis; individualized monitoring is recommended instead. Staff can use simple CMJ testing as a daily readiness screen in the days following competition.

## Athlete Development Notes
For developing football players, CMJ-based monitoring — particularly FT:CT — provides an objective, cost-effective method to assess neuromuscular readiness after games and demanding practices. Training loads in the 42 hours following high-load match play should be reduced to allow recovery. Teaching young athletes and support staff to interpret FT:CT trends relative to individual baselines (not population norms) builds a readiness-monitoring culture that supports long-term health and performance development.

## Return to Play Notes
The study establishes that neuromuscular function, as measured by FT:CT, is impaired for at least 42 hours after high-load match play. In RTP contexts, monitoring CMJ FT:CT before and during return-to-participation progressions provides an objective indicator that neuromuscular readiness has been restored. Athletes should demonstrate FT:CT values returning toward individual baseline before being cleared for full-contact practice or match participation. The dose-response relationship between PlayerLoad and FT:CT supports using objective load tracking as part of any RTP decision framework.
