# Renaghan E, Wittels HL, Feigenbaum LA, Wishon MJ, Chong S, Wittels ED, Hendricks S, Hecocks D, Bellamy K, Girardi J, Lee S, Vo T, McDonald SM, Wittels SH. Exercise Cardiac Load and Autonomic Nervous System Recovery during In-Season Training: The Impact on Speed Deterioration in American Football Athletes. Journal of Functional Morphology and Kinesiology. 2023;8:134.

## TLDR
This study shows that as cumulative training cardiac load increases over a college football season, maximum running speed progressively declines—a sign of deteriorating autonomic nervous system recovery. Higher resting heart rate and lower HRV the morning after practice are strong indicators of reduced sprint performance. Coaches should use cardiac load monitoring to avoid overloading athletes during the in-season and preserve sprint speed for competition.

## Abstract
This 13-week prospective study examined how in-season exercise cardiac load (ECL) relates to autonomic nervous system (ANS) recovery and maximum running speed in 30 Division I collegiate football starters. Athletes wore armband monitors (Warfighter Monitor) capturing ECG and inertial movement data. ECL (average HR x session duration) was computed for each training session, with next-day baseline heart rate (HR) and heart rate variability (HRV: SDNN and rMSSD) representing ANS recovery, and maximum running speed representing ANS function. Linear regression models revealed significant negative associations between both acute and cumulative ECL and maximum running speed (acute: β=−0.11, p<0.0001; cumulative: β=−0.15, p<0.0001). Higher baseline HR was negatively associated with speed (β=−0.45, p=0.001), while higher HRV (SDNN and rMSSD) was positively associated with speed. These findings suggest that excessive cardiac load during in-season training degrades sprint performance via ANS deterioration.

## Methods
Prospective 13-week observational study in 30 Division I collegiate football starters (heavy-running positions: cornerback, running back, tight end, wide receiver) at a southeastern US university. Athletes wore Warfighter Monitor (WFM) armbands throughout 128 training sessions. ECL = average HR (≥85 bpm) x session duration (minutes). Baseline HR and HRV (SDNN, rMSSD) measured during 5-minute morning seated rest. Maximum running speed derived from 9-DOF IMU accelerometer/gyroscope data. Linear regression models used for all associations; normality assessed via Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. α=0.05.

## Findings
Acute ECL was negatively associated with maximum running speed (β=−0.11±0.00, R²=0.64, p<0.0001). Cumulative ECL was also negatively associated with speed (β=−0.15±0.04, R²=0.73, p<0.0001). Higher baseline HR was negatively associated with speed (β=−0.45±0.12, R²=0.56, p=0.001). SDNN was positively associated with speed (β=0.32±0.09, R²=0.55, p=0.029). rMSSD was positively associated with speed (β=0.35±0.11, R²=0.52, p=0.015). Mean maximum running speed was 17.3±1.4 mph. Mean daily ECL was 21,800±4,600 total heartbeats; weekly cumulative ECL was 108,700±22,800 heartbeats.

## Limitations
Single D1 university limits generalizability. Metrics were not collected during regulation games, preventing full quantification of weekly physiological load. Small sample size precluded position-level subgroup analyses. Sleep quality, ergogenic aids, and psychological stress were not controlled. Conflict of interest exists as several authors are paid employees of the company that manufactured the monitoring device.

## Practical Implications
Strength and conditioning coaches should use cardiac load (HR x duration) monitoring to individualize in-season training volumes and identify early signs of ANS deterioration. When cumulative ECL is high, training volume and intensity should be reduced to preserve maximum running speed. Morning resting HR and HRV provide actionable daily readiness indicators. Failure to reduce load after games risks progressive speed deterioration that may also increase soft-tissue injury risk.

## Athlete Development Notes
Tracking ECL, baseline HR, and HRV across an entire season provides a longitudinal view of how physiological load affects sprint performance development. Young college athletes in skill positions are particularly sensitive to cumulative cardiac load. Integrating wearable monitoring to detect ANS deterioration early can inform individualized periodization decisions throughout the developmental career.

## Return to Play Notes
ANS recovery metrics (baseline HR and HRV) are sensitive indicators of an athlete's readiness to perform at maximum sprint speed. Following injury or illness-related absence, ECL and HRV monitoring during the return-to-play process could help determine appropriate training load progression. Athletes with elevated resting HR and suppressed HRV should not be exposed to high-intensity sprint work until ANS function has normalized.
