# Aso T, Tamura M, Kaji N, Akiyama S, Maeda T, Takahashi Y, Furuyama S, Furuya K, Sambe T, Nishinaka N. Exploratory analysis of relationship between trunk and pelvic motion and shoulder motion during throwing in American football quarterbacks. JSES International. 2026. doi:10.1016/j.jseint.2026.101630.

## TLDR
In American football QBs, how the trunk and pelvis rotate at stride foot contact directly influences shoulder position at the critical late cocking phase of the throw. QBs who rotate their trunk more toward the non-throwing side at SFC show greater shoulder horizontal abduction at MER—a position associated with increased shoulder joint load. Conversely, greater hip-shoulder separation promotes a more optimal shoulder position. These findings suggest that improving trunk and pelvic rotation mechanics could reduce throwing-related shoulder and elbow injury risk in QBs.

## Abstract
This exploratory study examined the kinematic relationship between trunk and pelvic motion at stride foot contact (SFC) and shoulder motion at maximum external rotation (MER) during throwing in 11 American football quarterbacks (QBs). Throws were recorded at 240 Hz using an iPad with PitchAI markerless motion capture software. Trunk flexion/extension, trunk rotation, pelvic rotation, and hip-shoulder separation at SFC were correlated with shoulder horizontal adduction/abduction, abduction/adduction, and external/internal rotation at MER. Trunk rotation (rp=0.779, p=0.005), pelvic rotation (rs=0.691, p=0.019), and hip-shoulder separation (rp=0.667, p=0.025) at SFC were positively correlated with shoulder horizontal adduction at MER. Trunk (rp=−0.635, p=0.036) and pelvic (rs=−0.682, p=0.021) rotation at SFC were negatively correlated with shoulder abduction at MER. Controlling trunk and pelvic rotation during QB throwing may assist in optimizing shoulder mechanics and reducing injury risk.

## Methods
Eleven male American football QBs (age 25.1±6.3 yr; mass 81.9±9.3 kg; height 179.1±5.3 cm; all right-hand dominant) from a university team or Japan American Football League Xcellence club team participated. Each threw five balls 30 yards at maximum effort. Throwing motion recorded at 240 Hz (iPad rear camera, 1080p) using PitchAI (3Motion AI Inc.) markerless motion capture. Trunk flexion/extension, trunk rotation, pelvic rotation, and hip-shoulder separation angles calculated at SFC. Shoulder horizontal adduction, abduction, and external rotation angles calculated at MER. Correlations computed using Pearson's (rp) or Spearman's (rs) correlation coefficients as appropriate. Statistical significance set at p<0.05.

## Findings
Trunk rotation at SFC was positively correlated with shoulder horizontal adduction at MER (rp=0.779, p=0.005) and negatively correlated with shoulder abduction at MER (rp=−0.635, p=0.036). Pelvic rotation at SFC was positively correlated with shoulder horizontal adduction at MER (rs=0.691, p=0.019) and negatively correlated with shoulder abduction at MER (rs=−0.682, p=0.021). Hip-shoulder separation at SFC was positively correlated with shoulder horizontal adduction at MER (rp=0.667, p=0.025). No significant correlations were found for trunk flexion or shoulder external rotation at MER. Mean shoulder abduction at MER was 98.9±4.7° and external rotation was 105.2±18.3°.

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=11, below the calculated requirement of n=29) limits statistical power and generalizability. PitchAI has not been validated for QB throwing specifically; shoulder joint angles may exhibit errors exceeding 10 degrees compared to marker-based systems. All throws were performed in non-game conditions, limiting applicability to actual game scenarios with defensive pressure. Kinetic data (joint forces and torques) were not available through PitchAI. Multiple correlation tests increase the risk of Type I errors. All participants were Japanese, limiting generalizability to other populations.

## Practical Implications
QB throwing mechanics assessments should include trunk and pelvic rotation analysis at stride foot contact, as these proximal kinematics influence downstream shoulder position at the vulnerable late cocking phase. Coaches and sports medicine staff can use affordable video-based tools like PitchAI to screen throwing mechanics. Training interventions that promote greater hip-shoulder separation and controlled trunk rotation toward the throwing side at SFC may reduce the risk of shoulder horizontal abduction-related injuries.

## Athlete Development Notes
For developing QBs, teaching proper sequencing of trunk and pelvic rotation during the throwing motion is foundational to both performance and shoulder health. Programs should emphasize rotational core strength, hip mobility, and hip-shoulder dissociation exercises. Markerless motion capture tools offer a scalable, low-cost method for coaches and trainers to assess and track throwing mechanics longitudinally in non-clinical settings.

## Return to Play Notes
QBs returning to throwing after shoulder or elbow injury should have their trunk and pelvic mechanics assessed, not just their arm motion. Aberrant trunk rotation patterns at stride foot contact—particularly excessive rotation toward the non-throwing side—may increase shoulder horizontal abduction load at MER and contribute to re-injury risk. RTP criteria should include biomechanical thresholds for proximal kinematic variables alongside traditional clinical milestones.
