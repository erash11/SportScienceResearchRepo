# Hollabaugh W, Hill T, Davidson C, Pennings J, Strasser N, Porras L, Cox C, Fitch R. Application of the British Athletics Muscle Injury Classification in Collegiate Football Athletes: A Retrospective, Observational Study. Sports Health. 2025;XX(X).

## TLDR
BAMIC classification did not significantly predict time to return to play or reinjury rate in college football athletes, suggesting its prognostic value in this population remains limited and warrants larger studies.

## Abstract
This retrospective cohort study evaluated the clinical utility of the British Athletics Muscle Injury Classification (BAMIC) in NCAA Division I college football athletes who sustained hamstring (HS) or quadriceps (QD) muscle tears during the 2023 season. Twenty-one athletes with 34 total injuries underwent MRI within 7 days of injury and were classified by BAMIC. The median time to return to play (TRTP) was 26 days (IQR 17.8–33.0), and the reinjury rate (RIR) was 19.0% with 75% of reinjuries occurring before full RTP. There was no statistically significant relationship between BAMIC grade and TRTP or RIR, though small effect sizes were observed.

## Methods
Retrospective observational cohort study of 21 NCAA-DI American football players who sustained HS or QD tears during the 2023 season. All underwent MRI within 7 days and were classified using BAMIC (grades 0a–4c). TRTP was calculated from injury date to full unrestricted return. RIR was tracked through rehab and 3 months post-RTP. Statistical analysis used Kruskal-Wallis H and Mann-Whitney U tests; separate linear regressions assessed TRTP by grade and injury site controlling for age and muscle group.

## Findings
• Most injuries (60%) were BAMIC grades 2a–3b; 89.3% occurred before the season and all during practice
• Median TRTP was 26 days (IQR 17.8–33.0), approximately 4 weeks of missed play
• No statistically significant relationship between BAMIC and TRTP (H=0.9, p=0.99, effect size=0.055)
• No significant relationship between BAMIC and RIR due to low reinjury numbers (n=4)
• Grade 0 injuries trended toward shorter TRTP; site 'c' (intratendinous) injuries trended toward longer TRTP
• 75% of reinjuries occurred before full RTP and were all grade 2 injuries

## Limitations
Small sample size from a single institution limits statistical power; insufficient injuries in each BAMIC category to establish definitive conclusions; team medical staff were not blinded to BAMIC; rehabilitation was not standardized to BAMIC; only 1 grade 0 injury and no grade 4 injuries in the cohort.

## Practical Implications
BAMIC grading alone should not drive RTP or rehabilitation decisions for college football athletes. Clinicians should use BAMIC as one informational tool alongside functional testing, GPS load monitoring, and clinical markers. The ~4-week missed-play average regardless of BAMIC grade is useful for game planning. Prevention programs are critical given the high preseason and practice incidence.

## Athlete Development Notes
Applicable directly to American football. Hamstring and quadriceps tears are among the most common injuries in college and NFL football. The ~4-week absence estimate is relevant for roster and depth-chart management. Identifying that most tears occur in practice and preseason supports load management strategies during high-volume training blocks.

## Return to Play Notes
Median TRTP was 26 days across all BAMIC grades, translating to approximately 3–4 missed games. BAMIC grade did not significantly predict individual RTP timelines. RTP decisions should incorporate bilateral strength symmetry (within 10% knee flexion strength), range of motion, pain resolution, and GPS load progression rather than relying on imaging classification alone. One in five athletes sustained a reinjury, with 75% of reinjuries occurring before full RTP.
