# Connolly JG, Nathanson JT, Sobotka S, Haider S, Gometz A, Lovell M, Choudhri T. Effect of Playing and Training at Altitude on Concussion Incidence in Professional Football. Orthop J Sports Med. 2018;6(12):2325967118794928. DOI: 10.1177/2325967118794928

## TLDR
This is a duplicate file of ID 174 with a different filename casing. NFL teams that both train and play at altitude above 644 feet have significantly fewer concussions (28% reduction vs. league average). The protection requires chronic altitude adaptation—not acute exposure—since low-altitude teams visiting high-altitude venues gained no benefit. High-altitude teams playing away at low-altitude venues had the highest concussion rates, suggesting protection disappears rapidly upon descent. Away teams had more concussions than home teams regardless of altitude.

## Abstract
NOTE: This entry (ID 175) is the same paper as ID 174 — identical content, authors, journal, and DOI. The two files differ only in filename casing and spacing (ID 174 uses lowercase with a double space before 'altitude'; ID 175 uses title case with single spaces). This descriptive epidemiology study examined whether the protective effects of altitude on NFL concussion incidence are due to acute exposure or chronic adaptations. Data from 2012–2015 NFL seasons showed teams that trained AND played at high altitude (≥644 ft) had a 28% lower concussion rate than the league average.

## Methods
Descriptive epidemiology study using publicly available data from Frontline's Concussion Watch covering 2012–2015 NFL regular seasons (weeks 1–16). A total of 1920 team exposures across 960 games were analyzed. Teams were categorized into four altitude groups based on training and game venue elevation relative to a 644-foot threshold. Stadium elevations were approximated via web-based tools. Statistics included Spearman rho, Wilcoxon signed rank, unpaired t-tests, and one-way and two-way ANOVA.

## Findings
Total of 569 concussions across 1920 team exposures (overall rate 0.29/exposure). Away teams had significantly higher concussion rates than home teams (0.32 vs. 0.27/exposure; P=.03). High-altitude teams playing at high altitude had a significantly lower concussion rate (0.20/exposure) compared with low-low (0.31; P<.05), high-low (0.36; P<.05), and overall (P<.05). Low-high teams (0.32/exposure) showed no significant difference from other groups. No dose-response relationship between stadium altitude and concussion rate was found (R²=0.005).

## Limitations
Use of the binary 644-foot threshold may oversimplify a physiological spectrum. Altitudes studied are below the 2000-foot threshold where most documented physiological adaptations occur. Preseason altitude training not accounted for. Data from a public aggregator subject to underreporting. Small sample of high-altitude NFL teams (n=9 stadiums).

## Practical Implications
Same as ID 174: NFL and college football programs based at altitude may have an intrinsic concussion-risk advantage that diminishes when teams travel to lower elevations. Medical and performance staffs at high-altitude programs should note that protection appears to dissipate rapidly upon descent. Low-altitude programs visiting high-altitude venues should not expect acute concussion protection for their players.

## Athlete Development Notes
Same as ID 174: Chronic altitude adaptation—not short-term exposure—appears to drive the concussion-protective effect. For athlete development, programs at elevation may benefit from related physiological adaptations (erythropoietic, vascular) that could support training quality and recovery. Short altitude training camps are unlikely to replicate these benefits based on current data.

## Return to Play Notes
Same as ID 174: This study does not directly address RTP protocols, but the finding that high-altitude teams playing away games had the highest concussion rate (0.36/exposure) suggests altitude descent could transiently increase concussion vulnerability. Clinicians managing RTP for altitude-based players should consider whether rapid environmental changes after travel could influence concussion symptom presentation or re-injury risk.
