# Crossland BW, Rigby BR, Duplanty AA, King GA, Juma S, Levine NA, Clark CE, Ramirez KP, Varone NL. Acute Supplementation with Cannabidiol Does Not Attenuate Inflammation or Improve Measures of Performance following Strenuous Exercise. Healthcare. 2022;10(6):1133.

## TLDR
Acute CBD supplementation at 5 mg/kg did not reduce inflammation, limit muscle damage, or restore performance in well-trained female collegiate athletes following intense eccentric exercise, suggesting CBD is not an effective acute recovery intervention.

## Abstract
This double-blind, placebo-controlled, crossover RCT investigated whether acute supplementation with 5 mg/kg CBD isolate attenuates inflammation, muscle damage, and performance decrements following strenuous eccentric exercise in 24 female NCAA collegiate athletes. Participants completed 100 repetitions of unilateral eccentric leg extension (muscle-damaging protocol) with CBD or placebo administered at −2.5 h, immediately post, and +10 h. Blood markers (IL-6, IL-10, IL-1β, myoglobin) and performance measures (vertical jump, isokinetic peak torque at 60°/180°/300°/s, isometric torque) were assessed at baseline, 4 h, 24 h, and 48 h post-damage. No significant differences were observed between CBD and placebo treatments for any inflammatory marker, muscle damage marker, or performance variable at any timepoint.

## Methods
Double-blind, placebo-controlled, crossover RCT; 24 female NCAA DI/DII athletes (mean age 21.2 ± 1.8 yr); 5 mg/kg CBD isolate (0% THC verified) vs. matched placebo (microcrystalline cellulose); administered at −2.5 h, 0 h (post muscle damage), and +10 h; ~28-day washout to control for menstrual cycle; muscle damage: 10×10 eccentric leg extensions at 30°/s on Biodex; performance: countermovement vertical jump (Just Jump mat), concentric isokinetic torque at 60°/180°/300°/s, 5-s isometric torque; VAFS fatigue scale; blood draws at −2.5 h, 4 h, 24 h, 48 h; cytokines (IL-6, IL-10, IL-1β) via MILLIPLEX bead panel; myoglobin via Abnova ELISA; RM MANOVA and post hoc RM ANOVA with Šídák adjustment; IBM SPSS v.24.

## Findings
• No significant treatment × trial interaction for IL-6, IL-10, or IL-1β (p = 0.573 for treatment effect across cytokines).
• No significant differences between CBD and placebo for myoglobin (p = 0.116).
• Myoglobin rose significantly from baseline at 4 h when treatments combined (p = 0.002), confirming successful muscle damage.
• No significant treatment differences for vertical jump (p = 0.806), isokinetic strength at 60° (p = 0.479), 180° (p = 0.426), or 300°/s (p = 0.927), or isometric torque (p = 0.671).
• 60°/s peak torque was significantly lower than baseline at 4 h and 24 h regardless of treatment (p = 0.003 and p = 0.001).
• VAFS fatigue scores increased significantly from baseline at 4 h, 24 h, and 48 h (p < 0.001) with no treatment difference (p = 0.126).

## Limitations
Female-only sample limits generalisability to male athletes; acute dosing design may not reflect chronic supplementation benefits; absolute dose range was 224–408 mg per timepoint (potentially below therapeutic threshold for humans); only one form of eccentric exercise used; no pharmacokinetic confirmation of CBD absorption; PCA composite score failed, requiring MANOVA.

## Practical Implications
Practitioners and athletes should not expect acute CBD supplementation to meaningfully reduce inflammation, accelerate muscle damage recovery, or restore strength performance following high-intensity eccentric training. Current evidence does not support its use as an acute post-exercise recovery supplement. Chronic supplementation protocols and effects in male athletes require further investigation before any recommendations can be made.

## Athlete Development Notes
Relevant to American football athletes considering CBD as a recovery supplement during pre-season two-a-days or following high-volume eccentric training phases. Given null findings, sports dietitians and strength staff should direct athletes toward evidence-based recovery strategies (nutrition timing, sleep, progressive loading) rather than CBD for acute recovery.

## Return to Play Notes
No evidence that acute CBD supplementation accelerates recovery of strength or reduces inflammatory markers following muscle-damaging exercise. Athletes returning from muscle strains or surgery who are using CBD for recovery should be counselled that current data do not support acute benefits on objective performance or inflammatory biomarkers.
