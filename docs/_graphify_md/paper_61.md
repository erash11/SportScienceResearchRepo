# Elstak I, Salmon P, McLean S. Artificial intelligence applications in the football codes: A systematic review. Journal of Sports Sciences. 2024;DOI:10.1080/02640414.2024.2383065.

## TLDR
AI is widely applied across football codes for player evaluation, event detection, and outcome prediction, but current implementations face significant risks including data bias, usability gaps, and inappropriate athlete load management that must be addressed before widespread adoption.

## Abstract
This PRISMA-compliant systematic review identified and synthesised 190 peer-reviewed articles describing AI applications across four football codes: association football (soccer), American football, rugby, and Australian Rules Football (AFL). Nine distinct AI application areas were identified, with player evaluation (n=44), event detection (n=40), and match outcome prediction (n=39) being most prevalent. Twenty-seven AI model types were used, with artificial neural networks (n=35), decision trees (n=23), and extreme gradient boosting (n=22) most common. Four categories of risk were identified: data security, biased data, usability, and inappropriate load management. The review concluded that while AI applications are broad and expanding, further work is needed to manage risks and improve model robustness.

## Methods
Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines. Five databases searched (Scopus, PubMed, Web of Science, SportDiscus, IEEEXplore) on 19 July 2022. Initial search yielded 2,586 articles; 190 met inclusion criteria after deduplication and full-text screening. Inclusion required original peer-reviewed articles describing or formally evaluating an AI model in a football code setting. Data extracted included authors, year, football code, AI model type, application function, evaluation metrics, and identified risks.

## Findings
• Nine AI application areas identified: player evaluation (44), event detection (40), match outcome prediction (39), injury prediction/detection (27), player monitoring/workload (10), activity prediction (9), team evaluation (9), financial (8), match attendance (4)
• 27 different AI model types used; artificial neural networks most frequent (n=35), followed by decision trees (n=23) and XGBoost (n=22)
• Soccer received the most research attention; American football was second most studied code
• Five AI evaluation metrics identified: accuracy, specificity, sensitivity/recall, precision, and F1-score; accuracy was most reported (n=88)
• Four risk categories: data security, biased data, usability of equipment, and inappropriate load management
• Injury prediction AI is currently limited — categorizing athletes as high/low risk rather than true prediction
• Match outcome prediction accuracy was generally low across codes
• Only 5 of 190 articles formally reported on AI-associated risks

## Limitations
Search was completed July 2022 and does not reflect post-ChatGPT AI advances; quality appraisal of included articles was not conducted given the broad synthesis aim; development details of AI models were beyond the review scope; findings limited to applications formally described in peer-reviewed journals.

## Practical Implications
Sport science staff integrating AI tools should critically evaluate model inputs for data bias, ensure outputs are interpretable by non-technical end users, and avoid over-relying on load management AI that may inappropriately over- or under-train athletes. Transparent evaluation metrics (accuracy, F1-score) should be required before any AI tool is adopted for athlete management decisions. Human oversight remains essential.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly relevant to American football as the second most studied football code in the review. AI applications for player evaluation, activity prediction (offensive play-call prediction), and talent identification are active areas in NFL and college football. The review highlights opportunities for AI to process large tracking and biometric datasets to improve draft evaluation, performance profiling, and workload management — all key components of American football athlete development programs.

## Return to Play Notes
Injury prediction and detection was the fourth most common AI application (n=27 articles). Current AI tools categorize athletes as high or low injury risk rather than predicting specific injury events, limiting clinical utility for RTP decisions. False-positive rates remain a concern — incorrectly flagging low-risk athletes could lead to unnecessary restrictions. AI-assisted load monitoring tools carry risk of over- or under-loading athletes if not properly calibrated, which has direct RTP implications.
