# Ryan S, Kempton T, Coutts AJ. Data Reduction Approaches to Athlete Monitoring in Professional Australian Football. International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance. 2020;(Ahead of Print). DOI: 10.1123/ijspp.2020-0083

## TLDR
When monitoring professional footballers, many commonly collected variables measure the same underlying construct and add data complexity without adding insight. Using PCA on a full AFL season of data, Ryan and colleagues show that daily monitoring reduces to three key constructs (external load, perceived wellness, internal load) and chronic monitoring reduces to four (chronic speed exposure, 28-day external load, 7-day external load, chronic internal load). Total distance is the single best representative of daily external load; sRPE is the best internal load measure; and maximum speed exposure should be tracked over 28-day windows rather than 7-day windows for greater stability and injury risk relevance. Critically, perceived soreness is a separate monitoring construct from general wellness (fatigue, sleep, stress, motivation) and should not be combined into a composite wellness score. These findings provide a statistically grounded framework for streamlining athlete monitoring dashboards in football without losing meaningful information.

## Abstract
This study applied principal component analysis (PCA) to athlete monitoring data from 45 professional Australian footballers across a full AFL season to address the problem of data overload in practitioner settings. Two PCAs were conducted — one for daily monitoring measures (11 variables) and one for chronic monitoring measures (12 variables) — to identify uncorrelated components representing distinct monitoring constructs. The Daily PCA identified three components: daily external load, perceived wellness, and daily internal load. The Chronic PCA identified four components: chronic maximum speed exposure, 28-day external load, 7-day external load, and chronic internal load. Perceived soreness was found to fall below the redundancy threshold, indicating it is a distinct construct separate from the other wellness variables (fatigue, sleep, stress, motivation).

## Methods
Prospective, longitudinal design. Data collected from 45 professional Australian footballers (age: 24.6±4.0 yr; height: 1.9±0.1 m; body mass: 86.0±9.1 kg) from one AFL club during the 2018 competition season (rounds 1-23; March to August). External load measured via 10-Hz GPS units (OptimEye S5; Catapult Sports) for training and most matches, with ClearSky T6 used for 8 indoor matches: total distance (TD), high-speed running (HSR; >20 km/h), very high-speed running (VHSR; >23 km/h), maximum speed, and inertial movement analysis (IMA). Internal load measured via session-RPE method within 30 minutes post-session. Perceptual wellness assessed weekly via 1-5 Likert scale questionnaire covering muscle soreness, sleep quality, fatigue, stress, and motivation. Derivative load measures calculated at daily, 7-day, and 28-day rolling windows. Two separate PCAs (Daily and Chronic) conducted using 84,294 data points across 23 monitoring variables; orthogonal rotation; redundancy threshold of factor loading >0.70; components identified via scree plot and eigenvalue >1; Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin adequacy and Bartlett's sphericity tests applied. Analyses performed in jamovi (v0.9).

## Findings
Daily PCA identified 3 components from 11 variables: Component 1 — daily external load (34.4% variance; TD loading 0.92, HSR 0.90, VHSR 0.88, max speed 0.81, IMA 0.81); Component 2 — perceived wellness (27.6% variance; motivation 0.84, stress 0.84, fatigue 0.81, sleep quality 0.73); Component 3 — daily internal load (9.1% variance; sRPE 0.99). Perceived soreness fell below the redundancy threshold (factor loading <0.70), identifying it as a distinct monitoring construct. Chronic PCA identified 4 components: Component 1 — chronic maximum speed exposure (33.6% variance; 28-day measures loading higher than 7-day); Component 2 — 28-day external load (21.9%; TD and HSR month load both 0.89); Component 3 — 7-day external load (13.7%; HSR week load 0.90); Component 4 — chronic internal load (8.4%; sRPE 28-day 0.91, sRPE 7-day 0.90). Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin values were 0.80 (Daily) and 0.59 (Chronic).

## Limitations
External load data from 8 of 22 matches were collected using an alternative GPS system (ClearSky T6) due to indoor venue requirements, and no published research has established technical agreement between the two systems. The study did not model any of the 23 variables against injury or performance outcome measures, limiting conclusions about predictive utility of the identified components. Data were collected from a single AFL club during one season, which may reflect club-specific demographics and periodization strategies rather than universal patterns. The Chronic PCA had a relatively low Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin measure (0.59), indicating moderate sampling adequacy. Generalizability to American football requires consideration of sport-specific differences in contact demands, positional heterogeneity, and training structure.

## Practical Implications
For NFL and college football performance staff dealing with data overload from GPS, wellness questionnaires, and RPE systems, this study provides a statistically grounded framework for streamlining monitoring to key representative variables. Total distance is the single best daily external load variable; sRPE is the best internal load measure. Wellness questionnaires should treat perceived soreness separately from fatigue, sleep, stress, and motivation — a composite wellness score combining all five variables conflates distinct constructs and reduces sensitivity. Maximum sprint speed exposure should be tracked over 28-day rolling windows for the most stable and injury-relevant chronic load metric. The two proposed reduction methods — single-variable and summed-variable approaches — give practitioners concrete options for simplifying dashboards without sacrificing statistical validity.

## Athlete Development Notes
Football development programs can apply PCA-informed variable selection to design leaner, more interpretable athlete monitoring systems. For daily monitoring, collecting total distance (GPS external load), sRPE (internal load), and a 4-item wellness questionnaire (fatigue, sleep, stress, motivation) plus a separate soreness rating covers all three daily monitoring constructs with minimal redundancy. For chronic load tracking, total distance over 28 days, HSR over 7 days, maximum speed exposures over 28 days, and 28-day sRPE provide a complete picture of chronic training state. This framework is particularly applicable to development academies with limited staff resources where efficient, non-redundant data collection is essential.

## Return to Play Notes
The PCA framework is directly applicable to return-to-play monitoring in football. During rehabilitation, tracking daily external load (total distance), internal load (sRPE), and perceived soreness as a distinct construct separate from general wellness provides a comprehensive, non-redundant picture of training response to guide load progression decisions. The identification of perceived soreness as a separate monitoring construct is especially relevant for RTP, where tracking post-exercise soreness independently from general fatigue or sleep quality provides more specific information about tissue recovery and readiness for escalating demands. Chronic maximum speed exposure over 28 days provides a key benchmark for determining when an athlete returning from soft-tissue injury has reached adequate high-speed running volumes relative to pre-injury norms.
