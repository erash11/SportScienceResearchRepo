# Teramoto, M., Cushman, D. M., Cross, C. L., Curtiss, H. M., & Willick, S. E. (2017). Game schedules and rate of concussions in the National Football League. The Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine, 5(11), 2325967117740862.

## TLDR
Contrary to public concern, Thursday Night Football and overseas games do not increase NFL players' concussion risk. The only notable finding was a higher concussion rate in the second half of the season, suggesting cumulative fatigue may be a more meaningful risk factor than schedule format. Team medical staffs should heighten vigilance later in the season rather than specifically around short-rest games.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiological study examined whether unconventional NFL game schedules—including Thursday games (3 days of rest) and overseas games—are associated with an increased rate of concussions. Using PBS Frontline Concussion Watch data, 582 concussions were analyzed across the 2012–2015 NFL regular seasons. Chi-square goodness-of-fit tests and logistic regression were used to examine the relationship between days of rest, game location, bye week timing, and concussion rates. No significant association was found between concussion rates and days of rest, game location, or bye week timing. Significantly more concussions occurred in the second half of the season (56.2% vs. 43.8%, P < .01).

## Methods
Descriptive epidemiological study using publicly available data from PBS Frontline Concussion Watch and Pro-Football-Reference.com. Analyzed 582 concussions across 2012–2015 NFL regular seasons. Chi-square goodness-of-fit tests compared observed concussion distributions by days of rest (3, 5–7, 9–14 days) and game location (home, away, overseas) to expected distributions based on actual game scheduling proportions. Two-way contingency table analysis and logistic regression assessed repeat concussion risk, controlling for player position.

## Findings
No significant association between concussion rate and days of rest (P = .17), game location (P = .23), or bye week timing by team or opponent (P > .05). Game schedules were not predictive of repeat concussions (P > .05). Significantly more concussions occurred in the second half of the season than the first half (56.2% vs. 43.8%, P < .01). Non-significant trends included ~15 more concussions than expected after 9–14 days of rest and 39 more in away games than home games.

## Limitations
Reliance on a publicly available database rather than the NFL Injury Surveillance System may have resulted in missed or unverified concussions. Small sample size reduced statistical power, particularly for Thursday and overseas games. Individual player factors (condition, skill level, prior concussion history) were not controlled. Observational design prevents causal inference. Concussion underreporting in football is a known issue.

## Practical Implications
Current NFL game schedules—including Thursday Night Football and international games—do not appear to independently elevate concussion risk. Scheduling policy changes related to player safety should focus on other risk factors. Medical and training staffs may consider increased concussion monitoring during the second half of the season when cumulative fatigue is highest.

## Athlete Development Notes
For player development contexts, findings suggest that schedule density (short rest) is less influential on head injury risk than cumulative seasonal load. Programs should incorporate fatigue management across the full season rather than focusing primarily on short-week preparations.

## Return to Play Notes
The absence of a significant relationship between days of rest and repeat concussions provides some reassurance when managing return-to-play timelines around short rest weeks. However, clinicians should remain vigilant for players returning late in the season when overall concussion rates are elevated.
