# Bedard G, Lawrence DW. Five-Year Trends in Reported National Football League Injuries. Clin J Sport Med. 2019;29(1):1-6.

## TLDR
Over five NFL seasons (2012–2016), all-cause injury rates are high but trending downward, with a significant drop in 2016 coinciding with rule changes targeting dangerous blocking and tackling. Concussion rates spiked significantly in 2015—likely reflecting improved detection from new spotter rules and medical timeout protocols rather than a true increase in events. The knee and ankle remain the most frequently injured body areas, and defensive backs sustain the most injuries of any position group. Continued rule changes and concussion education programs are needed across all levels of football.

## Abstract
This prospective descriptive epidemiological study analyzed official NFL injury report data across five regular seasons (2012–2016) to determine all-cause injury, concussion, and knee injury incidence trends. A total of 10,934 injuries were identified across 2,460 team games (TGs). The all-cause injury rate was 444.47 per 100 TGs (404.07 per 1,000 athletes at risk [AAR]); concussion incidence was 30.57 per 100 TGs (27.79 per 1,000 AAR). Defensive backs had the highest injury rates of all positions in every season. Significant findings included a decrease in all-cause and knee injury incidence in 2016 vs. 2015, and an increase in concussion incidence in 2015 vs. 2014. These trends were evaluated in context of rule changes implemented between seasons.

## Methods
Prospective descriptive epidemiological study. Official NFL injury reports were collected weekly for all 32 teams across the 2012–2013 through 2016–2017 regular seasons. A new injury was defined as any condition not reported in the prior week's injury report. Concussion was defined as any injury coded as concussion or head injury. Injuries were expressed as counts per team game (TG) and per 1,000 athletes at risk (AAR; 11 players per TG). The Kruskal–Wallis rank-sum test with Tukey and Nemenyi post hoc comparisons was used to assess year-by-year differences. Analysis included 2,460 of 2,560 total possible TGs (96.1%), with Week 17 reports available only for the 12 playoff-qualifying teams.

## Findings
10,934 total injuries over 5 seasons; 752 (6.9%) were concussions. Lower extremity injuries accounted for 62.3% of all injuries. Top 5 injury locations: knee (17.2%), ankle (13.6%), shoulder (8.8%), hamstring (8.4%), concussion (6.9%). All-cause injury rate: 444.47/100 TGs (404.07/1,000 AAR). Concussion incidence: 30.57/100 TGs (27.79/1,000 AAR). All-cause injury rates were significantly lower in 2016 vs. 2015 (396.75 vs. 512.20/100 TGs; P<0.001). Knee injury incidence was significantly lower in 2016 vs. 2015 (62.40 vs. 86.38/100 TGs; P=0.029). Concussion incidence increased significantly from 2014 to 2015 (22.97 to 36.59/100 TGs; P=0.012). Defensive backs had the highest all-cause injury count in every season. Kickers/punters had the lowest.

## Limitations
All injuries were assumed to have occurred during games rather than practice, potentially overestimating game-specific injury burden. Only publicly available team-reported data were used; validity and reliability of team reporting cannot be fully verified despite NFL reporting mandates. New vs. recurrent injuries could not be reliably distinguished. The study is limited to reported injuries and may not capture all actual injuries. Selection bias is possible as Week 17 injury reports were only available for playoff teams. No mechanism (contact vs. non-contact) or severity data beyond injury report status were available.

## Practical Implications
The significant reduction in all-cause and knee injuries in 2016 following specific rule changes (elimination of chop blocks, expanded horse collar rule) demonstrates that targeted rule modifications can reduce injury rates at the elite level, with potential downstream influence on rules at the college and high school levels. The spike in concussion reporting in 2015 following implementation of independent athletic trainer spotters and medical timeouts illustrates that improved detection infrastructure increases reported incidence even if true event rates are unchanged—a finding relevant to interpreting injury surveillance data at all levels.

## Athlete Development Notes
Defensive backs face the highest all-cause injury burden of any position group in the NFL, followed by linebackers, defensive linemen, and offensive linemen. Position-specific injury exposure data can inform targeted prehabilitation, strength and conditioning programming, and load management strategies. The high rate of knee and ankle injuries across all positions reinforces the importance of lower extremity injury prevention programs (e.g., neuromuscular training, ACL prevention protocols) as a standard component of NFL and collegiate football preparation.

## Return to Play Notes
Knee injuries (17.2% of all injuries) and Achilles/tendon injuries are the most frequent season-impacting injuries in the NFL. The year-to-year variability in injury incidence—particularly the significant knee injury reduction in 2016—demonstrates that environmental factors (rules, playing surface) influence RTP population dynamics. Medical staff should track league-wide rule changes that may affect injury mechanisms and adjust clinical protocols and RTP timelines accordingly. The high concussion incidence (6.9% of all injuries, 27.79/1,000 AAR) demands robust concussion protocols and sideline detection infrastructure at all levels of play.
