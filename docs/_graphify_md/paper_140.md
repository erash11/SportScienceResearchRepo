# Heintz EC, Breaux L, Lewis A, Foreman JJ. Determinants of Missed Games Following Concussions in the National Football League. Frontiers in Sports and Active Living. 2020;2:595445. DOI: 10.3389/fspor.2020.595445

## TLDR
Not all NFL players receive equal time off after a concussion—player value and role appear to influence recovery time beyond medical factors alone. Quarterbacks (the most cognitively demanding position) were given significantly more recovery time, while heavily relied-upon players were returned faster despite their high play volume. Each successive concussion added approximately 0.25 games missed, and time-off has increased each year from 2012 to 2015, likely reflecting evolving protocols and media pressure. For football performance and medical staffs, this highlights potential disparities in concussion management and the importance of applying consistent, evidence-based return-to-play protocols regardless of position or role.

## Abstract
This study examined which factors beyond medical severity predict the number of NFL games missed following a concussion. Using PBS Frontline Concussion Watch data from 2012–2015 (643 reported concussions), researchers applied OLS regression with player salary, position (quarterback vs. other), average plays per game, prior concussions, season, and week of injury as predictors. Results showed that quarterbacks missed approximately one more game than non-quarterbacks, while players involved in more plays per game missed fewer games. Prior concussions and later seasons also predicted more missed games, while salary and week of injury did not reach significance.

## Methods
Longitudinal observational design; secondary analysis of publicly available data; concussion data from PBS Frontline Concussion Watch (2012–2015 NFL seasons); N=643 reported concussions; independent variables: player salary (millions), quarterback status (dummy variable), average plays per game pre-injury, prior concussions, season count (2012=1 through 2015=4), and week of season; dependent variable: games missed; OLS regression with multicollinearity checks (VIF ≤1.15); stepwise regression used for model validation.

## Findings
Quarterbacks missed approximately 0.91 more games than other positions (p=0.003). Players averaging more plays per game missed fewer games (coefficient=-0.007, p=0.033); a player averaging 27 plays/game was expected to miss ~0.28 more games than one averaging 67 plays/game. Each prior concussion added ~0.25 games missed (p=0.059). Each subsequent season from 2012–2015 added ~0.16 games missed (p=0.050). Salary (p=0.897) and week of injury (p=0.470) were not significant predictors. Mean games missed was 0.99 (range 0–13).

## Limitations
Study did not account for concussion severity, which is the primary medical determinant of recovery time. Data are limited to three seasons (2012–2015) and do not include more recent seasons with updated protocols. Only reported concussions were included; an estimated 56% of NFL players admit to hiding concussions, meaning a substantial proportion of events are absent from the dataset. Individual differences in concussion response and symptoms were not captured.

## Practical Implications
NFL medical and performance staffs should be aware that non-medical factors—particularly player value and position—may be influencing time-off decisions after concussions. Players who are heavily relied upon (high average plays per game) appear to be returned to play sooner, potentially before full recovery, which increases long-term CTE and repeat-injury risk. Consistent application of evidence-based concussion protocols regardless of a player's roster value is essential for long-term player health and liability management.

## Athlete Development Notes
From an athlete development standpoint, the finding that high-usage players receive less recovery time post-concussion is a concern at all levels of football, not just the NFL. Programs should build concussion monitoring systems that are independent of playing time or positional value, ensuring that players whose roles demand high snap counts are not pressured back prematurely.

## Return to Play Notes
This study provides direct evidence that return-to-play decisions in the NFL are influenced by factors other than medical recovery, specifically player position and role importance. Quarterbacks are protected longer, suggesting cognitive position demands are recognized informally even if not formalized in protocols. Medical staffs should advocate for position-blind, symptom- and biomarker-based RTP standards and monitor whether high-utilization skill players are being cleared using the same rigor applied to quarterbacks.
