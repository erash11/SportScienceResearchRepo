# Ghelich Afshar, N., Soltani, M., & Naderinasab, H. (2025). Identifying key factors influencing hamstring muscle strains in football players: A narrative review with descriptive analysis. International Journal of Sport Studies for Health, 8(1), 64–78.

## TLDR
Hamstring strains in football are multifactorial, requiring attention to both intrinsic risk factors (strength imbalances, poor flexibility, prior injury, age, fatigue, neuromuscular deficits) and extrinsic factors (excessive training load, artificial turf, congested match schedules, inadequate warm-up and coaching methods) to effectively reduce injury rates and recurrence. The strongest evidence supports Nordic hamstring exercises for eccentric strength development, dynamic warm-ups incorporating progressive activation, and load monitoring with acute-to-chronic workload ratios. Rehabilitation must progress through acute management, eccentric strengthening, neuromuscular retraining, and sport-specific drills before return to play. Gaps remain regarding genetic predisposition and long-term rehabilitation program outcomes.

## Abstract
This narrative review with descriptive analysis synthesized peer-reviewed literature published between 2010 and 2024 to identify intrinsic and extrinsic factors contributing to hamstring muscle strains in football players and evaluate existing prevention and rehabilitation strategies. Intrinsic factors examined included muscle strength imbalances (quadriceps-to-hamstring ratio), flexibility, injury history, age, fatigue, neuromuscular control, and genetic predisposition. Extrinsic factors included training load and intensity, playing surfaces, environmental conditions, footwear, and coaching methods. Evidence-based interventions such as Nordic hamstring exercises, dynamic warm-ups, and workload management were evaluated. Hamstring strains represent 12–16% of all football injuries and occur at an annual rate of 4–7 per 1000 match-hours in professional players. Recurrence rates are 30–40%, primarily within the first two months after return to play.

## Methods
Narrative review with descriptive analysis. Literature search conducted in PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and Google Scholar using keywords including 'hamstring strain,' 'football injuries,' 'muscle injury risk factors,' 'biomechanics of hamstrings,' and 'injury prevention in athletes.' Inclusion criteria: peer-reviewed original research articles, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and clinical trials published in English between January 2010 and December 2024 directly addressing hamstring strains in football. Studies from other sports included if providing relevant insights. Quality assessment used Newcastle-Ottawa Scale for cohort studies and Cochrane Risk of Bias Tool for RCTs. Data synthesized thematically into intrinsic and extrinsic factor categories.

## Findings
Hamstring strains account for 12–16% of all football injuries with incidence of 4–7 per 1000 match-hours in professional players; annual increase of ~4% reported. Recurrence rate is 30–40%, mostly within first two months post-RTP. Intrinsic factors: low Q:H strength ratio is a well-documented risk factor; limited hamstring flexibility increases tensile stress during sprinting/kicking; previous injury history is among the strongest predictors of reinjury; players over 30 have significantly higher susceptibility; fatigue and neuromuscular control deficits compound risk. Extrinsic factors: excessive training loads and congested schedules elevate fatigue-related risk; artificial turf associated with higher hamstring strain incidence versus natural grass; inadequate warm-up routines increase likelihood of strain; coaching methods and scheduling affect recovery adequacy. Nordic hamstring exercise reduces injury rates and reinjury; dynamic warm-ups with progressive activation reduce incidence; acute-to-chronic workload monitoring helps tailor training to individual capacity.

## Limitations
Narrative (rather than systematic) review design introduces selection and publication bias. Studies included are heterogeneous in design, population characteristics, and outcome measures, limiting meta-analytic synthesis. Genetic predisposition research remains sparse and mechanistically unclear. Long-term effectiveness and sustainability of rehabilitation programs are poorly documented — most studies focus on short-term outcomes. Variations in protocol standardization, adherence, and implementation across teams and competitive levels limit generalizability of prevention program findings.

## Practical Implications
Football medical and performance staff should screen players using Q:H strength ratio assessments and eccentric hamstring strength tests (isokinetic dynamometry, functional movement screens) to identify at-risk individuals. Nordic hamstring exercises should be integrated consistently into training programs, not only in pre-season. Dynamic warm-ups with progressive neuromuscular activation should precede all training sessions and matches. Acute-to-chronic workload monitoring (GPS, workload ratios) should guide training prescription, particularly during congested fixture periods. Post-injury rehabilitation must address eccentric strength deficits and neuromuscular retraining before sport-specific progressions and RTP.

## Athlete Development Notes
Young football players face unique hamstring strain risk related to growth-related muscle imbalances and reduced flexibility during rapid growth phases. Development programs should incorporate age-appropriate hamstring strengthening (eccentric focus), dynamic flexibility training, and education on injury prevention principles. Screening for Q:H imbalances at developmental ages can identify players who need targeted interventions before high-load competitive environments increase their risk. Coaching methods that allow adequate recovery between high-intensity sessions are particularly important for youth populations.

## Return to Play Notes
Premature return to play is identified as a primary driver of the 30–40% hamstring reinjury rate, often within the first two months post-RTP. RTP criteria must include restoration of eccentric strength and neuromuscular control to pre-injury or symmetrical levels, pain-free performance of high-intensity activities, and absence of compensatory movement patterns. Nordic hamstring exercise should be a component of rehabilitation and maintained post-RTP. Structured sport-specific progressions — sprinting, cutting, kicking — should be introduced gradually with monitoring tools (strength assessments, functional movement tests) to verify readiness at each stage. Ongoing load management and education post-RTP are essential to prevent recurrence.
