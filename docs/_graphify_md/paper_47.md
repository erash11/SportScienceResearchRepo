# O'Malley GR Jr, Sarwar SA, Posner KM, Polavarapu H, Adams D, Cassimatis ND, Munier S, Hundal JS, Patel NV. Analysis of Concussions Reported in American Professional Football Injury Reports in the 2019 through 2023 Regular Seasons. World Neurosurg. 2024; doi: 10.1016/j.wneu.2024.05.026.

## TLDR
NFL concussion rates remained largely stable across 2019–2023 with no significant change in weeks missed, though concussions per game increased after the Tua Tagovailoa incident and subsequent protocol changes; cornerbacks and wide receivers sustained the most concussions, and playoff teams had significantly shorter concussion RTP times.

## Abstract
This observational study examined trends in concussion diagnosis and return-to-play (RTP) across five NFL regular seasons (2019–2023) using publicly available NFL injury reports. The authors identified 664 of 692 (96%) officially recorded in-game concussions across the study period. Key outcomes included concussion rate per game, weeks missed following concussion, position-specific concussion frequency, and the impact of the Tua Tagovailoa injury and subsequent 'Tua Rule' protocol update on concussion reporting and RTP timelines.

## Methods
Observational study; NFL injury reports queried for 2019–2023 regular seasons; weeks missed calculated from NFL game participation logs; player position and team data extracted from NFL player profiles and Pro Football Reference; statistical analysis using R (tidyverse, skimr, FactoMineR packages); Z-scores for means/proportions comparisons; alpha=0.05; excluded concussions concurrent with other injuries, roster changes, or at season end.

## Findings
• 664 of 692 (96%) official in-game concussions identified across 2019–2023 (132, 125, 119, 141, 147 per season). • No significant difference in concussions per game across seasons (F=0.568, p=0.6836). • 31% of players returned without missing a game; 39% missed one game; 30% missed two or more games. • Cornerbacks led all positions with 119 concussions; wide receivers had 116; offensive linemen 94. • Playoff teams had significantly shorter concussion RTP than non-playoff teams (0.86 vs. 1.37 weeks, p=0.002). • Concussion rate per game increased after the Tagovailoa injury (0.521 vs. 0.429, p<0.01). • No difference in weeks missed before vs. after the Tua Rule implementation (1.51 vs. 1.31, p=0.24). • Players with second concussions in a season missed more games than for their first (2.68 vs. 1.00 weeks, p=0.000245).

## Limitations
Reliance on publicly available injury reports may undercount concussions not appearing on the weekly report; lack of access to NFL electronic medical records prevents severity assessment; psychosocial factors and motivational influences on RTP not assessable; concussion rate increase post-Tagovailoa may partially reflect improved detection rather than true incidence increase.

## Practical Implications
NFL-level concussion surveillance data demonstrate that protocol changes (such as the Tua Rule adding ataxia as a mandatory removal criterion) increased detection rates without shortening RTP timelines, suggesting enhanced identification without over-restriction. Teams should monitor players with recurrent same-season concussions closely given significantly longer missed time for second events.

## Athlete Development Notes
Cornerbacks and wide receivers face the highest NFL concussion burden—positions defined by high-speed open-field collisions. American football programs should consider position-specific concussion prevention strategies targeting skill players, including technique coaching, helmet optimization, and contact exposure monitoring during practice.

## Return to Play Notes
31% of NFL players returned without missing a game following concussion, and mean weeks missed ranged 1.23–1.61 across seasons. Players sustaining a second concussion in the same season missed nearly three times as many weeks as for their first. Playoff teams demonstrated significantly faster RTP (0.86 weeks), which may reflect less severe concussion profiles or psychosocial motivation—a distinction with important clinical implications for RTP decision-making.
