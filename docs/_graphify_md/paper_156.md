# Young TS, Chatham LS, Carpenter RD, Yakacki CM. Does pad displacement correlate to impact severity metrics in football helmets? Proc IMechE Part P: J Sports Engineering and Technology. 2024;1-13. doi:10.1177/17543371241280709

## TLDR
Helmet pad normal displacement is a meaningful indicator of impact severity—but only at specific impact locations, most notably the side upper (SU) region, which carries the highest weight in the NFL's Helmet Performance Score. A helmet that maximizes normal displacement without the pad fully densifying (bottoming out) will produce lower HIC and HARM scores. Tangential displacement is largely irrelevant to severity metrics. For equipment managers and performance staff, this study provides mechanistic insight into why certain helmets outperform others in NFL testing: the geometry and deformation mechanics of the internal padding system matter more than outer shell shape. The Riddell helmet showed lowest peak normal displacements, while Vicis showed the highest HIC despite comparable displacements due to its buckling column design densifying earlier. When selecting helmets, understanding pad displacement characteristics by impact location—not just overall performance scores—may guide more informed decisions.

## Abstract
This computational study used finite element (FE) models of three NFL-tested football helmets (2016 Riddell Speed Classic, 2016 Xenith X2E, 2017 Vicis Zero1) to investigate whether helmet pad displacement—separated into normal and tangential components—correlates with impact severity metrics (HIC, DAMAGE, HARM) across six impact locations and three speeds per NFL testing protocol. When evaluated across all locations combined, normal displacement did not correlate strongly with any severity metric (R²<0.44); however, at the side upper (SU) location, normal displacement correlated strongly with HARM, HIC, and PRLA (R²=0.79–0.80). Tangential displacement was not found to correlate with any impact severity metric regardless of location, and helmets that maximized normal displacement without bottoming out generally performed better on severity metrics.

## Methods
FE models of three helmets (2016 Riddell Speed Classic, 2016 Xenith X2E, 2017 Vicis Zero1) from BioCore LLC were used in LS-DYNA simulations per NFL testing protocol. The Hybrid III (HIII) headform and neck assembly were paired with a linear impactor. Six impact locations: side (C), rear (D), side upper (SU), oblique front (OF), facemask side (FMS), facemask center oblique (FMCO) at impact speeds of 5.5, 7.4, and 9.3 m/s. MATLAB code extracted time history data for three nodes from the outer pad surface and three from the inner pad surface to calculate peak normal and tangential displacement per impact. Impact severity metrics calculated: HIC15 (Head Injury Criterion, 15 ms window), DAMAGE (Diffuse Axonal Multi-Axis General Evaluation), HARM (Head Acceleration Response Metric, weighted combination of HIC and DAMAGE). Model validation used CORA scores: Riddell 0.79, Xenith 0.79, Vicis 0.74. Correlations between peak displacement components and severity metrics assessed using R² values by location and combined.

## Findings
Normal displacement correlated strongly with severity metrics at the SU location (HARM R²=0.79, HIC R²=0.80, PRLA R²=0.79) but weakly at other locations (OF: HARM R²=0.06, HIC R²=0.00). Across all locations combined, R²=0.33–0.44 for normal displacement versus severity metrics. Tangential displacement showed weak correlations across all locations (R²<0.2 for all metrics except PRAA at D location, R²=0.65). Peak normal displacement increased with impact speed for most locations; maximum deformations ranged 12–37mm across helmets at highest speed. At the rear location, Vicis showed highest HIC despite comparable displacements due to premature pad densification from buckling column design. Riddell had lowest normal displacements and HIC was variable by location; Xenith showed lowest HIC at the OF location. At the SU location, all three helmets reached similar peak normal displacements but Vicis showed highest HIC (Figure 6c) due to acceleration time-history shape. The hypothesis that helmets maximizing normal displacement without bottoming out will perform better was supported at SU and OF but not uniformly at all locations.

## Limitations
Only three impact speeds per location were tested, limiting the ability to statistically assess correlations (three data points per helmet per location). FE models are several years old and have largely been replaced by newer helmet versions. FE model definitions for rear location may not fully represent real-world conditions due to omission of rear nape of head geometry. Tangential displacement is calculated as a resultant without directional information. HIC, DAMAGE, and HARM each have inherent limitations in capturing all aspects of concussion risk. Results depend on specific materials and geometries in the BioCore FE models and may not generalize to all helmet designs.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football equipment managers should understand that impact location is critical when interpreting helmet performance scores. A helmet that performs well at the side upper (SU) location—the most heavily weighted in NFL HPS rankings—will benefit most from maximizing normal pad displacement without bottoming out. The Vicis buckling column design, while innovative, may reach pad densification earlier than traditional foam designs, which can paradoxically increase HIC at some locations. Equipment staff should use NFL helmet testing data alongside this biomechanical framework to select helmets that balance protection across all impact zones, not only those with the highest aggregate HPS score. Design engineers should prioritize pad systems that sustain energy absorption (displacement) across a range of impact speeds before full densification.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football programs selecting helmets for developmental athletes, this study indicates that helmet fit and the internal pad-to-head interface—ensuring pads make full contact and displace effectively during impact—are critical for optimal protection. Helmets with larger internal offset (like Vicis) may provide less effective energy absorption if the design leads to premature densification at moderate impact speeds typical of youth and collegiate play. Position-specific helmet selection considerations are supported by the location-dependent nature of impact severity correlations; linemen experiencing frequent frontal impacts may benefit from helmets with different pad characteristics than skill players experiencing lateral impacts.

## Return to Play Notes
While this study does not directly address return-to-play, it provides mechanistic context relevant to concussion prevention protocols. The finding that helmet pad normal displacement correlates with HARM scores at high-risk impact locations (SU) reinforces that proper helmet fit and pad condition are critical components of concussion risk management. Equipment staff conducting return-to-play evaluations should include helmet inspection—checking pad integrity and displacement capacity—as part of the clearance process, particularly after a concussive impact. Helmets whose pads have experienced full densification from a prior impact may have reduced protective capacity for subsequent impacts.
