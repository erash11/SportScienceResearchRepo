# Yang J, Hodax JD, Machan JT, Krill MK, Lemme NJ, Durand WM, Hoffman JT, Hewett TE, Owens BD. Factors affecting return to play after primary Achilles tendon tear: a cohort of NFL players. Orthop J Sports Med. 2019;7(3):2325967119830139. doi:10.1177/2325967119830139

## TLDR
Only 61.3% of NFL players return to play after a primary Achilles tendon tear, lower than prior estimates, with a mean recovery of nearly 12 months. Unlike other serious NFL injuries, neither draft round nor position type predicts who returns. The strongest predictor of not returning is declining game participation in the season before injury — possibly a marker of underlying tendon degeneration. For those who do return, performance normalizes after the first post-return season, suggesting that players given the opportunity to compete through the initial transition year can recover to preinjury levels.

## Abstract
This case-control study evaluated factors associated with return to play (RTP) and performance changes following primary Achilles tendon (AT) tears in NFL players. Eighty players with primary AT tears between the 2009 and 2014 seasons were identified from a previously established NFL injury database. RTP was defined as participating in at least one regular season or postseason game within 18 months of injury. Kaplan-Meier analysis was used to model probability of RTP over time. The overall RTP rate was 61.3% — the lowest reported for this population — with a mean recovery time of 11.90 months. Age, draft round, position type, and number of prior seasons did not significantly influence RTP probability. Players who successfully returned had played in significantly more games (13.74) the season immediately preceding injury versus non-returners (8.71; P=.011). Players who did not return exhibited a significant decline in games played in the season prior to injury compared to two seasons prior (12.69 to 8.71; P=.019), possibly reflecting prodromal AT degeneration. Among returners, performance was significantly reduced only in the RTP season; performance returned to preinjury levels when players competed for more than one season following return.

## Methods
Retrospective case-control study. 80 NFL players with primary (first-ever, isolated) AT tears in the 2009–2014 seasons identified via NFL injury reports, team disclosures, and news reports. Players with prior ipsilateral or contralateral AT tears excluded. Twelve players (15%) with subsequent AT tears excluded from performance analyses. Player demographics from NFL.com. RTP defined as ≥1 play in a regular season or postseason game within 18 months. Kaplan-Meier survival analysis modeled probability of RTP over time; generalized linear models assessed demographic predictors; generalized estimating equations modeled within- and between-player performance changes. Statistical significance at alpha=0.05 with Holm method for family-wise error correction.

## Findings
Overall RTP rate: 61.3% (49/80 players) within 18 months; mean time to RTP: 11.90 months. Unskilled position players had higher RTP (75%) vs. skilled (52.8%), but this difference was not statistically significant by Kaplan-Meier (P=.19). Draft round did not influence RTP (P=.49). Players who RTP'd played 13.74 games the prior season vs 8.71 for non-returners (P=.011). Non-returners showed significant pre-injury performance decline (12.69 games 2 seasons prior vs 8.71 one season prior; P=.019). In the RTP season, returners played significantly fewer games (10.94) than the two prior seasons (P<.001). After the RTP season, no significant differences in games played or started were found, indicating return to preinjury performance levels. 15% of players sustained a subsequent AT tear.

## Limitations
RTP evaluated only within 18 months; longer follow-up was underpowered. Performance analysis limited to games played/started — position-specific metrics (e.g., yards per carry) not analyzed. Small positional subgroup sizes precluded robust position-specific analysis. Data sourced from publicly available records; some injuries may be missing. Causation between preinjury performance decline and AT rupture cannot be established. Players who did not RTP may have been released for reasons unrelated to their injury. Subsequent tear cases excluded from performance analysis limit completeness.

## Practical Implications
Medical and performance staff should monitor for declining game participation in seasons preceding a known or suspected AT condition, as this may represent a prodromal window for intervention before rupture. RTP decisions after AT repair should account for the expected performance dip in the first return season and not be used as sole justification for roster release. Players who are given at least two post-return seasons demonstrate performance normalization, arguing for patience in roster management after AT injuries.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athlete development, this study highlights that Achilles tendon health is not static — declining performance and playing time may reflect subclinical tendon degeneration before overt rupture. Proactive tendon screening, load management, and eccentric strengthening programs (Nordic, calf raises, plyometric loading) during preseason and in-season training may help identify and protect at-risk athletes. Young players should be educated about tendon health warning signs.

## Return to Play Notes
The 61.3% RTP rate represents the lowest reported for NFL players with primary AT tears, with a mean timeline of approximately 12 months. Neither position type nor draft round predicted RTP, but robust preinjury game participation was the strongest positive predictor. Performance in the first post-return season is significantly reduced but normalizes thereafter. These findings suggest RTP criteria following AT repair should include functional performance benchmarks beyond simply surgical healing, and that return timelines in the NFL context are substantially longer than for recreational athletes (4–6 months vs ~12 months) due to the demands of elite competition.
