# Lee TA, Lycke RJ, Lee PJ, Cudal CM, Torolski KJ, Bucherl SE, Leiva-Molano N, Auerbach PS, Talavage TM, Nauman EA. Distribution of Head Acceleration Events Varies by Position and Play Type in North American Football. Clinical Journal of Sport Medicine. 2020;00:1-6. doi:10.1097/JSM.0000000000000778

## TLDR
Offensive and defensive linemen absorb the greatest total number of head impacts in football, though most are lower magnitude (20–40 g). Tight ends and linebackers carry the highest proportion of high-magnitude impacts (above 60 g), with tight ends uniquely vulnerable due to dual blocking/receiving roles. A key actionable finding: offensive linemen who start in a 3- or 4-point 'down' stance have a significantly higher likelihood of sustaining a head impact than those in a 2-point 'up' stance, regardless of play type. Run plays generate nearly twice as many HAEs per play as pass plays. These data support rule changes eliminating down stances for linemen and reducing full-contact practice reps as immediate, practical strategies to reduce cumulative head exposure.

## Abstract
This prospective pilot study evaluated the distribution and frequency of head acceleration events (HAEs) across positions, play types, and starting stances in 78 male postcollegiate North American football athletes at a skill development and professional recruitment camp. HAEs were measured using the xPatch sensor (X2 Biosystems) during up to three practice sessions and one exhibition game. Video footage of the exhibition game was used to analyze the effect of offensive linemen's starting stance (2-point 'up' vs. 3- or 4-point 'down') on HAE rates.

## Methods
Prospective cohort study. 78 male adult football athletes at a postcollegiate skill development camp. Up to 3 practice sessions and 1 exhibition game. HAEs measured using the xPatch sensor affixed behind the right ear. Events recorded when peak linear acceleration (PLA) exceeded 10 g; analysis included only events ≥20 g to exclude non-impact accelerations. Players categorized as DB, DL, LB, OL, TE, RB, WR, or skill (QB/K/P). Video footage from 8 cameras used to classify play type (run vs. pass) and OL/TE starting stance (up = 2-point; down = 3- or 4-point). Hit rates compared using chi-square tests with Bonferroni correction; distributions compared with Wilcoxon rank-sum test.

## Findings
437 HAEs recorded across 3 practices; 272 HAEs during the exhibition game (265 during run or pass plays). OL had the most game HAEs (n=98), followed by DL (n=52). LBs had 9.68% and TEs had 18.75% of game HAEs above 60 g PLA—highest proportions of high-magnitude impacts. Position distributions differed significantly in both practice (p=0.011) and game (p=0.017). Run plays generated 5.57 ± 3.00 HAEs per play vs. 2.98 ± 1.65 for pass plays (p<0.001). Players in a down stance had a significantly higher likelihood of sustaining a HAE than those in an up stance (p=0.001). Down-stance players on run plays had the highest hit rate, significantly greater than up-stance players on run or down-stance players on pass plays.

## Limitations
Small sample size: only 2 full practice sessions and 1 game analyzed. Small TE group (n=3) limits validity of trends for that position. xPatch sensor has RMS and mean absolute errors of ~20%. Clack recognition algorithm not used, so non-impact events were not filtered by mechanism. Findings are from a postcollegiate recruitment camp and may not perfectly represent typical NFL or collegiate game scenarios.

## Practical Implications
Football programs at all levels should consider transitioning offensive linemen to a 2-point stance to immediately reduce HAE rates without requiring rule changes by the governing body. Reducing full-contact practice frequency is another readily implementable strategy. Tight ends warrant special attention from medical and performance staff due to their uniquely high proportion of high-magnitude impacts. Coaches should be aware that run-heavy game plans generate substantially more head contact than pass-heavy schemes.

## Athlete Development Notes
Athlete development staff should incorporate blocking technique coaching that minimizes head-first contact, particularly for OL and TE. Given that down-stance linemen are more frequently exposed to HAEs, teaching a quicker transition to an upright blocking posture may reduce cumulative head impact burden over a career. Tight end development should include specific neck strengthening and collision preparation given their disproportionate exposure to high-magnitude impacts.

## Return to Play Notes
While this study does not directly address concussion or return-to-play, its findings on HAE distribution have indirect RTP relevance: athletes returning from concussion who play OL, LB, or TE positions face the highest ongoing impact exposure. Post-concussion return protocols should account for position-specific impact risk, and modified practice participation (e.g., restricting full-contact down-stance reps for returning linemen) is a reasonable protective strategy during the RTP process.
