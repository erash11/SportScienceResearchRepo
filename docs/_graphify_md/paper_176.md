# Timmins RG, Bourne MN, Hickey JT, Maniar N, Tofari PJ, Williams MD, Opar DA. Effect of Prior Injury on Changes to Biceps Femoris Architecture across an Australian Football League Season. Med Sci Sports Exerc. 2017;49(10):2102–2109. DOI: 10.1249/MSS.0000000000001333

## TLDR
AFL players with a prior hamstring strain injury end the season with shorter biceps femoris fascicles than they began with—in both the previously injured and the contralateral uninjured limb. Players without injury history maintain fascicle length across the full season. All athletes see a brief early-season fascicle lengthening (peaking around week 5), but the subsequent shortening is more severe and sustained in previously injured athletes. The previously injured limb was shorter than controls at every measurement point throughout the year. This suggests that prior hamstring injury impairs structural adaptation across a full competitive season, likely due to residual neuromuscular inhibition, and that injury history must be a central consideration in any in-season hamstring management program.

## Abstract
This observational cohort study examined in-season changes in biceps femoris long head (BFlh) fascicle length in 30 elite Australian Football League players—12 with a prior unilateral hamstring strain injury (HSI) and 18 controls without HSI history. BFlh architecture was assessed via 2D ultrasound six times across the 23-week 2016 AFL season. Previously injured limbs had shorter fascicles than controls at all time points throughout the season. All groups showed an early-season increase in fascicle length followed by progressive shortening, with athletes who had prior HSI ending the season with shorter fascicles than they started with.

## Methods
Observational retrospective cohort study during the 2016 AFL season (23 competitive weeks). Thirty elite male AFL players from two clubs participated: 18 controls (no HSI history) and 12 with a prior unilateral BFlh strain in the preceding 12 months. BFlh muscle thickness, pennation angle, and fascicle length were assessed via 2D B-mode ultrasound (12 MHz, GE Vivid-i) at weeks 1, 5, 9, 13, 17, and 23. Fascicle length was estimated using the validated Blazevich equation. Log-transformed data and Cohen's d effect sizes with 90% CIs were used; effects ≥0.2 = small, ≥0.5 = moderate, ≥0.8 = large.

## Findings
Previously injured limbs had shorter fascicles than controls at all time points (moderate to large effects; d range: −1.15 to −0.77). All groups peaked in fascicle length at week 5. The control group's fascicle length was similar at season start and end (trivial change). Previously injured limbs ended the season shorter than they started (moderate effect; d=−0.75). Contralateral uninjured limbs also ended shorter than at week 1 (small to moderate effects). The contralateral uninjured limb had shorter fascicles than controls from weeks 9–23 (moderate to large effects; d range: −0.87 to −0.54). Fascicle length peaked at week 5 across all groups then progressively shortened through week 23.

## Limitations
No preseason architecture data were collected (November–February), so fascicle changes during that period are unknown. No concurrent collection of match/training load, internal/external load, or resistance training variables. Two-dimensional ultrasound requires fascicle length estimation since full fascicles exceed the probe field of view (typical error ~0.30 cm). Three athletes sustained new HSIs during the study, requiring removal from later time points. Relatively small sample (n=30) from two clubs.

## Practical Implications
For football programs, this study reinforces that prior hamstring injury is a chronic risk factor that persists structurally throughout an entire season—not just in the immediate post-injury period. Staff should use in-season ultrasound screening to identify players with short BFlh fascicles (particularly those with prior HSI) as candidates for targeted eccentric loading. The early in-season window (weeks 1–5) appears to be the period most responsive to fascicle lengthening interventions. After week 5, fascicles progressively shorten through the remainder of the season, making in-season eccentric hamstring maintenance critical.

## Athlete Development Notes
Athletes with a prior hamstring strain show blunted structural adaptation to season demands compared to uninjured peers—a finding consistent with residual neuromuscular inhibition impairing the eccentric stimulus needed to add sarcomeres in series. Eccentric loading programs (e.g., Nordic hamstring curl, hip extension exercises) during the early in-season period and throughout the off-season are important tools for restoring BFlh fascicle length in previously injured athletes. Coaches should integrate systematic hamstring architecture monitoring into annual screening, recognizing that the contralateral 'uninjured' limb also shows reduced adaptation following unilateral HSI.

## Return to Play Notes
This study highlights that returning a player to play after hamstring strain injury does not reset the structural deficits in BFlh fascicle length. The previously injured limb maintained shorter fascicles than controls at every in-season assessment point, even when the athlete was cleared for full participation. RTP decisions should incorporate fascicle length assessment via ultrasound alongside traditional strength and functional criteria. Athletes with persistently short fascicles post-RTP should be considered for continued eccentric loading prescription and heightened hamstring strain recurrence monitoring throughout the season.
