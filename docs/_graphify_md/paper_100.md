# Barlow MA, Hesterly WS, Verhaal JC. Catching a falling star: Mobility of declining star performers, peer effects, and organizational performance in the National Football League. Journal of Business Research. 2023;165:114053. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbusres.2023.114053

## TLDR
Signing a veteran star NFL quarterback who is past his peak still benefits the new team: offensive teammates earn more Pro Bowl selections and the team wins roughly one additional game per season. The mechanism is primarily complementarity — the star's presence elevates second-tier offensive players who work closely with him, not simply through mentorship but through how his skills and experience complement their abilities. NFL front offices and coaching staffs should look beyond declining individual statistics when evaluating late-career star acquisitions, as the peer spillover value may justify roster investment, particularly when supported by strong second-tier skill position players.

## Abstract
This study examines how falling star quarterbacks (QBs) — defined as star performers in the twilight of their careers with declining individual performance — impact their new teammates and organizations in the NFL. Using a dataset of 36 star QB mobility events across 22 years of NFL data (1993-2015) and nearest-neighbor matching models, the study tests whether falling stars generate peer spillover effects and organizational performance benefits despite declining individual output. Results reveal that acquiring a falling star QB increases the number of offensive Pro Bowl selections for the new team by 0.53 and improves team wins by 0.89 per season, with complementarity in job design identified as the primary mechanism.

## Methods
Observational cohort study using NFL data from 1993-2015 (the free agency era). Star QBs were defined as those selected to 3 or more career Pro Bowls; 30 star players and 47 good players (1-2 Pro Bowls) were identified from 340 total QBs. A total of 36 star QB mobility events were identified. Nearest-neighbor matching using Mahalanobis distance metric compared teams that added a star QB to similar teams that did not, controlling for lagged offensive rank, lagged defensive rank, lagged wins, team expected to improve, coaching tenure, coaching change, and year. Random-effects and split-sample fixed-effects panel regression models also used. Dependent variables: change in wins, change in offensive rank, change in defensive rank, and number of offensive Pro Bowl selections.

## Findings
Star QBs follow a nonlinear inverted-U career performance arc (rise/middle/fall; b=-25.85, p=0.00), supporting H1. Adding a falling star QB increased offensive Pro Bowl selections by 0.53 (95% CI: 0.06-1.01, p<0.05), supporting the peer spillover hypothesis. Teams adding a falling star QB won 0.89 more games on average (95% CI: 0.07-1.72), a statistically significant large effect. Offensive rankings improved significantly (ranking change -4.28, 95% CI: -1.62 to -6.94) while defensive rankings were unaffected. Position-specific split analyses showed wide receivers and running backs ranked 2nd benefited most, but only when the star QB played 50%+ of team games. Qualitative evidence consistently described falling stars in mentorship and complementarity roles across teams.

## Limitations
Small sample size inherent to the rarity of NFL star QBs (n=30 stars, 36 mobility events) limits statistical power in split-sample analyses. Sports context may limit generalizability to other industries, though the authors argue sufficient contextual overlap exists. The study cannot fully disentangle knowledge sharing from complementarity mechanisms using observational data. Individual-level performance metrics for non-QB offensive players (receivers, running backs) have limited statistical availability. The study is limited to the QB position and may not generalize to other positions.

## Practical Implications
NFL front offices should not dismiss the organizational value of late-career star players solely based on declining individual statistics. The peer and complementarity effects may justify roster investment, particularly when supporting cast quality at adjacent positions (wide receiver, running back) is high. When evaluating veteran QB acquisitions, organizations should assess how well the veteran's accumulated knowledge and style complement existing skill position players. Development staff should create intentional player pairings that maximize complementarity between veterans and developing players to amplify spillover effects.

## Athlete Development Notes
Findings are NFL QB-specific and directly applicable to American football roster strategy. Veteran star QBs in the twilight of their careers can serve as performance multipliers for younger offensive skill players through complementarity effects and accumulated organizational knowledge. Development programs should structure mentorship-oriented roster compositions that pair declining veterans with high-potential younger players at adjacent positions, particularly when the veteran is expected to play a significant portion of games.

## Return to Play Notes
This paper does not directly address return-to-play protocols as it is organizational and performance-economics focused. However, the finding that falling star QBs provide peer spillover value even at reduced individual performance levels has an indirect RTP implication: veteran players recovering from injury who are no longer at peak performance may still contribute meaningful team-level value through peer effects when reintegrated, supporting a role-adjusted RTP model for aging athletes rather than binary play/no-play decisions.
