# Eichner ER. College Football: Conditioning Them to Death; Splenic Infarction: Keeping it in the Family. Curr Sports Med Rep. 2018;17(9):278-279.

## TLDR
Thirty non-traumatic college football deaths since 2000 have occurred during conditioning sessions — not games or practice — driven by exertional sickling (12), cardiac disease (10), exertional heat stroke (7), and asthma (1). The common theme is 'too much, too fast, too soon, too long.' The NCAA's 2010 SCT screening program reduced ECAST deaths from 10 in the prior decade to 1 in 8 years of the program. EHS is preventable with sound conditioning practices. Football programs must adopt scientifically based, progressively loaded, appropriately monitored conditioning protocols — and coaches who enforce reckless sessions must be held accountable.

## Abstract
This editorial commentary by Dr. E. Randy Eichner reviews the epidemiology of non-traumatic college football deaths since 2000, identifying that 30 of 32 such deaths occurred during conditioning sessions rather than practice or gameplay. The 30 conditioning deaths are attributed to exertional sickling associated with sickle cell trait (ECAST, n=12), cardiac disease (n=10), exertional heat stroke (EHS, n=7), and asthma (n=1). The piece also presents two published family case reports of altitude-induced splenic infarction in individuals with sickle cell trait.

## Methods
Narrative editorial/commentary. Epidemiological data sourced from Scott Anderson (head AT, University of Oklahoma), who tracked college non-traumatic football deaths from 2000 onward and published in Journal of Athletic Training (2017). Second section presents two previously published case reports of altitude-related splenic sickling in father-son pairs with sickle cell trait (West J Med 1985; Int J Appl Basic Med Res 2017).

## Findings
30 of 32 college non-traumatic football deaths (since 2000) occurred during conditioning, comprising 12 ECAST, 10 cardiac, 7 EHS, 1 asthma. NCAA SCT screening program (initiated 2010) cut ECAST deaths in Division-I football from 10 in the prior decade to 1 in 8 program years. One university admitted negligence in the single ECAST death under the program. EHS deaths continued; a June 2018 lineman death followed 10x110-yard sprints and required emergency liver transplant. Liver transplant has been successfully performed for EHS but is not a reliable cure. Two familial SCT splenic infarction cases presented at altitude (7,200 ft and 12,756 ft); one pair required splenectomy, the other recovered conservatively in 5-10 days.

## Limitations
Editorial/opinion format without systematic data collection or statistical analysis; cause-of-death classification relies on available reports and may include misclassification (e.g., HCM misclassified as EHS); limited individual conditioning program detail; the cardiac death category may partially overlap with heat-related or sickling mechanisms; external reviews of implicated programs did not reliably prevent recurrence.

## Practical Implications
Strength and conditioning coaches must design sessions that are scientifically based, football-specific, progressively loaded, and appropriately monitored. Pre-participation SCT screening and strict adherence to NCAA SCT guidelines is mandatory and demonstrably saves lives. EHS prevention requires avoiding extreme single-session volumes, especially in heat and early in the conditioning cycle. Athletes with SCT traveling to altitude require counseling about splenic infarction risk. Programs with prior catastrophic events must implement sustained cultural and procedural change beyond external review.

## Athlete Development Notes
Conditioning programs for college and professional football athletes must apply progressive overload principles and avoid extreme single-session volumes such as mass repetitions of maximum-distance sprints for untested or returning players. Athletes with sickle cell trait require specific intensity and recovery modifications. Early-season and return-from-offseason conditioning sessions carry the highest catastrophic risk and demand the most careful medical oversight and dose escalation.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes who experience EHS or ECAST require complete medical clearance before return to conditioning or competition. Liver transplant recipients and those with severe multi-organ involvement face protracted and uncertain RTP timelines. Athletes with SCT who suffer splenic infarction can typically return after conservative management (descent, rest, monitoring) but must be educated about recurrence risk at altitude and during high-intensity exercise.
