# Clements, E., Ehrmann, F., Clark, A., Jones, M., McCall, A., & Duffield, R. (2023). Flight path and scheduling effects on perceived jet lag, fatigue, and sleep in footballers traveling to and from national teams. International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance. https://doi.org/10.1123/ijspp.2023-0027

## TLDR
Perceived jet-lag ratings are more sensitive to travel demands than general wellness scales in national-team footballers. Europe-to-Australia and Asia-to-Europe routes are most disruptive. Returning to clubs after national team duties is harder on players than traveling to camp. Scheduling arrivals during daytime (11 AM–5 PM) yields the best perceptual recovery outcomes.

## Abstract
This study examined posttravel perceptual responses of national-team footballers (soccer) following different flight paths, arrival/departure times, and trip contexts. Details of 396 flights from 68 national-team players were obtained. Each player provided ratings of perceptual fatigue, sleep, soreness, stress, and jet lag for 2 days before and after each trip. Linear mixed models compared the pretravel with posttravel change in perceptual responses based on flight path, context, and schedule. Perceived jet-lag ratings were more responsive to travel variables (R2 = .48) than other perceptual ratings (R2 < .26). Asia-to-Europe and Europe-to-Australia travel produced the highest jet-lag ratings. Fatigue and sleep scores were worst following Europe-to-Australia travel. Return travel from national team to club produced poorer perceptual responses than all other contexts. Daytime arrivals (11 AM–5 PM) resulted in better perceptual outcomes than early-morning or late-night arrivals.

## Methods
Observational longitudinal study using 396 verified flights from 68 senior male national-team soccer players (March 2018–July 2022). Players completed daily perceptual questionnaires (7-point Likert scales for fatigue, sleep, soreness, stress; 10-point jet-lag scale) for 2 days pre- and up to 3 days post-travel. Flight paths were categorized by departure/arrival continent, trip context (outbound, transition, return), and arrival/departure time windows. Linear mixed models with player as random effect were built stepwise; post hoc pairwise comparisons used estimated marginal means.

## Findings
Jet-lag scores were most sensitive to travel variables (R2 = .48). Asia-to-Europe (p < .05) and Europe-to-Australia (p < .001) produced highest jet-lag ratings. Fatigue was worst after Asia-to-Europe and Europe-to-Australia. Sleep was worst after Europe-to-Australia. Return travel (national team to club) produced poorer total wellness, fatigue, sleep, and soreness than outbound or transition travel (p < .05). Daytime arrivals yielded significantly lower total wellness, fatigue, sleep, and soreness than morning arrivals; night arrivals produced highest stress scores. Older players reported higher jet lag; more experienced players reported lower jet lag.

## Limitations
Limited control over athlete behavior before, during, or after travel. Low R2 values for wellness scales suggest external factors beyond travel affect scores. Pretravel baseline used a single-day measure, increasing susceptibility to noise. Perceived jet-lag ratings collected at a single daily time point. Insufficient Australia-to-Europe trip data due to match scheduling and COVID-related venue changes.

## Practical Implications
Use jet-lag ratings (not just wellness scales) to monitor national-team travel stress. Prioritize travel management interventions for Europe-to-Australia and Asia-to-Europe routes. Provide additional recovery support when athletes return to clubs post-camp. Where logistically possible, schedule arrivals between 11 AM and 5 PM. Develop individualized travel strategies for younger or less-experienced players who show greater jet-lag vulnerability.

## Athlete Development Notes
Travel load accumulation during multi-trip national team windows can significantly impair perceived recovery. Development of travel literacy and routine (sleep hygiene, light exposure, nutrition timing) in younger national-team players may reduce jet-lag susceptibility over time.

## Return to Play Notes
Return travel after national-team camp represents a distinct recovery challenge beyond the match itself. Practitioners managing athletes returning from international duty should factor in travel-induced fatigue layered on top of match load when planning RTP timelines and training loads in the days following return.
