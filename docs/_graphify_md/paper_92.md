# Schwarz E, Duffield R, Novak AR, Compton DA, Meyer T. Associations Between Match-Play Characteristics and Environmental Temperatures in 4 Professional Football Leagues. European Journal of Sport Science. 2025;00:e12256. https://doi.org/10.1002/ejsc.12256

## TLDR
Across four professional football leagues spanning Germany, Spain, and Australia, higher temperatures significantly reduced the volume of match actions—particularly passes, touches, and take-ons—while key passes, shots, goals, and pass success rates were maintained. These small but consistent effects suggest players adopt a pacing strategy in the heat, reducing overall match activity while preserving quality. Football performance and coaching staff should anticipate a more static, slower style of play in warm weather, and may consider heat acclimatization, hydration protocols, pre-cooling, and tactical adjustments that favor efficiency over volume of ball involvement.

## Abstract
This observational study investigated the association between environmental temperature (dry-bulb temperature [T] and wet-bulb globe temperature [WBGT]) and 17 match-play performance indicators (PIs) across 1,585 matches from four professional football leagues: German Bundesliga 1 and 2, Spanish La Liga, and Australian A-League. Environmental data were obtained retrospectively from public sources. Using linear regression per league and linear mixed models across all four leagues, 8 of 17 PIs were significantly associated with both T and WBGT (p<0.002). In hotter conditions, passes (especially short passes), touches, take-ons, and turnovers were all reduced, while pass success rates were slightly higher.

## Methods
Observational multi-league study of 1,585 professional male football matches (natural grass): Bundesliga 1 (306 matches, 2021–22), Bundesliga 2 (306 matches, 2021–22), La Liga (520 matches, 2021–23), Australian A-League (453 matches, 2016–20). Match performance data came from FBREF (European leagues) and A-League organizations, all generated via Opta/STATS PERFORM optical tracking. Environmental T and WBGT were obtained retrospectively via Meteostat.net (European leagues) and UBIMET GmbH (A-League); WBGT was estimated with the validated Liljegren et al. (2008) model, with wind speed standardized to 1 m/s. A total of 17 PIs across shooting, passing, dribbling, and defending categories were analyzed. For each league, linear regression modeled PI vs. T and WBGT. A combined linear mixed model (league as random effect) was fit across all 1,585 matches. Bonferroni correction applied (significance threshold p<0.00294). Effect size categories: small β 0.10–0.29, medium 0.30–0.49, large >0.50.

## Findings
Environmental conditions ranged from −1.7°C to 37.1°C T and −2.0°C to 29.6°C WBGT across leagues. Combined model results per 1°C T increase: total passes −2.3 (95%CI −3.1 to −1.5; p<0.001; β=−0.17); short passes −1.8 (95%CI −2.4 to −1.8; p<0.001; β=−0.17); pass success rate +0.06% (p=0.001); passes into final third −0.18 (95%CI −0.25 to −0.05; p=0.001); total touches −3.25 (95%CI −4.05 to −2.44; p<0.001; β=−0.23); touches in offensive third −0.9 (95%CI −1.2 to −0.6; p<0.001); take-ons −0.11 (p<0.001; β=−0.10); turnovers −1.27 (95%CI −1.53 to −1.02; p<0.001; β=−0.28). No significant associations for goals, shots, long passes, key passes, tackles, fouls, or cards (all p>0.04). All significant effects were small (β: 0.10–0.29).

## Limitations
Environmental data were obtained retrospectively rather than measured directly at stadiums, with possible error especially for solar radiation and wind. Analysis was at the match level (both teams combined), not individual team or player level. PI definitions varied across leagues, partially addressed by including league as a random effect. Very few matches occurred under high or extreme WBGT conditions (3 extreme, 10 high, 142 moderate), limiting power to detect effects at the most dangerous heat levels. Female cohorts were not studied. Player acclimatization status, playing style differences, and competition structures were not controlled.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football staff should be aware that heat stress meaningfully reduces match actions in football, with direct implications for warm-weather game planning and preseason camp design. While association football and American football differ structurally, the underlying physiology (impaired cognitive performance, early fatigue, altered pacing) is shared. Staff should anticipate reduced technical tempo in the heat, ensure adequate hydration and pre-cooling protocols, and note that quality metrics are maintained even as volume decreases—suggesting self-regulation rather than performance degradation.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football players training through summer conditioning or preseason in warm climates, heat acclimatization is important not only for safety but for maintaining technical output. The consistent reduction in touches and take-ons in hotter conditions suggests that technical training quality may decline in hot environments, which should factor into periodization decisions—scheduling high-skill technical work during cooler parts of the day or season.

## Return to Play Notes
Players returning from injury in warm conditions may face a reduced technical and physical demand environment that does not fully replicate the match stresses used to benchmark RTP readiness. Staff should account for environmental conditions when interpreting performance data collected during warm-weather training or competition, and may need to apply corrected benchmarks when heat is expected to suppress normal performance metrics.
