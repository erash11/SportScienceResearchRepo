# Krill MK, Borchers JR, Hoffman JT, Krill ML, Hewett TE. Analysis of Football Injuries by Position Group in Division I College Football: A 5-Year Program Review. Clin J Sport Med. 2018;0:1-8.

## TLDR
Skill position players had the highest overall injury rate—particularly in the preseason from noncontact mechanisms—while line and other position players sustained most injuries during the regular season from contact mechanisms; reducing preseason soft-tissue muscle strains in skill players and contact exposures for linemen represent the best opportunities to lower collegiate football injury rates.

## Abstract
This prospective, observational study evaluated injury characteristics across three position groups—line (offensive and defensive linemen), skill (defensive backs, wide receivers, running backs), and other (linebackers, tight ends, quarterbacks, special teams)—in a single major Division I collegiate football program over five fall regular seasons (2012–2016). A total of 200 injuries from 48,615 athlete exposures (AEs) were analyzed. Injury rates (IRs) per 1000 AEs and injury rate ratios (IRRs) were calculated across time of season, body part, type of injury, mechanism of injury, and type of exposure.

## Methods
Prospective observational study at a single Division I program (2012–2016); NCAA Injury Surveillance System (ISS) reportable injury definition; injuries classified by time in season (preseason, first half, second half), body part, type (sprain, strain, fracture, concussion, etc.), mechanism (contact vs. noncontact), and exposure type (noncontact, full-pad, live contact); injury rates per 1000 AEs with 95% CIs; IRRs compared using Mid-P exact tests; weighted average AEs by position group calculated from full team roster.

## Findings
• Overall 5-year IR was 4.11 per 1000 AEs (95% CI 3.57–4.72). • Skill players had highest IR (7.56 per 1000 AEs) vs. line (4.26) and other (4.10). • Skill players had significantly higher IRR in preseason vs. line (IRR 1.75, P<0.05) and other (IRR 1.85, P<0.05). • Preseason IR was highest across all groups (5.47 per 1000 AEs). • Skill players sustained 50.5% of injuries from noncontact MOI vs. ~65–74% contact MOI for line and other. • Lower extremity injuries most common across all groups; skill players had significantly higher upper leg/thigh IRR. • Sprains, strains, and concussions were the three most common injury types across all groups. • No significant IRRs between position groups and type of exposure.

## Limitations
Single institution limits generalizability to other Division I programs; inexact AE counts by position group on a daily basis required weighted averages; inability to track weather, playing surface, or other environmental variables; skill position players may be underrepresented in AE calculations due to positional roster variability.

## Practical Implications
Targeting preseason noncontact muscle strain reduction for skill position players (e.g., progressive loading, movement preparation protocols) represents a high-leverage injury prevention opportunity. Reducing contact exposures for linemen and other position players may have the greatest impact on concussions and contact mechanism injuries.

## Athlete Development Notes
American football programs should differentiate injury prevention strategies by position group: skill players (DBs, WRs, RBs) require preseason emphasis on soft-tissue strain prevention through workload management and neuromuscular training, while linemen and linebackers benefit most from contact exposure reduction protocols during camp. The preseason period carries the highest injury risk across all positions.

## Return to Play Notes
The study did not specifically analyze return-to-play timelines, but concussion was the third most common injury type across all position groups (IR 0.84 per 1000 AEs for all combined), with the other position group (LBs, TEs, QBs) sustaining the highest concussion IR (1.00 per 1000 AEs). Position-specific concussion data can inform targeted monitoring and RTP protocols for the most at-risk groups.
