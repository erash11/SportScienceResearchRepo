# Valente HG, Oliveira RR, Baroni BM. How are hamstring strain injuries managed in elite men's football clubs? A survey with 62 Brazilian physical therapists. Phys Ther Sport. 2023;61:73-81.

## TLDR
In elite Brazilian football, HSI rehabilitation is near-universally multimodal — combining electrophysical agents, manual therapy, progressive strengthening (almost always with eccentric overload), lumbopelvic stabilisation, and sport-specific exercises — yet meaningful heterogeneity exists in assessment depth and classification. Many practitioners rely on subjective range-of-motion and manual strength testing despite evidence supporting objective tools. Muscle strength is the dominant RTP criterion, aligning with expert consensus, but sprint and functional performance criteria are also frequently used.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional survey study describes the assessment and rehabilitation practices of physical therapists managing hamstring strain injury (HSI) in elite Brazilian men's football. Sixty-two physical therapists from 35 of 40 eligible clubs (87.5% representativeness) competing in the 2021 Brazilian Championship Series A and B responded. Despite heterogeneous assessment practices, all respondents used imaging exams, applied injury classification systems (most commonly Munich Consensus, 45.2%), and evaluated pain, range of motion, muscle strength, and functional status. Rehabilitation programs were typically divided into 3–4 phases. All respondents applied electrophysical agents and stretching; 98.4% used strengthening exercises (93.5% including eccentrics); 96.8% used manual therapy; 95.2% applied football-specific functional exercises; and 93.5% incorporated lumbopelvic stabilisation exercises. Muscle strength was the most frequently reported RTP criterion (71% of respondents).

## Methods
Cross-sectional study using an online questionnaire developed by the authors and delivered in Brazilian Portuguese. Participants: physical therapists from clubs in the 2021 Brazilian Men's Football Championship Series A (20 clubs) and Series B (20 clubs); 95 invited, 62 responded (65% adherence) from 35 clubs (87.5% representativeness). Questionnaire covered 30 questions in three sections: workplace structure/organisation, HSI assessment practices, and HSI rehabilitation practices. Distributed via WhatsApp Messenger or email; data collected March–December 2021. Researchers followed up with participants to clarify ambiguous responses. Descriptive statistics (frequency distributions, means, SDs) computed in Microsoft Excel. Ethics approval: Federal University of Health Sciences of Porto Alegre (#4.838.243).

## Findings
All 62 respondents used imaging exams; 100% used injury classification systems (Munich Consensus 45.2%, British Classification 24.2%). Initial evaluation timing varied: 35.5% preferred 12–24 h post-injury, 29% first 12 h. All assessed pain, strength, and functional status. Strength testing: 83.9% used manual resistance testing; only 35.5% used hand-held dynamometry. Range-of-motion assessment: 58.1% quantitative, 56.5% subjective. 90.3% organised rehabilitation into phases (3 phases: 45.2%; 4 phases: 33.9%). All used electrophysical agents (analgesic currents 93.5%, photobiomodulation 88.7%, cryotherapy 87.1%). Manual therapy: 96.8% (myofascial release 95.2%). Strengthening: 98.4%; eccentric exercises 93.5%. Lumbopelvic stabilisation: 93.5%. Football-specific exercises: 95.2%. Periodic rehabilitation assessments: 100% (pain 83.9%, strength 82.3%). RTP criteria: muscle strength 71%, sprint/high-speed running 58%, intense accelerations 43.5%, pain 43.5%.

## Limitations
Only Brazilian physical therapists participated; results may not reflect practices in other countries due to differences in professional training, culture, and club resources. Self-reported questionnaire data are subject to response bias and inconsistencies. The study captures perceptions and stated practices, which may differ from actual clinical practice. Female football clubs not included. Coordination of field transition phases between physical therapists and strength/conditioning coaches was not investigated.

## Practical Implications
The near-universal adoption of eccentric strengthening (93.5%), lumbopelvic stabilisation (93.5%), and sport-specific exercises (95.2%) by elite Brazilian football PTs aligns well with current clinical practice guidelines. However, the low uptake of objective strength measurement tools (only 35.5% using HHD) and objective ROM quantification suggests a gap between available evidence and common practice. Departments should invest in training staff in objective assessment and implementing validated RTP criteria beyond subjective judgment. Electrophysical agents are widely used despite limited evidence; resources may be better directed towards supervised exercise.

## Athlete Development Notes
The structured, phase-based rehabilitation approach (3–4 phases) and progressive incorporation of sport-specific football demands — including high-speed running, change of direction, sprints, jumps, and passing/kicking — reflects best-practice principles for returning athletes to full football performance. Early introduction of eccentric loading and lumbopelvic stabilisation exercises supports tissue remodelling and neuromuscular control critical for preventing re-injury. This survey provides a real-world benchmark for what comprehensive HSI rehabilitation looks like in elite football environments.

## Return to Play Notes
Muscle strength (71%) and sprint/high-speed running performance (58%) were the most commonly reported RTP criteria among elite Brazilian football physical therapists, broadly consistent with Delphi consensus recommendations. However, objective strength measurement tools were underutilised, potentially limiting the precision of RTP decisions. Staff should adopt standardised, objective RTP benchmarks — including hamstring strength symmetry, functional hop tests, and GPS-monitored sprint performance — to improve the reliability of RTP clearance and reduce the ~18% recurrence rate reported in the literature.
