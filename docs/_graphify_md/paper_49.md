# Baker HP, Volchenko E, Rossi D, Dayton SR, Tjong VK. Analysis of National Football League preseason injuries resulting in roster cuts: a retrospective review. Curr Orthop Pract. 2021;32(1):1-5.

## TLDR
A preseason injury results in a roster cut at a rate of 1.59 per 1,000 AEs, and 75% of those players never play another NFL season. Being undrafted or drafted in round 7 is the dominant risk profile — over 75% of cut players fell into these two groups.

## Abstract
This retrospective review examined all injuries sustained by NFL athletes during the 2014–2019 preseasons that resulted in a player being cut from the team roster. A total of 110 injuries were identified from publicly available injury reports across 32 NFL teams. The incidence of a preseason injury leading to a roster cut was 1.59 per 1,000 athletic exposures (AEs). The lower extremity was the most frequently injured body region (45% of injuries), and undrafted free agents comprised the majority of cut players (63%). Most athletes (75%) did not play another year in the NFL following a preseason roster cut.

## Methods
Retrospective review of publicly available NFL injury reports from the 2014–2019 preseasons; injury incidence calculated per 1,000 AEs (one AE = one athlete appearing in one game); demographic data including draft round, years in NFL, position, body area, and pathology collected from team websites and public sources; SPSS 25 used for descriptive statistics.

## Findings
• 110 injuries identified across 2014–2019 NFL preseasons resulted in a roster cut
• Injury incidence prior to cut: 1.59 per 1,000 AEs
• Most common position cut: defensive back (24 players, 22%), followed by linebacker (19, 17%) and tight end (16, 15%)
• 69 players (63%) were undrafted; 14 (13%) were 7th-round picks
• Rookies comprised 35% of cut players; 75% had 0 years of prior NFL experience
• Lower extremity most common body area (45%); ankle sprains most common pathology (14 injuries, 13%), followed by knee (12, 11%)
• 82 of 110 players (75%) did not play another NFL season after being cut
• Only 28 players (25%) made another 53-man roster; only 3 cases (3%) required surgery

## Limitations
Retrospective design relying on publicly available injury data without access to team physician records or NFL Injury Surveillance System (NFLISS); specific pathology details and surgical information often unavailable; unable to determine causal factors (player talent, team finances) for cut decisions; concussion data likely underreported; surgery data may be substantially underreported.

## Practical Implications
Preseason injury risk is concentrated in rookie and undrafted players, suggesting these athletes may benefit from enhanced preseason injury surveillance and load management protocols. NFL teams should weigh the financial disincentive to retain injured borderline-roster players against long-term development value. Defensive backs and linebackers are at highest positional risk for preseason roster cuts following injury.

## Athlete Development Notes
In American football, undrafted and late-round players face career-ending consequences from preseason injuries at disproportionate rates. Programs should identify these high-risk athletes early and implement position-specific lower extremity injury prevention programs. The data support prioritizing ankle and knee injury prevention — particularly for defensive backs — during preseason camps.

## Return to Play Notes
75% of NFL players who sustained a preseason injury that led to a roster cut never played another NFL season, underscoring the career-ending potential of preseason injuries for marginal roster players. Only 25% made another 53-man roster. RTP decisions for preseason injuries must account not only for clinical recovery but also roster and contract implications; early physician referral and transparent communication with player personnel staff is essential.
