# Guitart-Trench M, Sanchez-Sanchez J, Valle X, Garcia-Unanue J, Cos F, Alonso-Callejo A, Rodas G, Felipe JL. Does accumulated physical load in different time windows affect hamstring injuries in elite football players? Research in Sports Medicine. 2025. 10.1080/15438627.2025.2468799

## TLDR
Only the 28-day accumulated load window reliably discriminated between injured and non-injured periods in elite youth/development football players — the 7- and 14-day windows showed no significant differences. Players sustained hamstring injuries after 28-day periods of significantly higher total distance, HSR, sprint counts, acceleration/deceleration load, and high metabolic load compared to the preceding 28-day block. The ACWR was not associated with injury severity. For football staff this means that short-term workload spikes captured in weekly monitoring may be insufficient — chronic 28-day load accumulation, particularly in high-speed and sprint volume, should be the primary monitoring target for hamstring injury risk management.

## Abstract
This descriptive longitudinal study investigated how accumulated training load over 7-, 14-, and 28-day time windows influences the incidence of hamstring injuries in 343 elite academy football players (2nd team, U-19, U-18) from a Spanish La Liga club competing in UEFA Champions League across four seasons (2017/18–2020/21). External load variables — total distance (TD), high-speed running (HSR), accelerations, decelerations, sprint count, and high metabolic load (HML) — were collected via GPS (WIMU PRO) and compared between injury blocks and pre-injury blocks for each time window. The acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR) was also analysed.

## Methods
Descriptive longitudinal study; 343 male academy players from three teams of one Spanish La Liga club (2nd Team, U-19, U-18) across four seasons (2017/18 to 2020/21). Total 27,895 h training sessions, 1,488 h friendly matches, 6,551 h official matches. GPS data collected via WIMU PRO (RealtrackSystems, Spain; ICC x: 0.65, y: 0.85). External load variables: TD, MIR (18–21 km/h), HIR (21–24 km/h), HSR (>21 km/h), sprint distance (>24 km/h), sprint count, HI-ACC (>3 m/s²), HI-DCC (<−3 m/s²), HML (>25 W/kg²). Time windows: 7, 14, 28 days; injury block vs. pre-injury block. Injuries per UEFA consensus guidelines; classified using OSICS_10. Linear mixed models for repeated measurement; independent t-test for group comparisons; ACWR rolling 7:28. IBM SPSS 28.0.

## Findings
The 28-day time window showed significant differences between injury and pre-injury blocks for all analysed variables (p < 0.05): TD (+13,605.61 m; ES: 0.50), HSR (+725.41 m; ES: 0.48), HI-ACC (+119.42 m; ES: 0.49), HI-DCC (+133.99 m; ES: 0.48), sprint count (+18.81; ES: 0.48), HML (+2,689.54 m; ES: 0.55). Speed zone distances at 18–21 km/h (+581.83 m; ES: 0.51), 21–24 km/h (+333.08 m; ES: 0.52), and >24 km/h (+204.40 m; ES: 0.37) were also significantly higher. The 7- and 14-day windows showed no significant differences (p > 0.05). Hamstring muscle injury players showed higher load than medical attention players across all time windows for HSR and sprint variables. ACWR was not significantly different between injury and medical attention groups.

## Limitations
Study confined to academy players (2nd team and youth squads) from a single club, limiting generalisability to senior professionals. Goalkeepers were excluded. Individual variability in load response was not fully addressed despite linear mixed model use. Injuries classified as grade 0 (not forcing absence) were included alongside time-loss injuries, which may dilute findings. ACWR methodology has been criticised for its statistical properties in the broader literature.

## Practical Implications
Strength and conditioning coaches in football should extend their workload monitoring window to 28 days for hamstring injury risk assessment rather than relying exclusively on acute weekly loads. When the 28-day accumulated load — particularly HSR distance, sprint count, and HML — spikes significantly above the preceding 28-day period, hamstring injury risk is elevated. Weekly fluctuations (7-day windows) are insufficient predictors. Staff should compare current 28-day blocks to the prior 28-day block for each player, not to a seasonal average, when making rotation or load management decisions.

## Athlete Development Notes
Development players (U-18, U-19, reserve teams) accumulating high chronic loads over 28-day blocks — especially in sprint and high-speed running volume — face elevated hamstring injury risk. Programming for these age groups should build sprint and high-speed running capacity progressively across the season, avoiding abrupt 28-day load spikes. Periodisation models should account for match congestion periods, which can compress recovery and cause undetected chronic load accumulation in shorter monitoring windows.

## Return to Play Notes
When returning a player from hamstring injury to full training and match play, the accumulated load of the 28-day re-introduction period should be carefully managed to avoid surpassing the chronic load baseline from the prior 28-day period. The absence of ACWR differences between injury groups suggests ratio-based monitoring alone is insufficient for RTP load management decisions; absolute 28-day volume targets — particularly for HSR and sprint distance — should be established and tracked for each athlete returning from hamstring injury.
