# Wittrup EM, Fox LA, Breedlove KM, Memmini AK, Broglio SP. Evaluation of Musculoskeletal Re-Injury Occurrence in Previously Concussed National Football League Athletes. Open Access Journal of Sports Medicine. 2020;11:169–176.

## TLDR
NFL players who return to play after a concussion are more than twice as likely to suffer a musculoskeletal injury in the following 12 weeks compared to healthy matched controls. This elevated risk is comparable in magnitude to that seen after an acute MSK injury, suggesting concussion may leave residual neuromuscular vulnerabilities even after clinical clearance. These findings reinforce the need for continued neuromuscular monitoring beyond standard RTP protocols.

## Abstract
This retrospective cohort study examined whether NFL athletes face elevated musculoskeletal (MSK) injury risk following return to play (RTP) from sports-related concussion (SRC). Using publicly available injury reports from all 32 NFL teams across the 2013–2017 seasons, 91 concussed athletes were matched to 91 athletes with acute MSK injuries and 91 healthy controls (matched on team, season, and position). MSK injuries in the 12 weeks post-RTP were tracked. Concussed athletes were 2.35 times more likely (OR 2.35, 95% CI 1.25–4.44; p=0.01) to sustain a subsequent MSK injury compared to healthy controls. There was no significant difference in odds between SRC and MSK-injured athletes (p=0.55), and MSK-injured athletes were not significantly more likely than controls to sustain a subsequent injury (OR 1.87, p=0.08). SRC athletes also sustained significantly more total MSK injuries during follow-up.

## Methods
Retrospective cohort study using public NFL injury reports (2013–2017). Concussed athletes (n=91) returning to play between weeks 3–8 of the regular season were matched to an acute MSK-injured control and a healthy control on team, season, and position. MSK injury occurrence was tracked for 12 weeks post-RTP. Statistical analyses included Wilcoxon rank-sum tests for total injury counts, Fisher's exact test for odds ratios, and Cohen's d for effect sizes.

## Findings
Concussed athletes were 2.35 times more likely to sustain a subsequent MSK injury than healthy controls (OR 2.35, 95% CI 1.25–4.44; p=0.01). SRC athletes sustained significantly more total MSK injuries than controls (p<0.01), as did MSK-injured athletes (p=0.04). No significant difference in odds existed between SRC and MSK groups (p=0.55). Average cumulative MSK injuries per athlete: SRC=0.65, MSK=0.47, Control=0.27.

## Limitations
Injury reporting relies on public NFL records that may underreport injuries lasting less than one week or occurring during bye weeks or preseason. Injury details lack severity metrics beyond weeks missed. Prior injury history was not controlled. Equal playing time was assumed across athletes. NFL bye weeks were not factored into weeks-missed calculations. Study design was retrospective and observational.

## Practical Implications
Standard concussion RTP protocols based solely on clinical recovery may be insufficient. Staff should consider implementing extended neuromuscular screening and movement quality monitoring in the weeks following concussion RTP. Programs addressing the residual effects of concussion on postural stability, reaction time, and motor cortex function may reduce secondary MSK injury risk.

## Athlete Development Notes
Young athletes returning to play after concussion may carry elevated MSK injury risk that persists beyond symptom resolution. Development programs should integrate post-concussion neuromuscular training to address subclinical motor control deficits. Tracking injury occurrence in the 8–12 weeks post-RTP is warranted as part of a comprehensive athlete development monitoring framework.

## Return to Play Notes
Concussion RTP decisions should account for the 2.35-fold increased MSK injury risk in the 12 weeks following return. Objective neuromuscular assessments (e.g., balance, reaction time, gait) should be part of post-concussion RTP progressions. Staff should monitor for secondary MSK injuries and consider graduated reintegration into full-contact practice to reduce exposure risk during the vulnerable post-concussion window.
