# Iverson GL, Jamshidi P, Fisher-Hubbard AO, Deep-Soboslay A, Hyde TM, Kleinman JE, deJong JL, Shepherd CE, Hazrati L-N, Castellani RJ. Chronic traumatic encephalopathy neuropathologic change is uncommon in men who played amateur American football. Front Neurol. 2023;14:1143882. doi:10.3389/fneur.2023.1143882

## TLDR
In a postmortem brain bank sample of 186 men—over a quarter of whom played amateur American football—no definitive cases of CTE-NC were found, and only 5.4% had possible features by some raters. Critically, there was no statistically significant association between playing amateur football and having features of CTE-NC, nor between CTE-NC and suicide or mood disorders. This is an important counterpoint to studies focused on former professional players, suggesting that CTE-NC is uncommon in men who played football at the amateur (high school/college) level. For football programs at youth through college levels, this study provides meaningful context that the risk of CTE-NC from amateur participation appears to be low, though longitudinal population-based studies with quantified exposure are still needed.

## Abstract
This postmortem neuropathological study examined brain tissue from 186 men (median age 65 years, range 50–96) obtained from the Lieber Institute for Brain Development to assess the prevalence of CTE neuropathologic change (CTE-NC) using both the 2016 and 2021 consensus definitions. Of the sample, 25.8% had a history of playing American football. No case was definitively identified as CTE-NC by all five expert neuropathology raters; 10 cases (5.4%) were identified as having possible features of CTE-NC by three or more of five raters. There were no statistically significant associations between CTE-NC features and football history, TBI history, mood disorder, or suicide as manner of death.

## Methods
Postmortem brain tissue and clinical information from 186 men aged 50–96 were obtained from the Lieber Institute for Brain Development (LIBD) in collaboration with the Western Michigan University Homer Stryker M.D. School of Medicine Medical Examiner's Office. Next-of-kin telephone interviews provided medical, social, demographic, family, and psychiatric histories. A minimum of seven brain regions were sampled per donor and stained with p-tau (AT8 antibody, 1:250) and amyloid-beta (BAM01, 1:50) immunohistochemistry. Two authors blinded to clinical data screened all 186 cases; 15 positively screened cases were then reviewed by five independent neuropathology experts from three countries and four academic institutions. Both 2016 and 2021 consensus CTE-NC definitions were applied. Chi-square tests used for proportion comparisons; Mann-Whitney U for age comparisons.

## Findings
No case was rated as definitively having CTE-NC by all five raters under either definition. 10 cases (5.4%) had features of CTE-NC by three or more of five raters. Football history: 8.3% of football players vs 3.9% of those with no contact sport history had features of CTE-NC (chi-square not significant, p=0.235). Contact sports broadly: 8.6% vs 3.9% (p=0.187). Mood disorder: 5.5% with vs 6.0% without (p=0.725). Suicide as manner of death: 6.0% vs 5.0% (p=0.788). TBI history: 4.0% vs 6.2% (p=0.565). The 10 cases with CTE-NC features were a median 11.5 years older than those without (M=73.9 vs M=65.8), though not significant (p=0.09). Most of the 10 cases had modified Braak scores III–VI and neuritic plaques indicating concomitant Alzheimer-type pathology.

## Limitations
Football history was binary with no quantification of years played, position, level of play, or estimated head impact exposure. The sample is a convenience sample from a brain bank specializing in neuropsychiatric donors and is not representative of the general population; over one-third died by suicide. Brain sampling, while extensive, was less than recommended by consensus guidelines; additional sulcal sampling might have revealed more CTE-NC. Study was underpowered to detect small group differences. Exposure information obtained from next of kin is subject to recall bias. Nearly all participants were white (98.4%), limiting generalizability.

## Practical Implications
This study provides evidence that CTE-NC is uncommon in men who played amateur American football, which is relevant for counseling youth athletes, parents, and coaches about long-term brain health risk. The lack of significant association between CTE-NC features and amateur football history distinguishes this population from highly exposed former professional players. Football program administrators and sports medicine staff should continue to follow concussion protocols rigorously and minimize unnecessary head impacts in practice, while communicating to families that CTE risk from amateur participation appears to be low based on current neuropathological evidence.

## Athlete Development Notes
For youth through college football programs, this study supports a nuanced risk-communication approach: the neuropathological changes associated with CTE appear uncommon in men who played at the amateur level, and absolute prevalence of CTE-NC features was statistically similar regardless of contact sport history. Emphasis should remain on proper tackling technique, appropriate practice contact limits, and concussion identification and management, rather than wholesale avoidance of football participation. Sports scientists and team physicians should stay current with evolving consensus definitions of CTE-NC, as the diagnostic criteria changed substantially between 2016 and 2021.

## Return to Play Notes
CTE-NC is a postmortem neuropathological diagnosis and is not directly applicable to clinical return-to-play decisions. However, the broader CTE literature informs how repetitive head impacts are approached in RTP protocols. This study's finding that CTE-NC features were not significantly associated with TBI history at the amateur level suggests that individual TBI events alone may not reliably drive CTE pathology in this population. RTP after concussion should continue to follow established stepwise graduated protocols (e.g., NFL or CISG concussion protocols), prioritizing full neurological recovery and symptom resolution before contact resumption, regardless of cumulative exposure history.
