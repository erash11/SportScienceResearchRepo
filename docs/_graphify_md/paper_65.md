# Kercher KA, Steinfeldt JA, Rettke DJ, Zuidema TR, Walker MJ, Martinez Kercher VM, Silveyra P, Seo DC, Macy JT, Hulvershorn LA, Kawata K. Association Between Head Impact Exposure, Psychological Needs, and Indicators of Mental Health Among U.S. High School Tackle Football Players. Journal of Adolescent Health. 2023;xxx:1–8.

## TLDR
One season of head impact exposure and earlier age of first exposure to tackle football were not associated with worse acute mental health outcomes in high school players; however, psychological need satisfaction (autonomy, competence, relatedness) consistently predicted better mental health, underscoring the importance of psychosocial factors in adolescent athlete well-being.

## Abstract
This multisite, prospective cohort study investigated the effect of one season of head impact exposure (HIE) and age of first exposure to tackle football on acute mental health outcomes in 91 adolescent male football players (n=91) from four Midwestern high schools during the 2021 season. Sensor-installed mouthguards (Prevent Biometrics) were used to measure cumulative peak linear acceleration (PLA); mental health outcomes assessed included depressive symptoms (PHQ-9), anxiety symptoms (GAD-7), and thriving (BIT); and psychological need satisfaction was measured via the BPNSFS. A total of 9,428 head impacts were recorded with a mean of 102±113 impacts per player. Neither cumulative HIE nor age of first exposure was associated with postseason mental health outcomes or change scores from preseason to postseason. Greater psychological need satisfaction was significantly associated with fewer depressive symptoms, fewer anxiety symptoms, and greater thriving scores at postseason.

## Methods
Multisite prospective observational cohort study; 2021 high school football season; n=91 male players from four Midwestern high schools; sensor-installed mouthguards (Prevent Biometrics Impact Monitor) for head impact measurement at 3.2 kHz; film validation of 19% of impacts (94% agreement); mental health surveys at preseason (July) and postseason (November); PHQ-9 for depressive symptoms, GAD-7 for anxiety, 10-item Brief Inventory of Thriving (BIT), Basic Psychological Needs Satisfaction and Frustration Scale (BPNSFS); linear regression models; Bonferroni-corrected significance thresholds (p<.008 for postseason scores; p<.017 for change scores); age and school used as control variables.

## Findings
• 9,428 total head impacts recorded; mean 102±113 impacts per player
• Season-long cumulative PLA: 1,652±1,895 g; cumulative PRA: 117,602±145,014 rad/s²
• Neither cumulative HIE nor age of first exposure predicted postseason depression, anxiety, or thriving scores
• Cumulative HIE did not predict changes in mental health from preseason to postseason
• Greater psychological need satisfaction predicted fewer depressive symptoms (β=−0.035, p<.001), fewer anxiety symptoms (β=−0.021, p=.010), and greater thriving (β=0.278, p<.001) at postseason
• Effect sizes for psychological need satisfaction associations were moderate to large (R²=0.18–0.45)
• 4.4% of participants scored ≥10 on PHQ-9 at preseason; 5.5% at postseason

## Limitations
Predominantly White and non-Hispanic sample from Midwestern schools limits generalizability; single-season follow-up (~16 weeks) may be too short to detect neuropsychological changes; self-reported age of first exposure is subject to recall bias; male-only sample; external factors (team culture, win-loss records) not captured; study underpowered for some models; does not address chronic or long-term head impact effects.

## Practical Implications
Programs should prioritize psychosocial support and psychological need satisfaction (autonomy, competence, relatedness) for adolescent football players as a strategy to promote positive mental health. A single season of head impacts does not appear to acutely worsen mental health, but multiyear longitudinal studies are needed. Coaches and staff should create environments that fulfill athletes' basic psychological needs to support mental well-being.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American high school football. Findings suggest that the mental health of adolescent football players during a single season is more strongly influenced by psychosocial factors (psychological need satisfaction) than by the number or magnitude of head impacts sustained. This has implications for how programs structure team culture, athlete autonomy, and coach-athlete relationships to support player development and well-being.

## Return to Play Notes
The lack of acute mental health deterioration attributable to single-season HIE provides some reassurance for RTP decisions in adolescent players. However, clinicians should still screen for psychosocial factors such as psychological need satisfaction during RTP evaluation, as these predict mental health outcomes independent of head impact burden.
