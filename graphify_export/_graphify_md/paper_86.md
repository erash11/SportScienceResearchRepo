# Krill MK, Borchers JR, Hoffman JT, Krill ML, Hewett TE. Analysis of Football Injuries by Position Group in Division I College Football: A 5-Year Program Review. Clinical Journal of Sport Medicine. 2020;30(3):216-223.

## TLDR
Skill position players (DBs, WRs, RBs) carry the highest injury risk in Division I college football, especially during preseason from noncontact mechanisms targeting the upper leg and thigh, while linemen and hybrid positions sustain most injuries in the first half of the regular season from contact. For football medical and strength staffs, the clearest actionable finding is that preseason hamstring and quadriceps strain prevention in speed-position players represents the greatest single opportunity to reduce overall team injury rates. Reducing live contact exposures will have the greatest impact on concussions and contact injuries for linemen and other position groups.

## Abstract
This prospective observational study evaluated injury characteristics by position group (line, skill, other) in a single Division I collegiate football program over 5 fall regular seasons (2012-2016), recording 200 injuries from 48,615 athlete exposures (AEs). Injury rates (IRs) per 1,000 AEs and injury rate ratios (IRRs) were calculated across all monitored injury variables including time in season, body part, mechanism, injury type, and exposure type. Skill players demonstrated significantly higher IRs in the preseason, for noncontact mechanisms, and for upper leg/thigh injuries compared to line and other position groups.

## Methods
Prospective observational study of a single major Division I collegiate football program during the 2012-2016 fall regular seasons, including all games and practices. Attendance and injury data were prospectively collected by team physicians and certified athletic trainers using the NCAA Injury Surveillance System (ISS) reportable injury definition. Players classified into three position groups: line (OL/DL), skill (DBs, WRs, RBs), and other (LBs, TEs, QBs, special teams). Exposure types (noncontact, full-pad, live contact) were tracked per NCAA definitions starting in 2014. IRs per 1,000 AEs with 95% CIs and IRRs using Mid-P exact tests were calculated. Statistical significance set at p<0.05.

## Findings
200 injuries from 48,615 AEs. Overall 5-year IR: 4.11/1,000 AEs (95% CI 3.57-4.72). Skill players had the highest IR (5.18/1,000 AEs) vs. line (3.62) and other (3.27). Preseason IR: skill 7.56 vs. line 4.26 and other 4.10 (skill vs. line IRR 1.75, skill vs. other IRR 1.85, both p<0.05). Skill players had significantly higher IRR for noncontact MOI vs. line (IRR 2.07, p=0.004) and other (IRR 3.03, p<0.001). Upper leg/thigh injuries: skill vs. line IRR 6.91 (p=0.002), skill vs. other IRR 6.07 (p=0.005). Game IR (11.05/1,000 AEs) significantly higher than practice IR (3.14). Sprains, strains, and concussions were the three most common injury types; head and knee were the most common body parts injured (19.9% each). No significant differences in IRs between position groups by exposure type.

## Limitations
Single-institution study limits generalizability across all NCAA programs. Athlete exposures by position were estimated using weighted averages from roster data rather than individual daily position tracking. Weather, playing surface, and other environmental variables not recorded. Exposure-type analysis covers only 3 seasons (2014-2016). Broad position group categorization may mask individual position-level differences (e.g., within the skill group between WRs and RBs).

## Practical Implications
Preseason soft-tissue strain prevention programs targeting skill position players should be prioritized as the single greatest opportunity to reduce overall team IR. Hamstring and quadriceps screening, load management, and evidence-based eccentric training during fall camp are critical intervention points. Reducing live contact exposures will have the greatest impact on concussions and contact injuries for linemen and other positions. Position-specific warm-up and neuromuscular training protocols should reflect the distinct injury profiles of each group.

## Athlete Development Notes
Skill position players (speed/agility athletes — DBs, WRs, RBs) require targeted preseason conditioning and soft-tissue injury prevention programs. Gradual preseason volume progression, adequate recovery between high-intensity sessions, and progressive hamstring and quadriceps loading are essential during fall camp. Strength and conditioning staff should monitor preseason training loads for skill positions more closely than for linemen in the first weeks of fall practice, as 90.9% of all upper leg/thigh strains in the preseason were sustained by skill players.

## Return to Play Notes
The high incidence of upper leg/thigh strains in skill position players during preseason provides context for return-to-sport decision-making in college football. Athletes returning from lower extremity injury who re-enter full contact during preseason face elevated risk due to the already elevated preseason IR for skill positions. RTP progressions for hamstring and quadriceps strains should include sequential exposure-type progression (noncontact to full-pad to live contact) aligned with NCAA definitions before full clearance.
