# Medeiros TM, Ribeiro-Alvares JB, Fritsch CG, Oliveira GS, Severo-Silveira L, Pappas E, Baroni BM. Effect of Weekly Training Frequency With the Nordic Hamstring Exercise on Muscle-Strain Risk Factors in Football Players: A Randomized Trial. Int J Sports Physiol Perform. 2020; (Ahead of Print). DOI: 10.1123/ijspp.2018-0780

## TLDR
For high-level football players, two NHE sessions per week are needed to achieve hamstring strength gains, whereas one session per week is sufficient to increase biceps femoris fascicle length and muscle thickness. The twice-weekly group improved hamstring eccentric peak torque by ~11% and concentric torque significantly, while the once-weekly group showed no isokinetic strength change. Both groups increased BFlh fascicle length by ~8–9% (~1 cm). This has direct practical value: during congested match schedules when two weekly NHE sessions are not feasible, a single session can still preserve or enhance muscle architecture—a modifiable HSI risk factor. However, for full hamstring strengthening, two weekly sessions should be the minimum target.

## Abstract
This randomized trial compared the effects of performing the Nordic hamstring exercise (NHE) once versus twice per week across an 8-week program on hamstring strain injury (HSI) risk factors in 32 high-level football (soccer) players aged 18–23 years. Isokinetic strength (knee flexor/extensor peak torques, H:Q ratios) and BFlh muscle architecture (thickness, pennation angle, fascicle length) were assessed before and after the programs. Only the twice-weekly group demonstrated significant improvements in hamstring eccentric and concentric strength and H:Q ratios, while both groups achieved similar increases in BFlh muscle thickness and fascicle length.

## Methods
Randomized trial with 32 male high-level football players (under-20 and under-23 from a national premier division club; ages 18–23 years) completing an 8-week NHE program. Group 1 (G1, n=15) performed NHE once per week; Group 2 (G2, n=17) performed NHE twice per week. Both groups followed the same progressive periodization (sets/reps per session). Isokinetic knee flexor/extensor strength (Biodex, 60°/s) and BFlh architecture (B-mode ultrasound, GE Vivid-i, 13 MHz) were assessed pre- and post-training. Analysis of covariance with baseline as covariate, independent t-tests for percentage changes, and Cohen's d effect sizes were used.

## Findings
G2 had significantly higher hamstring concentric peak torque post-training than G1 (155–164 vs. 149–158 N·m; P=.043; ES=0.27). Trends favored G2 for hamstring eccentric peak torque (212–234 vs. 198–221 N·m; P=.098; ES=0.37), H:Q conventional ratio (P=.089; ES=0.31), and H:Q functional ratio (P=.076; ES=0.50). G2 showed significantly larger percentage changes in hamstring eccentric and concentric torque and H:Q functional ratio (P≤.028; large ESs). No between-group differences were found for BFlh thickness (P=.864), pennation angle (P=.289), or fascicle length (P=.406). Both groups increased BFlh thickness (small-moderate ESs) and fascicle length (~8–9%; small ESs) within-group.

## Limitations
No control group (without NHE) due to club requirements; prior evidence suggests outcomes would remain unchanged in a non-NHE group. High dropout rate due to in-season injuries (15 of 54 excluded), reducing sample size. Baseline strength values differed between groups by up to 8% in some measures, potentially giving G2 greater room for improvement despite statistical covariate adjustment. Only left limb was assessed for muscle architecture. Compliance was 100% by study design (minimum thresholds applied), which may not reflect real-world adherence.

## Practical Implications
Football coaching and medical staff should prioritize at least two NHE sessions per week to develop meaningful hamstring strength adaptations in high-level players. During match-congested periods where two sessions are impractical, a single weekly NHE session can still meaningfully increase BFlh fascicle length—preserving an important structural risk factor. This pragmatic dose-response finding helps staff make evidence-based decisions about NHE frequency when full evidence-based protocols (3x/week for 10 weeks) are not feasible. Programs should view the NHE as a year-round tool rather than a preseason-only intervention.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athlete development, this study clarifies the dose of NHE needed to drive different adaptations: muscle architecture (fascicle length, thickness) responds to even low weekly volumes, while strength gains require higher frequency. Given that short BFlh fascicles and eccentric hamstring weakness are independent predictors of future HSI, programs should design NHE progressions that achieve both targets. For younger or developing players who may have lower baseline strength, the twice-weekly protocol is especially important to build the strength reserve needed to withstand high-speed running demands.

## Return to Play Notes
During rehabilitation and return to play after hamstring strain injury, this study suggests that even a once-weekly NHE may be sufficient to begin restoring BFlh fascicle length—a key structural adaptation for reinjury prevention. However, achieving meaningful eccentric strength recovery prior to clearance for full training and competition requires at least twice-weekly NHE sessions. RTP criteria should include both fascicle length normalization (via ultrasound) and eccentric strength symmetry, with NHE frequency titrated accordingly as the athlete progresses through the rehabilitation continuum.
