# Humphreys BR, Ruseski JE. Air quality and employee performance in teams: Evidence from the NFL. Economics and Human Biology. 2023;51:101310.

## TLDR
Higher game-day PM2.5 air pollution increased passing yards per play in NFL games, indicating that defensive players — who must react to unscripted offensive actions — are more impaired by poor air quality than offensive players who execute predetermined plays.

## Abstract
This economics study examined the impact of ambient PM2.5 particulate matter concentration on NFL team performance using play-level data from approximately 90,000 offensive plays across 900 NFL games from 2008 to 2016. Air quality data from EPA monitoring stations within 10 miles of 22 NFL stadiums were linked to game-day PM2.5 readings. A reduced-form linear regression model estimated yards gained or lost per offensive play as a function of local PM2.5 concentration, controlling for game-specific and situational factors. Results indicated that higher PM2.5 concentration was associated with significantly more yards gained per passing play, suggesting that defensive team production is relatively more sensitive to poor air quality than offensive production.

## Methods
Retrospective observational study using play-by-play data from ArmchairAnalysis.com for NFL games 2008–2016 (n=113,782 plays; 65,421 pass plays, 48,361 rush plays across ~900 games at 22 stadiums with nearby EPA AQS monitoring stations). PM2.5 concentration was averaged over 12pm–6pm on game day. Reduced-form linear models estimated yards gained per play as a function of PM2.5, controlling for offensive/defensive team-season fixed effects, week-of-season, quarter, score difference, yards to go, yards to goal line, play number on drive, and domed stadium indicator. Robustness checks included nonlinear PM2.5 bins, outdoor-stadium subsamples with weather controls, ozone substitution, and incomplete pass exclusions.

## Findings
- A one standard deviation increase in PM2.5 (~5.63 µg/m³) was associated with approximately 4.3 additional passing yards per game per team.
- PM2.5 coefficient was 0.014 yards/play overall (p<0.05), driven entirely by passing plays (0.021, p<0.05); rushing plays showed no significant association.
- Defensive teams are affected more than offensive teams by poor air quality, based on the mechanism of reactive vs. scripted play execution.
- Home team PM2.5 air quality shocks reduced home rushing yards; visiting team PM2.5 shock increased visiting team rushing yards on defense, consistent with defense being the primary channel.
- Effect sizes are small but statistically significant and robust across multiple model specifications.

## Limitations
Cannot directly control for coaching decisions or play-calling adjustments in response to air quality. Sample limited to 22 stadiums with nearby EPA monitoring stations, excluding 9 NFL teams. Game-day PM2.5 is measured at the nearest monitoring station, not inside the stadium, introducing measurement error. Cannot fully account for within-game variation or exact game start times. Air quality on game days is generally in healthy or moderate ranges, limiting generalizability to high-pollution scenarios.

## Practical Implications
Air quality is an environmental factor that affects team athletic performance even at moderate concentration levels. For NFL and other professional teams, scheduling, venue selection, and indoor versus outdoor stadium considerations may have measurable performance implications. Staff monitoring environmental conditions on game day could inform preparation strategies, particularly for defensive personnel whose reactive performance appears most vulnerable to PM2.5 exposure.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly relevant to American football. Findings suggest that environmental air quality on game day impairs reactive decision-making and execution for defensive players more than offensive players. This distinction between scripted (offense) and reactive (defense) roles has implications for how player preparation and performance monitoring accounts for environmental stressors during the competitive season.

## Return to Play Notes
Not directly studied. However, the finding that even moderate ambient PM2.5 levels impair cognitive-physical performance in healthy elite athletes suggests that air quality monitoring could be a relevant environmental health consideration in return-to-activity protocols, particularly for athletes recovering from respiratory or neurological injuries.
