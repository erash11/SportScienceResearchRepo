# Grashow R, Tan CO, Thomas JD, DiGregorio H, Lee H, Rotem RS, Marengi D, Terry DP, Eagle SR, Iverson GL, Whittington AJ, Zafonte RD, Weisskopf MG, Baggish AL. Career Duration and Later-Life Health Conditions Among Former Professional American-Style Football Players. Occupational and Environmental Medicine. 2024;0:1-9.

## TLDR
Former professional football players with intermediate career lengths (not the shortest or longest) showed the highest risk of chronic pain, arthritis, mental health problems, and cognitive symptoms, revealing an inverted U-shaped relationship that challenges the assumption that longer careers mean worse health outcomes. Researchers and clinicians should avoid using career duration as a simple linear proxy for cumulative health risk in former football players.

## Abstract
This survey-based cross-sectional cohort study of 4,189 former professional American-style football (ASF) players examined associations between self-reported career duration and four self-reported health conditions: chronic pain, arthritis, mental health problems, and subjective cognitive symptoms. Using flexible regression with penalized B-splines to model non-linear relationships, the study found inverted U-shaped associations between career duration and all four health outcomes (all p<0.001), indicating adverse health effects were most common among men with intermediate career durations. Similar non-linear patterns were observed for four play-related exposures including concussion signs and symptoms, performance-enhancing drug use, intracareer surgeries, and average snaps per game. Findings challenge the assumption that career duration is a reliable linear proxy for neurotrauma exposure.

## Methods
Survey-based cross-sectional cohort study from the Football Players Health Study (FPHS) at Harvard University. Former professional ASF players (n=4,189; mean age 52±14 years; 39% Black, 34% lineman) recruited via residential and electronic mail. Health outcomes (chronic pain, arthritis, mental health problems, subjective cognitive symptoms via Neuro-QoL) self-reported. Career exposures included concussion signs and symptoms (CSS) score, performance-enhancing drug use, intracareer surgeries, and average snaps per game. Flexible regression with penalized B-splines and piecewise linear regression used to characterize non-linear career duration-outcome relationships. Models adjusted for age, race, and lineman status.

## Findings
• Inverted U-shaped relationships observed between career duration and chronic pain, arthritis, mental health problems, and subjective cognitive symptoms (all p<0.001).
• Players with 4–8 seasons had significantly higher odds of chronic pain (OR 1.21; 95% CI 1.02–1.42; p=0.02) and arthritis compared to those with 0–3 seasons.
• Players with 13+ seasons had similar or lower odds of adverse health outcomes compared to the 0–3 season reference group.
• Piecewise regression identified breakpoints at 6.9 seasons (chronic pain), 9.5 (arthritis), 4.5 (mental health), and 7.0 (cognitive symptoms) where slope reversed.
• Similar inverted-U patterns observed for all four football-specific exposures (CSS score, snaps per game, performance-enhancing drugs, intracareer surgeries).
• Findings were consistent across lineman and non-lineman subgroups.

## Limitations
All outcomes and exposures were self-reported, lacking clinical confirmation. Recall bias possible, though findings were consistent with intracareer surgery data (salient events less subject to recall bias). Healthy worker survivor bias (HWSB) may explain attenuation in longer-career players: those remaining active longest may be biologically or psychologically more resilient. Snap data available for only a subset (n=1,980). Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference.

## Practical Implications
Career duration is a poor and potentially misleading proxy for cumulative health risk in former ASF players. Clinicians and researchers should account for non-linear associations and consider using more direct exposure metrics (e.g., cumulative head impact index, total snaps). Medical providers caring for former players should not assume those with the longest careers are necessarily at greatest health risk — mid-career range players may carry the highest burden. Screening and intervention programs should target all career-length groups.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to NFL and professional American football. The finding that mid-range career players (roughly 5–12 seasons) show the highest health burden suggests that player development and athlete longevity programs should focus on reducing cumulative physical and neurological exposure during the middle portion of careers, not only at career endpoints. Monitoring concussion symptom burden and surgical load as ongoing exposure metrics may be more informative than career length alone.

## Return to Play Notes
Not a RTP study. However, the non-linear relationship between career duration and health outcomes — driven partly by concussion sign/symptom accumulation and intracareer surgeries — underscores that cumulative injury burden across a career significantly shapes long-term health. RTP decisions should consider cumulative exposure history, not just the presenting injury, and total career injury load should be a factor in ongoing RTP and load management frameworks.
