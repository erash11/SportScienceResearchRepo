# Bergstein VE, Lu AZ, Hodgens BH, Bodendorfer BM, DeFroda SF, Kaplan J, Aiyer AA. Fantasy football points capture performance declines in National Football League offensive skill players following an ankle injury. J Orthop. 2024. DOI: 10.1016/j.jor.2024.03.030.

## TLDR
Ankle injuries cause significant, measurable performance declines in NFL offensive skill players that persist through the following season, particularly in route runners (RBs, WRs, TEs). Fantasy football points serve as a validated composite performance metric that effectively captures these multidimensional declines. Quarterbacks are relatively unaffected in terms of passing production. These findings should inform RTP decision-making and rehabilitation protocols targeting the biomechanical demands unique to route-running positions.

## Abstract
This retrospective study used publicly available databases (Pro Football Reference; FantasyData) to evaluate the impact of ankle injuries on NFL offensive skill player performance using fantasy football points as a composite performance metric. Among 303 offensive skill players who sustained ankle sprains, high ankle sprains, or bone bruises from 2009-2020, average fantasy points per game (PPG) and total season fantasy points decreased significantly in the season following injury (p<0.0001). Route runners (running backs, wide receivers, tight ends) showed significant decreases across all performance metrics. Quarterbacks showed no significant performance decline except reduced games played and fewer interceptions. RTP time and injury type did not significantly influence PPG or total fantasy points after adjustment.

## Methods
Retrospective cohort study using Pro Football Reference injury data (2009-2020 seasons) to identify offensive skill players with ankle sprains, high ankle sprains, or bone bruises. Exclusions: multiple injuries in one season, <3 games played or zero fantasy points pre-injury, no return to play. 303 players included (17 QBs, 106 RBs, 55 TEs, 125 WRs). Fantasy points and position-specific metrics collected from FantasyData for the season before and after the injury year. Each injured player matched 1:1 to a healthy control by position, age, and BMI. Paired T-tests compared pre/post performance; ANCOVA assessed effects of RTP time and injury type. Post-hoc Bonferroni correction applied for injury type comparisons.

## Findings
Overall injured cohort: games played decreased from 13.25 to 9.89 (p<0.0001); PPG decreased from 6.20 to 4.77 (p<0.0001); total season fantasy points decreased from 85.15 to 62.97 (p<0.0001). Matched controls showed no significant changes. Route runners showed significant decreases in all metrics: rush yards, receptions, receiving yards, touchdowns, PPG, and total fantasy points (all p<0.0001). QBs showed no significant decline in passing yards, TDs, rush yards, or PPG (all p>0.10); only games played (p=0.0033) and interceptions (p=0.029) decreased. RTP time and injury type both confounded games-played changes but did not significantly affect PPG or total fantasy points after ANCOVA adjustment.

## Limitations
Retrospective design allows only associations, not causation. No access to NFL electronic medical records; injury data derived from media-reported NFL injury reports (possible under- or over-reporting). Fantasy football points are not a previously validated composite performance measure and involve differential weighting of performance components. Players with injuries prior to the 2009 study start date were not tracked. Mechanism of injury (contact vs. non-contact) and playing conditions were not available in the database. Some ankle injuries may have been classified as 'other' in the database, potentially undercounting ankle injuries.

## Practical Implications
Ankle injuries produce significant season-long performance decrements in NFL route runners that are not fully explained by severity or RTP time, suggesting residual neuromuscular or proprioceptive deficits after clinical clearance. Medical staff should consider extended rehabilitation protocols specifically targeting ankle stability, proprioception, and the biomechanical demands of cutting and route running. Return-to-play criteria may need to incorporate position-specific functional performance benchmarks beyond standard clinical milestones.

## Athlete Development Notes
The high prevalence of ankle injuries — with 53.2% of NFL Combine attendees having prior ankle injury history — underscores the importance of ankle injury prevention programming throughout a player's development. Running backs, tight ends, and wide receivers face the highest ankle injury rates and show the greatest performance impact, making them priority populations for preventive ankle strengthening, proprioceptive training, and load management strategies.

## Return to Play Notes
Route runners (RBs, WRs, TEs) demonstrate significant declines in every tracked performance metric in the season following ankle injury regardless of RTP timing or injury type, suggesting that current RTP protocols may be insufficient to restore full functional capacity in these athletes. Future research should focus on optimizing NFL rehabilitation protocols specifically for route runners, potentially incorporating performance-based RTP criteria using position-specific on-field metrics or composite measures like fantasy points as outcome benchmarks.
