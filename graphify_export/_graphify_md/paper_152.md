# Crow J, Semciw A, Couch J, Pizzari T. Does a recent hamstring muscle injury affect the timing of muscle activation during high speed overground running in professional Australian football players? Physical Therapy in Sport. 2020;43:188–194. 10.1016/j.ptsp.2020.03.005

## TLDR
Professional AFL players who completed comprehensive rehabilitation after a hamstring injury showed no differences in the timing of hamstring or gluteal muscle activation during high-speed running compared to uninjured teammates. This reassuring finding suggests that when rehabilitation is thorough — including high-speed running progressions — the neuromuscular sequencing of hamstring activation during sprinting is likely restored. For football staff, this underscores that the persistent altered muscle timing patterns reported in recreational athletes following hamstring injury may be a rehabilitation quality issue rather than an inevitable consequence of injury, supporting investment in high-quality, sport-specific RTP protocols.

## Abstract
This cohort study investigated whether the temporal characteristics of hamstring and gluteal muscle activation are altered during high-speed overground running in professional Australian Football players following hamstring muscle injury. Seven players with a recent hamstring injury (HS group; injury within past 6 months) and eight uninjured controls (CON group) from the same AFL club performed high-speed runs at approximately 90% of perceived maximum speed on a 100 m grass track. Surface EMG recorded muscle onset timing, offset timing, and duration of activation for the biceps femoris, medial hamstrings, and gluteus maximus.

## Methods
Cohort study; field-based testing. 15 male professional AFL players from one club: 7 with hamstring injury in the preceding 6 months (HS group; mean 108 ± 46 days since strain), 8 uninjured controls (CON group). All injuries MRI-confirmed. Participants ran 100 m at 60%, 70%, 80%, 90% of perceived maximum speed with 100 m walk recovery, then completed one practice trial. Two testing sessions within 7 days. Surface EMG via Delsys Trigno Wireless system (CMRR >80 dB; 2000 Hz; band-pass 20–450 Hz) on gluteus maximus, biceps femoris, medial hamstrings. Footswitches on plantar hallux and inter-phalangeal joint. GPS (MinimaxX Team 2.5, Catapult) for running speed. Two middle-20 m strides per trial selected for analysis (10 strides × 5 trials per participant). Muscle onset/offset at 15% peak amplitude threshold; duration as percentage of gait cycle. Mann–Whitney U tests and effect sizes (z-score/√n).

## Findings
No statistically significant differences between HS and CON groups for muscle onset, offset, or duration of activation for gluteus maximus, biceps femoris, or medial hamstrings (all p > 0.05). Groups were matched for age (HS: 22.3 ± 1.6; CON: 21.9 ± 1.6 years), height, weight, and average running speed (HS: 7.3 ± 0.3 m/s; CON: 7.2 ± 0.4 m/s). The grand ensemble EMG curves for all three muscles showed nearly identical patterns between groups throughout the gait cycle. These null results were consistent across both testing sessions.

## Limitations
Small sample from a single club limits generalisability. Could not control for training volume in the week preceding testing. EMG sensor dislodgement from one participant's medial hamstrings (CON group) reduced that muscle's sample to n=7 in CON. Study used onset/offset amplitude threshold approach rather than normalised amplitude, so differences in activation level rather than timing could have been missed. Overground GPS-confirmed running speeds (~7.3 m/s) may not represent true maximal sprint velocity. Environmental conditions (temperature, wind, ground hardness) were not fully controlled.

## Practical Implications
After comprehensive hamstring rehabilitation, professional football players appear to restore normal neuromuscular timing during high-speed running. This provides confidence that well-rehabilitated players are not carrying covert neuromuscular deficits at the point of RTP. Football staff should use this as validation for investing in thorough, progressive rehabilitation that includes sport-speed running rather than settling for pain-free jogging. Residual activation timing deficits identified in recreational athletes may reflect inadequate rehabilitation rather than unavoidable injury sequelae.

## Athlete Development Notes
Young football players recovering from hamstring strain should progress through a full high-speed running rehabilitation continuum. The data suggest that those who complete comprehensive rehabilitation programmes restore normal muscle activation patterns, indicating the nervous system is highly adaptable with appropriate loading. Coaches should not restrict athletes from high-speed running in late-stage rehabilitation out of fear of altered motor patterns, as this may ironically delay neuromuscular recovery.

## Return to Play Notes
This study supports returning players to full high-speed running after comprehensive hamstring rehabilitation without persistent concern about altered muscle timing patterns. RTP criteria should include demonstrated ability to run at high speeds (>7 m/s) without restriction, with confidence that normal gluteal and hamstring activation sequencing is likely restored. Practical implication for NFL/college football: do not rely on neuromuscular timing as an RTP screen unless the athlete is known to have had incomplete rehabilitation; instead focus on strength, range of motion, and sport-specific running load tolerance.
