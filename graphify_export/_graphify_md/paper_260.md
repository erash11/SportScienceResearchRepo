# Boone, R. T., Zambrotta, N. S., Manocchio, A. M., & Bowman, J. K. (2025). Head in the game: The impact of cognitive abilities on performance of National Football League quarterbacks. Frontiers in Psychology, 16, 1540498. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1540498

## TLDR
Cognitive performance measures—especially Reaction Time and Visual Spatial Processing—predict multiple NFL quarterback performance statistics above and beyond what draft pick alone predicts. Quarterbacks with faster reaction times have higher career value and passer ratings; better visual spatial processors gain more passing yards and throw more accurately. These findings support incorporating cognitive assessment in QB evaluation and development.

## Abstract
This study examined whether cognitive abilities measured by the Athletic Intelligence Quotient (AIQ)—Visual Spatial Processing, Reaction Time, Decision Making, and Learning Efficiency—predicted NFL quarterback performance beyond what is explained by draft pick. AIQ scores from 42 NFL quarterbacks collected at the Combine (2014–2020) were matched to performance metrics including Career Approximate Value (CAV), Quarterback Rating (QBR), passing yards, rushing yards, Big Time Throw rate, Turnover Worthy Plays, and Throwing Accuracy. Hierarchical regressions controlling for draft pick showed that Reaction Time predicted CAV, QBR, rushing yards, and games started; Visual Spatial Processing predicted passing yards; and an interaction between Visual Spatial Processing and Decision Making predicted Turnover Worthy Plays and Throwing Accuracy.

## Methods
Participants: 42 NFL quarterbacks who declared for the draft from 2014–2020 and played at least 3 games. Instrument: Athletic Intelligence Quotient (AIQ), administered at the NFL Combine via tablet software (AIM, LLC). Performance metrics sourced from Pro Football Reference (CAV, QBR, passing/rushing yards) and Pro Football Focus (Big Time Throw rate, Turnover Worthy Plays, Throwing Accuracy). Hierarchical multiple regression with draft pick entered at Step 1 and AIQ subscales entered at Step 2; trimmed models retained only significant AIQ predictors. Mixed model analysis used for PFF statistics (year nested within player).

## Findings
Reaction Time predicted games started per year (+6% variance beyond draft pick), CAV (+13%), QBR (+8%), and rushing yards per game (+9%). Visual Spatial Processing predicted passing yards per game (+7%). Interaction of Visual Spatial Processing × Decision Making significantly predicted Turnover Worthy Plays and Throwing Accuracy. Big Time Throw rate was not predicted by any AIQ factor. Learning Efficiency showed no significant relationships with any performance metric. Overall, AIQ cognitive factors added predictive power to 6 of 7 performance metrics examined.

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=42) limits statistical power and generalizability. Only quarterbacks who played at least 3 games were included, creating a survivor bias. Model excludes team-level factors such as offensive line quality and coaching scheme. Games started per year used as a proxy for ability may be confounded by organizational investment in high draft picks. Psychological facets such as arousal regulation and resilience are not captured by the AIQ.

## Practical Implications
Cognitive assessment tools like the AIQ offer meaningful predictive value for QB performance and should be considered alongside physical metrics in scouting and player development. Reaction Time in particular is a broadly useful cognitive marker. Coaching staff might focus on drill designs that develop rapid perceptual decision-making and visual field scanning to improve QB performance.

## Athlete Development Notes
For QB development programs, training environments that emphasize rapid decision-making under pressure and visual scanning of defensive formations could translate to improved on-field performance. The AIQ framework provides a structured way to identify cognitive development targets for young quarterbacks entering developmental pipelines.

## Return to Play Notes
Cognitive performance measures may have utility in RTP assessment for quarterbacks recovering from concussion or other neurological events. The AIQ subscales—especially Reaction Time and Visual Spatial Processing—could serve as sensitive markers of cognitive recovery, given their demonstrated relationships to on-field QB performance.
