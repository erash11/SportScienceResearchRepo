# Ferrari F, Stein R, Milani M. Electrocardiographic diversity in football players: how distinct ethnic patterns shape athletes' hearts. Eur J Prev Cardiol. 2026;33:6–7.

## TLDR
Brazilian football players—including Black and mixed-race athletes—show a lower rate of ECG abnormalities (3%) and a high prevalence of early repolarization (40%) compared to more homogeneous African cohorts, likely due to Brazil's extensive genetic admixture. Early repolarization in athletes appears benign in this context, while inferolateral T-wave inversion still warrants further cardiac workup. These findings highlight the need to update international ECG screening criteria to reflect ethnically diverse, admixed populations.

## Abstract
This expert interview presents findings from a series of four studies examining ECG patterns in over 6,000 Brazilian male soccer players across two decades. The research addresses the underrepresentation of mixed-race and ethnically diverse athletes in current international ECG interpretation guidelines. Key topics include the low prevalence of abnormal ECGs (3%), early repolarization patterns by ethnicity, and the rarity of the 'African/Afro-Caribbean' ECG pattern in Brazilian players. The authors argue that Brazil's high genetic admixture dilutes distinct ECG patterns seen in more homogeneous populations and that current guidelines inadequately account for admixed populations.

## Methods
Expert interview format summarizing findings from four peer-reviewed studies (B-Pro Foot ECG study) on resting ECGs of male Brazilian soccer players from multiple clubs across Brazil. Data were collected over approximately two decades from more than 6,000 athletes. In addition to ECGs, imaging data (echocardiography) were collected to assess structural correlates of ECG findings. Early repolarization was classified into seven distinct morphologies. Athletes were followed for a mean of four years.

## Findings
97% of ECGs were normal across ethnically diverse Brazilian players. Early repolarization was present in 40% of players overall, with higher rates in Black (48%) and mixed-race (41%) athletes. The classic J-wave with ascending ST-segment was the most common morphology and was benign. Higher-risk patterns (horizontal ST-segment, inferolateral J-waves) were rare. No sudden cardiac deaths occurred during four years of follow-up. The 'African/Afro-Caribbean' ECG pattern was rare in Brazilian Black and mixed-race players and, when present, was not associated with structural abnormalities. ECG abnormalities became more common with age (26–35 years).

## Limitations
All studies were limited to male athletes; female footballer data were not yet published at the time of the interview. The mixed-race category in Brazil is heterogeneous, making precise genetic comparisons difficult. The interview format limits direct access to full study methodology and data tables. Only Brazilian football players were studied, limiting generalizability to other admixed or non-Brazilian populations.

## Practical Implications
Clinicians screening ethnically diverse or admixed athlete populations should not over-interpret early repolarization as pathological—it is extremely common in Brazilian athletes and was not associated with adverse cardiac events. Inferolateral T-wave inversion, however, should always prompt further evaluation including cardiac MRI even when echocardiogram is normal. International ECG screening criteria should be updated to better reflect admixed populations to reduce unnecessary testing and false positives.

## Athlete Development Notes
For development programs working with ethnically diverse rosters—including significant proportions of Black, mixed-race, or non-European athletes—standard ECG interpretation guidelines may over-flag benign training-related adaptations. Understanding that early repolarization is highly prevalent and likely benign in this population allows medical staff to avoid unnecessary disqualifications or investigations that could disrupt athlete development pathways.

## Return to Play Notes
No specific RTP decision framework was outlined, but the study findings support that early repolarization alone is insufficient grounds to restrict return to play. Inferolateral T-wave inversion warrants cardiac MRI before clearance, even with a normal echocardiogram. These principles are relevant for pre-participation screening decisions and for evaluating athletes flagged during routine cardiac screening.
