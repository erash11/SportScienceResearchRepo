# Ekstrand J, Walden M, Hagglund M. Hamstring injuries have increased by 4% annually in men's professional football, since 2001: a 13-year longitudinal analysis of the UEFA Elite Club Injury Study. Br J Sports Med. 2016;50(12):731–737. doi:10.1136/bjsports-2015-095359

## TLDR
Hamstring injuries in elite European professional football increased by approximately 4% annually over a 13-year period — a significant and worsening epidemiological trend that has not been resolved by existing prevention efforts. However, injury burden (days lost per injury) did not increase proportionally, suggesting improved clinical management of individual episodes. The persistently high recurrence rate (~16%) indicates that return-to-play and rehabilitation standards need urgent improvement. These findings make a compelling case for mandatory adoption of evidence-based hamstring injury prevention programmes and more rigorous RTP criteria in elite football.

## Abstract
This 13-year longitudinal analysis of the UEFA Elite Club Injury Study examined hamstring injury trends in men's professional football from 2001–02 to 2013–14 across up to 36 European clubs. The study tracked hamstring injury incidence, burden, severity, and recurrence rates over 13 seasons using standardised injury recording and team exposure data. Hamstring injury incidence increased significantly by approximately 4% per year over the 13-year period (p<0.001). Despite this concerning trend, injury burden (days lost per 1000 hours exposure) did not increase significantly, suggesting that average hamstring injury severity and/or return-to-play times may have improved over the period. Recurrence rates remained high and consistent throughout, accounting for approximately 16% of all hamstring injuries. The silver lining identified was that while incidence rose, clubs and medical teams appeared to improve management of individual injury episodes.

## Methods
Longitudinal prospective cohort study using data from the UEFA Elite Club Injury Study, one of the largest and longest-running sports injury surveillance programmes in professional football. Up to 36 professional European clubs participated across 13 consecutive seasons (2001–02 to 2013–14). Injury data were recorded by club medical staff using a standardised reporting system and exposure time was tracked as team training and match hours. Hamstring injuries were defined as any acute hamstring muscle injury causing absence from training or match play. Injury incidence (per 1000 h exposure), injury burden (days lost per 1000 h exposure), mean days absent, and recurrence rates were calculated. Time trends were assessed using Poisson regression analysis.

## Findings
Hamstring injury incidence increased by ~4% per year over 13 seasons (p<0.001), representing a substantial and statistically significant rise. Total hamstring injury burden did not increase significantly, indicating average time lost per injury may have improved. Recurrence rates remained consistently high at approximately 16% of all hamstring injuries across all seasons, with no significant improving trend. Hamstring injuries were the single most common injury type across all seasons, accounting for approximately 12% of all injuries. Match play carried a substantially higher injury rate than training. Despite awareness of the problem, no systematic reduction in incidence was achieved during the study period.

## Limitations
Injury surveillance relied on club medical staff reporting, which may introduce inter-club variability in recording practices. The study captured only time-loss injuries, potentially underestimating the full burden of hamstring problems. Specific injury mechanisms, anatomical locations, and MRI grading were not systematically captured in early seasons. Changes in training methods, match schedules, player populations, and prevention programme adoption were not controlled for. The silver lining interpretation (stable burden despite rising incidence) may also reflect earlier return-to-play decisions rather than genuine improvements in recovery.

## Practical Implications
The persistent 4% annual increase in hamstring injury incidence across elite European clubs underscores the inadequacy of current prevention approaches at the population level. Sports medicine and performance staff should prioritise implementation of validated hamstring injury prevention programmes (e.g., the Nordic Hamstring Exercise protocol) and rigorous, criteria-based return-to-play frameworks to address both rising incidence and persistently high recurrence rates. Investment in surveillance, early detection, and evidence-based rehabilitation infrastructure is justified by the scale of the burden.

## Athlete Development Notes
The growing hamstring injury epidemic in professional football highlights the need for systematic hamstring injury prevention to be embedded throughout the athletic development pathway — not only at the elite level. Young footballers should develop robust hamstring eccentric strength, sprint mechanics, and neuromuscular control as foundational qualities, with progressive loading across development phases to reduce lifetime hamstring injury risk before players reach elite competition.

## Return to Play Notes
With recurrence rates consistently around 16% across 13 seasons, return-to-play criteria for hamstring injuries in professional football are clearly insufficient. Objective, criteria-based RTP frameworks incorporating eccentric strength symmetry, running mechanics, sport-specific sprint loading, and functional testing should replace time-based or symptom-based clearance. The lack of improvement in recurrence rates over 13 years despite increased awareness represents a critical quality gap that must be addressed through standardised, evidence-based RTP protocols.
