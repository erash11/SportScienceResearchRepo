# Blake AL, McVicar CL, Retino M, Hall EE, Ketcham CJ. Concussion history influences sleep disturbances, symptoms, and quality of life in collegiate student-athletes. Sleep Health. 2019;5(1):72-77. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.sleh.2018.10.011

## TLDR
Collegiate athletes with a history of concussion continue to experience significantly more sleep disturbances, ongoing concussion symptoms, and reduced quality of life even after returning to play and academics. The number of sleep disturbances strongly correlated with both symptom severity (r = 0.48) and QOL dysfunction (r = 0.35). For football staff and athletic trainers, these findings emphasize that post-concussion monitoring must extend well beyond initial symptom clearance and RTP milestones—athletes with multiple concussions may require ongoing sleep screening and multidisciplinary support to protect academic performance, athletic output, and mental health.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study determined whether self-reported concussion history was associated with persistent sleep disturbances, concussion symptoms, and quality of life (QOL) dysfunctions in 82 collegiate club and varsity student-athletes not currently recovering from a concussion. Participants completed an online survey including concussion history categorized as 0, 1-2, or 3+ concussions, the Rivermead Post-Concussion Symptom Questionnaire (RPQ), and an adapted Rivermead Head Injury Follow-Up Questionnaire (RHFUQ). Athletes with prior concussions reported significantly more sleep disturbances, concussion symptoms, and QOL dysfunctions than those with no concussion history, even after returning to sport and academics.

## Methods
Cross-sectional online survey of 82 collegiate student-athletes (club and varsity; ages 18-25, mean 20.3 ± 1.5 years) from a small private liberal arts university in the southeastern United States, not currently recovering from a concussion. Concussion history was self-reported as 0, 1-2, or 3+ concussions. Sleep disturbances were measured using a checklist of 6 common disturbances (difficulty falling asleep, restlessness, sleeping too much, sleeping too little, difficulty staying asleep, fatigue). Concussion symptoms were assessed with the 16-item RPQ (5-point Likert scale, max score 64). QOL was assessed with an adapted 10-item RHFUQ (5-point Likert scale, max score 40). One-way ANOVAs with LSD post hoc tests compared concussion history groups (alpha < 0.05). Pearson correlations assessed relationships between sleep disturbances, symptoms, and QOL.

## Findings
The most common sleep disturbances were fatigue (32%), sleeping too little (27%), restlessness (24%), and difficulty falling asleep (24%). Significant group differences were found in total sleep disturbances (F = 3.73, P = .028), with the 0-concussion group having significantly fewer than the 3+ group (P = .012). Significant differences existed in total symptoms (F = 3.72, P = .029) and symptom severity (F = 4.51, P = .014), with the 0-concussion group reporting fewer symptoms than both 1-2 (P = .020) and 3+ (P = .046) concussion groups. Significant differences were found in QOL dysfunctions (F = 6.20, P = .003) and QOL dysfunction severity (F = 4.34, P = .016), with the 0-concussion group reporting fewer than both concussion groups (P = .005 and P = .006). Sleep disturbances positively correlated with symptoms (r = .54, P < .001), symptom severity (r = .48, P < .001), QOL dysfunctions (r = .35, P = .001), and QOL severity (r = .34, P = .002). Sport level (varsity vs. club) did not influence outcomes.

## Limitations
Modest sample size (N = 82) from a single institution limits generalizability. Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference and time since last concussion varied across participants. Self-reported concussion history is subject to recall bias. Sport-specific differences could not be fully evaluated due to small within-sport sample sizes. No objective sleep measures (e.g., actigraphy) were used. The RHFUQ was adapted from its original format, which may affect validity.

## Practical Implications
Football medical and performance staff should implement routine sleep and symptom screening for athletes with any concussion history, not only during the acute recovery window. Athletes with 3 or more concussions are particularly vulnerable to persistent sleep disturbances that impair recovery, cognitive function, and academic performance. Integrating sleep quality assessments into annual pre-participation physicals and ongoing wellness monitoring programs can help identify athletes who may benefit from referral for sleep interventions, neurological follow-up, or performance support services.

## Athlete Development Notes
Persistent sleep disturbances after concussion impair muscle recovery, immune function, cognitive processing, attention, and motivation—all of which are critical for athlete development. Football programs should address sleep hygiene as a performance variable for any athlete with a concussion history, potentially integrating wearable sleep monitoring and structured sleep education as components of the development program, particularly for athletes with 3 or more prior concussions.

## Return to Play Notes
This study demonstrates that concussion symptoms and QOL dysfunctions persist even after formal return-to-play, indicating that current RTP protocols may not fully capture lingering recovery deficits. Clinicians should consider extending post-clearance follow-up to include validated sleep assessments, especially in athletes with multiple concussion histories. Athletes reporting ongoing sleep disturbances after neurological clearance may warrant re-evaluation before resuming full-contact practice or high-demand training.
