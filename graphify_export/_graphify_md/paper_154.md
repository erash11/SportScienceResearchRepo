# Lapré MA, Palazzolo EM. Does Draft Currency Promote Competitive Balance? An Empirical Investigation of the National Football League 2002–2021. Journal of Sports Economics. 2024;1-23. doi:10.1177/15270025241264238

## TLDR
The NFL draft's intent to promote competitive balance fails in practice because what matters is not where you pick but how smartly you trade. Original draft position has no effect on reaching the playoffs, but teams that acquire more total draft value through trading are significantly more likely to reach the playoffs 2–3 years later. Most teams use McCoy's outdated Pick Value Chart (PVC), which overvalues early picks relative to more accurate models. Teams like New England that recognized this mispricing gained systematic advantages and remained dominant for over two decades. For football front offices and performance staff, this study reinforces that roster building through strategic draft-pick trading—guided by empirically derived draft-value curves—is more predictive of playoff success than simply having an early pick.

## Abstract
This study empirically investigates whether the NFL draft promotes competitive balance by examining whether draft-pick value (draft currency) increases the probability of reaching the playoffs across the 2002–2021 seasons. The authors distinguish between original draft currency (value of picks assigned before any trades) and final draft currency (value of picks used after all trades) using eight different draft-value curves. Logistic regression models with team-strength controls show that original draft currency does not affect playoff probability, but final draft currency (reflecting pick trading) does—with a delay of 2–3 years. Teams that outperformed others in trading draft picks, most notably the New England Patriots, perpetuated competitive imbalance by exploiting outdated pick-value frameworks used by most teams.

## Methods
Retrospective analysis of all NFL drafts and game results for the 1998–2021 seasons (focused on 2002–2021 post-realignment to 32 teams). Primary data from pro-football-reference.com. Eight draft-value curves compared: McCoy's PVC, Massey-Thaler MT1 (1983–2008) and MT2 (2001–2008), Stuart's PFRAV, Schuckers' APVC, and Lopez's Average, Star, and Blended curves. Original and final draft currency computed for each team-year. Team strength measured via least squares ratings (scoring margin-based). Logistic regression (equation 1) with random effects clustered by team estimated probability of making the playoffs, controlling for prior team ratings (up to 4 lags), strength of schedule, new head coach, division, and first-round QB selections. Dataset: 635 team-season observations (32 teams, 2002–2021; Cleveland Browns and Houston Texans have fewer due to expansion). Robustness tests included team dummies, compensatory-pick-only models, Patriots exclusion, and win percentage as dependent variable.

## Findings
Original draft currency was not statistically significant in any of the 10 models across all 8 draft-value curves (Table 1), indicating the assigned draft position does not influence playoff probability. Final draft currency (after pick trades) was significant for the Star, Blended, PFRAV, APVC, Average, and Linear draft-value curves (Table 2), primarily at lags 2 and 3 years. Model fit (log pseudolikelihood) was substantially better for models using less-steep draft-value curves (PFRAV, APVC, Average, Linear) than for PVC-based models. Final draft currency is strongly predicted by original draft currency (β≈1.00, R²=0.67 for PVC) but varies more when using APVC (R²=0.29) and Average (R²=0.28), reflecting that some teams systematically gain or lose value in trades. The New England Patriots had the least original draft currency under APVC but ended up with more final draft currency than 21 of 31 other teams on average, contributing to 17 playoff appearances over 20 seasons. An average team improving its final draft currency from 2.6 to 2.9 (APVC) increases its estimated playoff probability from 33.8% to 38.9% (15% increase).

## Limitations
The authors cannot fully control for trades involving veteran players alongside draft picks, so final draft currency coefficients may be biased downward. Positional depth within the draft and team-specific roster needs are not modeled. The analysis focuses on a single dependent variable (playoff appearance) and is underpowered to study Super Bowl appearance or divisional round advancement due to small sample sizes. The surplus value curve's critical dependence on 1994–2008 compensation data limits its applicability post-2011 CBA. Results may not generalize beyond the 32-team, post-2002-realignment NFL structure.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football front offices should replace McCoy's PVC—still widely used in negotiations—with empirically derived, less-steep draft-value curves (APVC, Average, Linear, PFRAV) that better capture actual player value by draft position. Teams using updated valuation frameworks will systematically acquire more draft value in trades, which translates into measurably higher playoff probability 2–3 years later. General managers and football operations staff should treat draft-pick trading as a capital allocation problem: the total value of picks acquired post-trade is a stronger predictor of organizational success than pick position. Performance directors should understand that late-round picks are undervalued by the PVC, making them advantageous assets to acquire.

## Athlete Development Notes
For college football programs and NFL teams with draft responsibilities, this study underscores the importance of talent identification across all draft rounds rather than over-investing in top picks. Because less-steep value curves (PFRAV, Average) correlate better with playoff success, organizations that develop late-round picks effectively gain disproportionate value. Athlete development infrastructure—including depth chart management, position flexibility training, and accelerated development pipelines for later-round selections—is a strategic competitive advantage. Programs should build systems that reduce developmental risk for mid-to-late round picks, where true market inefficiencies exist relative to conventional pick valuation.
