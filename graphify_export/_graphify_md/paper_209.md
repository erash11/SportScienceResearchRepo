# Ito N, Lin YC, Martin JA, Blemker SS, Opar DA, Kliethermes SA, Heiderscheit BC, HAMIR Consortium Investigators. Exploring Sprint Kinematics in American Football Athletes After Anterior Cruciate Ligament Reconstruction. Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports. 2026;36:e70200.

## TLDR
Division-1 collegiate American football athletes approximately 2 years post-ACLR did not show asymmetrical knee joint kinematics or stance time during maximal effort on-field sprinting, despite these asymmetries being well-documented at submaximal speeds. High-demand sprinting may force full utilization of the knee joint, masking deficits present at lower effort levels. IMU-based field testing provides a clinically feasible method for assessing sprint biomechanics post-ACLR in American football players.

## Abstract
This secondary analysis compared stance time and knee joint kinematics between limbs during sprinting in 20 Division-1 collegiate American football athletes with a history of unilateral anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction (ACLR). Sprint biomechanics were collected using wearable inertial measurement units (Xsens MVN Awinda) during maximal effort overground sprints. Kinematic variables included stance time, knee flexion angle at initial contact (KFIC), peak knee flexion angle (PKF), and knee flexion excursion during stance. Linear mixed effects models evaluated main effects of limb, time from surgery, and their interaction, adjusted for peak sprint speed. No significant limb × time interactions (p=0.30–0.60) or main effects of limb (p=0.23–0.84) or time from surgery (p=0.08–0.84) were found. Mean time from surgery was 28.0±18.5 months; average peak sprint speed was 7.5±0.6 m/s.

## Methods
Secondary analysis of the multicenter HAMIR prospective cohort study (NCT05343052) with NCAA Division-1 American football athletes (n=20 males with prior unilateral ACLR, mean age 21.2±1.3 yr). Seven-IMU system (Xsens MVN Awinda) worn during maximal effort overground sprints on turf (20–60 yards). MATLAB scripts identified 2–5 consecutive strides near peak speed. Stance time, KFIC, PKF, and knee flexion excursion were computed from a 4th-order Butterworth low-pass filtered (20 Hz) joint angle signal. Linear mixed effects models (Satterthwaite method) with random athlete intercepts; adjusted for peak running speed.

## Findings
No significant limb × time interactions or main effects of limb or time from surgery were observed for any kinematic variable. Between-limb differences (uninvolved minus involved): stance time −1.3 ms [95% CI: −6.2, 3.6]; KFIC 1.4° [−2.0, 4.8]; PKF 0.3° [−2.8, 3.4]; knee flexion excursion −1.2° [−3.2, 0.8]. Peak sprint speed significantly predicted stance time (β=−15.13, p<0.01). Uninvolved limb KFIC was 26.3° [21.7, 31.0] and involved limb KFIC was 24.9° [20.3, 29.5], both comparable to healthy reference values.

## Limitations
Study limited to stance phase kinematics only; kinetic asymmetries (joint moments, power) remain unexplored. Small sample size (n=20) with wide range of time from surgery (7.3–68.1 months) and variable sprint distances by position. IMU methods most valid in the sagittal plane; frontal and transverse plane mechanics not examined. Findings may not generalize beyond high-level male American football players. Concomitant injuries, graft type, and quadriceps strength were not fully analyzed due to sample size constraints.

## Practical Implications
Clinicians and S&C staff working with ACLR athletes in Division-1 football can use IMU-based on-field sprinting assessments as a feasible, repeatable tool to evaluate biomechanical readiness. The absence of sprint kinematic asymmetries at ~2 years post-ACLR in fully participating athletes is reassuring, but underlying kinetic deficits and quadriceps atrophy may still persist and should be independently assessed. Rehabilitation should continue to prioritize restoring quadriceps strength and knee extension function even when sprint kinematics appear symmetric.

## Athlete Development Notes
For American football player development, this study establishes that elite athletes cleared for full participation after ACLR can sprint with symmetrical knee kinematics. High-demand sprinting may serve as a useful functional benchmark during return-to-performance progressions, distinguishing it from submaximal running where asymmetries often persist. Wearable IMU sprint testing is a practical on-field tool scalable to team settings across practice seasons.

## Return to Play Notes
At approximately 2 years post-ACLR, Division-1 football athletes demonstrated no sprint kinematic asymmetries, suggesting that athletes who meet return-to-sport criteria and are fully participating do not exhibit limb deficits during high-intensity sprinting. However, quadriceps atrophy and potential kinetic asymmetries may persist independent of kinematics. Sprint symmetry alone should not be used as the sole RTP criterion; comprehensive assessment of muscle volume, strength, and kinetics is recommended.
