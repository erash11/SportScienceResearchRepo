# Binney ZO, Bachynski KE. Estimating the prevalence at death of CTE neuropathology among professional football players. Neurology. 2019;92:43-45. doi:10.1212/WNL.0000000000006699

## TLDR
The frequently cited '99% of NFL players have CTE' statistic from the largest brain bank study is heavily influenced by selection bias, as symptomatic players are far more likely to donate their brains. After correcting for this bias across a fully enumerated cohort of 1,142 deceased NFL players, estimated CTE prevalence ranges from approximately 10% to near 100% depending on the strength of the selection effect. Even at conservative assumptions, CTE prevalence in deceased professional football players remains meaningfully elevated — a fact that carries important implications for long-term athlete health monitoring and policy.

## Abstract
This brief methodological analysis used bias analysis to estimate the plausible range of CTE prevalence at death among former NFL players, contextualizing the widely-reported 99% finding from a 2017 JAMA brain bank study (Mez et al.). The authors obtained a fully enumerated cohort of 1,142 NFL players who died between February 2008 and May 2016 from Pro-Football-Reference.com. By modeling the selection bias inherent in voluntary brain donation — where symptomatic players are more likely to donate — the estimated true CTE prevalence ranges from 9.6% to near 100% depending on the assumed donation probability among those with CTE. At a 50% donation probability, prevalence is estimated at 19.3%; at 90%, it is 10.7%. CTE prevalence remains high under most plausible scenarios.

## Methods
Bias analysis applied to the Mez et al. (2017) JAMA brain bank study. A complete list of 1,142 former NFL players who died during the study timeframe (February 2008 to May 2016) was obtained from Pro-Football-Reference.com as the fully enumerated eligible cohort. The selection effect was quantified using two of three possible bias parameters: (1) the probability that a player with CTE donated his brain, and (3) the ratio of donation probability among CTE cases versus non-cases. These were used to calculate corrected CTE prevalence estimates across a range of plausible selection scenarios and graphed as a sensitivity curve.

## Findings
Estimated CTE prevalence ranges from 9.6% to near 100% depending on the proportion of CTE cases captured by the brain bank. Assuming 50% of CTE cases donated their brains yields an estimated prevalence of 19.3%, with those having CTE being 461 times more likely to donate than those without. Assuming 90% of CTE cases donated yields a prevalence of 10.7%. Under most plausible scenarios, CTE prevalence in deceased NFL players remains high. The uncorrected (unadjusted) prevalence from the Mez et al. study was 99% (110/111 NFL players).

## Limitations
This analysis does not estimate CTE prevalence in college or semi-professional players, where rates may be lower. The proportion of CTE neuropathology cases who exhibit neurologic symptoms while alive remains unclear, complicating interpretation. If CTE causes earlier death on average, CTE cases may still be overrepresented even after this correction. The analysis is based on only 111 retired NFL players with donated brains. The most probable strength of the selection effect is not estimated; only a range is provided.

## Practical Implications
The true prevalence of CTE in professional football players is substantially uncertain due to the voluntary, symptomatic nature of brain donation. While the 99% headline figure is likely an overestimate due to selection bias, corrected estimates still suggest meaningfully elevated CTE risk in this population. Organizations responsible for athlete long-term health should not dismiss CTE risk based on the over-inflation argument, and continued investment in in-vivo CTE biomarker development and longitudinal cognitive monitoring of former players is warranted.

## Athlete Development Notes
This study does not directly address athlete development during playing careers. However, its finding that CTE risk — even after selection-bias correction — may affect a meaningful proportion of professional football players supports efforts to reduce cumulative head impact exposure during player development, including limiting full-contact practice, minimizing helmet-to-helmet contact in technique training, and implementing progressive contact exposure models across developmental levels.

## Return to Play Notes
This study does not address return to play from acute injury. However, the elevated CTE prevalence estimates — even after conservative bias correction — reinforce the importance of stringent concussion RTP protocols and cumulative head impact monitoring across a professional career. The findings support the use of longitudinal cognitive baseline testing and post-career neurological monitoring programs as components of comprehensive athlete health management.
