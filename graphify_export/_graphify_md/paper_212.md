# Buchheit M, Settembre M, Hader K, McHugh D. Exposures to near-to-maximal speed running bouts during different turnarounds in elite football: association with match hamstring injuries. Biology of Sport. 2023;40(4):1057–1067.

## TLDR
In elite football, the majority of players arrive at match day without having been exposed to near-to-maximal sprinting speed training during the current turnaround. For half the turnarounds examined, there were no match hamstring injuries when players reached >95% MSS during training. Critically, there were no match hamstring injuries when >95% MSS exposures occurred specifically on D-2 (two days before the match), suggesting that programming high-speed running at D-2 may be a protective strategy against match day hamstring injuries.

## Abstract
This retrospective analysis of 36 team-seasons (16 elite European football teams; 627 players; 5,052 training session days; 24,486 player-turnarounds; 152 non-contact hamstring injuries >3 days time loss) examined the occurrence of near-to-maximal sprinting speed (near-to-MSS) running bouts at >85%, >90%, and >95% MSS during training across different turnaround lengths (3–8 days between matches) and their association with match hamstring injury rates. Data were collected from the Kitman Labs platform across EPL, Serie A, Ligue 1, Bundesliga, Scottish Premiership, MLS, and Dutch Eredivisie (January 2018–December 2021). Match hamstring injury rates were compared between turnarounds with and without near-to-MSS exposures and by the day(s) within the turnaround when exposures occurred.

## Methods
Retrospective observational study using data from 36 team-seasons across 19 elite football clubs in top European leagues plus MLS (Jan 2018–Dec 2021) via the Kitman Labs platform. After exclusion criteria, 620 players, 5,052 training session days, and 24,486 player-turnarounds were analyzed. Near-to-MSS was defined as individual speed thresholds at >85%, >90%, and >95% of each player's maximal sprinting speed (MSS; derived from GPS data or highest recorded in-season speed). Turnarounds from 3–8 days were examined. Injury rates expressed per 1,000 player-turnarounds with 95% confidence intervals (exact binomial). Substantial differences assumed when CIs did not overlap.

## Findings
Overall, 60%, 76%, and 90% of player-turnarounds contained no >85%, >90%, or >95% MSS exposures, respectively. For half the turnarounds, no match hamstring injuries occurred when players were exposed to running bouts >95% MSS. Injuries still occurred in 85% of turnarounds when there were no or only lower relative speed exposures (>85% or >90%). No hamstring injuries occurred when >95% MSS exposures were programmed at D-2 (n=480; 0 injuries) compared to D-3 and earlier (multiple injuries observed). For the pooled 4- to 8-d turnarounds, D-2 with >95% MSS was the only day pattern associated with zero injuries. Injury rates were not significantly different between turnarounds with vs. without exposures when all turnarounds were pooled.

## Limitations
Observational design precludes causal inference. MSS was inconsistently tested across teams; average of three highest GPS speeds used as proxy when testing unavailable, which may underestimate true MSS. Injury records rely on practitioners' entries. Analysis is univariate; chronic training loads, match minutes, player age, and injury history were not simultaneously controlled. Teams in the dataset may be biased toward evidence-based practitioners already valuing high-speed work. The low number of exposures at specific day/turnaround combinations limits some comparisons.

## Practical Implications
Programming >95% MSS running bouts at D-2 (two days before the match) appears to be the most strategically advantageous timing for near-to-MSS exposures in terms of match hamstring injury mitigation. Finishing and transition drills covering >30 m are among the few training activities that reliably elicit near-to-MSS intensities. Staff should not rely on small-sided games to achieve near-to-MSS exposures. During congested fixture periods (3–4 day turnarounds), near-to-MSS work may only be feasible for substitutes and non-starters.

## Athlete Development Notes
For player development and long-term athletic preparation in football, this study reinforces that regular exposure to near-to-maximal sprinting is necessary to prepare hamstrings for the demands of match play. Developing players should be systematically exposed to >90–95% MSS running in their weekly microcycle, with awareness that longer turnarounds allow more flexibility in programming these high-speed bouts. A chronic dose of near-to-MSS exposure is hypothesized to reduce hamstring injury susceptibility over a season.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes returning from hamstring injury should be progressively re-exposed to near-to-maximal sprint velocities (>85%, then >90%, then >95% MSS) before full match reintegration, as insufficient speed exposure is associated with elevated match hamstring injury risk. Timing of high-speed re-exposure in the week leading to return-to-competition is critical; D-2 appears to be an optimal programming day. RTP criteria should include demonstration of near-to-MSS tolerance in training before match availability is confirmed.
