# Brett BL, Huber DL, Wild A, Nelson LD, McCrea MA. Age of First Exposure to American Football and Behavioral, Cognitive, Psychological, and Physical Outcomes in High School and Collegiate Football Players. Sports Health. 2019;Vol XX(No X).

## TLDR
Starting football before age 12 was not associated with worse behavioral, cognitive, psychological, or physical outcomes in high school and college players; earlier starters actually reported lower somatic symptom severity, suggesting youth football participation does not impair functioning during adolescence.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study examined the relationship between age of first exposure (AFE) to American football and behavioral, cognitive, psychological, and physical outcomes in 1,802 active high school and collegiate football players. Players were dichotomized into AFE before age 12 vs. age 12 or older, with general linear models used to assess differences across numerous clinical outcome measures. After controlling for covariates including age, concussion history, race, and ADHD diagnosis, AFE before age 12 did not significantly predict poorer performance on any clinical outcome measures (Ps >0.05). A single statistically significant association was found: AFE <12 was associated with lower somatization scores, suggesting potentially less somatic distress.

## Methods
Cross-sectional study; N=1,802 active high school (9 schools) and collegiate (4 colleges) football players enrolled in the Project Head to Head 1 and 2 studies (August 2012–September 2017). AFE was self-reported and dichotomized at age 12. Potential covariates identified via t-tests and chi-square tests were entered into a multivariable binary logistic regression. Single-predictor general linear models (GLMs) and covariate-adjusted GLMs were performed for each clinical outcome variable including neurocognitive (ImPACT, SAC, TMT, WAIS-IV), psychological (BSI-18, BSSS, DIS-11), and physical (BESS, King-Devick) measures.

## Findings
- AFE <12 vs. ≥12 did not significantly differ on any clinical outcome after covariate adjustment (all Ps >0.05 except somatization).
- AFE <12 was significantly associated with lower BSI-18 somatization scores (F=11.39, p=0.01) after covariate adjustment.
- Without covariates, AFE <12 showed significantly higher ImPACT visual memory scores (F=6.98, p<0.01).
- Covariates of age, race, ADHD, and concussion history differed significantly between AFE groups and were more strongly associated with clinical outcomes than AFE itself.
- Effect sizes for AFE on clinical outcomes were uniformly near zero (partial η² ≤ 0.005).

## Limitations
Cross-sectional design limits causal inference. AFE relied on athlete self-report, introducing recall bias. Other sport participation throughout development was not controlled. High correlation between AFE and total football exposure (r=0.80) makes it difficult to disentangle the two. Results may not generalize to former or older athletes where long-term sequelae may be more apparent.

## Practical Implications
Current evidence does not support restricting youth football participation before age 12 based on cognitive or psychological harm during adolescence. Screening and management of ADHD, concussion history, and demographic factors are more clinically relevant predictors of outcome than age of football start. Programs focused on athlete well-being should address these modifiable health factors rather than focusing solely on AFE.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly relevant to American football player development. Findings suggest that beginning tackle football before age 12 does not impair neurocognitive or psychological development through high school and college. This supports age-appropriate youth football participation without evidence of intermediate-term harm, though long-term effects in former players remain an open question.

## Return to Play Notes
No direct return-to-play implications studied. However, the null finding for AFE on concussion-related outcomes (SAC, SCAT3 symptom scores) suggests that a younger start to football does not inherently elevate concussion symptom burden at baseline, which is relevant context for baseline testing and RTP protocols in young athletes.
