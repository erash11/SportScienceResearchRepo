# Farnell A. False Start? An Analysis of NFL Penalties With and Without Crowds. J Sports Econ. 2023. DOI: 10.1177/15270025211148997.

## TLDR
Using the COVID-19 natural experiment of crowd-free NFL games, this study finds that crowd noise meaningfully reduces home defensive penalties by making it harder for the visiting Quarterback's cadence to fool the defense. False start penalties by offenses were unaffected by crowd size. This isolates a specific crowd mechanism — communication disruption — that contributes to home advantage, beyond any referee bias explanation.

## Abstract
Using play-by-play data from 108,059 NFL snaps across the 2018-2020 regular seasons (including COVID-19-restricted 2020 season with dramatically reduced crowds), this study examined whether crowd absence affected Quarterback ability to manipulate opposing defenses via cadence. Logistic regression models estimated the probability of pre-snap line-of-scrimmage penalties (offensive false starts; defensive offside, encroachment, and neutral zone infractions) as a function of stadium capacity utilization, home/away status, and controls. Results showed that false starts by offenses were not significantly affected by crowd absence. However, home defenses were significantly less likely to commit defensive pre-snap penalties when crowds were larger, consistent with crowd noise drowning out the visiting Quarterback's cadence.

## Methods
Play-by-play data from NFL Savant and attendance data from Pro Football Reference for 2018-2020 regular seasons (n=108,059 snaps after excluding international games, kneels, and kickoffs). Outcome variables: binary indicators for offensive false start penalties and defensive line-of-scrimmage penalties (offside, encroachment, neutral zone infraction). Key predictor: stadium capacity utilization (Percent Full). Logistic regression models estimated separately for offensive and defensive penalties, with controls for quarter, minute, down, yards to go, yard line, score difference, closed roof, and offense/defense team fixed effects. Interaction terms included Percent Full × Home Team. Robustness checks used binary empty/near-empty stadium indicators and excluded games with backup Quarterbacks.

## Findings
Home teams enjoyed a ~5-point advantage consistent with prior literature. For false start penalties, higher capacity utilization was positively associated with false starts in baseline models, but the interaction with offense-at-home was not significant, indicating crowd effects on false starts did not differ between home and away offenses. For defensive penalties, the interaction of Percent Full × Defense at Home was negative and significant (marginal effect: -0.003 percentage points per 1% capacity increase), meaning home defenses committed fewer defensive penalties with larger crowds, while away defenses showed no significant crowd effect. Robustness checks with empty/near-empty dummies confirmed: empty or close-to-empty stadiums were associated with significantly more defensive penalties called against the home defense (interaction coefficients negative and significant). Quarterback absence did not explain findings.

## Limitations
Study relies on the COVID-19 natural experiment, which introduced confounders beyond crowd absence (changed training regimes, isolation protocols, backup QB usage). Cannot perfectly identify individual QB fixed effects due to data limitations, though offense team fixed effects are used as a proxy. Crowd noise is the assumed mechanism but cannot be directly measured. The study covers only three NFL seasons and only pre-snap non-discretionary penalties; findings may not generalize to all penalty types or other sports. The 2021 season was excluded due to rule changes (17-game season).

## Practical Implications
Crowd noise confers a measurable defensive advantage for home teams by disrupting the visiting Quarterback's ability to use cadence manipulation. NFL teams seeking to maximize home advantage should actively encourage crowd noise on defensive snaps, particularly on 3rd and 4th down situations where cadence deception is most impactful. Away teams preparing for loud road environments should dedicate practice time to simulating crowd noise to maintain cadence communication with the offensive line.

## Athlete Development Notes
Effective communication between a Quarterback and his offense under crowd noise conditions is a trainable skill. Offensive linemen and skill players should practice snap count recognition under noise during training camp and in-season preparation for loud away environments. Teams that rely heavily on no-huddle or up-tempo offenses are particularly dependent on this communication skill.

## Return to Play Notes
Not directly applicable to return-to-play clinical decision-making. However, the study demonstrates that environmental stressors (crowd noise) measurably impact player execution of pre-snap communication tasks, suggesting that rehabilitation and return-to-play protocols for players in communication-critical roles (e.g., Quarterbacks, offensive linemen) should consider cognitive and communication readiness under simulated game-environment stressors.
