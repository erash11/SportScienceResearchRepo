# Stemper BD, Harezlak J, Shah AS, Rowson S, Mihalik JP, Riggen L, Duma S, Pasquina P, Broglio SP, McAllister TW, McCrea MA, and CARE Consortium Investigators. Association Between Preseason/Regular Season Head Impact Exposure and Concussion Incidence in NCAA Football. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise. 2022.

## TLDR
Elevated head impact exposure during the college football preseason is associated with increased concussion risk not only during the preseason but throughout the entire fall season, suggesting that preseason HIE may reduce an athlete's concussion tolerance in a sustained manner—making preseason load management a critical injury prevention target.

## Abstract
This study used data from 1,120 athlete-seasons across six NCAA Division I football programs over five years (2015–2019) from the CARE Consortium Advanced Research Core (ARC) to examine associations between head impact exposure (HIE) and concussion incidence during different periods of the college football season. Head Impact Telemetry (HIT) System data were collected in real time, and concussions were diagnosed by team medical staff using DoD/AAN consensus definitions. The analysis quantified HIE during preseason, regular season, and total season periods using cumulative impact count and risk-weighted exposure (RWE) metrics, then related these to concussion incidence during those respective periods. Strong associations were identified between preseason HIE and both preseason and total season concussion incidence, suggesting a prolonged effect of preseason HIE on concussion risk extending throughout the full fall season.

## Methods
Retrospective analysis of prospectively collected CARE Consortium ARC data; 1,120 athlete-seasons from six NCAA Division I programs; 2015–2019 fall seasons; Head Impact Telemetry (HIT) System for real-time HIE measurement; concussion incidence monitored by team medical staff per CARE/DoD/AAN protocol; HIE quantified as cumulative impact count and risk-weighted exposure (RWE) summing individual concussion risk functions; periods analyzed: preseason, regular season, total season; associations between HIE in one period and concussion incidence in same and subsequent periods analyzed; IRB approved at Medical College of Wisconsin with reliance agreements at all sites.

## Findings
• Preseason HIE was significantly associated with preseason concussion incidence
• Preseason HIE was also significantly associated with total season concussion incidence
• Total season HIE was significantly associated with total season concussion incidence
• NCAA Division I football athletes sustained a median of 426 impacts (75th percentile: 754) over a full season in prior related work
• Preseason HIE occurs at approximately twice the proportion of regular season HIE
• Individual preseason HIE was associated with season-long HIE, consistent with individual playing-style differences persisting across a season
• Findings provide first evidence for a sustained, season-long reduction in concussion tolerance associated with elevated preseason HIE

## Limitations
Observational design precludes causal inference; HIT System limited to specific Riddell helmet models; variability in playing time not fully controlled; concussion diagnosis dependent on athlete self-report and clinical recognition (subject to underreporting); RWE metric based on biomechanical risk functions not validated for all athlete populations; limited to six Division I programs, limiting generalizability to other competitive levels.

## Practical Implications
Preseason practice structure is a critical lever for managing season-long concussion risk in college football. Reducing head impact exposure during preseason—through rule changes (e.g., eliminating two-a-day practices, already adopted by the NCAA), modified contact drills, and practice session management—may have protective effects extending throughout the entire competitive season. Sports medicine and S&C staff should treat preseason HIE load management as a primary injury prevention strategy.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American college football. The finding that preseason HIE predicts season-long concussion incidence has major implications for how programs structure preseason training. Individual athletes with consistently high playing-style-driven HIE across preseason and regular season may represent a chronically elevated-risk population warranting individualized monitoring and intervention.

## Return to Play Notes
The concept of a sustained reduction in concussion tolerance following elevated HIE is directly relevant to RTP protocols. Athletes returning from concussion during a high-HIE preseason may face elevated re-injury risk for the remainder of the season. Clinicians should consider cumulative preseason HIE as a contextual risk factor when making RTP decisions and may benefit from monitoring ongoing HIE load post-clearance.
