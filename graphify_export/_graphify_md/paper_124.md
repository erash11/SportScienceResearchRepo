# Rim AJ, Liu C, Jackson M, Miller JT, Chukwumerije N, El Chami R, Ibrahim R, Kauser T, Miller A, Simpson E, Vaughn WL, Galante A, Clark C, Putukian M, Grashow R, Baggish AL, Kim JH. Concussions Are Associated With Increases in Blood Pressure and Cardiovascular Risk in American-Style Football Athletes. JACC Advances. 2025;4(5):101717. DOI: 10.1016/j.jacadv.2025.101717

## TLDR
Collegiate football players who sustain concussions develop measurable and progressive cardiovascular damage — including arterial stiffening and rising blood pressure — that extends well beyond the brain. This is the first prospective longitudinal study demonstrating that concussions are an independent cardiovascular risk factor among active ASF athletes, with hypertension prevalence rising from 36% to 86% in concussed athletes over two seasons. Sports medicine and performance staff should incorporate blood pressure surveillance into standard post-concussion management protocols, as hypertension may develop silently and compound long-term health risks for football athletes.

## Abstract
This longitudinal case-controlled study examined whether clinically diagnosed concussions during collegiate American-style football (ASF) participation are associated with increases in systolic blood pressure (SBP) and maladaptive cardiovascular phenotypes. Thirty-nine ASF athletes who sustained concussions were followed for up to 2 seasons and compared to 39 weight- and position-matched controls at two NCAA Division-I programs (Georgia Tech, Furman). Cardiovascular assessments included blood pressure, echocardiography, and vascular applanation tonometry. Concussions were independently associated with increased pulse wave velocity (PWV), elevated SBP and DBP, increased LV mass index, and decreased diastolic function (E' velocity), findings that persisted and worsened through 2 seasons of follow-up.

## Methods
Longitudinal case-controlled analysis at Georgia Institute of Technology and Furman University from 2014–2019 and 2021–2022. Cases (n=39) sustained at least one clinically diagnosed concussion (per SCAT5 criteria, adjudicated by team physicians); controls (n=39) were matched by weight (≤5 kg difference) and player position (lineman vs. non-lineman). Assessments included manual blood pressure (average of 2 readings after ≥15 min rest), 2D transthoracic echocardiography (LV mass index, tissue Doppler E' velocity for diastolic function), and vascular applanation tonometry (carotid-femoral pulse wave velocity, PWV). All 39 pairs completed 1 year; 14 pairs completed 2 years. Mixed-effects linear regression models adjusted for weight, player position, and race were used. Concussion coded as a cumulative lasting effect.

## Findings
After 1 season postconcussion, cases showed significantly increased PWV (case Δ=0.4 [0.2] m/s, P=0.036; control Δ=−0.1 [0.1] m/s, P=0.38). After 2 seasons, cases demonstrated increased SBP (Δ=10.1 [3.6] mm Hg, P<0.01), increased DBP (Δ=8.2 [2.9] mm Hg, P<0.01), and decreased E' velocity (Δ=−2.8 [0.8] cm/s, P=0.001); none of these changes occurred in controls. In multivariable analysis over 1 season, concussion was independently associated with higher PWV (β=0.33, 95% CI: 0.09-0.56, P=0.007). Over 2 seasons, concussions were associated with higher PWV (β=0.42, P=0.03), higher DBP (β=5.89, P=0.01), higher LV mass index (β=11.01, P<0.001), and lower E' velocity (β=−2.11, P<0.001). Prevalence of combined stage 1 and 2 hypertension among cases increased from 36% to 86% over 2 seasons (P=0.02) versus no change in controls.

## Limitations
NSAID use, sleep apnea, alcohol intake, prior exercise exposures, and PED use are potential confounders, though no PED test positives or differences in prescription stimulant use were found between groups. Concussions sustained prior to college matriculation were not captured. Study focused only on collegiate ASF athletes, limiting generalizability to other levels of play. Detailed clinical concussion data (LOC, symptom severity, time away from competition) were not fully available. Subject attrition occurred over the 2-year follow-up, reducing the 2-season cohort to 14 pairs.

## Practical Implications
Blood pressure monitoring should be integrated into post-concussion management protocols at all levels of football — not only at RTP clearance but throughout recovery and into the following season. Athletes with concussion histories should receive regular cardiovascular screening inclusive of arterial stiffness and BP tracking. Clinical management of the concussed athlete may need to extend beyond neurologic recovery to include cardiovascular surveillance, with antihypertensive pharmacotherapy initiated when appropriate per current hypertension guidelines.

## Athlete Development Notes
This study raises important questions about the long-term health trajectory of football athletes who sustain concussions during their development years. Coaches, strength staff, and sports medicine personnel should understand that concussions may produce cardiovascular consequences that persist after neurological clearance. Reducing concussion exposure through proper technique development, helmet fitting, and contact practice limitations is not only a brain-health strategy but potentially a cardiovascular health strategy as well.

## Return to Play Notes
Current RTP protocols focus on neurologic recovery, but this study suggests cardiovascular markers — particularly blood pressure and arterial stiffness — may remain abnormal even after clinical neurologic clearance. Adding blood pressure assessment as a component of the concussion RTP evaluation could identify athletes with developing hypertension. Persistent PWV elevation or rising blood pressure post-concussion may warrant extended monitoring before full RTP, and referral to sports cardiology should be considered for athletes with repeated concussions.
