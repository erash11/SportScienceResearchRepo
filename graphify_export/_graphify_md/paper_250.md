# Luxenburg D, Wasserman N, Geller J, Perez J, Burke J, Kaplan L. Hamstring injuries in the national football league: An epidemiological study. Journal of Orthopaedics. 2023;35:111–114.

## TLDR
This NFL epidemiological study found that Linebackers and Defensive Backs sustain hamstring injuries at roughly twice the expected rate relative to their time on the field, while Offensive and Defensive Linemen are at substantially lower risk. Neither artificial turf nor playing on short rest (Thursday games) significantly increased hamstring injury rates compared to other injury types or to games played on normal rest. Hamstring injuries tended to occur slightly earlier in the season than other injuries. For football staff, position-specific monitoring and targeted prevention programs for skill-position players — particularly those who sprint, cut, and change direction frequently — are warranted.

## Abstract
NOTE: IDs 249 and 250 are confirmed duplicates — the same paper by Luxenburg et al. (2023). ID 250 is the final published version (Journal of Orthopaedics, vol. 35, pp. 111–114); ID 249 is the journal pre-proof. Content is identical. This study used official NFL game books from the 2013–2016 regular seasons to analyze 78 in-game hamstring strain injuries (HSIs). Primary aim was to examine relative HSI incidence by position using standardized incidence ratios (SIRs). Secondary aims examined field surface, short rest, and week of season. Linebackers (SIR 2.02) and Defensive Backs (SIR 1.62) had significantly higher relative incidence than expected; Offensive (SIR 0.28) and Defensive Linemen (SIR 0.29) had significantly lower incidence. No significant differences for turf vs. grass or short-rest games were found.

## Methods
Retrospective epidemiological analysis using publicly available official NFL game books from the 2013–2016 regular seasons. Seventy-eight qualifying in-game hamstring strain injuries (strains, partial tears, complete tears) were identified and cross-referenced with Foxsports.com. Standardized incidence ratios (SIRs) were calculated by dividing actual relative positional incidence by expected relative positional incidence (based on 2020–2021 personnel data). Two-proportion Z-tests, Chi-Squared tests of independence, and unpaired two-sample t-tests were used for statistical comparisons (α=0.05).

## Findings
78 qualifying HSIs identified; 10.3% were re-injuries within the study period. Linebackers and Running Backs had the highest SIRs (both 2.02); Defensive Backs had SIR 1.62. Offensive Linemen (SIR 0.28) and Defensive Linemen (SIR 0.29) had significantly lower incidence than expected. No significant relationship between field surface (turf vs. grass) and HSI (p=0.082 for proportion difference; p=0.283 for chi-squared independence test). No significant difference in HSI rate on short rest (4-day weeks) vs. regular rest (p=0.959). HSIs did not occur more on short rest than other injury types (p=0.861). Mean week of HSI was 8.05 (95% CI: 7.06–9.04); median 7.5, slightly earlier in the season than the midpoint.

## Limitations
Data derived solely from official game books; no information on prior medical history or injuries outside the study window. Game books do not report injury mechanism or severity. Positional frequency data (2020 defensive, 2021 offensive) are from different seasons than the injury data (2013–2016), though minimal positional changes have occurred since 2018. Only in-game regular-season injuries captured; practice and preseason injuries excluded.

## Practical Implications
Position-specific hamstring injury prevention programs should prioritize Linebackers and Defensive Backs. Thursday night game scheduling (short rest) does not appear to elevate hamstring injury risk. Field surface choice (turf vs. grass) also does not significantly alter hamstring injury rates. Early-season heightened monitoring may be appropriate as HSIs trend toward occurring in the first half of the season.

## Athlete Development Notes
Position-specific strength and conditioning programs targeting sprint mechanics, eccentric hamstring strength, and H:Q ratio are especially important for skill players (LBs, DBs, RBs) who repeatedly accelerate, decelerate, and change direction. Development programs should incorporate sports-specific movements replicating the takeoff phase of sprinting where hamstring injuries most often occur.

## Return to Play Notes
NFL players have a 38.4% HSI recurrence rate and a 13.4% increased reinjury risk if returning within two weeks. RTP decisions for Linebackers and Defensive Backs warrant additional conservatism given their high re-injury risk and positional demands. A robust RTP protocol should confirm adequate H:Q ratios and bilateral strength symmetry before clearance.
