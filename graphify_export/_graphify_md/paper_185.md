# Clements E, Ehrmann F, Clark A, Jones M, McCall A, Duffield R. Effects of Prematch Travel and Training on Physical and Technical Performance in International Football Matches. International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance. 2024 (Ahead of Print). doi:10.1123/ijspp.2023-0511

## TLDR
Prematch international travel had minimal effects on physical and technical match performance when players arrived at least 48 hours before kickoff. Eastward travel was associated with small increases in total and very high-speed distance and a 5% reduction in pass accuracy, though this likely reflects playing against higher-ranked opponents rather than travel fatigue per se. The most actionable finding is that training high-speed running in the 3 days following arrival was positively related to match high-speed running output, meaning readiness to train on arrival should be the primary focus for national team management.

## Abstract
This study examined the relationship between prematch travel variables (duration, direction, time-zone change, arrival time) and in-camp training load on ensuing physical and technical match performance in 68 national-team footballers competing in 108 international matches. GPS-derived match running data and Opta technical statistics were aligned with travel records and 3-day training load sums. Linear mixed models assessed the relationships, accounting for player, match, and position as random effects.

## Methods
Observational study; 68 male senior national team footballers (age 25.4±3.6 y); N=108 matches (October 2018–March 2023, including 2022 FIFA World Cup qualifying); GPS (STATSports Apex) for physical data; Opta for technical data; travel variables: duration (0.85–31 h), direction, time-zone difference, hours from arrival to kickoff; 3-day training load sum prior to each match; linear mixed models with player ID, match ID, and position as random effects.

## Findings
Travel variables explained little variance in outcomes (R²=0.02–0.16). Eastward travel was associated with more total distance (+3.003 m/min, p=0.042) and very high-speed distance (+0.336 m/min, p=0.030) per match, and a 5% reduction in pass accuracy (p=0.012). Greater time-zone difference was associated with more match decelerations (p=0.027). Higher training high-speed distance was associated with greater match high-speed distance (p=0.004) and more decelerations (p<0.001). Higher training total distance was associated with fewer match decelerations (p=0.002). 95% of athletes arrived ≥48 hours before matches.

## Limitations
Single national team limits generalisability to teams with different travel strategies. Small sample (n=103 data points after exclusions); only 2 of 14 matches were against higher-ranked opponents; 71% of matches resulted in wins, potentially masking performance decrements. Jet lag mitigation strategies were not controlled. No control over athlete behaviour before, during, or after travel.

## Practical Implications
National team football staff should prioritise having players arrive at least 48 hours (ideally longer for multi-time-zone travel) before international matches to minimise travel-related performance risk. Because training high-speed running loads in the 3 days prior to matches relate to match running outputs, staff should monitor and optimise athlete readiness to train upon arrival. When early arrival is unavoidable, jet lag management strategies (light therapy, sleep hygiene, strategic napping) should be implemented.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football players required to travel for national team duty, managing the transition from club to national team training is critical. Building high-speed running exposure into the first 3 days of camp, contingent on athlete recovery status, supports match-day running performance. Position-specific training plans should be adapted to the travel demands experienced by each player upon arrival.

## Return to Play Notes
The finding that travel had minimal impact on performance when 48+ hours of recovery was allowed is relevant for players returning to competition after medical travel or injury rehabilitation away from base. Clinicians should allow adequate recovery windows after air travel when scheduling return-to-play progression milestones, particularly for high-speed running and technical skill reassessment.
