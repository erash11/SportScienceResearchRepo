# May JM, Angileri HS, McLoughlin DE, Owen MM, Terry M, Tjong V. Decreased Concussion Incidence Following the Implementation of the Targeting Rules: An Updated Epidemiology of National Football League Concussions From 2017 to 2022. Cureus. 2023;15(12):e50997. DOI 10.7759/cureus.50997

## TLDR
NFL concussions dropped 38% after the 2018 Targeting rule was enacted, and games missed per concussion fell 38% as well — suggesting both fewer and less severe head injuries. Defensive backs and tight ends had the highest concussion rates (10.46 and 10.69 per 1,000 AEs respectively), while the defensive line had the highest average games missed per concussion (3.97). The addition of a 17th game in 2021 did not increase total concussions. Offensive and defensive units experienced similar reductions. For football performance staff, these trends validate rule-driven and equipment-driven safety investments, but concussions causing multiple missed games persist, particularly in defensive linemen.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiological study retrospectively reviewed concussions across five NFL seasons (2017-18 through 2021-22) using publicly available injury reports to characterize incidence, games missed, and positional differences. A total of 368 concussions resulting in at least one missed game were identified across 1,275 regular and postseason games. There was a statistically significant 38% decrease in total concussions following the 2017-18 season (p=0.02), coinciding with the introduction of the NFL Targeting rule in 2018, with the 2021-22 season recording the fewest concussions (61) despite the addition of a 17th regular season game.

## Methods
Retrospective descriptive epidemiological study of five NFL seasons (2017-18 to 2021-22). Data extracted from publicly available injury databases (Pro Football Reference, NFL.com, ESPN) and cross-referenced for accuracy. Only concussions resulting in at least one missed regular season or postseason game were included; preseason and training camp concussions were excluded. Concussion incidence normalized per 1,000 athlete exposures (AEs), with 1 AE = 1 game per player. Sub-analyses by year, offensive/defensive unit, position group, BMI, and game week. Single-factor ANOVA used for statistical comparisons; significance set at p ≤ 0.05.

## Findings
368 total concussions over five seasons; rate of 0.30 per game and 7.04 per 1,000 AEs. The 2017-18 season had 102 concussions (highest); subsequent four seasons averaged 66.75 (p=0.02, 38% decrease). Total games missed fell 53% post-2017-18 (p=0.05); average games missed per concussion fell from 3.46 to ~2.14. Defensive players had a significantly decreased concussion rate post-2017-18 (p=0.04); offensive players did not reach significance. Defensive backs: 10.46 per 1,000 AEs; tight ends: 10.69 per 1,000 AEs (highest by position). Defensive line: lowest concussion rate (3.70 per 1,000 AEs) but highest average games missed (3.97). Concussion concentration was significantly higher in the final third of the regular season (p=0.03). BMI global maximum at 27, local maximum at 38.

## Limitations
Only concussions causing at least one missed game were captured, excluding return-same-day or practice concussions (estimated 20% of total by prior research). Concussion remains a clinical diagnosis with known player non-disclosure bias. No direct injury mechanism data (game vs. practice) or medical clearance details were available. Severity could not be directly assessed; games missed used as proxy. The 38-percent reduction correlates with but cannot be causally attributed to the Targeting rule alone, as concurrent helmet technology changes and other rule modifications also occurred. Only one season of 17-game data exists, limiting generalizability.

## Practical Implications
The 38% reduction in NFL concussions following the Targeting rule and kickoff rule changes validates that administrative and equipment controls meaningfully reduce head injury burden. NFL and college football programs should continue investing in helmet technology, enforce targeting penalties rigorously, and monitor concussion clusters in the final third of the season when fatigue-related risk increases. Defensive linemen require special attention: despite the lowest concussion rate, they miss the most games per event, suggesting more severe injuries when they occur — likely due to cumulative microtrauma. Staff should implement fatigue-monitoring protocols as the season progresses.

## Athlete Development Notes
Positional concussion risk data provide critical guidance for athlete development programs. Tight ends and defensive backs face the highest rates of game-missing concussions, reinforcing the need for position-specific contact technique education and progressive load management during full-contact practices. Young defensive and offensive linemen should receive particular attention regarding head contact biomechanics and proper blocking technique to reduce the cumulative microtrauma associated with the high average games-missed observed in that group. BMI-related concussion clustering (peaks at BMI 27 and 38) suggests that body composition extremes warrant targeted monitoring.

## Return to Play Notes
Defensive linemen average nearly 4 games missed per concussion — the highest of any position — despite having the lowest concussion rate, underscoring that their concussions tend to be more severe or complex. Return-to-play timelines for this group should not be abbreviated based on position-group norms. The significant reduction in average games missed per concussion (from 3.46 in 2017-18 to ~2.14 in subsequent years) league-wide suggests that improved protocols and rule changes may be facilitating faster, safer returns. Medical staffs should be alert to elevated late-season concussion risk and factor cumulative season fatigue into RTP decision-making.
