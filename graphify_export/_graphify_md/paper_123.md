# Kerr ZY, Register-Mihalik JK, Kay MC, DeFreese JD, Marshall SW, Guskiewicz KM. Concussion Nondisclosure During Professional Career Among a Cohort of Former National Football League Athletes. Am J Sports Med. 2017. DOI: 10.1177/0363546517728264

## TLDR
Half of former NFL players who recalled sustaining concussions during their professional career (50.3%) reported not disclosing at least one concussion to team medical staff. Nondisclosure was more prevalent among nonwhite players (adjusted PR = 1.19), linebackers and linemen, those who played after the 1976 spearing rule change, and those with higher career concussion totals—ranging from 35.5% among players with 1-2 career concussions to 75.0% among those with 10 or more. For current NFL and college football programs, these findings reinforce that concussion detection cannot rely on athlete self-disclosure alone; proactive sideline surveillance, independent neurotrauma consultants, racially and culturally concordant medical staff, and a team culture that removes perceived penalties for disclosure are essential for meaningful concussion management.

## Abstract
This retrospective cross-sectional study examined the prevalence of and factors associated with concussion nondisclosure to team medical staff among 829 former NFL players who played from pre-WWII through 2001, using data from the Retired NFL Players General Health Survey administered in 2001 and 2010. Among those who recalled sustaining concussions, 50.3% reported not disclosing at least one concussion to medical staff during their professional career. Multivariable binomial regression identified race/ethnicity, position, playing era, and career concussion history as significant factors associated with nondisclosure, with nonwhite players and those with more career concussions showing the highest rates.

## Methods
Retrospective cross-sectional survey using the Retired NFL Players General Health Survey (GHS). The baseline 2001 GHS was sent to all living NFL Retired Players Association members (n = 3,729; 68.0% response rate). A 2010 follow-up GHS was sent to 2,102 eligible participants (62.6% response). The final analytical sample was 829 players who reported at least one career concussion with complete disclosure data. Concussion was defined as a blow to the head followed by associated symptoms. Multivariable binomial regression with Poisson residuals and robust variance estimation estimated adjusted prevalence ratios (PR) with 95% confidence intervals, controlling for race/ethnicity, number of years played, primary position played, professional career concussion history, and playing era (pre vs. post-1976 spearing rule change). Interaction terms between concussion history and playing era were included given P < .10 threshold.

## Findings
Overall, 417 (50.3%) of 829 former players reported not disclosing at least one concussion during their professional career. Nonwhite players had a significantly higher adjusted prevalence of nondisclosure than white/non-Hispanic players (PR = 1.19; 95% CI 1.02-1.38). In multivariable models, linebackers (PR = 1.53; 95% CI 1.11-2.11), offensive linemen (PR = 1.46; 95% CI 1.06-2.00), and defensive linemen (PR = 1.48; 95% CI 1.04-2.08) had significantly higher nondisclosure than running backs. A significant interaction between playing era and concussion history was found (P = .08): among players with 1-2 career concussions, post-spearing-rule-change players had 41% higher nondisclosure prevalence (PR = 1.41; 95% CI 1.05-1.89); among those with 3-4 concussions, 37% higher (PR = 1.37; 95% CI 1.07-1.74). Nondisclosure prevalence ranged from 35.5% (1-2 career concussions) to 75.0% (10+ career concussions).

## Limitations
Retrospective self-report design is subject to recall bias, social desirability bias, and measurement error inherent to reports of nonreports. The cohort does not include players who retired after 2001, limiting generalizability to modern NFL athletes. Reasons for nondisclosure were not captured. Findings may not generalize to other competition levels or sports. Concussion diagnoses relied on participant recall and may not meet clinical definitions. The study cannot establish causation.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football programs must recognize that concussion nondisclosure is systemic and cannot be solved by athlete education alone. Proactive concussion identification strategies—including independent sideline neurotrauma consultants, video review protocols, and structured symptom check-ins—are essential. Programs should evaluate racial and cultural concordance between athletes and medical staff, as discordance may reduce disclosure comfort. Team culture must explicitly remove perceived career penalties for disclosure. Athletes with high career concussion histories require enhanced active surveillance beyond standard self-report mechanisms.

## Athlete Development Notes
Nondisclosure of concussions during developmental years compounds long-term brain health risk, as unmanaged concussions are associated with depression, cognitive impairment, and chronic traumatic encephalopathy. Programs must build a culture of psychological safety around concussion reporting from the earliest developmental stages—youth through collegiate and professional levels—emphasizing that disclosure protects career longevity rather than threatening it. Trust between athletes and medical staff is a prerequisite for effective concussion management.

## Return to Play Notes
Because nondisclosure is highest among players with the most career concussions, current RTP protocols that rely heavily on athlete self-report of symptoms are structurally vulnerable. Active surveillance tools including validated sideline assessment instruments, independent neurotrauma consultants, and video review—as currently employed in the NFL—are essential complements to self-report. RTP protocols should be designed with the explicit assumption that some proportion of concussed players will not voluntarily disclose, particularly among linemen and linebackers who show the highest nondisclosure rates.
