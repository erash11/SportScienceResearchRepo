# Mack C, Matava M, Zeidler K, Sills A, Solomon G. Clinical Research in the National Football League: The Player Scientific and Medical Research Protocol. Current Sports Medicine Reports. 2020;19(5):168-174.

## TLDR
The NFL and NFLPA jointly created the Medical Research Approval Process (MRAP) to govern how player injury and illness data can be used for research. Seven pathways cover everything from internal club use to third-party publications, each with a specific approval chain. All research using NFL EMR data must go through this system. Key points for staff: research using NFL player data requires multi-level institutional approval; publicly available injury data (news reports, injury reports) are unreliable as primary sources; and any research project, even internally focused, must follow MRAP to avoid sanctions including committee removal and club discipline.

## Abstract
This invited commentary describes the NFL and NFLPA's jointly developed Medical Research Approval Process (MRAP), a structured, multilevel governance framework governing the use of NFL player workplace injury and illness data for clinical research. Established as a side letter to the 2011 CBA (effective January 26, 2015), MRAP outlines seven distinct research pathways based on the requester's affiliation, research intent, and data sensitivity. The protocol ensures research feasibility, protects player confidentiality, avoids duplication of effort, and requires sequential approvals from IQVIA, NFL legal, the NFL Chief Medical Officer, NFLPA, and IRBs where applicable.

## Methods
This is an invited commentary and descriptive article outlining the MRAP governance structure. The authors describe the seven sequential research pathways (I: Interventional; II: Internal Club EMR; III: Medical Committee De-identified; IV: Medical Committee Identifiable; V: EMR Research for Publication; VI: Case Study for Publication; VII: Third-Party Data Request), the approval parties required at each pathway, and the process for initiating, reviewing, and disseminating research under the MRAP framework. No experimental design or statistical methods are employed.

## Findings
MRAP consists of seven distinct pathways governing NFL medical research by type of requester, data sensitivity, and intended use. All research requires sequential approval; denial at any stage terminates the request. The protocol explicitly states the NFL and NFLPA may offer nonbinding comments but have no editorial control over publications. Publicly sourced injury data are identified as unreliable, particularly for lower-grade injuries not resulting in missed playing time. MRAP-governed EMR data linked with game statistics and player demographics provides the most reliable injury incidence and outcome data. Since 2019, individual clubs have access to injury dashboards comparing their data to league averages including return-to-play metrics.

## Limitations
As a descriptive commentary, no empirical limitations apply. MRAP is specific to the NFL-NFLPA collectively bargained environment, which may limit direct generalizability to other sports leagues or non-unionized athletic contexts. The article does not provide data on the volume or outcomes of research proposals reviewed through MRAP.

## Practical Implications
NFL team physicians, athletic trainers, and performance staff who wish to conduct or publish research on player data must route all requests through the MRAP system via the NFL MRAP web portal. Research proposals require feasibility review by IQVIA and approval from NFL management council legal, the NFL Chief Medical Officer, and NFL EVP of Health and Safety. Drafts must be submitted 60 days before journal submission and 30 days before final publication. Teams should avoid using publicly available injury data as primary sources due to systematic underreporting of lower-grade injuries.

## Athlete Development Notes
The MRAP framework enables systematic, high-quality research on NFL player health across the career arc, including injury prevention, performance outcomes post-surgery, and long-term health. Access to comprehensive, curated EMR-linked game statistics allows researchers to study position-specific injury patterns, recovery trajectories, and performance-based return-to-sport outcomes at a scale not possible with public data.

## Return to Play Notes
MRAP is directly relevant to return-to-play research quality. Studies using MRAP-approved NFL EMR data capture the full spectrum of injuries — including lower-grade injuries not reported publicly — providing more accurate incidence rates and RTP timelines. Practitioners should prioritize research published through MRAP-governed data channels over studies relying on publicly sourced injury reports when setting evidence-based return-to-play benchmarks for NFL players.
