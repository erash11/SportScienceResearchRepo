# de Oliveira, D. M., Silva, A. K. A. C., Macedo, A. G., Fernandes, M. B., & Fernandes, E. V. (2025). Global research trends in sports nutrition and football over the last 20 years (2004–2024). Sports, 13, 365.

## TLDR
Research on sports nutrition applied to soccer has grown dramatically over 20 years, shifting from a primary focus on hydration to increasingly complex topics including low energy availability, polyphenol supplementation, body composition, and recovery strategies. Underexplored areas—oxidative stress markers, beta-alanine supplementation, and low energy availability—represent high-priority gaps for future research. For practitioners, this landscape review highlights that the evidence base for individualized nutritional strategies in football is expanding rapidly, with particular attention warranted on energy deficiency and antioxidant support.

## Abstract
This scientometric review mapped global scientific production on sports nutrition applied to soccer (football) from 2004 to 2024. A database search across Web of Science, PubMed, and Scopus yielded 2,636 documents, of which 526 original articles were included after removing duplicates, reviews, meta-analyses, and out-of-scope studies. Data were analyzed using Bibliometrix and VOSviewer software. The study identified publication trends, leading authors, journals, countries, collaboration networks, and thematic clusters. Annual publication growth rate was 14.6% with a 1,450% increase over the 20-year period. The United Kingdom led in total output and citations, with Nutrients as the top publishing journal.

## Methods
Scientometric analysis of original research articles published from January 2004 to December 2024. Databases searched: Web of Science, PubMed, and Scopus. Boolean search strategy combined nutritional terms (dietary intake, sports nutrition, energy availability, hydration, supplementation, macronutrient/micronutrient intake) with football/soccer terms. After deduplication and eligibility screening, 526 articles were included. Bibliometrix 4.3.2 (R 4.4.2) used for descriptive analyses, production trends, authorship, journal distribution (Bradford's Law), citation analysis, and country production. VOSviewer 1.6.20 used for keyword co-occurrence networks and author/institution collaboration mapping. Conceptual structure mapped via multiple correspondence analysis (MCA).

## Findings
Annual scientific production grew 1,450% from 2004 (4 articles) to 2024 (62 articles) at a 14.6% annual growth rate. Nutrients was the leading publication source (53 articles, 29.6% of total). Morton J. (n=12) and Maughan R. (n=10) were the most productive authors. Liverpool John Moores University was the primary institutional collaboration hub. United Kingdom led in both volume (245 articles, 46.6%) and citations (1,847). Early research focused on hydration and thermoregulation (2006–2012); later research expanded to macronutrient intake, body composition, and energy balance (2013–2020); most recent trends (2021–2024) emphasize low energy availability and polyphenol supplementation. Co-occurrence network identified underexplored topics including lipid peroxidation, alpha-tocopherol, beta-alanine supplementation, and oxidative stress markers.

## Limitations
Restricted to three databases; gray literature and non-indexed journals were excluded. The 20-year time frame, while comprehensive, may not capture pre-2004 foundational research. Exclusion criteria removed American football, Australian rules football, rugby, futsal, and adapted football studies. Scientometric methods identify patterns but do not evaluate the quality or methodological rigor of individual studies. Database coverage discrepancies may affect results.

## Practical Implications
The field of sports nutrition in soccer is rapidly maturing toward individualized, performance-focused strategies. Practitioners should be aware of the growing evidence base for low energy availability as a risk factor in soccer players, polyphenol supplementation for recovery and oxidative stress management, and beta-alanine for high-intensity anaerobic performance. Hydration and carbohydrate periodization remain foundational, while emerging topics offer opportunities for evidence-based innovation in nutritional protocols.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athlete development, the scientometric landscape confirms that nutritional support should be integrated across multiple dimensions: hydration and thermoregulation, carbohydrate and energy availability management, supplementation (polyphenols, vitamin D, beta-alanine), and body composition monitoring via anthropometry. Programs should build nutritional protocols informed by the most cited research clusters and be aware of underexplored areas—particularly low energy availability in younger and female players—that may have meaningful developmental implications.

## Return to Play Notes
The growing research emphasis on recovery nutrition, oxidative stress management, and energy availability in soccer players has direct relevance to return-to-play contexts. Nutritional strategies targeting inflammation, muscle damage (polyphenols, antioxidants), and energy resynthesis (carbohydrates, total energy intake) should be integrated into RTP rehabilitation protocols. Low energy availability—an emerging focus of the field—may compromise recovery and tissue healing, making energy intake monitoring a key component of injury return management.
