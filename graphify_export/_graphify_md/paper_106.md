# Garlapaty AR, Jones M, Baumann J, Gunn C, Cook JL, DeFroda SF. Characterization of distal biceps tendon and triceps tendon injuries in National Football League players from 2009 to 2022. J Orthop. 2025;68:20–26. doi:10.1016/j.jor.2025.01.033

## TLDR
Distal biceps and triceps tendon ruptures are season-ending injuries for virtually all affected NFL players, requiring nearly a full year for return to play. While more than 70% return to the NFL, only about half regain prior performance—with triceps injuries carrying worse functional recovery than biceps injuries. Linemen on both sides of the ball account for the vast majority of cases. Key risk factors are BMI ≥31, ≥4 years of NFL experience, and for triceps injuries, age ≥26. Sports medicine and strength staff should consider load monitoring and pre-injury screening of high-BMI veteran linemen as a risk-reduction strategy.

## Abstract
This retrospective epidemiological study analyzed distal biceps and triceps tendon injuries in NFL players across 14 seasons (2009–10 to 2022–23) using publicly available injury reports and Pro Football Focus performance data. A total of 50 tendon ruptures were identified (26 biceps, 24 triceps), predominantly affecting linemen on both sides of the ball. RTP rates exceeded 70% for both injury types, but fewer than half of players returned to pre-injury performance levels. Significant risk factors included BMI ≥31, ≥4 years of NFL experience, and for triceps injuries, age ≥26. Average RTP time was approximately 332 days for biceps and 344 days for triceps injuries.

## Methods
Publicly available NFL injury reports (ESPN, NFL.com, Pro-Football-Reference.com) were screened for players on injured reserve or physically unable to perform for at least one game during 2009–10 through 2022–23. Injury inclusion required confirmation from at least two independent news sources. Performance data collected from Pro Football Focus (PFF) grades (0–100 scale). RTP defined as participation in at least one regular-season play post-injury. Age (cutoff 26 years), experience (cutoff 4 years), and BMI (cutoff 31 kg/m²) cutoffs based on NFL averages. Chi-square, Fisher's exact, and McNemar tests used; p<0.05 considered significant.

## Findings
50 total tendon ruptures: 26 biceps, 24 triceps. Biceps injuries predominated in defensive players (73.1%), especially defensive linemen (42.3%); triceps injuries more common in offensive players (58.3%), especially offensive linemen (45.7%). Most injuries in games (84–85%) and regular season (69–79%). Operative management: 76.9% biceps, 70.8% triceps. RTP rates: 73.1% biceps, 70.8% triceps. Return to prior performance: 52.6% biceps, 41.2% triceps. Average RTP: 332 days biceps, 344 days triceps. Significant risk factors for biceps: BMI ≥31 (OR=5.5, p=0.0008), ≥4 seasons experience (OR=2.7, p=0.031). Significant risk factors for triceps: BMI ≥31 (OR=5.5, p=0.01), age ≥26 (OR=11, p<0.0001), ≥4 seasons experience (OR=4.5, p=0.006). Younger players (<26) with ≤4 years experience significantly more likely to return to prior performance levels.

## Limitations
Reliance on public injury reports may result in missed or misclassified injuries. Non-operative and operative cases were combined, limiting comparison with prior surgical-only studies. Results not generalizable to first-year NFL players since no pre-injury baseline exists. PFF grades as a performance proxy may not capture all dimensions of player contribution. Sample size of 50 injuries over 14 seasons limits statistical power for subgroup analyses.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football strength and conditioning staffs should view veteran linemen with elevated BMI as the highest-risk population for biceps and triceps tendon ruptures. These injuries are functionally devastating, season-ending, and carry meaningful rates of permanent performance decline. Proactive load monitoring, elbow and forearm strength assessments, and attention to cumulative upper extremity fatigue during training camps and the early regular season—when most injuries occur—may reduce risk. The high surgical management rate underscores the importance of early access to an experienced upper extremity surgeon.

## Athlete Development Notes
Given that risk increases with ≥4 years of experience and BMI ≥31, long-term athlete development programs for offensive and defensive linemen should incorporate progressive tendon loading strategies (e.g., eccentric elbow flexion and extension exercises) throughout a player's career, not only during rehabilitation. Younger players with fewer years of experience have significantly better recovery trajectories, suggesting early career tendon health monitoring may pay long-term dividends. Body composition management to limit excessive BMI in linemen may serve as a modifiable risk reduction strategy.

## Return to Play Notes
Both biceps and triceps tendon ruptures require approximately 11 months of recovery before NFL return. Despite RTP rates exceeding 70%, only about half of biceps and 41% of triceps injury patients return to prior performance levels, making these among the more consequential injuries for career outcomes. Triceps injuries carry a worse prognosis for returning to starting as many games. Rehabilitation should progress through full elbow ROM restoration, eccentric strengthening, and sport-specific contact readiness before clearance. Younger players and those with fewer years of experience have statistically better functional recovery, informing realistic RTP counseling.
