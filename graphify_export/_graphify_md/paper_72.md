# Hirase T, Bondar K, Doucet D, Moore B, Saifi C, Marco RAW. At-Risk Tackling Techniques and Effectiveness in NCAA Division I Collegiate Football. The Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine. 2023;11(7):23259671231182968.

## TLDR
In NCAA Division I football, the inside-shoulder tackle with a head-up technique is the most effective (93.2% success rate) and safest approach, while head-down, head-across-the-bow, and helmet-to-helmet techniques are both less effective and place players at greater risk for cervical spine and head injuries.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional retrospective cohort study assessed tackling techniques and success rates in 1,000 consecutive solo defensive tackling attempts from the 2021 NCAA Division I Southeastern Conference football season. Three orthopaedic surgery residents independently reviewed slow-motion game footage to evaluate head position, tackle method, waist relationship, and tackle success. The study found that head-up tackling was used in 90.2% of attempts and was significantly more successful (76.2%) than head-down tackling (55.1%). The inside-shoulder technique yielded the highest success rate (93.2%) and was most associated with head-up positioning and contact at or above the waist.

## Methods
Cross-sectional retrospective cohort study. Three orthopaedic surgery residents analyzed 1,000 consecutive defensive solo tackling attempts made during the 2021 season (October–December) by 8 universities in the NCAA Southeastern Conference. Slow-motion replays used to evaluate: tackle success, tackle method (head-across-the-bow, helmet-to-helmet, inside-shoulder, arm), head position (up vs. down; cutoff >30° to ground), and relationship to offensive player's waist (at/above vs. below). Chi-square or Fisher exact tests used for categorical data; 2-tailed Student t-test or Mann-Whitney U test for continuous data. Interrater reliability assessed via Landis and Koch kappa (mean κ=0.718).

## Findings
- Head-up tackling occurred in 902/1,000 attempts (90.2%); head-down in 98 (9.8%)
- Head-up tackles successful in 76.2% vs. 55.1% for head-down (P<.001)
- Tackles at/above waist: 777 (77.7%); below waist: 223 (22.3%)
- At/above waist tackles successful in 77.6% vs. 61.9% for below-waist tackles (P<.001)
- Inside-shoulder method: 592 (59.2%) attempts; 93.2% success rate
- Arm method: 317 (31.7%) attempts; 41.6% success rate
- Head-across-the-bow: 72 (7.2%) attempts; 59.7% success rate
- Helmet-to-helmet: 19 (1.9%) attempts; 73.7% success rate
- Inside-shoulder resulted in head-up tackling 92.9% of the time vs. 41.7% for head-across-the-bow (P<.001)
- No injuries recorded to tacklers; 2 minor injuries to ball carriers
- Head-across-the-bow and helmet-to-helmet techniques associated with high-risk head positioning

## Limitations
Retrospective design using publicly available game footage introduces potential selection bias. Study not designed to directly measure injury rates per tackle; cannot correlate technique with injury incidence on a per-tackle basis. Findings based solely on Southeastern Conference games; generalizability to other conferences or levels of play uncertain. Helmet-to-helmet sample (n=19) was small. No data on coaching instructions or technique teaching among teams.

## Practical Implications
Coaches and athletic trainers should reinforce the inside-shoulder technique as both the safest and most effective tackling method at the collegiate level. Teaching athletes to maintain head-up positioning (>30° to ground) at point of contact and initiate contact at or above the waist reduces injury risk while improving tackling effectiveness. Head-across-the-bow and head-down techniques should be actively discouraged and corrected in practice settings.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football. The inside-shoulder technique should be the foundational tackling method taught and reinforced throughout player development at all levels. Data show that head-up, waist-level or above contact naturally follows from proper inside-shoulder mechanics, creating a technique that simultaneously maximizes tackle success rates and minimizes injury risk—an ideal combination for player development programming.

## Return to Play Notes
Although no tackler injuries were recorded in this study, the documented association between head-down, head-across-the-bow, and helmet-to-helmet techniques and high-risk head positioning underscores their role in cervical spine and head injuries. Sports medicine staff should collaborate with coaching staff to identify and remediate at-risk tackling patterns in athletes returning from cervical spine or concussion-related injuries, as these techniques place previously injured structures at recurrent risk.
