# McHale KJ, Vopat BG, Beaulieu-Jones BR, Sanchez G, Whalen JM, McDonald LS, DiGiovanni CW, Theodore GH, Provencher MT. Epidemiology and outcomes of Lisfranc injuries identified at the National Football League Scouting Combine. Am J Sports Med. 2017;45(8):1901–1908.

## TLDR
Lisfranc injuries identified at the NFL Scouting Combine adversely affect draft status and early NFL career performance. Players with prior Lisfranc injuries played significantly fewer games in their first two NFL seasons (16.9 vs. 23.3) and had worse draft positions than matched controls. Residual joint displacement greater than 2 mm is the key radiographic predictor of poor outcomes—athletes with greater displacement had far fewer games played and started. Surgically treated players also fared worse in the draft than those managed nonoperatively, likely reflecting underlying injury severity.

## Abstract
This retrospective cohort study examined the epidemiology, treatment patterns, and NFL career impact of Lisfranc injuries identified at the annual NFL Scouting Combine from 2009 to 2015. Of 2,162 Combine participants, 41 (1.9%) had a prior Lisfranc injury; 63.4% were managed operatively. Quarterbacks were at significantly elevated risk (OR 4.4). Lisfranc-injured players had worse NFL draft position, played fewer games, and had shorter early NFL career productivity compared to matched controls. Residual Lisfranc joint displacement greater than 2 mm was associated with significantly worse NFL performance outcomes.

## Methods
Retrospective review of medical records from all NFL Scouting Combine participants (2009–2015) who had a prior Lisfranc injury identified during comprehensive medical evaluation. Epidemiological characteristics, player position, treatment methods (operative vs. nonoperative), and missed collegiate games were recorded. NFL performance outcomes (draft position, career length ≥2 years, games played and started in first 2 NFL seasons) were analyzed for 2009–2013 Combine participants and compared with position-matched controls (no midfoot injury, no prior surgery, ≤2 missed college games). Radiographic analysis of Combine weightbearing AP foot radiographs assessed residual Lisfranc joint displacement versus the contralateral foot. Logistic regression and two-sample t-tests were used for statistical analysis.

## Findings
41 of 2,162 (1.9%) Combine participants had prior Lisfranc injuries. Quarterbacks had significantly elevated risk (OR 4.4; P=.004). Of 41 injuries, 26 (63.4%) were treated operatively (76.9% ORIF with screws/plates, 19.2% suspensory fixation, 3.8% arthrodesis). Mean missed collegiate games: 2.9 ± 4.9 (operative 4.3 vs. nonoperative 0.5; P=.008). Compared to controls, Lisfranc-injured players had worse draft position (142 vs. 111.3; P=.04) and fewer games played (16.9 vs. 23.3; P=.001). Operatively managed players had higher undrafted rates (38.5% vs. 13.3%; P=.04). 41.5% of athletes had residual displacement >2 mm; these players had significantly worse draft position (156.9 vs. 111.2; P=.009) and fewer games played (14.4 vs. 23.3; P=.001) and started (3.1 vs. 10.5; P=.03) vs. controls. 31.7% had radiographic evidence of midfoot degenerative joint disease.

## Limitations
Retrospective design relying on Combine records; some Lisfranc injuries may have been missed. Initial injury radiographs unavailable, preventing assessment of injury severity at time of occurrence and preventing differentiation between purely ligamentous and fracture-associated injuries. Study was underpowered to differentiate outcomes by specific operative technique. A prior Lisfranc injury may decrease the probability of receiving a Combine invitation, introducing selection bias. Overall NFL career success is multifactorial and not fully captured by the variables studied.

## Practical Implications
Radiographic assessment of residual Lisfranc joint displacement versus the contralateral foot at the time of evaluation is critical—greater than 2 mm residual displacement is a significant predictor of worse draft position and reduced NFL game participation. Medical staff evaluating Combine or pre-draft athletes should compare bilateral weightbearing AP radiographs. Achieving anatomic reduction (≤2 mm) during operative management is essential for preserving career longevity. Quarterbacks warrant particular attention given their 4.4x elevated odds of Lisfranc injury.

## Athlete Development Notes
Lisfranc injuries in developing football players—particularly quarterbacks—carry significant career implications. Residual midfoot instability or displacement directly reduces the number of games an athlete can participate in early in their professional career. For development programs, ensuring complete rehabilitation and confirming radiographic healing before return to full football activities is essential. Athletes with residual displacement above 2 mm may need revised performance expectations and potentially revision surgery.

## Return to Play Notes
None of the 24 Lisfranc-injured Combine participants missed an NFL game during their first two professional seasons due to a foot reinjury, suggesting adequate short-term RTP once athletes reach the professional level. However, despite RTP, these athletes played significantly fewer games overall, indicating a persistent performance deficit. Operative management was associated with significantly more missed collegiate games (4.3 vs. 0.5 for nonoperative), reflecting prolonged initial recovery timelines. Residual displacement >2 mm should serve as a radiographic benchmark guiding clearance decisions before return to competitive football.
