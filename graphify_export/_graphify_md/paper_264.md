# Flatt AA, Allen J, Bragg A, Keith C, Earley R, Esco MR. Heart Rate Variability in College Football Players throughout Preseason Camp in the Heat. Int J Sports Med. 2020. DOI: 10.1055/a-1145-3754

## TLDR
HRV and resting heart rate improved across preseason camp in the heat, consistent with heat acclimation, even though subjective wellbeing declined. Non-linemen adapted faster and more robustly than linemen. A day of passive rest (day 12) produced the most favorable autonomic readings for both groups. Practitioners should interpret HRV improvements during hot-weather training as cardiovascular adaptation rather than perceptual recovery, and should monitor linemen closely early in camp.

## Abstract
This study characterized cardiac-autonomic responses to a 13-day preseason camp in the heat in 28 Division-I college football players (10 linemen, 18 non-linemen). Daily resting heart rate (RHR), LnRMSSD (HRV), and subjective wellbeing (LnWellness) were measured. Despite reductions in LnWellness across the camp, cardiac-autonomic parameters demonstrated responses consistent with heat acclimation. Non-linemen showed earlier and more frequent improvements in RHR and HRV than linemen. Both groups had their lowest RHR and highest LnRMSSD on day 12 following passive rest. Linemen experienced a moderate reduction in LnRMSSD after their first heat exposure (day 2) while non-linemen showed an unclear increase.

## Methods
Observational longitudinal study of 28 Division-I college football players (10 linemen, 18 non-linemen) during a 13-day preseason camp (15 sessions, avg heat index 38.1°C). Daily measures included: RHR and LnRMSSD via optical photoplethysmography finger sensor (HRV Fit), subjective wellbeing questionnaire (adapted McLean et al. 9-point scale), fasted body mass (Tanita scale), and accelerometer-derived PlayerLoad (Catapult 100 Hz). Linear mixed models with position as a fixed effect, day as a repeated measure, and athlete ID as a random effect. Hedges effect sizes ± 90% CI used to interpret standardized differences.

## Findings
Linemen showed a moderate ES reduction in LnRMSSD on day 2 and a moderate increase on day 12; non-linemen showed small-to-large LnRMSSD increases on days 3–5, 7, 12, and 13 relative to day 1. RHR decreased small-to-large across 9 days for non-linemen but only 3 days for linemen. LnWellness decreased moderately on days 4–10 and 13 for linemen and small-to-moderately on days 6–9, 12, and 13 for non-linemen. Both groups recorded their lowest RHR and highest LnRMSSD on day 12 following passive rest. Position × day interaction was significant for LnRMSSD (p=0.036). Body mass changes were unclear for both groups.

## Limitations
Plasma volume not directly measured; interpretation of autonomic changes relies on prior literature. No direct fitness or performance markers collected. RHR parameters were acquired ~2 hours earlier on days 6, 8, and 12 due to two-a-day scheduling. PlayerLoad data available for only 5 linemen due to preferential sensor allocation. Small sample size limits generalizability.

## Practical Implications
Linemen should be closely monitored in the first few days of preseason heat camp for sustained HRV decrements. Improvements in HRV during heat training reflect cardiovascular adaptation, not perceptual recovery — practitioners should not use rising HRV to infer that players feel recovered. Subjective wellbeing measures may more sensitively detect fatigue and should be used alongside HRV. Passive rest days have a powerful restorative effect on autonomic function and are particularly beneficial for linemen. Both objective physiological and subjective psychological variables should be used together for athlete monitoring.

## Athlete Development Notes
Provides a framework for daily HRV and wellbeing monitoring during American football preseason camp in the heat. Identifies that non-linemen adapt more favorably and earlier than linemen, suggesting position-specific monitoring thresholds and recovery protocols should be developed. Demonstrates the value of incorporating a passive rest day mid-camp to maximize physiological recovery.

## Return to Play Notes
Not directly applicable to return-to-play; however, the finding that HRV improvements reflect heat acclimation rather than overall recovery has implications for clearance decisions during injury rehabilitation conducted in hot environments, where HRV may not accurately reflect an athlete's readiness to resume full activity.
