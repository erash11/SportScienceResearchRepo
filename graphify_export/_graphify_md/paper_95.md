# Chalmers S, Siegler J, Lovell R, Lynch G, Gregson W, Marshall P, Jay O. Brief in-play cooling breaks reduce thermal strain during football in hot conditions. Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport. 2019;22(8):912–917. doi:10.1016/j.jsams.2019.04.009

## TLDR
Brief 3-minute cooling breaks during each half of a football match played in hot, humid conditions (30°C WBGT) meaningfully reduce core body temperature rise (~0.25°C), heart rate (~10–13 bpm), and perceived exertion (~1.3–1.4 RPE units) compared to playing without breaks. Simply stopping play and having players drink chilled water for 3 minutes at the 30-minute mark of each half is as effective as adding an ice towel. An extended half-time of 20 minutes produces similar benefits and may be preferred when mid-half stoppages are not desired. These findings validate FIFA's current policy of sanctioning cooling breaks when WBGT exceeds 32°C, and are directly applicable to American football pre-season and early-season hot-weather practices and games.

## Abstract
This laboratory study examined whether three in-play cooling interventions could attenuate the rise in core (rectal) temperature during simulated soccer/football matches in hot, humid conditions (35°C, 55% RH, 30°C WBGT). Twelve well-trained football players completed four counterbalanced trials: a regular match (REG), a match with two 3-min cooling breaks using chilled water at the 30-min mark of each half (COOLwater), the same with an ice towel added around the neck (COOLtowel), and a match with an extended 20-min half-time (HTextended). All three interventions significantly reduced core temperature rise, exercising heart rate, and session RPE compared to REG, with no significant differences between the three interventions in core temperature effect.

## Methods
Twelve healthy well-trained football players (mean age 24±7 y, VO2max 58±3 mL/kg/min) completed four counterbalanced experimental trials in an environmental chamber set to 35°C, 55% relative humidity, and 30°C WBGT. The treadmill simulation covered 10.8 km over 90 minutes using an intermittent sprint protocol. Continuous monitoring of rectal temperature (self-inserted probe 15 cm past the anal sphincter), heart rate, skin temperature, and thermal sensation (200-mm ASHRAE scale) was performed. Urine specific gravity, body mass change for sweat loss, and session RPE were also collected. One-way and two-way repeated measures ANOVA with post-hoc paired t-tests were used for analysis.

## Findings
All three cooling interventions significantly reduced rectal temperature change versus REG: COOLwater −0.25°C (p=0.004), COOLtowel −0.28°C (p<0.001), HTextended −0.21°C (p<0.001). No significant differences in core temperature effect between the three interventions. Mean exercising HR was lower in COOLwater (−13 bpm, p<0.001), COOLtowel (−10 bpm, p=0.010), and HTextended (−8 bpm, p=0.011) versus REG; COOLwater also produced lower HR than HTextended (−5 bpm, p=0.016). Session RPE was lower in all three intervention trials versus REG (COOLwater −1.4 au, COOLtowel −1.3 au, HTextended −0.9 au; all p<0.05). Skin temperature and thermal sensation did not differ significantly between any conditions. Whole-body sweat rate was similar across all four trials (~1185–1208 g/h).

## Limitations
The fixed treadmill protocol does not replicate self-paced field play, where players may increase intensity after breaks, offsetting cooling benefits. No true control trial without fluid during breaks was included, making it impossible to isolate rest from fluid intake as the primary mechanism. No exercise warm-up was performed before each trial, potentially influencing thermoregulatory responses. Findings require field-based validation in uncontrolled match conditions.

## Practical Implications
For NFL and college football teams practicing or competing in hot, humid conditions (WBGT ≥30°C), incorporating brief 3-minute structured cooling breaks at approximately the 30-minute mark of each half is supported by this evidence. Providing chilled water during breaks is a low-cost, logistically simple strategy that attenuates thermal strain meaningfully. An extended half-time cooling protocol (20 minutes) with more comprehensive strategies — cold towels, hand or forearm cold water immersion, cooling vests — is an alternative when mid-game breaks are not feasible. Athletic trainers should use WBGT thresholds to trigger cooling break protocols during summer camp and early-season games.

## Athlete Development Notes
During summer two-a-days and pre-season camps in hot climates, scheduling structured 3-minute cooling breaks with chilled fluid at the midpoint of each practice period can reduce cumulative thermal load and fatigue, potentially allowing players to maintain higher training intensities and reducing heat illness risk. The magnitude of core temperature attenuation (~0.25°C) is comparable to benefits provided by a formal heat acclimatization program, suggesting these breaks can serve as a meaningful and practical heat-management tool within existing practice structures.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes returning from heat-related illness or those deemed high-risk (e.g., sickle cell trait, prior exertional heat stroke) can benefit from structured in-play cooling protocols that reduce peak core temperature during activity. This research supports mandatory fluid and rest breaks as a safety measure for high-risk athletes during hot-weather practice or competition, providing physiological justification for individualized activity modification plans during the return-to-sport process following heat illness.
