# Garlapaty A, Parola R, Kluge M, Blankenship QP, Njai A, Stannard JT, Bischoff C, Cook JL, DeFroda SF, Nuelle CW. Anterior Cruciate Ligament Injuries in National Football League Players from 2012 to 2022: A Descriptive Epidemiology Study. Journal of Knee Surgery. 2025;doi:10.1055/a-2652-2567.

## TLDR
Over a 10-year span in the NFL, ACL tear incidence did not differ between artificial turf and natural grass; wide receivers are the highest-risk position, and injuries cluster early in the season and during games rather than practice.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiology study identified 520 NFL players who sustained ACL tears across the 2012-13 to 2022-23 seasons using publicly available databases, game film, and injury reports. The study characterized injuries by playing surface, player position, timing in season, mechanism of injury, and player age. No significant difference in ACL tear incidence was found between artificial turf and natural grass. Wide receivers had significantly higher incidence of ACL tears relative to other positions, while specialists had significantly lower incidence. More tears occurred before week 9 of the season, and 67% occurred during games rather than practice.

## Methods
Retrospective descriptive epidemiology study using publicly available data (Pro-Football-Reference.com, ESPN.com, NFL injury reports, game film) to identify all NFL ACL tears from 2012-13 to 2022-23 seasons. Variables collected included playing surface, player position, week of injury, practice vs. game setting, and mechanism of injury (contact vs. non-contact, determined by video review). Chi-squared tests compared incidence by surface, position, and position group. Stepwise multivariate logistic regression identified correlates of in-game ACL injury. Statistical analyses performed in R software; significance set at p<0.05.

## Findings
• 520 total ACL tears identified across 11 NFL seasons
• No significant difference in ACL tears on artificial turf vs. natural grass (265 vs. 255; p=0.670)
• Game incidence rates: 0.058/game on grass vs. 0.061/game on artificial turf (p=0.661)
• Wide receivers had significantly higher incidence vs. other positions (p=0.027)
• Receivers (WR + TE) as a group had higher incidence (p=0.007); specialists had significantly lower incidence (p<0.001)
• 291 ACL tears occurred before week 9; 126 on or after week 9 (early season predominance)
• 132 ACL tears during preseason
• 67% of tears (352) during games; 32.3% (168) during practice
• Contact mechanism: 245 tears; non-contact: 172 tears; unknown: 113 game, 67 practice
• No significant difference in injury mechanism between playing surfaces (p=0.765)
• Average player BMI: 30.6 kg/m²; average age: 25.2 years; average NFL experience: 2.7 years

## Limitations
Publicly available data may underreport injuries that did not result in missed games. Mechanism of injury could not be confirmed by video for all cases (practice injuries, off-camera plays). Practice incidence rates could not be calculated because total practice exposure was unknown. The dataset began in 2012-13 due to the 2011-12 lockout affecting data availability. Selection bias is possible as only football-related injuries were included.

## Practical Implications
Playing surface type (artificial vs. natural grass) does not independently determine NFL ACL injury risk, suggesting surface alone should not be the primary prevention focus. Early-season ACL injury clustering highlights the importance of progressive preseason conditioning and load management. Wide receivers and tight ends require targeted neuromuscular training and monitoring. Injury prevention programs should account for the early-season window and game-setting exposure.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football. The position-specific risk data (wide receivers and receivers group at highest risk; specialists at lowest) should inform position-targeted ACL injury prevention programming and screening in NFL and collegiate football programs. Early-season clustering reinforces the need for appropriate preseason ACL injury prevention protocols.

## Return to Play Notes
20% of NFL running backs and wide receivers who sustain an ACL injury never return to NFL play; of those who do return, on-field production decreases by 33%. 35.7% of offensive/defensive linemen never return. NFL players who return after ACL earn on average $2.07M less over 4 years than non-injured counterparts. Return to sport typically requires more than 9 months. These statistics frame the high stakes of ACL injury for NFL roster decision-making and RTP planning.
