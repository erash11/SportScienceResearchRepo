# Binney Z, Smoliga JM. Bad Altitude: Categorizing Elevation Produces Spurious Association With Concussions in the National Football League. Journal of Orthopaedic & Sports Physical Therapy. 2022. https://doi.org/10.2519/jospt.2022.11039

## TLDR
When stadium altitude is treated as a continuous variable rather than categorized, there is no association between elevation and NFL concussion rates. The previously reported finding of lower concussion rates at altitudes above 644 ft was a statistical artifact of inappropriate variable categorization—the result was specific to the 2012–13 seasons, was sensitive to the exact cutpoint chosen, and disappeared in subsequent seasons and with continuous analysis. NFL staff, clinicians, and equipment managers should not alter concussion safety, rehabilitation, or equipment practices based on stadium elevation, and should be skeptical of claims that altitude-mimicking devices (e.g., jugular compression collars) reduce concussion risk.

## Abstract
This cohort replication study assessed whether prior findings of an association between NFL stadium altitude and concussion rates would replicate using a larger dataset and altitude analyzed as a continuous variable. All NFL regular-season games from 2012–2019 were analyzed (1,159 concussions in 867 players). Stadium elevations were identified via USGS and Google Maps. Generalized additive models (GAMs), Poisson regression, and logistic mixed models found no association between continuous altitude and concussion rates (IRR=1.00, 95%CI 0.99–1.01 per 100 ft increase). The previously reported 644 ft categorical cutpoint was significant in 2012–13 but did not replicate in 2014–19.

## Methods
Cohort replication study using publicly available data from all NFL regular-season games (weeks 1–16) from 2012 to 2019. Concussion data sourced from PBS Concussion Watch (2012–15) and Football Outsiders injury database (2012–19), based on official NFL injury reports. A total of 1,159 concussions in 867 players (1,103 player-seasons) were identified. Stadium altitude was obtained via USGS elevation maps and Google Maps coordinates for each venue. Concussion rates were calculated per game per stadium. GAMs with restricted cubic splines examined non-linear continuous altitude-concussion relationships. Mixed-effects Poisson and logistic regression models (team-level random effects, adjusting for home/away) assessed continuous and categorical (644 ft cutpoint) associations across three time periods: 2012–13, 2014–19, and 2012–19. Sensitivity analyses tested alternative cutpoints (±100 ft) and NFL median altitude split (≥397 ft vs. <397 ft).

## Findings
No continuous association between altitude and concussion rates was found in any time period: Poisson model IRR=1.00 (95%CI 0.99–1.01) per 100 ft increase for 2012–19. GAM plots were flat across all time periods. The 644 ft categorical cutpoint produced a significant result for 2012–13 only (IRR=0.71, 95%CI 0.54–0.94), replicating Myer et al., but did not replicate in 2014–19 (IRR=0.99, 95%CI 0.84–1.14) or for the full 2012–19 period (IRR=0.91, 95%CI 0.80–1.04). Moving the cutpoint ±100 ft substantially attenuated the association (IRRs of 0.76 and 0.92 respectively, both non-significant). Using the true median altitude split (397 ft) eliminated the association entirely (IRR=1.00, 95%CI 0.79–1.25). Denver (5,195 ft, highest NFL stadium) showed approximately average concussion rates. Logistic model results were consistent with Poisson results.

## Limitations
Concussion data relied on publicly reported injury reports, estimated to be missing approximately 10% of total NFL concussions based on comparison to official NFL Health and Wellness data for 2015–19. Study could not differentiate between concussions occurring in practice versus games, though 95% of regular-season concussions are game-related. Multiple concussions in close succession could not be confirmed as separate incidents. No college or high school American football data were available for comparison. Findings may not generalize to other sports or settings with much greater altitude ranges.

## Practical Implications
NFL medical, performance, and equipment staff should not adjust concussion safety or rehabilitation protocols based on stadium elevation, as no epidemiological evidence supports an altitude effect at NFL-relevant elevations. The previous finding of lower concussion rates above 644 ft was a statistical artifact and should not inform clinical or equipment decisions. Specifically, the evidence does not support the use of jugular compression collar devices based on their altitude-mimicking rationale. Continuous variables should not be arbitrarily categorized in injury epidemiology research, as this practice routinely generates spurious findings.

## Athlete Development Notes
No altitude effect on concussion rates exists at the elevations of any NFL stadium, meaning training site elevation should not factor into concussion risk assessments for developing American football players. Athlete development programs should direct resources toward evidence-based concussion prevention strategies—blocking and tackling technique, helmet fit, and rule compliance—rather than elevation-based interventions.

## Return to Play Notes
Concussion RTP protocols in American football should not be modified based on the elevation of a team's home or away stadium. RTP decisions should be governed solely by the NFL's established concussion management protocol and player-specific symptom resolution. The previously reported spurious finding that higher-altitude games produce fewer concussions should not influence RTP timelines, clearance thresholds, or equipment decisions at any level of play.
