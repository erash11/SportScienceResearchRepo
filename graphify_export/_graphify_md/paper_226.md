# Orchard JW, Chaker Jomaa M, Orchard JJ, Rae K, Hoffman DT, Reddin T, Driscoll T. Fifteen-week window for recurrent muscle strains in football: a prospective cohort of 3600 muscle strains over 23 years in professional Australian rules football. Br J Sports Med. 2020;54(10):1185-1190.

## TLDR
After a muscle strain, a footballer's recurrence risk is dramatically elevated—9% for hamstrings, 6% for groin, and 5% for quadriceps in the very first match back—and this elevated risk does not fully resolve for approximately 15 weeks following return to play. Prior injury to the same muscle is by far the strongest predictor, with adjusted odds ratios of 13–25x for recent injury history. This 15-week window where players are 'carrying' a prior injury represents a critical management period that spans more than half an AFL season and should inform return-to-play decision-making and monitoring in all football codes.

## Abstract
This study analyzed 23 years of Australian Football League (AFL) injury data (1992–2014) to quantify recurrence risk and identify risk factors for the four major muscle strain types—hamstring, quadriceps, calf, and groin. A total of 3,647 muscle strains occurred across 272,759 player matches. Binary logistic regression identified recent injury history (within 8 weeks) as the dominant risk factor for all four strain types. Week-by-week recurrence rate analysis demonstrated that elevated reinjury risk follows a logarithmic decay pattern and persists for at least 15 weeks after return to play for hamstring and quadriceps strains, 18 weeks for calf, and 19 weeks for groin. First-match-back recurrence rates were 9% (hamstring), 5% (quadriceps), 2% (calf), and 6% (groin).

## Methods
Prospective cohort study using the AFL Injury Surveillance System database from 1992 to 2014. The unit of analysis was the 'player match' (n = 272,759). Muscle strains causing a missed match were classified as hamstring, quadriceps, calf, or groin. Multivariable binary logistic regression with forward stepwise selection identified independent risk factors including recent history (≤8 weeks), non-recent history (>8 weeks) of each strain type, histories of ACL/cartilage/ankle/concussion/lumbar injuries, age (dichotomized at 24 years), indigenous race, match level, and substitute rule era (2011–2014). Week-by-week recurrence rates were plotted and fit with logarithmic trendlines to determine when risk returned to baseline.

## Findings
3,647 muscle strains (1,932 hamstring, 418 quadriceps, 458 calf, 839 groin) occurred across 272,759 player matches. Recent history (≤8 weeks) was the strongest risk factor: adjusted ORs of 13.1 (hamstring), 13.3 (calf), 25.2 (quadriceps), and 20.6 (groin). Non-recent history ORs: 3.5 (hamstring), 4.4 (calf), 5.2 (quadriceps), 3.5 (groin). First-match-back recurrence rates: 9% hamstring, 5% quadriceps, 2% calf, 6% groin. Elevated recurrence risk persisted for 15 weeks (hamstring, quadriceps), 18 weeks (calf), and 19 weeks (groin). Recent hamstring injury elevated subsequent quadriceps (OR 1.8) and calf strain risk (OR 1.8). Age was an independent risk factor for calf strains only (OR 1.6 for age ≥24). Indigenous race was associated with hamstring strain. During the substitute rule era (2011–2014), hamstring, quadriceps, and groin strains decreased but calf strains increased.

## Limitations
Injury side was not analyzed due to incomplete recording. Specific muscle within each group (e.g., biceps femoris vs. semimembranosus) was not distinguished. Injury history prior to AFL listing was not captured. Player workload, sprint volume, and real-time movement data were not included, despite being known risk factors. Repeated inclusion of each player across multiple matches introduces dependency between observations, potentially narrowing confidence intervals. Sparse data bias is possible given the large number of binary risk factor combinations. The AFL plays one match per week, limiting generalizability to codes with greater fixture congestion.

## Practical Implications
Return-to-play clearance after muscle strain should not be treated as a binary event. The 15-week elevated risk window means players require careful monitoring and potentially modified load management well beyond symptom resolution and initial return to training. Staff should communicate this extended risk window to athletes and coaches when making RTP decisions. Recurrence in the first match back (up to 9% for hamstrings) is sufficiently high to justify conservative load progression protocols for the initial weeks after return.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athletes at all levels, a prior muscle strain is the single most powerful risk factor for future strain—far outweighing age, workload, or other variables in this dataset. Development programs should implement longitudinal injury tracking to flag athletes with prior strain history for targeted screening, prehabilitation, and individualized load planning. Cross-muscle risk (hamstring strain elevating quadriceps and calf risk) suggests monitoring adjacent muscle groups after any lower limb strain. Younger players (under 24) are relatively protected from calf strains, but not from hamstring or groin strains.

## Return to Play Notes
The data provide a quantitative framework for RTP decision-making: hamstring and quadriceps risk remains elevated for 15 weeks post-return, calf for 18 weeks, and groin for 19 weeks. The highest single-match recurrence risk is in the first game back (up to 9% for hamstring). RTP protocols should incorporate progressive return with enhanced monitoring across this entire window. Clinicians should explicitly inform athletes and coaches that RTP does not equal full biological resolution of injury risk, and that the player is 'carrying' the injury throughout the 15-week window.
