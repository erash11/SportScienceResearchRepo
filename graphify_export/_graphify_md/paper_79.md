# Pollock JR, Doan MK, Asprey WL, Dulle DL, Moore ML, Makovicka JL, Hassebrock JD, Foley BM, Chhabra A. Can NFL Combine Results be Used to Estimate NFL Defensive Players Longevity? Sports Medicine International Open. 2021;5:E59-E64.

## TLDR
Specific NFL Combine drill results — particularly the 20-yard shuttle, 40-yard dash, and 3-cone drill — are significantly associated with 1-year and 5-year career longevity for defensive players, with associations varying by position. NFL teams and medical staff can use these metrics as supplementary tools when assessing long-term career prospects for defensive players.

## Abstract
This retrospective study examined the relationship between NFL Scouting Combine drill performance and career longevity for all defensive players who competed in the combine from 2005 to 2015. Combine results (height, weight, BMI, 10-/20-/40-yard dash, bench press, vertical jump, broad jump, short shuttle, 3-cone drill) were collected from NFLdraftscout.com and normalized by position using ratio-scaled BMI adjustments. Career length data (1-year and 5-year roster status) were cross-referenced from Pro Football Reference and NFL.com. Student's t-tests evaluated the influence of each performance metric on roster status, with significance set at p<0.05. The study found position-specific combine metrics that were significantly associated with both 1-year and 5-year career longevity.

## Methods
Retrospective review of NFL Combine data (2005–2015) from NFLdraftscout.com for 339 cornerbacks, 356 linebackers, 185 safeties, and 278 inside defensive linemen. Combine results were ratio-scaled normalized by position using BMI. Career length (1-year and 5-year roster status) collected from ProFootballReference.com. Statistical analysis via Student's t-test in R 3.4.2; p<0.05 considered significant. No correction for multiple testing was applied.

## Findings
• Linebackers: weight, 10-/20-/40-yard dash, broad jump, 3-cone drill, and shuttle all significantly associated with 1-year roster status; 10-/20-/40-yard dash, 3-cone drill, and shuttle associated with 5-year status.
• Cornerbacks: 20-/40-yard dash and broad jump significant for 1-year; weight, 10-/20-/40-yard dash, 3-cone drill, and shuttle significant for 5-year status.
• Inside defensive linemen: weight and 10-yard dash significant for 1-year; 40-yard dash and shuttle significant for 5-year status.
• Safeties: fewest significant associations — no drill linked to 1-year status; height and shuttle linked to 5-year status.
• 20-yard shuttle had the most significant associations across positions for both 1-year and 5-year roster status.

## Limitations
Reliance on publicly available combine data introduces potential recording inaccuracies. Normalized data used BMI-based ratio scaling rather than allometric adjustments, which may be less precise in athletes at body composition extremes. No correction for multiple comparisons. Injury mechanism and player position depth were not accounted for. Small safety sample may have underpowered position-specific analyses.

## Practical Implications
Combine drill performance — especially the 20-yard shuttle and 40-yard dash — can supplement scouting decisions for defensive positions. Staff should interpret results with caution at the individual level, as exceptional combine results do not guarantee longevity. Using normalized position-specific benchmarks may improve predictive accuracy over raw scores.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to NFL football defensive player evaluation. Position-specific combine benchmarks (LB: weight and multi-directional speed; CB: speed and agility; IDL: weight and short-area quickness) can guide athlete development programming to target traits most associated with career durability. Speed and agility training that improves 20-yard shuttle and 40-yard dash times may support longer career trajectories for defensive players.

## Return to Play Notes
Not a return-to-play study. However, understanding which physical traits predict career longevity may inform RTP decisions by highlighting baseline performance profiles that correlate with sustained NFL participation. Players returning from injury whose combine metrics fall into longevity-associated ranges may have better career outlooks.
