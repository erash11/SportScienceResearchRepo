# Yang J, Hodax JD, Machan JT, Krill MK, Lemme NJ, Durand WM, Hoffman JT, Hewett TE, Owens BD. Factors affecting return to play after primary Achilles tendon tear: a cohort of NFL players. Orthop J Sports Med. 2019;7(3):2325967119830139. doi:10.1177/2325967119830139

## TLDR
Duplicate of ID 216. Only 61.3% of NFL players return to play after a primary Achilles tendon tear, lower than prior estimates, with a mean recovery of nearly 12 months. Unlike other serious NFL injuries, neither draft round nor position type predicts who returns. The strongest predictor of not returning is declining game participation in the season before injury — possibly a marker of underlying tendon degeneration. For those who do return, performance normalizes after the first post-return season.

## Abstract
NOTE: This entry (ID 217) is a duplicate of ID 216. Both PDFs contain identical content — the same paper by Yang et al. (2019) published in Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine. The only difference is filename formatting (ID 216 uses a hyphenated subtitle with lowercase; ID 217 uses title case without a hyphen). All fields are identical to ID 216. This study evaluated factors associated with return to play (RTP) and performance changes following primary Achilles tendon (AT) tears in NFL players. 80 players with primary AT tears (2009–2014 seasons) were analyzed. The overall RTP rate was 61.3% at a mean 11.90 months. Age, draft round, and position type did not significantly predict RTP. Declining preinjury game participation was the strongest predictor of failure to return. Players who returned experienced a performance dip only in the RTP season, with normalization to preinjury levels when given more than one post-return season.

## Methods
Duplicate of ID 216. Retrospective case-control study. 80 NFL players with primary AT tears in the 2009–2014 seasons. Kaplan-Meier survival analysis; generalized linear models; generalized estimating equations. RTP defined as ≥1 play in a regular season or postseason game within 18 months of injury.

## Findings
Duplicate of ID 216. Overall RTP rate 61.3% (mean 11.90 months). Position and draft round not significant predictors of RTP. Pre-injury game participation (13.74 vs 8.71 games; P=.011) was the key differentiator between returners and non-returners. Non-returners showed significant preinjury decline in games played (P=.019). Performance reduced in RTP season but normalized after >1 post-return season.

## Limitations
Duplicate of ID 216. RTP window limited to 18 months; performance metrics limited to games played/started; small positional subgroups; publicly sourced data may be incomplete; causation cannot be established; subsequent tear cases excluded from performance analysis.

## Practical Implications
Duplicate of ID 216. Monitor for declining game participation as a potential prodromal sign of AT degeneration. Allow players at least two post-return seasons before drawing conclusions about long-term performance impact. RTP decisions should not rely solely on the first return season as a performance benchmark.

## Athlete Development Notes
Duplicate of ID 216. Proactive Achilles tendon screening, eccentric loading programs, and load monitoring during development phases can help identify and protect at-risk football athletes before overt rupture occurs.

## Return to Play Notes
Duplicate of ID 216. RTP rate of 61.3% at approximately 12 months is the lowest reported for NFL players with primary AT tears. Preinjury activity level is the strongest RTP predictor. First post-return season performance is reduced but subsequent seasons normalize, supporting patience in roster decisions and comprehensive functional RTP criteria beyond surgical healing timelines.
