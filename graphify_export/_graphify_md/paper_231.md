# Cosio, P. L., Moreno-Simonet, L., Fernández, D., Lloret, M., Padulles, X., Padulles, J. M., Farran-Codina, A., Rodas, G., & Cadefau, J. A. (2024). Football (soccer) match-derived hamstring muscles residual fatigue can be monitored using early rate of torque development. European Journal of Applied Physiology. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00421-024-05694-x

## TLDR
Early rate of torque development (RTD at 0–50 ms and 0–100 ms) is a more sensitive indicator of hamstring residual fatigue than MVIC torque alone after a soccer match. Hamstring rapid force-generating capacity is not fully recovered within 3 days post-match. The 90°hip:20°knee IPC test provides a practical, portable, cost-effective tool for monitoring hamstring recovery during congested schedules, and findings support the IOC recommendation of at least 96 hours between matches to allow adequate recovery.

## Abstract
This study investigated whether a soccer match affects the rapid force-generating capacity of the hamstring muscles using rate of torque development (RTD) and serum biomarkers. Nineteen male, regional first-division soccer players (age 20.9 ± 2.0 years) were assessed before and at 24, 48, and 72 hours post-match. Hamstring MVIC torque and RTD were measured via the 90°hip:20°knee isometric posterior chain (IPC) test. Serum biomarkers included CK, sMtCK, transaminases, MDA, and irisin. Early RTD (0–50 ms, 0–100 ms) remained significantly reduced at MD+3 in both dominant and non-dominant hamstrings (p < 0.001, g = −1.24 and −1.06). MVIC torque was unrecovered at MD+2 but recovered at MD+3. Neuromuscular deficits were coupled with elevated myocyte structural and mitochondrial damage biomarkers persisting through MD+3.

## Methods
Single-group repeated measures design in 19 male regional first-division soccer players. Neuromuscular assessments (MVIC torque and RTD at 0–50, 0–100, 0–250 ms and peak RTD) were performed using the 90°hip:20°knee IPC test with a custom rigid strain gauge before the match (MD), and at MD+1 (24h), MD+2 (48h), and MD+3 (72h). Match load was monitored via 10-Hz IMU devices. Serum biomarkers (CK, CK/ALT, CK/AST, sMtCK, MDA, irisin) were collected at each time point. Statistical analysis included two-way repeated measures ANOVA for neuromuscular data and one-way ANOVA for biomarkers, with post hoc Bonferroni-corrected paired t-tests.

## Findings
Early RTD0–50 and RTD0–100 remained significantly reduced at MD+3 (p < 0.001, g = −1.24 and −1.06) in both dominant and non-dominant hamstrings. MVIC torque was unrecovered at MD+2 (p = 0.004, g = −0.91; p = 0.002, g = −0.98) but recovered by MD+3. RTD0–250 and RTDpeak did not change significantly. Normalized RTD (expressed as % MVIC torque/s) also showed significant decreases at 0–50 and 0–100 ms intervals through MD+3. CK peaked at MD+1 (+499%), remained elevated through MD+3 (+124%). sMtCK, CK/ALT, CK/AST, MDA, and irisin were all elevated through MD+3, confirming sustained structural and oxidative muscle damage.

## Limitations
The strain gauge had a low baseline noise amplitude and lacked 1000 Hz sampling frequency as recommended by Maffiuletti et al. (2016); however, recent evidence shows this does not substantially affect reliability at the frequencies used. Small sample size (n = 19) in a single competitive standard (regional first division). Monitoring was limited to 72 hours; full recovery timeline beyond 72 hours was not captured. Individual nutritional intake between meals was not fully controlled despite dietary standardization around the match.

## Practical Implications
Use the 90°hip:20°knee IPC test and early RTD metrics (0–50 ms, 0–100 ms) as a non-exhaustive, cost-effective, portable tool to track hamstring recovery in soccer players during congested schedules. Early RTD deficits persist beyond MVIC recovery and represent a distinct, clinically relevant neuromuscular target. Practitioners should not rely on MVIC torque alone when clearing players from post-match hamstring fatigue, particularly given the risk of hamstring strain injury during sprinting with impaired rapid force production.

## Athlete Development Notes
Monitoring hamstring rapid force-generating capacity through RTD is important for long-term athlete management in soccer. Developing hamstring strengthening programs that target rapid force production (e.g., flywheel, sprint-specific eccentric work) may help buffer post-match RTD decrements and reduce hamstring strain injury incidence across high-volume developmental and professional schedules.

## Return to Play Notes
Early RTD at 0–50 ms and 0–100 ms should be incorporated into hamstring RTP criteria following hamstring strain injury, as these metrics are more sensitive indicators of residual neuromuscular deficit than peak torque alone. Players returning to full match participation within 72 hours of a prior match face elevated hamstring injury risk due to unresolved rapid force-generating deficits and persistent biochemical markers of muscle damage.
