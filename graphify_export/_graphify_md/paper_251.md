# Nilsson T, Fransson D, Borjesson M, Lundblad M, Ivarsson A. Hamstring injury risk in male professional football: do external training loads play a role? BMJ Open Sport & Exercise Medicine. 2025;11:e002649.

## TLDR
This study found that short-term GPS training load metrics (7- and 14-day windows) do not reliably predict hamstring injury risk at the group level in professional football players. The key finding was massive individual variation: some players had higher loads before injury, others had lower loads — with no consistent direction. This challenges practitioners to avoid over-relying on group-average TL thresholds and instead develop individualized TL baselines for each athlete. Predicting HSI from external TL metrics alone is not feasible, and a multifactorial approach incorporating fatigue, sprint mechanics, wellness, and other contextual factors is recommended.

## Abstract
This exploratory observational cohort study examined the relationship between short-term external training load (TL) and hamstring strain injury (HSI) occurrence in 25 male senior professional football players from three Swedish 1st division clubs over four seasons. GPS-derived TL variables (total distance, high-speed running distance >19.8 km/h, maximal sprint distance >29.8 km/h, very intense accelerations >3.00 m/s², very intense decelerations <-3.00 m/s², and maximal velocity) during the 7 and 14 days preceding 25 HSIs were compared to matched control periods from the same player using linear mixed models and non-overlap of all pairs (NAP) statistics. No statistically significant differences in any TL parameter were found between pre-injury and control periods. Considerable interindividual variability was observed, with TL values distributed both above and below control period values, yielding NAP values of 0.45–0.54 across all variables — indicating no systematic group-level trends.

## Methods
Observational cohort study over four seasons with 25 male senior professional footballers (age 26±4 years) from three Swedish 1st division clubs. GPS data (10 Hz Catapult Vector S7) collected during all training and match sessions. A total of 35 HSIs were recorded; 10 excluded due to missing TL data, leaving 25 for analysis. For each injury, TL from the 7-day and 14-day periods preceding injury was compared to the 7-day and 14-day control periods (immediately preceding the injury window) for the same player. Linear mixed models (players as random effect) tested average TL differences; non-overlap of all pairs (NAP) quantified individual-level effects. Exclusions: goalkeepers, missing/unreliable TL data, same-season reinjuries.

## Findings
25 HSIs analyzed; 5 (20%) in players with prior hamstring injury history. Linear mixed models revealed no statistically significant differences in any TL variable (total distance, HSRd, MSd, VID, VIA, VMax) for either 7-day or 14-day pre-injury periods vs. control periods (all p>0.05). Group-level NAP values ranged 0.45–0.54, indicating no systematic difference. Individual-level NAP ranged widely (e.g., total distance: 0.29–0.65 for 7-day; 0.29–0.65 for 14-day), confirming large interindividual variability. Maximal sprint distance showed smallest variation (NAP 0.36–0.57 for 7-day), likely because players rarely exceed the 29.8 km/h threshold in most training sessions.

## Limitations
Small sample (25 injuries) limits generalizability. Only time-loss injuries included, which may underestimate subclinical load-symptom relationships. Study confined to male professional footballers from southern Sweden; results may not generalize to other populations. Multiple TL factors (age, injury history, strength, fatigue, biomechanics, sprint phase) could not be controlled. The selected control period (immediately preceding the pre-injury period) may itself be influenced by the upcoming injury (e.g., player may have already been protecting or modifying load). Only external TL measured; internal load (RPE, HR) not included.

## Practical Implications
Short-term GPS TL monitoring alone is insufficient to predict HSI risk in professional football. Practitioners should establish individualized TL baselines per player rather than using group thresholds, and flag deviations (both spikes and sudden drops) from each player's norm. TL monitoring should be interpreted alongside subjective data (wellness, RPE, fatigue scores), recent match exposure, travel demands, and recovery time. A multifactorial risk profiling approach that includes sprint mechanics assessment and neuromuscular fatigue measures is recommended.

## Athlete Development Notes
For developing professional players, this study underscores that progressive TL exposure and long-term adaptation (tissue robustness) is more protective than short-term TL adjustments alone. Athletes should build tolerance to high-velocity running over sustained periods rather than solely avoiding acute load spikes. Individualized TL tracking from early career stages can establish personal baselines that guide safer loading progressions.

## Return to Play Notes
The high interindividual variability in TL patterns preceding HSI means there is no universal load threshold that indicates RTP readiness or re-injury risk from an external TL perspective alone. RTP decisions should incorporate sprint mechanics assessment, eccentric hamstring strength testing, and fatigue monitoring in addition to TL metrics. A graduated return-to-running protocol benchmarked against the player's own pre-injury TL baseline — rather than group norms — is most appropriate.
