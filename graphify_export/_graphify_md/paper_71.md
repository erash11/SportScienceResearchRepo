# Wang D, Weiss LJ, Abrams M, Barnes RP, Warren RF, Rodeo SA, Taylor SA. Athletes With Musculoskeletal Injuries Identified at the NFL Scouting Combine and Prediction of Outcomes in the NFL: A Systematic Review. The Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine. 2018;6(12):2325967118813083.

## TLDR
NFL Combine athletes with histories of cervical/lumbar spine injuries, rotator cuff tears, ACL reconstruction, full-thickness chondral lesions of the knee, or Lisfranc injuries played significantly fewer games early in their careers and had shorter careers, while shoulder labral, hip, and many foot injuries had minimal impact on NFL participation outcomes.

## Abstract
This systematic review evaluated the published literature on clinical predictors of outcomes associated with musculoskeletal injuries identified at the NFL Scouting Combine. PubMed, Embase, and Cochrane Library were searched in July 2018 using terms related to NFL, combine, injury, and surgery. Of 1,885 records identified, 32 studies met inclusion criteria, including 30 injury-specific retrospective studies. Outcomes assessed included draft status, games played and started, career length, and clinical signs/symptoms. The review synthesized findings across injuries of the cervical spine, lumbar spine, shoulder, knee, hip/groin, and foot.

## Methods
Systematic review conducted according to PRISMA guidelines. PubMed, Embase, and Cochrane Library searched with terms: 'National Football League,' 'NFL,' 'combine,' 'injury,' and 'surgery.' Inclusion criteria: studies reporting musculoskeletal injuries at NFL Combine and their association with NFL participation outcomes (games played, career length). Exclusion criteria: case reports, epidemiologic studies without clinical outcome associations, and non-NFL injuries. Methodological quality assessed with MINORS criteria for nonrandomized studies. 32 studies included: 22 level 3 retrospective studies and 8 level 4 studies.

## Findings
- Cervical spine diagnosis: athletes less likely to be drafted (P=.001) and had decreased career length vs. controls (mean 3.7 vs. 4.6 years)
- Lumbar spine diagnosis: decreased games played (P<.0001) and games started (P=.02) vs. controls; spondylolysis associated with decreased career longevity
- Rotator cuff tear: less likely to be drafted (P=.002); drafted athletes started fewer games (P=.02) and played fewer years (P=.04)
- SLAP repair: fewer games played (P=.049) and started (P=.036) vs. controls
- ACL reconstruction: drafted lower (P=.019), played and started fewer games (P<.001), lower snap percentage; defensive linemen, DBs, and LBs most affected
- Full-thickness chondral lesions: fewer games played (P<.001); subchondral bone edema and full-thickness injuries most impactful
- Lisfranc injury: worse draft position (P=.04), fewer games played (P=.001) and started (P=.08)
- Navicular injury: 57% undrafted vs. 31% controls; only 29% played ≥2 years vs. 70% controls
- Athletic pubalgia repair, hip arthroscopy, shoulder labral repair, MCL injury: no significant differences in draft status or games played vs. controls

## Limitations
All included studies were retrospective (level 3-4 evidence); only 2 prospective studies existed. Career length follow-up and athletic participation measures were highly variable among studies. Matching criteria for control groups differed substantially across studies. Studies predominantly collected data from 2009 onward at a single annual combine event. No randomized controlled trials identified. Methodological quality was moderate (mean MINORS score 16.4/24 for level 3 studies).

## Practical Implications
Medical staff and scouts can use this synthesis to guide orthopaedic grading at the NFL Combine and counsel prospects on how specific injury histories may affect professional career longevity. Injuries to the cervical/lumbar spine, rotator cuff, ACL, knee cartilage, and Lisfranc ligament carry the greatest evidence of impacting NFL participation. Athletes and agents should have realistic expectations about career projections following these injuries.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly relevant to American football player development and transition from college to professional football. Understanding which combine-identified injuries are associated with decreased NFL participation can inform rehabilitation priorities for college players with these injury histories. High school and collegiate programs should prioritize prevention and optimal treatment of ACL, spinal, and knee cartilage injuries given their demonstrated impact on professional career outcomes.

## Return to Play Notes
This review provides the strongest available evidence for expected return-to-performance outcomes following specific musculoskeletal injuries in elite American football. Athletes with cervical/lumbar spine injuries, ACLR, rotator cuff tears, and Lisfranc injuries face measurably reduced participation in the NFL. Medical staff should use these benchmarks to set realistic RTP timelines and performance expectations, and to identify athletes who may need extended rehabilitation or modified roles following these injuries.
