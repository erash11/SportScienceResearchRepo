# Wannop JW, Stefanyshyn DJ, Anderson RB, Coughlin MJ, Kent R. Development of a Footwear Sizing System in the National Football League. Sports Health. 2018. DOI: 10.1177/1941738118789402

## TLDR
Proper shoe fit in football is a meaningful injury prevention issue—this paper documents the NFL's development of the first sports league-specific 3D footwear fitting system. Using 3D foot scans of 1,451 players and radiographic internal measurements of hundreds of shoe models, the system matches individual foot shapes to available footwear. Key finding: nominal shoe sizes are highly variable (a size-12 shoe can differ by more than 1.5 US sizes across brands), meaning players who select footwear by label alone may be chronically wearing ill-fitting shoes. For football equipment and sports medicine staffs, implementing 3D-scan-based fitting can reduce foot and lower extremity injury risk.

## Abstract
This descriptive review details the development and deployment of an objective 3D footwear fitting system in the NFL, motivated by evidence that improperly fitted footwear contributes to foot injuries including fifth metatarsal stress fractures, turf toe, blisters, and foot deformities. The authors synthesized literature from 1980–2017 (65 sources) and documented three development phases: scanning 1,451 NFL players to characterize foot shape, measuring the internal dimensions of all relevant shoe models via radiographic contrast imaging, and building a proprietary shoe-foot matching algorithm refined with player comfort feedback. Measurement of 50+ nominal size-12 shoes revealed a 15 mm range in internal length (equivalent to >1.5 US shoe sizes) and 12 mm range in width, confirming the inadequacy of nominal sizing alone.

## Methods
Descriptive review synthesizing literature from 1980–2017 (65 sources); three-phase NFL system development: (1) foot scanning of 1,451 NFL players in locker rooms using commercially available 3D scanners; (2) internal length and width measurement of 50+ shoe models via PlusMed12 device and radiographic contrast imaging; (3) development of a proprietary shoe-foot matching algorithm using existing foot scan databases, subjective comfort assessments from 43 scanned players (15 completed full survey), and expert fitting input from NFL equipment managers; findings compared to general population anthropometric data.

## Findings
Internal length of nominal size-12 shoes ranged from 287–302 mm in 2016 (15 mm range, >1.5 US size difference) and 291–302 mm in 2017 (11 mm range after manufacturer feedback). Internal width ranged 93.5–105.5 mm in 2016 and 92–107 mm in 2017. More than 50% of NFL players had at least a half nominal size difference between their right and left feet. Brands labeled as 'wide' were not consistently wider than 'regular' versions before manufacturer feedback. NFL player foot length and width exceeded general population norms.

## Limitations
The fitting algorithm and shoe measurement methods are proprietary, limiting independent validation. No published outcome data yet demonstrate whether the system reduces lower extremity injury rates. Player comfort assessments were completed by only 15 of 43 scanned players. Shoe libraries require constant updating as new models are introduced yearly, and dimensional scaling across sizes could not be validated. Individual fit preferences introduce variability that a purely geometric algorithm cannot fully capture.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football equipment staffs should consider implementing 3D foot scanning as part of pre-season or draft evaluations. The demonstrated variability in shoe internal dimensions—even within the same nominal size—means that relying on labeled sizes alone is inadequate for injury prevention. Properly sized footwear can reduce the incidence of fifth metatarsal stress fractures, turf toe, and blisters, all of which affect availability and performance. This system could also be used to standardize footwear procurement and better match position-specific footwear needs.

## Athlete Development Notes
For developmental football programs, this research supports proactive foot scanning as part of the equipment intake process. Athletes entering programs with improperly sized footwear from youth or high school levels may already have developing foot pathology. Identifying fit mismatches early—particularly in linemen who bear extreme ground reaction forces—can protect long-term foot health and reduce chronic injury burden over an athlete's career.

## Return to Play Notes
While not a clinical RTP study, this work has indirect RTP relevance: foot injuries from poor footwear fit (fifth metatarsal fractures, turf toe) are among the most common causes of missed games in football. An objective fitting system that reduces the incidence of these injuries reduces the RTP burden on medical staffs. Additionally, for athletes returning from foot or ankle injuries, a 3D-based fitting system could ensure that return-to-play footwear properly accommodates swelling, post-surgical anatomy, or orthotic use.
