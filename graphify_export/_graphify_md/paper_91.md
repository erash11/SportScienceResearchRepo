# Varillas-Delgado D. Association of Genetic Profile with Muscle Mass Gain and Muscle Injury Prevention in Professional Football Players after Creatine Supplementation. Nutrients. 2024;16:2511. https://doi.org/10.3390/nu16152511

## TLDR
A player's polygenic profile—summarized as a TGS from six muscle-performance SNPs—significantly predicted both muscle mass response to creatine supplementation and non-contact muscle injury risk in 161 professional Spanish LaLiga football players. The AMPD1 CC genotype and the C allele were particularly linked to favorable muscle mass response under creatine loading. Players with lower TGS had drastically higher injury rates during the season. Performance staff should consider that genetic profiling alongside creatine supplementation may allow personalized supplementation strategies and targeted injury prevention programs for professional football players.

## Abstract
This longitudinal study examined the relationship between a total genotype score (TGS) derived from six muscle-performance SNPs—ACE I/D, ACTN3 c.1729C>T, AMPD1 c.34C>T, CKM c.*800A>G, MLCK c.49C>T, and MLCK c.37885C>A—and changes in BMI, fat mass, and muscle mass in 161 professional male football players following a standardized creatine supplementation protocol during the 2022/2023 LaLiga season. The study also assessed TGS as a predictor of non-contact muscle injury risk during the season. Players with TGS above 54.16 a.u. had ~3x greater odds of gaining muscle mass (OR=2.985; p=0.001) and those below 54.16 a.u. had ~9x greater odds of sustaining a non-contact muscle injury (OR=9.385; p<0.001).

## Methods
Longitudinal study of 161 male professional football players (age 26.44±4.54 years; weight 71.64±7.23 kg; height 180.71±6.12 cm) from LaLiga Santander and LaLiga Smartbank clubs, 2022/2023 season. Six SNPs (ACE, ACTN3, AMPD1, CKM, MLCK×2) were genotyped via Single-Nucleotide Primer Extension (SNPE) with SNaPshot Multiplex Kit on ABI3500 capillary electrophoresis. TGS calculated on 0–100 a.u. scale. Creatine protocol: 20 g/day (5-day loading) followed by 3–5 g/day (7-week maintenance) of creatine monohydrate (Myprotein) under medical supervision. Anthropometric data (BMI, fat mass, muscle mass) collected before and after supplementation per ISAK guidelines (SECA 220 rod; Inbody 770). Injury data (non-contact lower limb muscle tears) recorded from October 2022 to May/June 2023. Responders vs. non-responders identified via smallest worthwhile change (SWC). ROC curves and binary logistic regression evaluated TGS discriminatory power. Statistical analysis: SPSS v21.0.

## Findings
Of 161 players, 41 (25.5%) were BMI responders and 68 (42.2%) were muscle mass responders after creatine supplementation. TGS was significantly higher in muscle mass responders (58.69±11.38 a.u.) vs. non-responders (51.25±12.28 a.u.; p<0.001). AUC for muscle mass response: 0.668 (95%CI 0.584–0.751; p<0.001); TGS threshold 54.16 a.u. yielded OR=2.985 (95%CI 1.560–5.711; p=0.001). AMPD1 c.34C>T CC genotype was significantly more frequent in muscle mass responders (78.0% vs. 59.2%; trend p=0.082 before correction). ACTN3 CC and CKM GG genotypes were also more frequent in BMI responders. TGS did not significantly predict fat mass reduction (AUC=0.591; p=0.096). Players with TGS below 54.16 a.u. had OR=9.385 (95%CI 4.535–19.425; p<0.001) for non-contact muscle injury during the season. Allele frequencies for ACE and AMPD1 differed significantly between muscle mass responders and non-responders (all p<0.05).

## Limitations
Single-sex cohort of Spanish Caucasian professional players limits generalizability across ethnicities and genders. The creatine supplementation protocol was not randomized or placebo-controlled. Only non-contact lower-limb muscle injuries were analyzed; upper-limb and contact injuries were excluded. Players using other supplements were excluded, potentially limiting external validity. The same cohort was analyzed in a prior study by the same author (Maestro et al.), which limits independence of conclusions.

## Practical Implications
Performance staff should consider that an athlete's genetic profile significantly influences both the muscle mass response to creatine supplementation and injury risk during the competitive season. Players with lower TGS (below ~54 a.u.) are far less likely to gain muscle mass from standard creatine protocols and are at markedly elevated non-contact injury risk—suggesting enhanced monitoring and injury prevention programming is warranted. The AMPD1 CC genotype appears to be a particularly useful marker of creatine responsiveness and should be included in future genetic screening panels for professional football players.

## Athlete Development Notes
In American football—where creatine supplementation is widely used for strength and mass development—genetic profiling could help optimize supplementation strategies at the individual player level. High-TGS players may derive greater muscle mass benefit from creatine loading protocols, informing off-season and preseason supplementation planning. Low-TGS players might benefit from alternative muscle mass strategies or more aggressive injury prevention programming in parallel with supplementation.

## Return to Play Notes
Players with a lower TGS (fewer protective alleles across AMPD1, ACE, ACTN3, CKM, MLCK) face approximately 9x higher odds of non-contact muscle injury. In an RTP context, low-TGS players returning from muscle injuries should be treated as elevated re-injury risks, warranting more conservative load progression and extended rehabilitation timelines. Creatine supplementation during rehabilitation may also be less effective at restoring muscle mass in these players, which should be factored into body composition-based RTP readiness assessments.
