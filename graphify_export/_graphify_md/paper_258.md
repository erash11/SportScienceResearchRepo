# Swartz, E. E., Mihalik, J. P., Decoster, L. C., Kelly, E. A., Register-Mihalik, J. K., McCaffrey, M. A., & Duma, S. M. (2024). Head impact exposure in Hawaiian high school football: Influence of adherence rates on a helmetless tackling and blocking training intervention. Journal of Athletic Training, 59(7), 723–732. https://doi.org/10.4085/1062-6050-0014.24

## TLDR
High school football players who consistently practiced helmetless tackling drills (≥60% adherence) saw 34–37% fewer significant head impact events and substantially lower cumulative impact burden compared to low-adherence peers. Technique training to reduce head contact works, but only when coaches and athletes commit to it—adherence is the critical variable separating programs that benefit from those that don't.

## Abstract
This study examined the effect of adherence to a helmetless tackling and blocking training (HuTT) intervention on head impact exposure in Hawaiian high school football players over the second and third years of a multi-year program. Among 496 players monitored with Riddell InSite helmet sensors, those with ≥60% adherence to the HuTT protocol experienced 34–37% reductions in impact above-threshold events (ImpAE) compared to lower-adherence counterparts. Cumulative impact burden was significantly lower in the high-adherence group (2,105g vs. 3,158g; p = 0.020). Results demonstrate that the protective effect of helmetless tackling drills is dose-dependent—benefit accrues only when the intervention is consistently applied.

## Methods
Prospective multi-school cohort in Hawaii across Year 2 and Year 3 of a HuTT intervention program. N = 496 high school football players. Riddell InSite helmet-mounted sensors recorded head impact data throughout practice and competition. Adherence to HuTT protocol defined as percentage of sessions in which prescribed helmetless drills were performed. High adherence: ≥60% of sessions; low adherence: <60%. Outcomes: ImpAE (impacts exceeding predefined g-threshold) and cumulative impact burden (sum of all impact magnitudes). Comparisons between adherence groups via t-tests and Mann-Whitney U where appropriate.

## Findings
High-adherence group (≥60%): 34–37% reduction in ImpAE vs. low-adherence group across both years. Cumulative impact burden: 2,105g (high adherence) vs. 3,158g (low adherence); p = 0.020. Effect was consistent across Year 2 and Year 3, suggesting sustained benefit with continued protocol use. No significant differences in player demographics or position distribution between adherence groups.

## Limitations
Adherence was measured at the team/session level rather than individual athlete level, which may obscure within-team variation. Hawaiian high school football may not generalize to all regions given different coaching cultures and resources. Sensor-based impact measurement has known limitations in accuracy at lower magnitudes. Long-term cognitive outcomes were not assessed, so impact reduction cannot yet be linked to neurocognitive benefit.

## Practical Implications
Programs adopting helmetless tackling interventions must prioritize coach buy-in and structured adherence tracking to realize impact reduction benefits. A ≥60% adherence threshold appears to be the minimum necessary for meaningful effect. Implementation strategies should include coach education, scheduled HuTT drill blocks within practice plans, and season-long monitoring of drill execution rates—not just initial adoption.

## Athlete Development Notes
For American football development programs, this study validates the use of helmetless tackling drills as a practical head impact reduction tool when properly implemented. Youth and high school programs developing tackling technique should embed HuTT-style drills throughout the pre-season and in-season practice calendar. Emphasizing technique quality over contact volume aligns with broader long-term athlete development principles.

## Return to Play Notes
While this study does not directly address RTP, it supports the principle that technique modification can meaningfully reduce head impact exposure even in contact-heavy sports. Athletes returning from concussion who are reintegrating contact should be introduced to HuTT protocols during the graduated RTP contact phases to minimize re-exposure risk while re-establishing proper tackling mechanics.
