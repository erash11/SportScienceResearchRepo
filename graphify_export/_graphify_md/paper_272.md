# Mack, C. D., Hershman, E. B., Anderson, R. B., Coughlin, M. J., McNitt, A. S., Sendor, R. R., & Kent, R. W. (2019). Higher rates of lower extremity injury on synthetic turf compared with natural turf among National Football League athletes: Epidemiologic confirmation of a biomechanical hypothesis. The American Journal of Sports Medicine, 47(1), 189–196.

## TLDR
NFL players are 16% more likely to sustain a lower extremity injury per play on synthetic turf than on natural grass, with the risk rising to over 100% greater for noncontact ankle and foot injuries requiring 8+ days off. The pattern of findings—stronger associations for distal injuries and noncontact mechanisms—is consistent with the biomechanical explanation that synthetic turf fails to release cleats at injurious torque levels. Natural turf's ability to divot under load serves as a force-limiting mechanism absent in synthetic surfaces. This has direct implications for surface selection, footwear choice, and field maintenance standards.

## Abstract
This cohort study examined whether lower extremity (LE) injury rates in NFL regular season games (2012–2016) were higher on synthetic turf than natural turf, epidemiologically testing a prior biomechanical hypothesis that synthetic surfaces generate greater shear force on the foot due to poor cleat release. All 32 NFL teams contributed injury data under mandated consistent reporting protocols. Poisson models estimated adjusted incidence rate ratios (IRRs). Synthetic turf was associated with a 16% increase in LE injuries per play (IRR 1.16; 95% CI 1.10–1.23). This effect was more pronounced for noncontact/surface contact injuries (IRR up to 2.03 for ankle/foot with ≥8 days missed), and increased as injury locations were more distal (closer to the playing surface).

## Methods
Retrospective cohort study using NFL injury surveillance data from 2012–2016 regular season (1,280 games; 213,935 plays). All 32 teams reported injuries through mandated electronic health records. LE injuries causing any missed participation or ≥8 days missed were included; subcategories of knee and ankle/foot examined. Poisson regression models estimated crude and adjusted IRRs, controlling for surface hardness (Gmax) and field moisture. Secondary analysis restricted to noncontact/surface contact injuries. Sensitivity analyses excluded potentially heterogeneous synthetic fields (Seattle, New England, Dallas).

## Findings
4,801 LE injuries occurred across 1,280 games. Synthetic turf: 16% more LE injuries per play vs. natural turf (IRR 1.16; 95% CI 1.10–1.23). Effect persisted for injuries with ≥8 days missed. Noncontact/surface contact injury IRRs: overall LE 1.27 (1.15–1.41), knee 1.46 (1.20–1.77), ankle/foot 1.68 (1.36–2.08). For ≥8 days missed ankle/foot noncontact injuries: IRR 2.03 (1.42–2.89). Contact injuries attributed to player-to-player contact showed no surface difference (IRR 1.06; 0.97–1.14). Adjustment for hardness and moisture did not meaningfully alter results. If natural turf rates applied to synthetic turf games in the study, approximately 319 fewer LE injuries would have been expected.

## Limitations
Findings are specific to NFL conditions (elite athletes, professional maintenance, certified surfaces) and may not generalize to other levels of play or sports. Injury mechanism classification is subjective and prone to sideline reporting error. Return-to-play data may be incomplete for players who leave the league. Variability in synthetic turf design, age, and maintenance across stadiums was not fully captured. The study cannot fully account for systematic behavioral differences in play style by surface type.

## Practical Implications
NFL teams and athletic staff should prioritize footwear selection optimized for synthetic surfaces, as cleat pattern significantly affects torque and shear loading. Players on synthetic turf may benefit from additional lower limb protection. Stadium operators and synthetic turf manufacturers should pursue designs that allow cleat release at injurious load thresholds, similar to natural turf divoting. Teams playing significant portions of their schedule on synthetic turf should factor surface type into injury prevention programming, including extra emphasis on ankle and foot stability training.

## Athlete Development Notes
The biomechanical mechanism identified—inadequate cleat release on synthetic turf generating excess shear force—is relevant at all levels of football. Youth and collegiate programs using synthetic fields should invest in surface-appropriate footwear education and should consider this injury risk when programming high-intensity change-of-direction and sprint work. Developing players who compete on synthetic surfaces may benefit from targeted ankle/foot strength and proprioception training to compensate for the elevated mechanical demands of synthetic fields.

## Return to Play Notes
The elevated ankle and foot injury rates on synthetic turf—particularly for noncontact mechanisms—are directly relevant to RTP decisions. Athletes returning from lower extremity injuries (especially ankle sprains, foot injuries, or knee ligament injuries) should be cautiously managed when returning to play on synthetic surfaces. Clinicians should consider surface type as part of RTP criteria, potentially requiring higher functional thresholds before clearing athletes to compete on synthetic turf. Footwear reassessment and surface-specific functional testing should be incorporated into RTP protocols.
