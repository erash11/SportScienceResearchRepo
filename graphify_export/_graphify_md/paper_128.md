# Puga TB, Schafer J, Thiel G, Scigliano N, Ruan T, Toledo A, Agbedanu PN, Treffer K. COVID-19 NFL Injury Prevalence Analysis, A Follow-Up Study. medRxiv preprint. 2023. DOI: 10.1101/2023.01.11.23284292

## TLDR
NFL injury rates spiked during the 2020 COVID-19-impacted season due to facility shutdowns and detraining, then returned to significantly lower and stable levels in 2021 and 2022 once athletes regained full access to training facilities — even with an additional week added to the season. This is a large-scale natural experiment confirming that training continuity is a direct injury prevention strategy. Football performance staff should treat any significant training disruption (pandemic, lockout, illness, suspension) as a detraining event requiring a structured progressive retraining protocol before return to full competition demands.

## Abstract
This follow-up study to 'COVID-19 Return to Sport: NFL Injury Prevalence Analysis' examined the longitudinal effects of COVID-19 pandemic-related training disruptions on NFL injury prevalence across the 2020, 2021, and 2022 regular seasons. Injury tallies from publicly available weekly NFL team injury reports were compared using mean injuries per team per week to normalize for the change from a 17-week (2020) to an 18-week (2021, 2022) season. The COVID-19-impacted 2020 season produced a league-wide mean of 146.1 injuries per week (4.6 per team per week), compared to 122.8 (3.8) in 2021 and 125.4 (3.3) in 2022. Differences between 2020 vs. 2021 (P=.03) and 2020 vs. 2022 (P=.02) were statistically significant, while 2021 vs. 2022 did not differ significantly (P=.79). Note: this paper is a medRxiv preprint and has not been peer reviewed.

## Methods
Retrospective follow-up epidemiological study using publicly available NFL weekly injury reports for the 2021 and 2022 18-week regular seasons, combined with 2020 season data from the companion prior study. Injuries tallied from each team's official injury reports or the NFL website; athletes listed with the same injury for consecutive weeks counted once unless a new anatomical region was involved. COVID-19 sick days, illnesses, and non-medical absences excluded. Mean injuries per team per week used to normalize the 17-week (2020) vs. 18-week (2021–2022) season length difference. Unpaired t-tests compared mean injuries per team per week between each pair of seasons (2020 vs. 2021, 2020 vs. 2022, 2021 vs. 2022). Statistical significance: P≤.05.

## Findings
2020 season (17 weeks, COVID-19 impacted): 2,484 total injuries; league-wide mean 146.1 injuries/week; mean per team per week=4.6. 2021 season (18 weeks): 2,210 total injuries; league-wide mean 122.8/week; per team per week=3.8. 2022 season (18 weeks): 2,257 total injuries; league-wide mean 125.4/week; per team per week=3.3. 2020 vs. 2021: statistically significant (P=.03). 2020 vs. 2022: statistically significant (P=.02). 2021 vs. 2022: no significant difference (P=.79). Injury counts in 2021 and 2022 were lower than 2020 despite an additional week of play, indicating a return to pre-pandemic injury levels.

## Limitations
Preprint: not peer reviewed at time of publication. Data limited to publicly available injury reports, which may include underreporting, omissions, or reporting errors. Exact training hours lost during COVID-19 shutdowns are not quantifiable. Injury severity, imaging, and medical clearance details were unavailable. Cannot isolate COVID-19 detraining effects from other confounders (rule changes, roster composition, season length change). Contact injuries — a non-modifiable risk factor — were included in totals. Pre-pandemic seasons (2018, 2019) from the companion study show a rising injury trend, complicating absolute baseline interpretation.

## Practical Implications
Consistent, uninterrupted access to high-quality training facilities and coaching staff is a direct injury prevention strategy for NFL and college football players. Extended training interruptions — from pandemics, lockouts, illness, or suspension — should be followed by structured, progressive retraining protocols that allow adequate physiological adaptation before return to full competition. Performance departments should have detraining-response plans in place to identify athletes most at risk and prioritize their systematic reintegration into full training loads.

## Athlete Development Notes
The COVID-19 facility shutdowns (late March–late May 2020) created a natural experiment demonstrating the injury consequences of detraining in elite football players. The rapid rebound in injury rates to pre-pandemic levels by 2021 and 2022 supports the efficacy of structured preseason preparation programs. For athlete development staff, this study confirms that the physiological adaptations underlying injury resilience — musculotendinous strength, neuromuscular control, cardiovascular conditioning — require continuous training stimuli to maintain and can be restored with appropriate retraining given sufficient time and preparation.

## Return to Play Notes
This study has direct implications for RTP following extended training disruptions at the team or league level. The elevated injury rates observed during the 2020 season reinforce that RTP protocols following any extended absence should include a progressive physical preparation phase — analogous to a structured return-to-training program — before full-contact practice and competition exposure. Medical and performance staff should assess each athlete's physical readiness and detraining state, not just injury status, when making RTP clearance decisions following prolonged absences.
