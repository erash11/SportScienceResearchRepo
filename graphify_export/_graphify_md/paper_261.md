# Park, G., Gabler, L. F., Bailey, A. M., Dau, N. Z., Sherwood, C., Crandall, J. R., & Arbogast, K. B. (2021). Head shape analysis of National Football League players. Proceedings of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers, Part P: Journal of Sports Engineering and Technology. https://doi.org/10.1177/17543371211020614

## TLDR
NFL player heads are larger and more variable in shape than standard male populations, and current helmet fitting based solely on circumference misses important shape dimensions. Two principal components—head height/length and oval vs. round transverse profile—explain 87% of shape variance. Adding breadth and height measurements to fitting protocols could substantially improve helmet fit and safety for professional football players.

## Abstract
Current football helmet fitting guidelines rely solely on head circumference, which may not optimize fit or protection for every player. This study used statistical shape analysis (SSA) and principal component analysis (PCA) on 3D light scans of 87 NFL players to identify key features of head shape variability. Two principal components explained 87% of variance: (1) head height and length in the sagittal plane and (2) prominent occiput with oval transverse cross-section versus prominent forehead with round cross-section. Four measurements—circumference, length, breadth, and height—were defined and quantified. NFL players had significantly larger circumference, length, and breadth than U.S. Army soldiers in the ANSUR II database.

## Methods
3D structured light scans (Artec Eva, 0.5 mm resolution) of 129 NFL players from three teams prior to the 2019–2020 season; 87 scans included after excluding those with hair obstructing scan quality. Five anatomical landmarks defined a head coordinate system; pseudo-landmarks (N=1,278) were mapped from each scalp in spherical coordinates. Correlation-based PCA performed in MATLAB on normalized pseudo-landmark data. Four head shape measurements (circumference, length, breadth, height) compared between NFL sample and ANSUR II database (n=4,082 male U.S. Army soldiers) using Student t-tests.

## Findings
First PC (74.8% of variance): tall vs. short head—primarily related to sagittal height and length. Second PC (12.3%): prominent occiput with oval transverse shape vs. prominent forehead with round shape. Together, two PCs explain 87% of head shape variability. NFL players had significantly larger circumference (592.3 vs. 574.4 mm), length (212.2 vs. 199.5 mm), and breadth (168.2 vs. 154.3 mm) than ANSUR II soldiers (all p<0.05). Height was also significantly larger (152.8 vs. 131.1 mm). Correlations among shape parameters were similar between groups.

## Limitations
Sample of approximately 5% of current NFL players limits generalizability. Insufficient data to analyze head shape differences by playing position. Players with certain hairstyles (dreadlocks, ponytails) were excluded, which may introduce sampling bias. Effect of hair on helmet fit was not addressed. Standard headforms used in laboratory helmet testing (NOCSAE, Hybrid III) do not represent NFL player head shapes.

## Practical Implications
Helmet manufacturers should consider multi-dimensional head shape parameters beyond circumference when designing and sizing helmets for NFL players. Incorporating length, breadth, and height measurements into fitting protocols could improve protection. Current laboratory headforms underrepresent NFL player head size and shape, which may affect the validity of helmet performance testing for this population.

## Athlete Development Notes
Proper helmet fit is fundamental to player safety. Equipment staff should ensure that helmet fitting accounts for head shape dimensions beyond circumference—particularly for players with oval vs. round head profiles—as poor fit may compromise both comfort and concussion mitigation. Position-specific helmet designs may eventually be warranted.

## Return to Play Notes
Helmet fit has been linked to concussion risk; helmets that fit poorly due to shape mismatch may increase impact transmission. Understanding an athlete's individual head shape could inform post-injury helmet fitting reviews as part of a comprehensive RTP protocol to reduce re-injury risk.
