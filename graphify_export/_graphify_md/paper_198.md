# Anderson MJJ, Mack CD, Herzog MM, Levine WN. Epidemiology of shoulder instability in the National Football League. Orthop J Sports Med. 2021;9(5):23259671211007743. DOI: 10.1177/23259671211007743

## TLDR
Shoulder instability in the NFL averages 67 missed-time injuries per year, with subluxations roughly twice as common as dislocations but dislocations causing far more missed time. Posterior instability is more prevalent in NFL players than previously recognized—especially in quarterbacks and offensive linemen—likely due to sport-specific blocking and throwing mechanics. Preseason carries the highest instability rates, and quarterbacks have the highest per-play game rate.

## Abstract
A retrospective observational descriptive epidemiology study examining all documented missed-time shoulder instability injuries in the NFL from the 2012 through 2017 seasons. Over 6 years, 355 players sustained 403 missed-time shoulder instability injuries (average 67/year). Most injuries occurred during games (65%) via contact (85%). The overall game incidence rate was 3.6 per 100,000 player-plays, highest during the preseason (4.9). After excluding unspecified events, 70% were subluxations and 30% were dislocations; 75% of dislocations were anterior, while subluxations were more evenly split between anterior (45%) and posterior (52%). Median missed time was 47 days for dislocation vs. 13 days for subluxation. Quarterbacks and offensive linemen most commonly experienced posterior instability (73% and 53%); all other positions predominantly had anterior instability.

## Methods
Retrospective observational study using NFL injury databases (ISS 2012–2014; EMR 2015–2017) covering all 32 teams. Only missed-time shoulder instability injuries were included to maintain consistency across both data systems. Injuries classified by type (subluxation, dislocation), direction (anterior, posterior, inferior, multidirectional), laterality, injury setting, and mechanism. Missed time defined as days from injury to return to full participation. Game incidence rates calculated per 100,000 player-plays using NFL Game Statistics and Information System data. Descriptive statistics and median IQR reported for missed time.

## Findings
403 missed-time instability injuries in 355 players; average 67/year; 11% suffered multiple episodes. Left shoulder more affected (57% vs. 43%). 65% of injuries in games, 85% contact mechanism. Overall game rate: 3.6/100,000 player-plays; preseason highest (4.9). Excluding unspecified: 70% subluxations, 30% dislocations. Directions (excluding unspecified): 75% of dislocations anterior; subluxations nearly evenly split anterior (45%) vs. posterior (52%). Posterior instability accounted for ~40% of all specified injuries. Median missed time: 47 days (dislocation) vs. 13 days (subluxation). Quarterbacks highest game rate by position (5.5/100,000 player-plays). QBs: 73% posterior instability; OL: 53% posterior. All other positions: predominantly anterior. Inferior/multidirectional instability rare but associated with greatest missed time.

## Limitations
Only missed-time injuries included, underestimating true prevalence—particularly for subluxations. ISS (2012–2014) and EMR (2015–2017) differed in reporting criteria, necessitating separate analysis. Incidence analysis limited to games due to unavailable practice exposure data. Return-to-play dates missing for 26% of athletes, limiting missed-time analysis. Injury characterization relied on athletic trainer reporting without mandatory imaging confirmation. Player movement in/out of NFL complicates RTP percentage calculations.

## Practical Implications
The high proportion of posterior shoulder instability in NFL players—particularly offensive linemen and quarterbacks—should prompt clinicians to include posterior capsulolabral assessment in these athletes and not assume anterior pathology. Preseason shoulder instability rates are highest, consistent with deconditioning and full-contact drill patterns. Dislocation requires substantially longer recovery than subluxation; treatment planning and roster management should account for this. Posterior shoulder strengthening and sport-specific motor control training are important preventive components.

## Athlete Development Notes
Offensive linemen and quarterbacks develop posterior instability through repetitive sport-specific loading—blocking mechanics with the shoulder flexed, adducted, and internally rotated, and the late cocking/follow-through phases of throwing. Developing posterior capsulolabral strength and rotator cuff endurance as part of systematic shoulder development programs can reduce chronic posterior loading stress. Early identification of labral laxity or pain during position-specific drills can allow for early intervention.

## Return to Play Notes
Median RTP after subluxation was 13 days; after dislocation 47 days. Approximately 92.8% of NFL players successfully return to full competition after in-season shoulder instability per publicly available data. Inferior and multidirectional instability carried the greatest missed time. Return-to-play decision-making should be informed by instability type and direction, surgical vs. nonoperative treatment pathway, and position-specific shoulder demands—particularly for quarterbacks who require full throwing motion and offensive linemen who require forceful blocking mechanics.
