# Omari AM, Paul RW, Fliegel B, Osman A, Bishop ME, Erickson BJ, Alberta FG. Effect of COVID-19 on Injury Rates and Timing in the National Football League. Orthop J Sports Med. 2022;10(6):23259671221098749. DOI: 10.1177/23259671221098749

## TLDR
The cancellation of the 2020 NFL preseason due to COVID-19 was associated with significant increases in Achilles tendon (+35%) and hamstring tendon (+25%) injuries during the regular season, while ACL injuries remained constant. Injuries that occurred in weeks 1-4 of the 2020 season mirrored the pattern typically seen during preseason in prior years, suggesting displaced injury risk rather than eliminated risk. For football staff, this underscores that a structured preseason is critical for injury prevention — removing it shifts soft-tissue injury risk into the regular season rather than preventing it.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiology study compared ACL, Achilles tendon, and hamstring tendon injury rates in the NFL between the pre-pandemic seasons (2017-2019) and the COVID-19-affected 2020 season, during which the entire preseason was cancelled and roster sizes were reduced. Data were collected from publicly available sources for 181 ACL, 87 Achilles tendon, and 633 hamstring tendon injuries across 2017-2020. Statistical comparisons used ANOVA, t-tests, and chi-square analysis.

## Methods
Descriptive epidemiology study (Level 4 evidence). Publicly available data were collected for all NFL players sustaining ACL, Achilles tendon, or hamstring tendon injuries between April 1, 2017 and March 31, 2021. Player characteristics, career workload, and season-of-injury workload were assessed. ANOVA and independent-samples t-tests compared 2017-2019 averages to 2020. Chi-square tests were used for categorical data. Significance set at p < .05.

## Findings
Achilles tendon injuries were significantly higher in 2020 vs. 2017-2019 average (27 vs. 20, P=.024). Hamstring tendon injuries were significantly higher in 2020 vs. 2017-2019 average (186 vs. 149, P<.001). ACL injuries remained constant (43 vs. 46 average, P=.175). In 2020, ACL injuries were most common in weeks 1-4 (34.9%) rather than preseason as in prior years (52.9%). Athletes sustaining ACL injuries in 2020 played significantly more regular season games before injury (4.40 vs. 2.78, P=.042). No significant differences in player demographics or career workloads between seasons.

## Limitations
Publicly available databases may underreport minor injuries. Specific training program details for each team or player were unavailable, preventing conclusions about type of training regimen and injury risk. Numerous confounding factors related to the COVID-19 pandemic itself (mental health, access to facilities, individual training variability) could not be controlled. Causality cannot be established from this observational design.

## Practical Implications
Football staff should treat preseason training and competition as essential injury-prevention infrastructure. The 2020 data suggest that when preseason is removed, soft-tissue injury risk (particularly hamstring and Achilles) is not eliminated but shifts into the early regular season. For NFL/college programs facing disrupted off-seasons (lockouts, pandemics, shortened camps), medical and strength staff should implement enhanced ramp-up protocols, increase monitoring of at-risk tissues (Achilles, hamstrings), and consider graduated loading programs to substitute for lost preseason exposure.

## Athlete Development Notes
Pre-training camp conditioning is critical for reducing injury risk in professional athletes. Young players entering the league or returning from extended absences require structured progressive loading before high-intensity competition. The finding that hamstring injuries are most common early in the regular season (weeks 1-4) regardless of season type highlights the importance of consistent year-round conditioning and sport-specific movement preparation.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes returning from extended training gaps (analogous to the COVID lockdown) face elevated risk of Achilles and hamstring tendon injuries. RTP protocols after prolonged inactivity or disrupted training should emphasize gradual reintroduction of sport-specific loads, with particular attention to eccentric hamstring loading and Achilles tendon preparation. The first 4 weeks after return to competition appear to be the highest-risk window for these injuries.
