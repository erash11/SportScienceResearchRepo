# Mack CD, Solomon G, Covassin T, Theodore N, Cárdenas J, Sills A. Epidemiology of concussion in the National Football League, 2015-2019. Sports Health. 2021. DOI: 10.1177/19417381211011446

## TLDR
NFL concussions declined 23% after targeted rule changes and education initiatives implemented before the 2018 season, demonstrating that systematic interventions can meaningfully reduce concussion incidence. Kickoffs and special teams plays remain the highest-risk play types per exposure. Tight ends and wide receivers have the highest concussion rates by position, while running backs and linebackers are most exposed during special teams plays.

## Abstract
A retrospective descriptive epidemiology study using NFL electronic health record (EHR) data from all 32 clubs across the 2015–2019 seasons. A total of 1,302 concussions were identified among 1,004 players. Approximately 80% occurred in game settings. Average annual in-season game concussion incidence decreased 23% from 230.7/season (2015–2017) to 177.0/season (2018–2019; P<0.01). The 5-year one-season concussion risk averaged 7.4%. Preseason game rates (70.6/100 games) were slightly higher than regular season rates (61.7/100 games). In regular season games, 790 concussions occurred across 4,657,243 player-plays at a rate of 1.70/10,000 player-plays. Kickoffs had the highest rate per play (0.69/100 plays). Defensive secondary and offensive linemen had the highest counts; tight ends and wide receivers had the highest rates per 10,000 player-plays.

## Methods
Retrospective observational study using the NFL's centralized EHR system (2015–2019) linked to the NFL Game Statistics and Information System for player-play level exposure data. Concussion incidence described by year, season, play type, and roster position. One-season concussion risk calculated using the exact binomial distribution. Game-related rates per 10,000 player-plays calculated using Poisson distribution; IRRs and 95% CIs reported. In-depth game analyses restricted to regular season. Repeat concussion risk calculated within single seasons.

## Findings
1,302 concussions in 1,004 players over 5 seasons. 80.3% in games; 17.0% in practice; 2.7% conditioning/other. Regular season had most concussions (n=790; 60.7%; 61.7/100 games). Preseason game rate slightly higher (70.6/100 games). Game concussions declined 23% from 2015–2017 to 2018–2019 (P<0.01). Overall 5-year regular season rate: 1.70/10,000 player-plays. Kickoffs had the highest rate per play (0.69/100 plays). Defensive secondary had highest count (n=227; 28.7%); tight ends had highest rate (2.81/10,000 player-plays). Running backs and linebackers had the highest proportion of concussions on special teams plays (32.7% and 32.3%). Defensive secondary concussion rate declined 41% from 2015 to 2019. One-season risk: 7.4% average; repeat concussion risk 6.8%.

## Limitations
Concussion is difficult to diagnose definitively; underreporting remains possible despite mandated EHR use. Risk calculations based on player signings rather than static roster size may underestimate risk relative to AE-based methods. Practice participation data unavailable, precluding practice concussion rate calculations. Observational design cannot establish causality between rule changes and incidence reduction.

## Practical Implications
The 23% reduction in game concussions following the 2018 rule changes (kickoff modifications, helmet restrictions, head-down tackle ban, blindside block elimination) demonstrates that evidence-based rule and equipment changes can reduce concussion incidence at elite levels. Kickoffs and special teams play remain the highest per-exposure risk activities and should continue to be targets for further rule modification. Helmet performance ratings should guide equipment selection at all levels of football.

## Athlete Development Notes
Concussion risk is elevated in early career high-speed and high-contact scenarios. Educating young players on proper tackling technique—particularly avoiding lowering the head on contact—and ensuring they wear high-performing helmets are foundational developmental priorities. Wide receivers and tight ends have elevated positional rates and may benefit from position-specific contact preparation and concussion awareness.

## Return to Play Notes
Approximately 6.8% of concussed NFL players sustained a repeat concussion within the same season. A thorough graduated RTP protocol is essential. The NFL's protocol includes standardized symptom assessment, exertional testing, and neurological clearance before return to full practice and competition. The high repeat concussion rate underscores the importance of conservative management and avoidance of premature return.
