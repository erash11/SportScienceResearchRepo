# Mohammad N, Ghali M, Rodriguez RJ, Israel H, Cannada LK. Analyzing Ankle Injuries in the NFL: A Video-Based Investigation of the Impact of Artificial Turf on Injury Mechanisms. J Orthop Exp Innov. 2026;7(1).

## TLDR
Slit-film turf in the NFL does not increase non-contact ankle injuries as commonly assumed — it actually showed the lowest non-contact injury rate (14.3%) while natural grass had the highest (46.4%). The relationship between turf type and ankle injury mechanism is more complex than the prevailing narrative suggests, and direct contact appears to drive most slit-film injuries rather than surface traction.

## Abstract
This retrospective video-based study examined 78 ankle and foot injuries sustained during the 2022–2023 NFL season to evaluate the relationship between playing surface type and injury mechanism. Injuries were classified as contact or non-contact and compared across three surface types: natural grass, monofilament/dual-fiber (MFDF) turf, and slit-film turf. Contrary to the prevailing hypothesis, slit-film turf exhibited the highest rate of contact injuries (85.7%) but the lowest rate of non-contact injuries (14.3%), while natural grass showed the highest proportion of non-contact injuries (46.4%). Statistically significant differences in injury mechanism across surface types were found (p=0.015).

## Methods
Retrospective review of publicly available NFL injury reports (2022–2023 season); 78 ankle/foot injuries identified in QBs, WRs, RBs, TEs, CBs, and safeties; game footage from NFL+ reviewed by two independent evaluators (E1, E2) to classify mechanism of injury (MOI: contact vs. non-contact); discrepancies resolved by a third evaluator (E3); surface types categorized as natural grass, MFDF turf, and slit-film turf; Mann-Whitney U test for ranked distribution comparison; Pearson chi-square for surface × MOI association; binary logistic regression; significance set at p<0.05.

## Findings
• 78 ankle/foot injuries analyzed: 30.8% non-contact, 69.2% direct contact
• Slit-film turf: highest contact injury proportion (85.7%), lowest non-contact (14.3%)
• Natural grass: highest non-contact injury proportion (46.4%)
• MFDF turf: intermediate non-contact proportion (27.6%)
• Significant association between surface type and MOI: chi-square p=0.015 (grass vs. slit-film p=0.018)
• No significant difference between grass and MFDF turf (p=0.14) or MFDF vs. slit-film (p=0.262)
• Offensive skill positions (WR, RB, TE) accounted for 84.9% of offensive injuries
• Fourth quarter had the highest injury rate (41%), suggesting fatigue as a contributing factor
• Average injured player age: 25.8 years; no significant association between surface and weather conditions

## Limitations
Single-season cohort (2022–2023) from one league limits generalizability and trend confirmation; retrospective video review with human evaluators introduces classification bias; publicly available injury reports may underreport less severe injuries; no biomechanical testing, cleat type, field maintenance data, or medical records available; variability in NFL team injury reporting practices; indoor stadiums standardized to 73°F, limiting temperature analysis for indoor games; reinjury not captured due to documentation inconsistency.

## Practical Implications
NFL teams and facility managers should not assume slit-film turf uniformly increases non-contact lower extremity injury risk — the actual relationship between surface type and ankle injury mechanism is surface-specific and complex. Injury prevention strategies for ankle injuries on slit-film surfaces should focus on contact mechanics and collision readiness rather than solely traction-related interventions. Fatigue management in the fourth quarter is an important modifiable factor across all surface types.

## Athlete Development Notes
In American football, ankle and foot injuries are most common in offensive skill positions (WR, RB, TE at 84.9% of offensive injuries), making ankle stability and strength training a priority for these position groups. The surface-dependent variation in injury mechanism (contact vs. non-contact) suggests that position-specific ankle injury prevention programs should account for the primary playing surface used in the athlete's venue. Younger players (mean age 25.8) are at the greatest risk, reinforcing the importance of ankle prehabilitation in early career development.

## Return to Play Notes
The finding that slit-film turf predominantly produces contact-driven ankle injuries (vs. non-contact injuries on natural grass) has implications for RTP decision-making: the surface on which an athlete will return should inform the functional testing emphasis. For athletes returning to slit-film fields, RTP protocols should emphasize contact readiness and lateral stability under external perturbation rather than traction-based stress tests alone. The elevated fourth-quarter injury rate also underscores the need for fatigue-condition testing before full RTP clearance.
