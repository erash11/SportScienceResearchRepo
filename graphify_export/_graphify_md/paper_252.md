# Buckthorpe M, Wright S, Bruce-Low S, Nanni G, Sturdy T, Gross AS, Bowen L, Styles B, Delvecchio M, Donahue M, Gimpel M, Stride M, Lemoine P, Faria T, Panday K, Sherrill E, Birch J, Farooq A, Della Villa F. Recommendations for hamstring injury prevention in elite football: translating research into practice. British Journal of Sports Medicine. 2019;53(7):449–456.

## TLDR
This practical consensus paper bridges the gap between hamstring injury science and applied football practice. It argues that HSI prevention requires a structured, multicomponent program rather than any single exercise or intervention. The core recommendations are: (1) screen players for known risk factors (prior HSI, older age, eccentric strength deficits, poor H:Q ratio); (2) use progressive eccentric hamstring strengthening programs; (3) manage sprint and high-speed running loads systematically; (4) ensure adequate recovery between high-intensity exposures; and (5) apply structured, criteria-based return-to-play protocols. For football staff, implementing these evidence-based practices in a systematic, player-individualized manner offers the best current approach to reducing the high and rising incidence of hamstring injuries in elite football.

## Abstract
This expert consensus and evidence-synthesis paper provides a practical framework for hamstring strain injury (HSI) prevention in elite football. Drawing on the latest scientific evidence and applied experience from leading European football clubs, the authors present a structured, multicomponent approach to HSI prevention organized around risk factor identification, load management, neuromuscular training, and rehabilitation. The paper translates complex research findings on HSI etiology — including the roles of previous injury, age, eccentric strength deficits, sprint exposure, and fatigue — into actionable protocols for elite sport settings. Key recommendations include systematic screening for risk factors, individualized eccentric hamstring strengthening (with emphasis on the Nordic hamstring exercise and hip extension exercises), progressive sprint and high-speed running exposure, and evidence-based return-to-play criteria.

## Methods
Narrative review and expert consensus synthesis. The authors reviewed current epidemiological data on HSI in elite football (drawing primarily from the UEFA Elite Club Injury Study), identified modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors, and mapped evidence-based interventions onto a practical prevention framework. The paper is structured around a four-phase prevention and management model: pre-participation screening, in-season prevention, acute injury management, and return to play. Evidence quality was appraised and recommendations graded based on available literature.

## Findings
HSI represents the most common injury in elite football (approximately 12–17% of all injuries) with incidence rising over recent decades. Key modifiable risk factors include eccentric hamstring weakness, strength imbalances, inadequate sprint conditioning, accumulated fatigue, and poor rehabilitation following prior injury. Non-modifiable risk factors are older age and previous HSI. The Nordic hamstring exercise reduces HSI incidence by approximately 51% when implemented correctly. Progressive sprint and high-speed running exposure is essential for hamstring tissue adaptation. Return-to-play criteria should be objective and criteria-based, not time-based, and should include eccentric strength testing, sprint biomechanics assessment, and sport-specific rehabilitation. A multicomponent prevention program addressing multiple risk factors simultaneously is more effective than single-exercise approaches.

## Limitations
The paper is a narrative review and consensus document rather than a systematic review or meta-analysis, which limits the rigor of evidence synthesis. Compliance with prevention programs in elite football settings is often low, reducing real-world effectiveness. Individual player variability in risk profiles means that population-level recommendations require individualization. Many studies referenced were conducted in soccer (association football) populations and may not fully generalize to American football or other contact sports. The rising HSI incidence despite widespread awareness of prevention strategies suggests that current evidence-based programs have not yet fully solved the problem.

## Practical Implications
Football medical and performance staff should implement a systematic, multicomponent HSI prevention program including: (1) pre-season and ongoing risk factor screening (prior injury history, eccentric strength assessment via Nordic hamstring or isokinetic dynamometry, H:Q ratio); (2) progressive eccentric hamstring loading (Nordic hamstring exercise, Romanian deadlifts, hip extension work) throughout the season; (3) structured sprint exposure programs that progressively build high-speed running tolerance; (4) load monitoring to prevent excessive acute spikes in sprint volume; and (5) criteria-based RTP protocols that do not rely solely on time since injury.

## Athlete Development Notes
Young players should develop eccentric hamstring strength and sprint mechanics early in their careers as a foundation for long-term injury resilience. Progressive exposure to high-speed running and maximum velocity sprinting during development reduces HSI risk by building tissue adaptability. Eccentric hamstring training (particularly Nordic hamstring exercise, which reduces HSI incidence by ~51%) should be integrated into long-term athletic development programs for football players from academy age.

## Return to Play Notes
Return-to-play after HSI should follow objective, criteria-based protocols rather than time-based timelines. Key RTP criteria include: pain-free full range of motion, restoration of eccentric hamstring strength to at least 90% of the contralateral limb (or pre-injury baseline), successful completion of progressive sprint and change-of-direction protocols, and sport-specific on-pitch rehabilitation. Re-injury risk is substantially elevated if players return before meeting these objective benchmarks. Post-RTP monitoring of sprint mechanics and load management is recommended for at least 4–8 weeks following return.
