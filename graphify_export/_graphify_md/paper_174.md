# Connolly JG, Nathanson JT, Sobotka S, Haider S, Gometz A, Lovell M, Choudhri T. Effect of Playing and Training at Altitude on Concussion Incidence in Professional Football. Orthop J Sports Med. 2018;6(12):2325967118794928. DOI: 10.1177/2325967118794928

## TLDR
NFL teams that both train and play at altitude above 644 feet have significantly fewer concussions than all other groups—a 28% reduction versus the league average. This protective effect appears to require chronic altitude adaptation, not just acute exposure, since low-altitude teams visiting high-altitude venues showed no benefit. Conversely, high-altitude teams traveling to play at low altitude had the highest concussion rate (0.36/exposure), suggesting the protection is quickly lost when players descend. For football programs, this data challenges the simple 'slosh effect' hypothesis and implies that long-term physiological adaptation—not just the game environment—is the key variable. Away teams also had significantly more concussions than home teams regardless of altitude.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiology study examined whether the protective effects of altitude on NFL concussion incidence are due to acute exposure (the 'slosh effect') or chronic adaptations to living and training at altitude. Data from 2012–2015 NFL regular seasons (1920 team exposures, 569 concussions) were subdivided into four groups based on whether teams trained and played at high altitude (≥644 feet) or low altitude. Teams that trained AND played at high altitude (high-high group) had a 28% lower concussion rate (0.20/exposure) than the overall rate (0.29/exposure), while teams from low altitude playing at high altitude showed no significant reduction.

## Methods
Descriptive epidemiology study using publicly available data from Frontline's Concussion Watch covering 2012–2015 NFL regular seasons (weeks 1–16). A total of 1920 team exposures across 960 games were analyzed. Teams were categorized into four altitude groups: low-altitude training/playing below 644 ft (low-low), low-altitude training/playing above 644 ft (low-high), high-altitude training/playing below 644 ft (high-low), and high-altitude training/playing above 644 ft (high-high). Stadium elevations were approximated via web-based tools. Statistics included Spearman rho, Wilcoxon signed rank, unpaired t-tests, and one-way and two-way ANOVA.

## Findings
Total of 569 concussions across 1920 team exposures (overall rate 0.29/exposure). Away teams had significantly higher concussion rates than home teams (0.32 vs. 0.27/exposure; P=.03). The high-high group had a significantly lower concussion rate (0.20/exposure) compared with low-low (0.31; P<.05), high-low (0.36; P<.05), and overall (P<.05). The low-high group (0.32/exposure) was not significantly different from other groups. No dose-response relationship between altitude and concussion rate was found (R²=0.005). Quartile analysis showed no significant differences across altitude tiers.

## Limitations
Use of the binary 644-foot threshold may oversimplify a physiological spectrum; teams near this threshold (e.g., Green Bay at 640 ft vs. Indianapolis at 714 ft) may experience negligible differences. Altitudes studied are below the 2000-foot threshold where most documented physiological adaptations occur. Preseason altitude training was not accounted for. Data sourced from a public aggregator subject to underreporting. Small sample of high-altitude NFL teams (n=9 stadiums) limits statistical precision.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football programs based at altitude may carry an intrinsic concussion-risk advantage that diminishes when teams travel to lower elevations. This finding does not suggest teams should artificially simulate altitude but highlights environmental factors that may influence head injury protocols. Medical and performance staffs at high-altitude programs should be aware that this protective effect appears to dissipate rapidly upon descending, which may have implications for travel scheduling and pregame concussion baselines for away games. Low-altitude programs playing at high-altitude venues should not expect acute protection.

## Athlete Development Notes
The altitude-concussion relationship is relevant primarily for programs located at elevation (e.g., Denver, Colorado Springs, Salt Lake City). For athlete development, the chronic physiological adaptations to altitude—including erythropoietic, vascular, and potentially neuroprotective changes—may have broader implications for training quality and recovery. Programs at low altitude would not be expected to gain concussion-risk benefits from short altitude training camps based on these data.

## Return to Play Notes
This study does not directly address return-to-play protocols, but the finding that high-altitude teams playing away games had the highest concussion rate (0.36/exposure) raises the possibility that altitude descent could transiently increase concussion vulnerability. Clinicians managing RTP for players from altitude-based teams should consider whether rapid environmental changes after travel could influence concussion symptom presentation or risk of re-injury in the short term.
