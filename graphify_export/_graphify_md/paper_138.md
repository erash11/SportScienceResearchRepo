# Hill KP, Wu A, Solomon G, Mack C, Ling G, Mayer T, Sills A. Design Considerations in the National Football League and National Football League Players Association Prescription Drug Monitoring Program: An Introduction and Overview. Current Sports Medicine Reports. 2021;20(11):614-616.

## TLDR
The NFL and NFLPA created the first league-wide prescription drug monitoring program in professional sports in 2019, mandating that all 32 teams document every pain medication prescription in a shared EMR system. The PDMP's goals are to understand prescribing patterns, identify potential opioid misuse, and guide evidence-based pain management — recognizing that NFL players face continuous cycles of performance, pain, and recovery that create unique opioid risk. For team medical and performance staff, this program means prescription practices are now tracked and benchmarked league-wide. Staff should be familiar with the PDMP's scope, use it to guide prescribing toward evidence-based alternatives to opioids, and treat it as a tool for player health rather than surveillance.

## Abstract
This special communication describes the design and implementation of the NFL/NFLPA Prescription Drug Monitoring Program (PDMP), established in May 2019 as part of the collectively bargained Pain Management Committee (PMC). The PDMP monitors all prescriptions entered in the NFL Electronic Medical Record (EMR) for all 32 clubs, tracking opioids, muscle relaxants, NSAIDs, nonnarcotic analgesics, and other pain medications from training camp through the week following each club's last game. Biannual reports are issued to NFL and NFLPA medical leadership, with deidentified trend data shared with team medical staffs to inform evidence-based prescribing practices.

## Methods
Special communication describing the design and implementation process of the NFL/NFLPA PDMP. Origin: May 2019 NFL/NFLPA collective bargaining agreement mandated documentation of all player medications. IQVIA (independent health IT company) contracted to design data collection protocols and monitor real-time reporting. Four PDMP components: (1) prescription data collection via NFL EMR, (2) required club reporting from training camp through season end, (3) required player reporting of all prescription medications to club physicians, (4) biannual deidentified reports to league medical leadership and individual clubs. Drug classification uses Generic Product Identifier (GPI) groups mapped from FDA National Drug Codes: opioids, muscle relaxants, NSAIDs, nonnarcotic analgesics, corticosteroids, narcotic antitussives, nerve pain medications, antimigraine medications.

## Findings
The PDMP was the first of its kind in professional or amateur sports. It captures all prescriptions written for NFL players by club and unaffiliated physicians entered into the NFL EMR. Clubs can access their own aggregated prescribing patterns benchmarked against the rest of the league via an interactive dashboard. The PMC used PDMP data to develop educational resources on evidence-based alternatives to opioids, including cannabinoids, for both players and medical staffs. The program identified that completeness and accuracy depend on diligent manual entry by club medical staff, creating potential for incomplete capture. No outcome data on prescribing changes or opioid misuse rates are reported in this introductory overview.

## Limitations
The EMR is not the only route through which prescriptions can be ordered; some prescriptions must be entered manually by busy club medical staff who may not always be the primary prescriber. The EMR records prescriptions written but does not record whether the prescription was filled or taken. Injury diagnosis linking prescriptions to specific conditions may be missing in some records. No consensus definition exists for aberrant prescribing behavior, reducing the PDMP's ability to detect problematic prescribing patterns. The program was in early stages at publication, with data collection processes still evolving. Prescriptions from unaffiliated physicians must be self-reported by players to club staff for entry, creating a dependency on player compliance.

## Practical Implications
NFL team medical and performance staff should understand that prescription practices for all pain medications — including NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, and opioids — are now monitored, benchmarked league-wide, and reported biannually to team and league medical leadership. This creates accountability and an opportunity to shift toward evidence-based alternatives to opioids for pain management. The PMC's educational resources on cannabinoids and non-opioid analgesics should be integrated into team pain management protocols. Staff should actively support complete and accurate EMR entry of all prescriptions to ensure the PDMP fulfills its patient safety mission. The program sets a precedent that could be adopted by college programs seeking to monitor prescription patterns in student-athletes.

## Athlete Development Notes
While the PDMP is focused on professional players, its underlying principle — systematic monitoring of prescription pain medication use in the context of performance, pain, and recovery cycles — is equally applicable to college football programs. Young players entering high-load training environments are at risk for inappropriate opioid use patterns that can persist post-career. Development programs should implement education on opioid risks, establish clear non-opioid-first pain management protocols, and consider tracking prescription patterns as a component of player health documentation. The PMC's emphasis on educating players about evidence-based alternatives to opioids is a model that college programs should adopt proactively.

## Return to Play Notes
The PDMP's recognition that NFL players are engaged in 'continuous cycles of performance, pain, and recovery' is directly relevant to RTP contexts. Pain medication prescribing during injury rehabilitation is a critical period where opioid overuse patterns can develop. Team medical staff making RTP decisions should audit prescription patterns during rehabilitation — enabled by PDMP data — to ensure that pain management is not masking incomplete recovery or driving premature return. The shift toward evidence-based non-opioid alternatives (NSAIDs, nerve pain medications, cannabinoids) for managing post-injury pain during rehabilitation aligns with both the PDMP's goals and best-practice RTP medicine.
