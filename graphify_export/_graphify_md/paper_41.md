# Heintz EC, Scott DP, Simms KR, Foreman JJ. Air Quality Is Predictive of Mistakes in Professional Baseball and American Football. International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health. 2023;20:542.

## TLDR
Worse annual air quality in the county where a professional baseball or football team plays is associated with more team errors in MLB, more interceptions by NFL quarterbacks, and lower overall quarterback performance ratings, indicating that air pollution meaningfully degrades elite cognitive-physical sport performance.

## Abstract
This study examined the impact of air quality (measured by the EPA's Air Quality Index, AQI) on performance errors in Major League Baseball (MLB) and NFL quarterback performance from 1999–2020 (MLB) and 2006–2021 (NFL). Linear regression analyses used median county-level annual AQI as the independent variable of interest, predicting team errors per game (MLB), interception percentage (NFL), and total quarterback rating (QBR, NFL). AQI was a significant predictor in all three models: higher AQI (worse air quality) was associated with more errors per game in MLB, higher interception percentage, and lower QBR in the NFL, even after controlling for relevant confounders including payroll, manager/experience variables, and prior-season performance.

## Methods
Two separate OLS regression models were used. MLB sample: 632 team-season observations (1999–2020) with errors per game as the dependent variable; predictors included median annual AQI, year, manager tenure, manager age, and natural log of team payroll. NFL sample: 705 quarterback-season observations (2007–2021) for QBs starting no earlier than 1999 and playing for one team per season; dependent variables were interception percentage and QBR; predictors included cumulative AQI, year, NFL experience, prior-season performance, and games started. Air quality data sourced from EPA (epa.gov); MLB performance data from baseball-reference.com; NFL data from pro-football-reference.com.

## Findings
- A one-point increase in AQI increased MLB errors by 0.000993 per game (p=0.005); teams in high-AQI counties (e.g., Phoenix) could commit 10+ more errors over a 162-game season.
- Cumulative AQI was a marginally significant positive predictor of NFL interception percentage (p=0.059) and a significant negative predictor of QBR (p=0.035).
- A 67-point worse AQI (e.g., average county vs. Phoenix/Maricopa County) was associated with an expected decrease in QBR of ~15.4 points (~0.7 SD) and ~1.27% higher interception rate (~0.4 SD).
- Team payroll and year were significant confounders in the MLB model; NFL experience and games started were significant for QB performance.
- Results are consistent with air pollution impairing both cognitive and physical performance in elite professional athletes.

## Limitations
Annual median AQI is a coarse measure that does not capture game-day or acute exposure fluctuations. Sample does not account for individual athlete adaptation to local air quality or acclimatization. Environmental factors such as temperature, altitude, and atmospheric pressure were not controlled. MLB analysis cannot examine cumulative individual effects due to roster turnover. Generalizability is limited to professional male athletes performing in short, intense sport contexts.

## Practical Implications
Teams located in or traveling to counties with poor annual air quality may face a compounding performance disadvantage. Performance staff and medical teams should consider monitoring AQI as part of environmental preparedness, especially when games or training camps are scheduled in high-pollution areas. The cognitive demand of quarterback decision-making and fielding accuracy appear particularly vulnerable to ambient air pollution exposure.

## Athlete Development Notes
Relevant to American football, specifically quarterback development and performance. Cumulative exposure to poor air quality across a career appears to degrade the high-level cognitive performance required for accurate decision-making and pass execution. Teams drafting or developing quarterbacks in high-AQI markets may face performance headwinds unrelated to talent or training quality.

## Return to Play Notes
Not directly studied. However, findings that even chronic moderate AQI exposure impairs elite QB cognitive performance suggest that air quality monitoring is relevant when determining readiness for return to full competition, particularly after respiratory illness or concussion, when cognitive-physical integration is already compromised.
