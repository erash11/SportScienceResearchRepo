# Luxenburg D, Wasserman N, Geller J, Perez J, Burke J, Kaplan L. Hamstring injuries in the national football league: An epidemiological study. Journal of Orthopaedics. 2023;35:111–114.

## TLDR
This NFL epidemiological study found that Linebackers and Defensive Backs sustain hamstring injuries at roughly twice the expected rate relative to their time on the field, while Offensive and Defensive Linemen are at substantially lower risk. Neither artificial turf nor playing on short rest (Thursday games) significantly increased hamstring injury rates compared to other injury types or to games played on normal rest. Hamstring injuries tended to occur slightly earlier in the season than other injuries. For football staff, position-specific monitoring and targeted prevention programs for skill-position players — particularly those who sprint, cut, and change direction frequently — are warranted.

## Abstract
NOTE: IDs 249 and 250 are the same paper. ID 249 is the journal pre-proof version (with cover page and '(1)' in filename); ID 250 is the final published version. Both contain identical content — the epidemiological study of hamstring strain injuries (HSIs) in the NFL from 2013–2016 regular season game books (n=78 HSIs). The study analyzed relative incidence by position (standardized incidence ratios), field surface (turf vs. grass), short rest (4-day weeks), and week of season. Linebackers (SIR 2.02) and Defensive Backs (SIR 1.62) had the highest relative incidence; Offensive and Defensive Linemen had the lowest (SIRs 0.28 and 0.29). No significant effect of field surface (p=0.082) or short rest (p=0.959) was found. Mean week of HSI was 8.05 in a 17-week season.

## Methods
Retrospective epidemiological analysis using publicly available official NFL game books from the 2013–2016 regular seasons. Seventy-eight qualifying in-game hamstring strain injuries (strains, partial tears, complete tears) were identified and cross-referenced with Foxsports.com. Standardized incidence ratios (SIRs) were calculated by dividing actual relative positional incidence by expected relative positional incidence (based on 2020–2021 personnel data). Two-proportion Z-tests, Chi-Squared tests of independence, and unpaired two-sample t-tests were used for statistical comparisons (α=0.05).

## Findings
78 qualifying HSIs identified; 10.3% were re-injuries within the study period. Linebackers and Running Backs had the highest SIRs (both 2.02); Defensive Backs had SIR 1.62. Offensive Linemen (SIR 0.28) and Defensive Linemen (SIR 0.29) had significantly lower incidence than expected. No significant relationship between field surface (turf vs. grass) and HSI (p=0.082 for proportion difference; p=0.283 for chi-squared independence test). No significant difference in HSI rate on short rest (4-day weeks) vs. regular rest (p=0.959). HSIs did not occur more on short rest than other injury types (p=0.861). Mean week of HSI was 8.05 (95% CI: 7.06–9.04); median 7.5, earlier in the season than the midpoint (week 8.5) though not significantly different from other injuries (mean 8.84).

## Limitations
Data derived solely from official game books; no information on prior medical history or injuries outside the study window, limiting recurrence analysis. Game books do not report injury mechanism or severity. Positional frequency data used (2020 defensive, 2021 offensive) are from different seasons than the injury data (2013–2016), though formation changes since 2018 are minimal. Only in-game regular-season injuries captured; practice and preseason injuries excluded. Small sample (n=78) may limit statistical power.

## Practical Implications
Position-specific hamstring injury prevention programs should prioritize Linebackers and Defensive Backs, who bear disproportionately high injury risk compared to linemen. Thursday night game scheduling (short rest) does not appear to elevate hamstring injury risk and should not be a primary concern. Field surface choice (turf vs. grass) also does not significantly alter hamstring injury rates, though player perception surveys suggest turf is associated with general injury concerns. Early-season monitoring may be warranted as HSIs trend toward occurring in the first half of the season.

## Athlete Development Notes
Position-specific strength and conditioning programs targeting sprint mechanics, eccentric hamstring strength, and hamstring-to-quadriceps ratio (H:Q ≥ 0.6) are especially important for skill players (LBs, DBs, RBs) who repeatedly accelerate, decelerate, and change direction. Development programs should incorporate sports-specific movements that replicate the takeoff phase of sprinting where hamstring injuries most often occur at the myotendinous junction.

## Return to Play Notes
NFL players have a 38.4% HSI recurrence rate and a 13.4% increased reinjury risk if returning within two weeks. RTP decisions for Linebackers and Defensive Backs warrant additional conservatism given their high re-injury risk and positional demands. A robust RTP protocol should confirm adequate H:Q ratios and bilateral strength symmetry before clearance.
