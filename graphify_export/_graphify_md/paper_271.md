# Iverson, G. L., Merz, Z. C., & Terry, D. P. (2022). High-school football and midlife brain health problems. Clinical Journal of Sport Medicine, 32(2), 86–94.

## TLDR
Men who played high-school football (American) do not report meaningfully worse mental health or cognitive functioning in midlife compared to men who played other sports or no sports. While former football players had more lifetime concussions and higher rates of chronic pain and headache medication use, they did not differ from comparison groups on depression, anxiety, memory, or post-concussion symptoms. Sleep difficulties were more prevalent in the football group. These findings are consistent with six prior studies and do not support the idea that high-school football participation necessarily leads to poor brain health in middle age.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional online survey study examined whether middle-aged men (ages 35–55) who played high-school football experience worse brain health outcomes compared to men who played other contact sports, noncontact sports, or no sports. A total of 407 men were stratified by high-school sport history (football n=123, contact sports n=69, noncontact sports n=97, no sports n=118). Validated measures included the PHQ-8 for depression symptoms and BC-PSI for post-concussion-like symptoms. No significant group differences were found in lifetime mental health treatment, rates of depression, anxiety, concentration or memory problems, or post-concussion symptom totals. Football players reported significantly more lifetime concussions, higher rates of chronic pain and headache medication prescriptions, and more sleep problems in the past year.

## Methods
Cross-sectional cohort study using an online survey via Amazon Mechanical Turk (mTurk). Participants: 407 US men aged 35–55. Groups: football (n=123), other contact sports (n=69), noncontact sports (n=97), no sports (n=118). Measures: PHQ-8 (depression), BC-PSI (post-concussion symptoms), and a custom survey on health history, current symptoms, concussion history, and medication use. Statistical analyses: one-way ANOVA, Kruskal-Wallis H tests, chi-square tests, Spearman/point-biserial correlations, and logistic regression. Validity items embedded to filter inattentive respondents.

## Findings
No significant group differences in: lifetime history of depression or anxiety medication, mental health treatment, rates of depression/anxiety/anger/concentration/memory/headache/chronic pain over the past year, PHQ-8 depression scores, or BC-PSI post-concussion symptom totals. Football players reported more lifetime concussions (mean 2.28 vs. 0.68–1.67 in other groups), higher rates of chronic pain medication prescription (66.7%), more headache medication prescriptions (26%), and greater sleep difficulties in the past year (39.0% vs. 20.6–30.5%). Predictors of memory problems: sleep difficulties (OR=3.19), anxiety (OR=2.90), concussion history (OR=2.33), and depression (OR=2.26); high-school football was not a significant predictor.

## Limitations
Self-reported data subject to recall and response bias. Consent language mentioning CTE may have introduced selection bias. Concussion history could not be independently verified. Multiple comparisons were not corrected for (though null findings dominate). The sample had a relatively high rate of depressive symptoms compared to the general population. Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference. All participants were current US residents which may limit demographic generalizability.

## Practical Implications
High-school football participation alone does not appear to predict worse brain health in midlife. Clinicians working with former football players should be aware that sleep difficulties, chronic pain, and headaches may be more prevalent in this population and should be assessed and treated. Depression, anxiety, sleep problems, and concussion history are the primary predictors of memory complaints—not sport participation per se. Evidence-based treatments for these comorbid conditions can substantially improve function and quality of life.

## Athlete Development Notes
These data provide reassurance that high-school-level football participation is not inherently linked to midlife cognitive or mental health deficits. For athlete development programs, this supports continued participation at the high-school level with appropriate concussion management protocols in place. The elevated concussion history in the football group underscores the ongoing importance of concussion education, recognition, and safe return-to-play protocols at the youth and high-school levels.

## Return to Play Notes
The study indirectly supports the importance of thorough concussion management: former football players had significantly more lifetime concussions, which was a significant predictor of memory problems in midlife. Proper identification and management of concussions during an athlete's playing career may help mitigate long-term cognitive risk. Current sleep difficulties and anxiety were stronger predictors of memory problems than sport history, suggesting that RTP protocols should include mental health and sleep screening, particularly following concussive events.
