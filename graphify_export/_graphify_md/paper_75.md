# Chopra A, Parekh AS, Ramanathan D, Parekh SG. Bilateral Achilles Tendon Ruptures in the NFL. Foot Ankle Spec. 2022; vol. XX(no. X):1–5.

## TLDR
NFL athletes who sustain bilateral Achilles tendon ruptures show a significant age progression between injuries and a declining trend in Pro Bowl recognition, indicating meaningful career-level performance consequences from repeated Achilles injuries.

## Abstract
This case series investigated 5 NFL athletes who sustained bilateral Achilles tendon ruptures (ATR) between the 2007 and 2021 seasons, identified through publicly available online injury and sports analysis sources. The study found a significant age difference at the time of each rupture (ATR1 mean age 27.8 years vs ATR2 mean age 30.4 years; P < .01) and a decreasing trend in Pro Bowl nominations following successive injuries (P = .027). No significant differences were observed in rehabilitation duration or games missed between the two rupture events. Among the defensive subgroup, forced fumbles was the only performance metric that significantly changed across successive ATRs (P = .02). The findings suggest bilateral ATRs can adversely impact career longevity and performance in NFL athletes.

## Methods
Case series design; publicly available online injury data from NFL.com, ESPN.com, and NCAA.com queried for bilateral ATR cases from 2007–2021 season start; 6 athletes identified, 5 included after exclusion criteria applied; demographics, Pro Bowl nominations, rehabilitation time, games missed, and defensive performance metrics recorded; descriptive and univariate statistics performed in Microsoft Excel (v16.52) and IBM SPSS (v25.0); statistical significance set at P < .05.

## Findings
• 5 NFL athletes identified with bilateral, independent, contralateral ATRs between 2007 and 2021
• Mean age at ATR1 was 27.8 ± 4 years vs 30.4 ± 4 years at ATR2 (P < .01)
• Mean NFL seasons played before ATR1 was 5.8 ± 4
• Pro Bowl nominations declined significantly: 2.6 ± 2 before ATR1, 0.4 ± 1 between ATRs, 0.2 ± 1 after ATR2 (P = .027)
• No significant difference in rehabilitation time (ATR1: 9.3 ± 2 months; ATR2: 10.9 ± 2 months; P = .42) or games missed
• Mean time between ATR1 and ATR2 was 37.0 ± 14 months
• Forced fumbles was the only defensive metric to significantly change across successive ATRs (P = .02)
• 4 of 5 athletes held defensive positions; 3 of 5 remained active on an NFL roster at time of publication

## Limitations
Small sample size (n = 5); reliance on publicly available online injury data with potential for incomplete records; inability to control for all performance-related confounders; retrospective case series design limits causal inference.

## Practical Implications
Bilateral ATR carries significant career implications for NFL players, including declining all-pro recognition and potentially reduced on-field contributions. Medical and performance staff should monitor contralateral Achilles tendon health closely following a unilateral rupture, especially given that individuals with a prior ATR have roughly 200 times greater odds of contralateral rupture.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly studied in NFL (professional American football) athletes. Mean career length of only 3.3 years for NFL athletes means a bilateral ATR spanning multiple seasons can effectively end or severely curtail a career. Findings highlight the need for long-term tendon load management programs for players with a prior ATR history.

## Return to Play Notes
Rehabilitation duration was not significantly different between ATR1 and ATR2 events (approximately 9–11 months each), and games missed were also similar. However, the declining performance trend post-ATR2 suggests that biological return to play does not equate to full performance recovery. RTP criteria following Achilles rupture in NFL athletes should incorporate performance benchmarks, not just clinical milestones.
