# Roberts, S. S. H., Urwin, C. S., Regan, J., Bowe, S. J., & Warmington, S. A. (2025). Game-day temperatures are predictive of National Football League game outcomes when teams from different climates compete against each other. Temperature. Advance online publication.

## TLDR
When NFL teams from cold-climate cities (north of 39°N) play against warm-climate teams, their win probability and point differential both decline as game-day temperature rises—even after accounting for home field advantage. Each 1°C increase in temperature reduces a northern team's odds of winning by roughly 2–3%. The effect is strongest for games played in the South, suggesting that players and staff from warmer regions may have behavioral or psychological adaptations to heat that confer a competitive advantage.

## Abstract
This observational study examined whether game-day air temperature recorded at NFL stadia predicts game outcomes when teams from cool climates (>39° N latitude, North teams, n=17) compete against teams from warm climates (<39° N, South teams, n=15). Data from 1,011 regular-season and playoff games across eight NFL seasons (2017–2025) were extracted from official NFL Gamebooks. Mixed effects logistic regression and mixed effects models tested whether temperature associated with win probability and point differential, respectively, while controlling for season year, home team ID, and end-season rank differential.

## Methods
Historical game data from eight NFL seasons (2017–2025) collected from NFL Gamebooks. Teams categorized as North (>39° N, n=17) or South (<39° N, n=15) based on home stadium latitude. Analyzed 1,011 games with reported game-day temperature. Mixed effects logistic regression modeled North team win probability per 1°C temperature increase; mixed effects models examined North team point differential per 1°C increase. Models run for all games combined and separately by game location (North vs. South). Random effects included season year, home team ID, and end-season rank differential. Humidity and game location added as covariates in follow-up models.

## Findings
North team win probability declined significantly as game-day temperature increased (OR = 0.974, 95% CI [0.951, 0.997], p = .026), representing a 2–3% reduction per 1°C rise. This association held when controlling for humidity and game location. North team point differential was negatively associated with temperature (β = -0.175, 95% CI [-0.286, -0.063], p = .002), but this became non-significant when game location was included as a covariate. By game location, North team point differential was significantly associated with temperature for games played in South locations (β = -0.152, p = .001) but not North locations (p = .318). North teams won 59% of games played in the North but only 49% of games played in the South.

## Limitations
The 39°N latitude cutoff for North/South classification is somewhat arbitrary. Data limited to eight seasons from official NFL Gamebooks. Difficult to fully control for inherent differences in team quality across games, though random effects were included. Home time-zone and circadian misalignment were not controlled. Temperature recording methodology at NFL stadiums is not publicly standardized. Observational design cannot establish causal mechanisms.

## Practical Implications
Teams based in cold-climate cities face a measurable competitive disadvantage when playing in warmer environments. Preparation strategies for warm-weather away games should include proactive heat acclimatization protocols, hydration planning (including potential IV hyperhydration), and in-game cooling strategies. Staff familiar with warmer conditions may have optimized behavioral routines that contribute to their teams' advantage. Northern teams should treat warm-weather road trips as a specific performance challenge requiring targeted environmental preparation.

## Athlete Development Notes
NFL players from northern franchises competing in warm environments may benefit from heat exposure training during the preseason or before warm-weather road trips. Psychological preparation—reducing perceptual discomfort and reinforcing confidence in warm conditions—may also be underutilized. Teams should audit their thermal management protocols for warm-weather games as a competitive edge opportunity.

## Return to Play Notes
While not directly an RTP study, findings are relevant for scheduling return-to-competition after injury: players returning to play in unfamiliar climate conditions (particularly warm environments after cold-climate training) may face additional physiological and cognitive demands. Rehabilitation and reconditioning programs should consider environmental acclimatization as part of full RTP readiness, especially for travel to warm-weather venues.
