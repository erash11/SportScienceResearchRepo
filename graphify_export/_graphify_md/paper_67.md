# Aita SL, Muchintala R, Suresh A, Patel S, Schuler B, Lichtenstein JD. Association of Use of a Mobile Tackling Dummy During College Football Practice With Reduced Sport-Related Concussion: Results of a Pilot Investigation. The Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine. 2024;12(2):23259671241228316.

## TLDR
Adding a mobile tackling dummy (MVP/Drive) to no-tackle practice culture was independently associated with significant reductions in in-season and in-game concussions at Dartmouth College and trended toward lower SRC frequency in other Ivy League teams, suggesting this technology is a promising primary concussion prevention strategy for college football.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiology pilot study investigated the association between two primary prevention strategies—no-tackle practices and mobile tackling dummy use—and sport-related concussion (SRC) frequency across 14 seasons (2007–2019, 2021) for Dartmouth College and 7 seasons (2013–2019) for seven other Ivy League football teams. SRC data were analyzed across total, preseason, in-season, in-practice, and in-game settings using correlations, generalized linear models, and basic descriptive statistics. There was a statistically significant downward trend in in-game SRCs for Dartmouth over the seasons studied (r=−0.52; P=.029). In multivariable models, mobile tackling dummy use was independently associated with lower total (β=−0.53; P=.049), in-season (β=−0.63; P=.023), and in-game (β=−0.79; P=.003) SRCs, while no-tackle practice alone did not reach significance. These findings suggest that mobile tackling dummy use during practice may be an effective primary prevention tool for reducing concussions in college football.

## Methods
Descriptive epidemiology pilot study; Dartmouth College men's football (n=14 seasons: 2007–2019 and 2021, excluding 2020 due to COVID-19) and 7 other Ivy League men's football teams (n=7 seasons: 2013–2019); SRC data from Dartmouth College Sports Medicine Department and Ivy League–Big Ten Epidemiology of Concussion Study; SRCs diagnosed by sports medicine professionals using Zurich or Berlin consensus criteria; primary prevention policies: no-tackle practice (Dartmouth from 2010; other Ivy League from 2016) and mobile tackling dummy use (Dartmouth from 2015); bivariate correlations for temporal trends; generalized linear models for independent associations of practice policies with SRC frequency; Tukey post hoc pairwise comparisons; 1-tailed P values for hypothesized directional effects (α=.05 for all).

## Findings
• Statistically significant downward trend in in-game SRCs for Dartmouth over seasons studied (r=−0.52; P=.029)
• Mobile tackling dummy independently associated with lower total SRCs (β=−0.53; P=.049), in-season SRCs (β=−0.63; P=.023), and in-game SRCs (β=−0.79; P=.003)
• No-tackle practice alone was not significantly associated with lower SRCs in any model at Dartmouth
• Post hoc comparisons: seasons with −tackling/+dummies had significantly fewer in-season (P=.032) and in-game (P=.004) SRCs vs. −tackling/−dummies
• Other Ivy League teams: no-tackle policy seasons associated with fewer total SRCs (mean 52.3 vs. 70.7; d=1.17), though not statistically significant due to limited power
• Effect sizes were robust (η²=0.24–0.51; d/g=0.83–1.99) despite limited statistical power
• Dartmouth's team performance did not worsen with adoption of no-tackle/dummy policies

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=14 seasons for Dartmouth; n=7 for other Ivy League schools) limits statistical power; observational/correlational design precludes causal inference; no data on mobile tackling dummy use from other Ivy League schools; unidentified confounders (coaching changes, roster size, referee enforcement, team culture) may influence SRC frequency; SRC underreporting is a pervasive concern in athletics; findings may not generalize beyond Ivy League to other conferences or competitive levels.

## Practical Implications
Implementing mobile tackling dummy technology (e.g., MVP/Drive by MVP Robotics) in practice—particularly in conjunction with a no-tackle practice culture—may be a meaningful primary prevention strategy for reducing concussions during games and across the season. Programs considering adoption should view this within a broader team culture emphasizing player safety. The technology appears to transfer safe tackling mechanics to game situations, reducing concussion frequency without apparent performance cost.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American college football. The mobile tackling dummy allows athletes to practice full tackling mechanics against a moving target without player-on-player contact, maintaining skill development while reducing cumulative head impact exposure during practice. Programs can integrate this tool into daily practice structures to develop safe and effective tackling technique that transfers to game performance.

## Return to Play Notes
While this study focuses on primary prevention rather than RTP, the findings are relevant for athletes managing concussion history. Programs using no-tackle and mobile dummy practices reduce overall practice-related head impact exposure, which may lower re-injury risk for athletes who have recently returned to full participation following concussion. These practice modifications represent a structural RTP consideration for high-risk athletes.
