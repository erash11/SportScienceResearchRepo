# Brown HA, Chalmers S, Topham TH, Clark B, Jowett A, Meyer T, Jay O, Périard JD. Efficacy of the FIFA cooling break heat policy during an intermittent treadmill football simulation in hot conditions in trained males. British Journal of Sports Medicine. 2024;0:1-8. doi:10.1136/bjsports-2024-108131

## TLDR
The current FIFA 3-minute cooling break policy (chilled fluid ingestion plus ice towel at 30 and 75 minutes) meaningfully reduces core temperature, heart rate, and perceived exertion during football in hot conditions. Combining the cooling breaks with a 5-minute extended half-time provides additional benefit. Simply stopping play without cooling (BRKno-cool) or extending half-time alone (ExtHTonly) provides minimal protection. For football in extreme heat (≥32°C WBGT), the FIFA cooling break protocol is an effective, low-cost strategy and should be implemented as intended — with cold fluids and ice towels, not just a rest break.

## Abstract
This randomised crossover study evaluated the efficacy of the FIFA cooling break heat policy in 12 trained males completing five 90-minute intermittent treadmill football match simulations at 40°C and 41% relative humidity (32°C WBGT). Five cooling configurations were compared: regular match (REG), 3-min breaks without cooling (BRKno-cool), 3-min breaks with cooling (BRKcool: current FIFA policy — chilled fluid and ice towel), 5-min extended half-time without breaks (ExtHTonly), and 3-min cooling breaks combined with extended half-time (ExtHTcool). Rectal temperature (Tre), heart rate, whole-body sweat rate, and RPE were recorded.

## Methods
Randomised crossover; 12 trained males (age 27±6 y, VO2peak 61±7 mL/kg/min); 5 experimental trials in 40°C±1°C, 41% RH (32°C WBGT); 10.7 km total distance across two 45-min halves; REG (no breaks), BRKno-cool (3-min break, no cooling), BRKcool (3-min break with 5 mL/kg of 13°C water and ice towel), ExtHTonly (20-min half-time, no mid-half breaks), ExtHTcool (BRKcool + 20-min half-time); outcomes: rectal temperature (telemetric capsule), heart rate, whole-body sweat rate, RPE (Borg), thermal comfort; linear mixed-effects models.

## Findings
Final Tre was lower in BRKno-cool (−0.20°C, p=0.038), BRKcool (−0.39°C, p<0.001), and ExtHTcool (−0.40°C, p<0.001) vs. REG (39.1°C). Mean Tre was lowest in ExtHTcool (38.2°C), then BRKcool (38.3°C), BRKno-cool and ExtHTonly (38.4°C), and REG (38.5°C). Mean heart rate was lower during BRKcool (−6 beats/min) and ExtHTcool (−7 beats/min) vs. REG (p<0.001). Whole-body sweat rate was similar across all trials (1.7±0.5 L/h). Fluid intake was ~645 mL higher in BRKcool and ExtHTcool vs. other conditions. RPE was lower in BRKcool (−0.4, p=0.004) and ExtHTcool (−0.5, p=0.002) vs. REG. ExtHTonly alone did not significantly reduce final Tre or RPE vs. REG.

## Limitations
Laboratory treadmill setting may underestimate real-world thermal strain due to absence of solar radiation and differences in air velocity. External workload was fixed, preventing self-regulation of effort. Fluid intake was prescribed rather than ad libitum. Study only included males; a separate female study was conducted. Results may not extend to harsher or more variable outdoor environments.

## Practical Implications
Football staff and medical personnel should ensure the FIFA cooling break protocol is implemented as designed — ice-water towels and chilled fluids are essential components, not optional accessories. Simply pausing play without cooling tools provides substantially less benefit. Extending half-time by 5 minutes in combination with cooling breaks provides the greatest thermal and cardiovascular protection and should be adopted when logistically feasible in hot match conditions (≥32°C WBGT). Adequate cold fluid availability (~1.2 L per player) should be planned at each cooling break station.

## Athlete Development Notes
For preseason or training in hot environments, implementing brief cooling breaks with cold fluids and ice towels during high-intensity sessions can reduce cumulative thermal load and attenuate perceived exertion, potentially allowing for better training quality and session completion. Heat acclimatisation combined with structured cooling breaks may be the most effective combination for developing athletes training in warm climates.

## Return to Play Notes
Players returning from heat-related illness or those with known elevated heat strain risk (e.g., post-exertional heat stroke) should have structured cooling breaks incorporated into their RTP training progression. The cooling break strategy tested in this study reduced both core temperature and cardiovascular strain without altering sweat rate, making it safe and practical for monitored progressive exposure to exercise in the heat during rehabilitation.
