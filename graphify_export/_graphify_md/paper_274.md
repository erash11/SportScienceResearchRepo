# Lonie TA, Brade CJ, Finucane ME, Jacques A, Grisbrook TL. Hip adduction and abduction strength and adduction-to-abduction ratio changes across an Australian Football League season. J Sci Med Sport. 2020;23(1):2-6.

## TLDR
Hip strength is not static across an AFL season — it rises meaningfully from pre-season to mid-season and drops post-season. Pre-season screening captures only a single snapshot. Because the preferred kicking leg develops a lower adduction-to-abduction ratio by mid-season (even in uninjured players), using the contralateral limb as a return-to-play reference requires caution. Regular in-season testing better reflects true player conditioning than a single pre-season measurement.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional repeated-measures study examined changes in maximal isometric hip adduction and abduction strength, and the adduction-to-abduction ratio, in 38 uninjured elite Australian Football (AF) players at four time points across a season: start of pre-season (T1), end of pre-season (T2), mid-season (T3), and post-season (T4). Strength was measured using a hand-held dynamometer with external belt fixation. Hip adduction strength and the adduction-to-abduction ratio were significantly greater at T3 compared to T1 (adduction by 22.71 N, p<0.001; ratio by 0.15, p<0.001), while both adduction and abduction were significantly weaker at T4 compared to T1. No overall difference between preferred and non-preferred legs in adduction or abduction strength was found; however, at T3 and T4 the adduction-to-abduction ratio of the preferred kicking leg was significantly lower than the non-preferred leg. Training load measured via session-RPE was not significantly associated with hip strength changes.

## Methods
Cross-sectional repeated measures. 38 uninjured elite AFL players (one club, 2016 playing list). Maximal isometric hip adduction and abduction strength measured at four time points (T1: November 2015 pre-season start; T2: February 2016 pre-season end; T3: June 2016 mid-season; T4: August 2016 post-season) using a hand-held dynamometer (HHD; PowerTrack II Commander, JTECH Medical) with external belt fixation following Thorborg et al. protocol. Three 5-second maximal contractions performed; highest value used. Adduction-to-abduction ratio calculated from peak scores. Training load quantified via session-RPE across three periods. Adjusted linear mixed regression models with player characteristics (training load, age, body mass, years of experience, games played) as covariates analysed changes over the season.

## Findings
Hip adduction strength was significantly greater at T3 vs T1 (+22.71 N, p<0.001) and significantly weaker at T4 vs T1 (-18.6 N, p=0.004). Hip abduction was significantly weaker at T4 vs T1 (-24.67 N, p<0.001). No significant difference between preferred and non-preferred legs overall in adduction (p=0.409) or abduction (p=0.602). A significant interaction between leg and time existed for the adduction-to-abduction ratio: at T3 the preferred kicking leg ratio was 0.14 lower (p=0.020) and at T4 it was 0.15 lower (p=0.019) than the non-preferred leg. Training load was not associated with strength changes. Body mass was associated with abduction strength (+0.989 N/kg, p=0.008) and inversely with the adduction-to-abduction ratio.

## Limitations
Single club sample limits generalisability. Post-season effort may have been reduced anecdotally. Training load was quantified via session-RPE only, which may not capture asymmetric sport-specific loading (e.g., kicking volume). The study cannot determine whether strength changes were driven by training adaptation, residual effects of previous injuries, or technique variation. No game-level GPS or kick-count data were collected to quantify limb-specific loading.

## Practical Implications
A single pre-season hip strength screen does not accurately represent a player's condition throughout the season; regular in-season testing at multiple time points is recommended. The preferred kicking leg shows a lower adduction-to-abduction ratio by mid- and post-season even in uninjured players, meaning the uninjured limb may not be a reliable reference for return-to-play decisions following a unilateral groin strain. Session-RPE-based training load monitoring alone is insufficient to explain individual hip strength variation.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athlete development, this study underscores the need to monitor hip adduction and abduction strength longitudinally rather than relying solely on pre-season data. Strength and conditioning programming should account for the natural in-season rise in adductor strength and the asymmetries that develop in kicking-dominant athletes over a season. Incorporating bilateral hip strength testing at multiple seasonal phases supports more targeted loading and conditioning interventions.

## Return to Play Notes
When making return-to-play decisions following a unilateral groin strain, practitioners should be cautious about using the contralateral (non-injured) limb as the gold-standard comparator, as even uninjured players demonstrate significantly lower adduction-to-abduction ratios in the preferred kicking leg by mid- and post-season. Establishing player-specific in-season baselines provides more clinically meaningful RTP benchmarks than a single pre-season measurement.
