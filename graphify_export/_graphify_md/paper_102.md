# Kepir E, Owen JA, Karatay GM, Car B, Akaras E, Yildirim NU, Yagiz G. Changes in eccentric hamstring strength and muscle soreness in younger and older male football players following match play. BMC Sports Science, Medicine and Rehabilitation. 2025;17:319. https://doi.org/10.1186/s13102-025-01336-7

## TLDR
Older professional football players (23+ years) lose meaningful eccentric hamstring strength after approximately 44 minutes of match play while younger players do not, confirming that age is an independent predictor of post-match eccentric strength decline. This is directly relevant to football injury prevention: reduced post-match eccentric hamstring strength in veteran players creates an acute injury risk window in late-game situations and subsequent training sessions. Strength and conditioning coaches should implement age-differentiated eccentric hamstring monitoring and loading programs, prioritizing recovery for older players after matches and incorporating regular eccentric strength assessments as a readiness marker across multi-game weeks.

## Abstract
This study investigated post-match changes in maximal eccentric hamstring strength and perceived muscle soreness in younger (n=11, mean age 20.2±1.1 years) and older (n=10, mean age 27.4±3.7 years) professional male football players using a 2×2 repeated measures design. Eccentric hamstring strength was measured via handheld dynamometer and perceived soreness via a 0-100 analogue scale before and after a pre-season friendly match. The older group experienced significant moderate declines in both absolute (−20 N, g=0.58, p=0.027) and relative (−0.26 N/kg, g=0.43, p=0.038) eccentric hamstring strength post-match, while the younger group showed no significant changes. Both groups reported significantly increased soreness post-match, with younger players showing greater relative soreness increases (p=0.025). A significant negative association existed between age and eccentric strength change (R=−0.447, R²=0.20, p=0.007).

## Methods
2×2 repeated measures design with two groups: younger (n=11, age 20.2±1.1 years, 11±3.5 total years playing) and older (n=10, age 27.4±3.7 years, 15.5±3.1 total years playing) professional male football players registered with the Turkish Football Federation. Intervention was a 90-minute pre-season competitive friendly match (data collected September 2023). Maximal eccentric hamstring strength measured via Lafayette Manual Muscle Test System handheld dynamometer (Model 01165) at 60° knee flexion in prone position, before and within 3 minutes post-match. Perceived soreness assessed via 0-100 analogue scale for dominant and non-dominant legs. Baseline established one week prior to the match. ICC for strength reproducibility = 0.888 (95% CI: 0.771-0.946). Two-way mixed model ANOVA with Bonferroni correction; Hedges' adjusted g effect sizes; IBM SPSS 29.0.

## Findings
Older group: significant post-match absolute eccentric strength reduction (−20 N±38.5, g=0.58, p=0.027) and relative strength reduction (−0.26 N/kg±0.5, g=0.43, p=0.038). Younger group: no significant absolute (3 N±36.1, g=0.07, p=0.773) or relative (−0.037 N/kg±0.54, p=0.783) strength change. No significant time × group interaction for eccentric strength (p=0.195, g=0.45). Both groups showed significantly increased soreness post-match (older: +29%±14.1, p<0.001; younger: +40%±14.9, p<0.001). Younger players reported significantly greater relative soreness increases than older players (difference 11%±17.2, p=0.025). Significant negative association between age and strength change: R=−0.447, R²=0.20, p=0.007.

## Limitations
Players did not play a full 90-minute match due to pre-season substitution protocol (younger: 34.5±8.8 min; older: 44±10.5 min), potentially underestimating full-match strength deficits. No half-time assessments were conducted. Small sample size (n=21) limits statistical power and generalizability to the broader football population. Study was conducted during the first 2 weeks of pre-season, which may reflect relative deconditioning. Running distances and workload metrics were not recorded. Playing positions and previous injury history were not homogeneous between groups. Goalkeeper inclusion (one per group) introduced positional heterogeneity.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football strength staff should implement age-stratified eccentric hamstring monitoring, particularly for players over 23 years old who demonstrate meaningful post-match eccentric strength deficits. Recovery protocols for older players should include post-game eccentric hamstring assessment, extended recovery windows before high-speed running exposure in subsequent practices, and proactive eccentric loading (Nordic hamstring curls, Askling L protocol, Russian Belt, single-leg hamstring bridge) to maintain protective strength capacity across multi-game weeks. Younger players' higher perceived soreness despite maintained objective strength suggests they may benefit from targeted soft tissue recovery interventions even when dynamometer readings are preserved.

## Athlete Development Notes
Football development programs should incorporate eccentric hamstring strength training from the earliest stages of a player's career to build a strong protective reserve before age-related vulnerability emerges. Younger players (under 23) appear to maintain eccentric hamstring strength through match demands, suggesting their neuromuscular systems are more resilient to fatigue. However, the trajectory of increasing age-related vulnerability demonstrated by R=−0.447 underscores the importance of building eccentric hamstring strength capacity well before players enter the higher-risk age bracket. Progressive eccentric training using the Nordic hamstring protocol and single-leg hamstring bridge should be systematically embedded in all developmental football programs.

## Return to Play Notes
The significant post-match eccentric hamstring strength decline in older players represents a clinically meaningful acute injury risk window. Return-to-play criteria following hamstring injury should include eccentric hamstring strength testing (handheld dynamometer at 60° knee flexion) demonstrating recovery to pre-injury baseline values. More conservative RTP timelines and higher eccentric strength clearance thresholds are warranted for players over 23 years old. During multi-game periods, monitoring eccentric strength at the start of each week can identify players whose residual strength deficit has not fully resolved between games, enabling targeted intervention before the next match to mitigate re-injury risk.
