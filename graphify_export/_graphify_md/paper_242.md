# Foster, G., O'Reilly, N., Devereux, J. B., & Shundi, M. (2023). General manager and head coach exits in the NBA and the NFL. Sport, Business and Management: An International Journal. Advance online publication.

## TLDR
In professional football and basketball, coaches are fired primarily based on losing records, while GMs are dismissed for a more complex mix of reasons beyond wins and losses. When one key executive leaves, the other is significantly more likely to follow—the 'cleaning house' effect is statistically real. Perhaps most practically, a club's past culture of firing coaches and GMs is the strongest predictor of future exits, meaning organizational instability tends to perpetuate itself and should be a key consideration for any executive evaluating a job offer.

## Abstract
This study investigated why head coaches and general managers (GMs) exit their positions in the NBA and NFL over a 30-year period. Using 891 GM and coach annual observations from NBA clubs (1988–2018) and 949 observations from NFL clubs (1987–2017), three hypotheses grounded in agency theory, upper-echelon theory, and institutional logic theory were examined. Methods included logit analysis for exit/retention modeling and latent Dirichlet allocation (LDA) topic modeling of qualitative press quotations. Findings show that simultaneous GM and coach exits are strongly correlated, win-loss record is more predictive of coach exits than GM exits, and past organizational culture of turnover strongly predicts future exits.

## Methods
Quantitative and qualitative analysis using 30 years of data. Logit analysis modeled the probability of GM and coach exit (binary: start vs. not start following season with same club) as a function of win-loss record, playoff progression, simultaneous exit of the other role, and historical turnover frequency. Separate models run for NBA, NFL, and pooled samples. Qualitative analysis used Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling on a database of press quotations compiled from Sports Business Daily, Bleacher Report, and ESPN.com for all GM and coach exit events. Two topics emerged from LDA: 'Win-Loss Record' and 'Beyond Win-Loss Record.'

## Findings
Hypothesis 1 (partially supported): Win-loss record significantly predicts coach exits in both NBA and NFL, but is much weaker as a predictor for GM exits. Hypothesis 2 (supported): Simultaneous exits of GMs and coaches are significantly correlated—a coach exit significantly increases the probability of a GM exit and vice versa (the 'cleaning house' effect; P < .001 across models). Hypothesis 3 (supported): Past organizational turnover frequency is strongly negatively associated with retention—clubs with high historical exit rates continue to exhibit high exit rates (P < .001). NFL GM mean tenure was 5.27 seasons; NFL coach mean tenure was 4.14 seasons. Annual exit rates averaged 16.0% for NFL GMs and 21.3% for NFL coaches.

## Limitations
Club ownership and ownership changes—a likely key driver of GM and coach exits—could not be fully modeled due to data limitations. The exit/retention classification is binary and may not capture nuanced departures (voluntary vs. involuntary). Press quotation databases are sparse for early years of the study period due to limited online content. The 'Beyond Win-Loss' LDA topic is heterogeneous, reflecting the complex and varied nature of non-performance-related GM exits. Causal inference is not possible from this observational design.

## Practical Implications
Organizations with a high historical rate of GM and coach turnover are likely to continue that pattern, creating instability that affects player recruitment, team culture, and long-term performance. Executives and coaches evaluating new opportunities should assess an organization's historical turnover culture as a key contractual risk factor. Clubs should consider that cleaning house rarely addresses the root cause (often ownership behavior) and may perpetuate dysfunction. Win-loss record remains the primary lever for head coach retention in both leagues.

## Athlete Development Notes
Organizational leadership stability is an underappreciated factor in athlete development. Frequent GM and coach turnover disrupts player development pipelines, scouting philosophies, and long-term roster building. For football programs, evaluating the historical turnover culture of an organization before committing long-term is critical for players, agents, and incoming coaches seeking environments conducive to sustained development.

## Return to Play Notes
This study does not directly address return-to-play from injury. However, organizational instability caused by frequent coaching and GM turnover can disrupt sports medicine infrastructure, rehabilitation protocols, and player health management continuity. Stable leadership environments are likely more conducive to consistent, evidence-based RTP decision-making.
