# Tribolet, R., Sheehan, W. B., Novak, A. R., Watsford, M. L., & Fransen, J. (2022). How does practice change across the season? A descriptive study of the training structures and practice activities implemented by a professional Australian football team. International Journal of Sports Science & Coaching, 17(1), 63–72.

## TLDR
Professional AFL coaches shift practice structure across the season, emphasizing more training-form (fundamentals, fitness, skills) activities pre-season and transitioning toward more playing-form (match simulation, line-specific) activities in-season. The overall split of training-form to playing-form remained roughly equal across the whole season (51%/49%), but the type composition changed meaningfully. Skill practice dominated both phases. This data-driven view of coaching practice gives sport scientists a framework for understanding where and how to advocate for more representative learning design throughout the season.

## Abstract
This observational study examined practice activities used by eight professional coaches across 72 training sessions in one season within a professional Australian Football League (AFL) team. Coaches implemented seven practice types categorized as training-form (fundamentals, fitness, skills) and playing-form (breakdown, transition, line-specific, match simulation). Practice type frequencies differed significantly between pre- and in-season (χ²=109.6, p<0.001). In-season activities had longer durations and coaches implemented more playing-form practice in-season (62.0±25.4%) versus pre-season (52.4±19.8%). Training-form practice frequency was higher pre-season, while playing-form frequency was similar across phases. Coaches altered the frequency of practice types more than exposure time.

## Methods
Observational study of one professional AFL club (48 male players). Data collected across 72 practice sessions (37 pre-season, 35 in-season) over one full season. Two cameras filmed each session; a single experienced observer coded time spent in each of seven coach-defined practice activity types. Practice types were reclassified by researchers as training-form or playing-form per Ford et al. frameworks. Chi-square tests of independence, paired samples t-tests, and effect size statistics (Cohen's d) were used. Intra-class correlation coefficients assessed coding reliability (r=0.97–0.99).

## Findings
Practice type frequencies differed significantly between pre- and in-season (χ²=109.6, p<0.001). The largest pre-season frequencies were skill (24.2%), fundamentals (17.5%), and fitness (15.2%); in-season leaders were skill (35.1%), match simulation (24.3%), and line-specific (19.5%). Fitness dropped to zero in-season. Practice duration was significantly longer in-season (8.0±0.4 vs 7.0±0.8 min, p=0.023, d=−0.17). Coaches altered training-form frequency significantly more than playing-form frequency between phases (χ²=30.8, p<0.001). The percentage of session time in playing-form activities was not significantly different between phases (in-season 62.0±25.4% vs pre-season 52.4±19.8%, p=0.112).

## Limitations
Data collected from a single professional AFL team, limiting generalizability to other teams or sports. Results are preliminary and rely on descriptive analysis. Variation in playing-form activities between pre- and in-season may reflect load management or scheduling constraints rather than intentional skill acquisition periodization. No individual player data were collected. Observer annotation was at the team rather than player level.

## Practical Implications
Sport scientists and skill acquisition specialists can use this framework to objectively monitor training practice types and their exposures throughout the season. Quantifying training-form versus playing-form proportions can highlight opportunities to increase representative learning design. The finding that coaches naturally shift toward more game-specific activities in-season aligns with skill acquisition principles, but the persistently high proportion of training-form activities (especially isolated skill drills) across both phases suggests room to advocate for more perceptually rich, coupled practice environments.

## Athlete Development Notes
AFL-context findings translate directly to American football development: coaches should progressively increase playing-form (scrimmage-style, situational) practice as competition approaches. Tracking the proportion of isolated drills versus representative game-play activities during training camp and regular season provides an objective coaching audit tool. Pre-season emphasis on fundamentals and fitness is appropriate, but integrating more small-sided and match-simulation activities earlier may accelerate perceptual-motor skill transfer.

## Return to Play Notes
Not directly applicable to return-to-play protocols. However, the classification framework (training-form vs. playing-form) could be applied to RTP progressions: athletes returning from injury should advance from isolated rehabilitation exercises (training-form analog) toward fully representative, contact-level match simulation (playing-form analog) in a structured, monitored sequence.
