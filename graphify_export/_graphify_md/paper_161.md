# Silva DS, Boullosa D, Pereira EVM, Alves MDJ, Fernandes MSS, Aidar FJ, dos Santos LF, de Souza RF. Does the sequence of plyometric and dynamic stretching exercises influence subsequent sprint performance? A randomized crossover intervention study. Biol Sport. 2024;41(2):13–18. https://doi.org/10.5114/biolsport.2024.129485

## TLDR
For sports performance coaches using warm-up potentiation strategies, the order of dynamic stretching and plyometric exercises matters: performing dynamic stretching before drop jumps (DS+DJ) improved final sprint acceleration (20–40 m) by approximately 0.12 m/s, while reversing the order (DJ+DS) actually impaired initial acceleration. Athletes with lower baseline lower-limb power (lower CMJ height) benefited the most from the DS+DJ sequence, suggesting this strategy is especially valuable for less powerful athletes. This simple, equipment-free protocol is directly applicable in practice and competitive settings.

## Abstract
This randomized crossover study examined the acute effects of exercise sequence — dynamic stretching followed by drop jumps (DS+DJ) versus drop jumps followed by dynamic stretching (DJ+DS) — on sprint performance in 13 competitive male track-and-field athletes (sprinters and jumpers). Athletes completed a standardized warm-up and then one of three conditions (DS+DJ, DJ+DS, or control) before performing 40-m sprints analyzed in 0–20 m and 20–40 m phases. The DS+DJ sequence improved final acceleration (20–40 m) speed compared to baseline, while DJ+DS worsened initial acceleration (0–20 m), and neither sequence improved overall 40-m performance.

## Methods
Randomized crossover design with 13 competitive male sprint and jump athletes (19±2 years; CMJ height 39.0±3.0 cm; 100-m PB 11.4±0.6 s). Five sessions over 3 weeks: 2 familiarization, 3 experimental (72 hours apart). Conditions: DS+DJ (2 dynamic stretches × 15 reps/leg + 3 drop jumps at optimal height), DJ+DS (reverse order), and control. Sprint performance assessed via MySprint app at 240 fps over 40 m with split times at 0–20 m and 20–40 m. CMJ assessed via Chronojump contact mat. Two-way repeated-measures ANOVA with Bonferroni post-hoc; Pearson correlation between CMJ and PAPE response.

## Findings
No significant effect of any condition on overall 40-m sprint performance. The DS+DJ condition significantly improved 20–40 m velocity vs. baseline (8.91±0.35 vs. 8.79±0.43 m/s; p=0.015). The DJ+DS condition significantly worsened 0–20 m velocity vs. baseline (6.22±0.26 vs. 6.26±0.25 m/s; p=0.002). A significant negative correlation was found between CMJ height and the percentage improvement in final acceleration in the DS+DJ condition (r=-0.741; p=0.004), indicating athletes with lower power benefited most.

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=13 after exclusions from an initial 16). All participants were competitive track-and-field athletes (sprinters and jumpers), limiting generalizability to team sport athletes. Only two dynamic stretching exercises and three drop jumps were used; results may differ with different exercise volumes or types. The study did not measure neuromuscular mechanisms (EMG, force-velocity characteristics) to explain the observed sequence effects.

## Practical Implications
Football coaches and sports performance staff can use DS followed by DJ (DS+DJ) as a simple, equipment-free warm-up potentiation strategy to enhance final-phase sprint acceleration. This is particularly relevant for skill positions requiring top-end speed after an initial acceleration phase. Athletes with lower lower-limb power levels benefit most, making this strategy valuable for developing players. The reverse sequence (DJ then DS) should be avoided as it can impair initial acceleration, which is critical in football contexts.

## Athlete Development Notes
Implementing DS+DJ sequencing in warm-up protocols can serve as a development tool for football skill positions (WR, DB, RB) requiring explosive acceleration in the 20–40 m range. Athletes with lower CMJ heights — often younger or less trained players — show the greatest performance enhancement, suggesting this PAPE strategy is especially valuable during developmental stages. Coaches should individualize potentiation warm-up strategies based on assessed lower-limb power levels.

## Return to Play Notes
This study does not directly address return-to-play populations. However, the emphasis on exercise sequencing during warm-up may be relevant for athletes returning from lower-limb injuries who are reintegrating sprint-based activities. Caution is warranted: the DJ+DS sequence impaired initial acceleration, which could stress healing tissue in RTP athletes; DS+DJ may represent a safer potentiation strategy for athletes in the later stages of return-to-activity progressions.
