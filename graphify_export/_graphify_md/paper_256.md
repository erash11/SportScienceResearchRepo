# Ukaj, N., Meyer, M., & Egger, F. (2024). Has COVID-19 led to more sudden cardiac deaths in football? Clinical Research in Cardiology, 113, 1429–1436. https://doi.org/10.1007/s00392-024-02569-1

## TLDR
Despite widespread concern that COVID-19 myocarditis could increase athlete cardiac mortality, this FIFA registry analysis found no statistically significant increase in sudden cardiac deaths or arrests among football players during the pandemic period compared to pre-pandemic rates. The feared COVID-19-related SCD epidemic in athletes did not materialize in this global dataset.

## Abstract
This study examined whether the COVID-19 pandemic was associated with an increase in sudden cardiac death (SCD) or sudden cardiac arrest (SCA) among football (soccer) players using the FIFA Sudden Death Registry. A 1,151-day pre-pandemic reference period was compared to a matched pandemic period. A total of 454 SCD/SCA events were recorded pre-pandemic versus 380 during the pandemic period. Statistical analysis revealed no significant increase in overall SCD/SCA incidence (p = 0.27). Myocarditis-related events were similarly not elevated (6 pre-pandemic vs. 1 pandemic). The authors conclude that COVID-19 did not lead to a measurable increase in sudden cardiac death among registered football players.

## Methods
Retrospective registry analysis using the FIFA Sudden Death Registry. Comparison of 1,151-day pre-pandemic period to a matched pandemic period. Outcomes: SCD (fatal) and SCA (non-fatal cardiac arrest). Myocarditis-related events analyzed as a sub-category. Incidence rate comparison using Poisson statistics. No control for exposure hours or match-play volume changes during pandemic.

## Findings
Pre-pandemic SCD/SCA: 454 events. Pandemic SCD/SCA: 380 events. No statistically significant difference in overall incidence (p = 0.27). Myocarditis-specific events: 6 pre-pandemic vs. 1 during pandemic (not significantly elevated). No evidence of a COVID-19-driven surge in athlete SCD.

## Limitations
Registry completeness is dependent on voluntary reporting, which may introduce ascertainment bias. Reduction in match-play and training volume during early pandemic phases could artificially suppress incidence. Myocarditis sub-group was very small (n = 7 total across both periods), limiting statistical power for that comparison. No individual-level COVID-19 infection status was available. Does not capture non-fatal cardiac events not classified as SCA.

## Practical Implications
Sports medicine programs should not alter standard cardiac screening protocols specifically in response to COVID-19 history without individualized clinical indication. The population-level registry data do not support a policy of universal post-COVID cardiac restriction for football players. Individualized assessment remains the standard of care for athletes with documented myocarditis or significant COVID-19 illness.

## Athlete Development Notes
For American football programs, this study provides reassurance that returning athletes with mild-to-moderate COVID-19 illness are not at demonstrably elevated population-level SCD risk. Standard pre-participation cardiac screening (ECG, echocardiogram where indicated) remains appropriate. Athletes with documented myocarditis should follow established RTP cardiac protocols regardless of this aggregate finding.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes with a history of COVID-19 and documented myocarditis should still be withheld from competition until cardiology clearance is obtained and inflammatory markers have normalized—typically following ACC/AHA and sports cardiology consensus guidelines (3–6 month restriction for confirmed myocarditis). This study provides no basis for bypassing individualized cardiac evaluation; rather, it contextualizes population-level risk as not elevated beyond pre-pandemic baselines.
