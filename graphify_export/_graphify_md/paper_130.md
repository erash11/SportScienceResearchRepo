# Brett BL, Kerr ZY, Aggarwal NT, Chandran A, Mannix R, Walton S, DeFreese JD, Echemendia RJ, Guskiewicz KM, McCrea MA, Meehan WP III. Cumulative Concussion and Odds of Stroke in Former National Football League Players. Stroke. 2022;53. DOI: 10.1161/STROKEAHA.121.035607

## TLDR
Former NFL players as a group have a lower stroke prevalence than the general US male population — likely due to lifetime physical fitness benefits — but those with 10 or more career concussions face over 5 times the odds of stroke compared to those with no concussion history. This threshold effect indicates cumulative brain trauma is an independent stroke risk factor. For football programs, this highlights the long-term neurological stakes of repeated head impacts and the critical importance of rigorous concussion management, cardiovascular health monitoring, and proactive medical follow-up for players with extensive concussion histories.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study examined the relationship between cumulative concussion history and stroke prevalence in 979 former NFL players aged 50 years and older. Participants were categorized into five concussion history groups (0, 1-2, 3-5, 6-9, 10+) and stroke history was self-reported via physician diagnosis. Indirect standardization against US male population data revealed that overall stroke prevalence was significantly lower in former NFL players than the general population (3.4% vs. expected 6.1%), but those with 10 or more prior concussions had 5.5 times the adjusted odds of stroke compared to those with no concussion history after controlling for age and cardiovascular disease. The findings suggest a possible threshold effect of cumulative concussion burden on cerebrovascular risk.

## Methods
Cross-sectional observational study following STROBE guidelines. Participants were 979 former NFL players aged ≥50 years (mean age 65.0±9.0 years, range 50-99) who played ≥1 year in the NFL, recruited via team and organizational communications. Self-reported lifetime concussion history was binned into five categories: 0 (n=119; 12.2%), 1-2 (n=152; 15.5%), 3-5 (n=242; 24.7%), 6-9 (n=201; 20.5%), and 10+ (n=265; 27.1%). Stroke history was self-reported via physician or healthcare professional diagnosis. Indirect standardization used 2018/19 National Health Interview Survey data for US men by decade (50-59, 60-69, 70-79, 80+) to calculate standardized prevalence ratios. Logistic regression with Firth's bias reduction method estimated adjusted odds ratios for stroke, controlling for age, coronary artery disease/myocardial infarction, race, and cardiovascular risk factor burden.

## Findings
Stroke prevalence was 3.4% (n=33) among former NFL players, significantly lower than expected based on US male population rates (standardized prevalence ratio=0.56, Z=-4.56, p<0.001). Former players with 10+ prior concussions had significantly greater odds of stroke compared to those with 0 concussions (aOR=5.51, 95% CI: 1.61-28.95). Older age (aOR=1.07 per year, 95% CI: 1.02-1.11) and cardiovascular disease/MI (aOR=2.24, 95% CI: 1.01-4.77) were also independently associated with stroke. No dose-response association was observed for lower concussion categories (1-2, 3-5, 6-9), suggesting a possible threshold effect at 10+ concussions. Race and cardiovascular disease risk factor burden were not significant covariates in the final model.

## Limitations
Cross-sectional design precludes causal inference between concussion history and stroke. Medical conditions including concussion history were self-reported, which may introduce recall bias, though this bias would not be expected to differ systematically between stroke and non-stroke groups. Lower overall stroke prevalence may reflect response bias (severe stroke survivors less likely to respond to a survey) or the protective effects of lifelong physical activity. The low event rate (n=33 strokes) limited statistical power to detect associations in lower concussion categories. Findings may not generalize to athletes competing at lower contact sport levels.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football medical and performance staff should recognize that players with a history of 10 or more concussions face substantially elevated long-term stroke risk independent of age and cardiovascular disease. This underscores the importance of rigorous concussion tracking and management at all levels of play. Post-career medical programs for former players should include cerebrovascular and cardiovascular health screening, particularly for those with extensive concussion histories. Active players with high cumulative concussion counts warrant enhanced monitoring and potentially more conservative return-to-play protocols. Proactive management of modifiable cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, hyperlipidemia, diabetes, sleep apnea) is especially important in this population.

## Athlete Development Notes
For athlete development staff, these findings reinforce that concussion management is not just a short-term performance issue but has profound long-term health consequences extending decades beyond playing careers. Education for athletes at all developmental levels about the cumulative risks of repeated head impacts — and creating a culture that encourages accurate concussion reporting — is essential. Developing robust baseline concussion tracking systems and lifetime concussion logs from youth through professional levels would improve the ability to identify at-risk individuals early and implement protective health strategies.

## Return to Play Notes
This study provides critical context for return-to-play decision-making: players with a high cumulative concussion burden (10+) represent a subgroup with substantially elevated long-term neurological risk. While the study does not directly address acute RTP timelines, the findings argue for more conservative and individualized RTP protocols for players with extensive concussion histories. Medical staff should factor cumulative concussion load — not just the current injury — into clearance decisions. Enhanced neurological monitoring and cardiovascular health assessment should be integrated into long-term RTP and career management protocols for high-concussion-history athletes.
