# Sancese A, Taylor L, Walsh G, Byrd E, Delextrat A. Effects of sprint versus strength training on risk factors for hamstring injury in football players. Journal of Sports Medicine and Physical Fitness. 2023;63(4):580-7. doi:10.23736/S0022-4707.22.14529-9

## TLDR
Both 4-week in-season sprint training and Nordic hamstring exercise programs improved key hamstring injury risk factors, but through different mechanisms. Both groups showed increased eccentric peak torque at 60°/s and a protective rightward shift in the angle of peak torque toward knee extension. The NHE group uniquely improved eccentric strength at faster velocities (180°/s), while the sprint group uniquely improved rate of torque development — the ability to quickly generate force — which may be particularly protective during the late swing phase of sprinting. This supports a personalised approach: players with low RTD may benefit most from sprint training, while those with low eccentric strength may benefit more from NHE.

## Abstract
This randomised controlled trial compared 4 weeks of in-season sprint training (5–6×30–40 m, twice weekly) versus Nordic hamstring exercise (NHE) training on a battery of modifiable hamstring strain injury (HSI) risk factors in 18 male university football players. Isokinetic dynamometry assessed eccentric hamstring peak torque, the functional H:Q ratio, angle of peak torque, rate of torque development, and hamstring torque at near-extension angles. Sprint mechanics (30 m time, F0, V0, Pmax, RFpeak) were also assessed pre- and post-intervention.

## Methods
Randomised controlled trial; 18 male university football players (age 20.9±2.5 y, 75.8±9.1 kg); sprint group (n=9) performed 5–6×30–40 m twice weekly; NHE group (n=7 after dropouts) performed 2×5 to 3×6 reps twice weekly; both interventions added at end of regular practice; 4-week in-season intervention; isokinetic dynamometry (Biodex) at 60°/s and 180°/s for Hecc, Qcon, Hecc:Qcon, APT, RTD200, torque at 30°/20°/10°; sprint mechanics via MySprint app (F0, V0, Pmax, RFpeak); mixed-design ANOVA with repeated measures.

## Findings
Both groups showed significant increases in Hecc at 60°/s (+8–9.9%; p<0.05) and rightward shifts in APT at 60°/s (−32–36%; p<0.05). Torque at 10° improved in both legs (+21–31%; p<0.05) and at 20° in the dominant leg (+15%; p<0.05) in both groups. Only the NHE group showed a significant increase in Hecc at 180°/s (+24.5%; p=0.035, d=0.89). Only the sprint group showed a significant improvement in RTD200 of the dominant leg at 60°/s (+29.4%; p=0.004, d=1.01). No significant changes in sprint performance (30-m time) or sprint mechanics (F0, V0, Pmax, RFpeak) were found in either group.

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=18) may have underpowered secondary outcomes. Post-testing in December coincided with high physical fatigue and academic pressure that may have limited adaptations. Potential confounders (hamstring flexibility, pelvic tilt) were not measured. Sprint training was unloaded, which may explain lack of improvements in sprint mechanics; resisted sprint training may be needed to improve F0.

## Practical Implications
Both sprint training and NHE are practical, low-resource in-season options for reducing hamstring strain injury risk in football players. A personalised approach is recommended: use sprint training for players with low RTD (rapid force development deficits) and NHE for those with low peak eccentric hamstring strength. The concurrent shift in angle of peak torque toward knee extension with both modalities supports their use in protective conditioning programs at any level of the sport.

## Athlete Development Notes
In-season hamstring injury prevention for football players should incorporate either sprint training or NHE based on individual risk factor profiling. Sprint training added post-practice twice weekly for 4 weeks can meaningfully improve RTD and shift the APT toward protective extended angles without requiring equipment. These programs are accessible for sub-elite and collegiate football programmes where performance facilities may be limited.

## Return to Play Notes
Following hamstring strain injury, the choice between NHE and sprint training during the later phases of return to play should be guided by the player's residual deficits. Players with reduced eccentric peak torque (particularly at faster velocities) may prioritise NHE, while those with deficits in rate of torque development — common in previously strained hamstrings — may benefit more from progressive sprint training. Both interventions shifted the APT toward extended positions relevant to the injury mechanism during sprinting.
