# Aita SL, Holding EZ, Bolander RP, Marshall K, Oberg C, Schuler B, Derosier M, Teevens EF III, Lichtenstein JD. Associations of Tackling Characteristics, Player Position, and Head Contact Risk During Game Play in College Football. Journal of Athletic Training. 2025. doi:10.4085/1062-6050-0669.24

## TLDR
Specific tackling techniques (especially neck flexion, front contact, blowup, and cut tackles) dramatically increase head contact risk, while the Dartmouth team—which banned player-on-player tackling in practice since 2010—used significantly fewer high-risk techniques than other Ivy League teams, suggesting that practice policy changes can reduce dangerous tackle mechanics in games.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study examined associations between tackling technique and head contact risk in Ivy League college football during the 2016 season, and compared tackle technique base rates between one team with a longstanding no-tackle practice policy (Dartmouth) and the rest of the league. A total of 3,701 tackles from 237 defensive players across the eight Ivy League teams were coded via video analysis for neck position, contact area, strike zone, tackle momentum, and tackle type. Binary logistic regression models were used to calculate odds ratios for head contact. The team with the no-tackle practice policy showed reduced use of high-risk tackling techniques relative to other Ivy League teams.

## Methods
Cross-sectional video analysis of all 2016 Ivy League Conference football games. Tackles (N=3,701) from 237 defensive players across 8 teams were coded using DV Sport video analysis software from two vantage points (press box and end zone). Tackle characteristics coded included: neck position (neutral, flexion, extension), contact area (side, front, back), strike zone (high, low, medium), tackle momentum (attack, blocked, multiple, follow-through, gathering, pursuing, QB sack), and tackle type (blowup, cut, follow-through, roll, swipe, wrap). Thirteen unique tackle combinations representing 93% of all tackles were analyzed. Binary logistic regression and chi-squared tests used; p-values adjusted with Benjamini-Hochberg FDR method.

## Findings
- Head contact occurred on 28.1% of all tackles (N=3,701)
- Low-risk tackle characteristics: neutral neck position (OR=0.1), back contact area (OR=0.3), pursuing momentum (OR=0.5), QB sack momentum (OR=0.3)
- High-risk tackle characteristics: flexion neck position (OR=14.2), extension neck position (OR=3.8), front contact area (OR=2.2), blowup tackle type (OR=2.5), cut tackle type (OR=3.0)
- High-risk tackle combinations: low-side-flexion (OR=4.9), low-front-flexion (OR=9.9), medium-side-flexion (OR=15.5), medium-front-flexion (OR=11.4)
- Dartmouth (no-tackle practice policy) showed lower odds of high-risk techniques vs. other teams: low-side-flexion OR=3.5, low-front-flexion OR=3.9, medium-side-flexion OR=6.3, medium-front-flexion OR=2.3
- Dartmouth showed reduced odds of low-risk combinations (high-side-neutral OR=0.4, high-back-neutral OR=0.6)

## Limitations
Cross-sectional design limits causal inference. The no-tackle practice policy team (Dartmouth) was blinded in the manuscript, limiting direct generalizability by name. Confounding player- and team-level variables not fully controlled. Study focused on defensive players only in the Ivy League; generalizability to other conferences or positions is limited. Observational classification of tackles introduces potential coding error despite inter-rater reliability checks.

## Practical Implications
Coaches should prioritize teaching neutral neck position and back/side contact area in tackling drills. Eliminating head-forward (flexion) tackling is the single highest-leverage intervention for reducing head contact risk. Implementing no-tackle or reduced-contact practice policies, as demonstrated by the Ivy League policy starting in 2016, may reduce the prevalence of high-risk techniques in games. Teaching specific safe tackle mechanics (e.g., pursuing approach, wrap technique) provides complementary injury prevention alongside contact restrictions.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football. Specific tackle combination data allows coaches to identify and eliminate the highest-risk mechanics (flexion-forward combinations) from their teaching progressions. The finding that a no-tackle practice policy was associated with lower game-time high-risk technique usage supports integrating tackle technique coaching and safe practice policies into player development programs at all levels.

## Return to Play Notes
Reducing high-risk tackling techniques has direct implications for concussion and cervical spine injury prevention, which are leading drivers of return-to-play protocols in football. Teams that implement no-tackle or reduced-contact practice policies may reduce in-game SRC incidence, thereby decreasing the burden on sports medicine and return-to-play pipelines. Coaching staff and ATCs should work collaboratively to monitor and remediate high-risk tackle mechanics as part of injury prevention programs.
