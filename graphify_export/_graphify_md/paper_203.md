# Guneşliol BE, Baş M. Evaluation of Dietary Intake and Body Composition of Collegiate American Football Players. Progress in Nutrition. 2021;23(4):e2021198. doi:10.23751/pn.v23i4.11548

## TLDR
Collegiate American football players — particularly linemen — follow nutritionally unbalanced diets marked by insufficient carbohydrate and protein intake relative to recommendations, alongside excessive fat, saturated fat, and sodium. Most DL and OL are obese by BMI and body fat percentage standards, elevating cardiovascular and metabolic disease risk. These findings call for individualized nutritional education and position-specific dietary strategies, especially for linemen who face the greatest long-term cardiometabolic risk.

## Abstract
This observational study evaluated the dietary intake and body composition of 185 collegiate American football players in Ankara, Turkey by position group (defensive linemen [DL, n=22], offensive linemen [OL, n=23], defensive backs [DB, n=71], offensive backs [OB, n=34], and receivers [R, n=35]) during the 2017-2018 mid-season. Body composition was assessed by anthropometric measurements and bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA). Dietary intake was assessed by 3-day diet records. Energy, carbohydrate, and protein intakes of DL and OL were significantly lower than receivers. Players consumed high fat, dietary cholesterol, and sodium but were low in carbohydrates and potassium. Average BMI and body fat percentage of DL (31.38 kg/m² and 26.13%) and OL (32.95 kg/m² and 30.06%) were significantly higher than other position groups. Most DL and OL were classified as obese.

## Methods
Observational study of 185 collegiate American football players from five university-level teams in Ankara, Turkey (2016-2017 season, measured mid-season 2017-2018). Players were categorized into five position groups: DL, OL, DB, OB, and R. Body composition was assessed via anthropometric measurements (InBody 570, Seca 213 stadiometer) including BMI, waist circumference, hip circumference, WHR, and WHtR, plus BIA measurements (BFP, BFM, LBM, TBW, BMR) using InBody 570. Dietary intake was determined via 3-day diet records analyzed using the computer-aided Turkish Nutrition Information System (NIS). Statistical analysis used IBM SPSS v24, with ANOVA and Tukey HSD (or Tamhane's T2) for group comparisons; significance at p < 0.05.

## Findings
Energy and carbohydrate intakes of DL and OL were significantly lower than R (p < 0.001). Protein intakes of DL and OL were also significantly lower than R (p < 0.001). Most players (88.1%) consumed carbohydrates below sports nutrition recommendations (5-7 g/kg/day); 95.1% consumed fiber below AI. All players consumed SFAs above recommended levels; omega-6/omega-3 ratios were above recommended in 97.3% of players. Most players consumed sodium above RDA and potassium below RDA. BMI and BFP of DL and OL were significantly higher than other groups; most DL and OL were obese. Average BFPs for DL: 26.13%; OL: 30.06%; DB: 16.81%; OB: 15.69%; R: 12.79%. Average LBMs of DL and OL were significantly higher than other groups.

## Limitations
Daily physical activity records were not collected, precluding calculation of individual energy requirements. Dietary data were self-reported by participants via diet records, which may include inaccuracies in portion size estimation. Data collection occurred during the season when tight schedules limited more objective dietary assessment methods. The study population was Turkish collegiate players, limiting direct generalizability to American collegiate or professional players. This is reportedly the first study evaluating dietary intake of collegiate American football players by position group in Turkey.

## Practical Implications
Position-specific nutritional strategies are needed for collegiate football players, with particular urgency for linemen (DL and OL) who show the highest obesity rates and lowest relative energy and carbohydrate intakes. Nutrition education should emphasize adequate carbohydrate intake (5-7 g/kg/day) for performance and recovery, increased whole-food protein sources, reduced saturated fat and processed sodium intake, and improved omega-6/omega-3 balance through food-first strategies (fish, walnuts, green leafy vegetables). Monitoring cardiometabolic health markers in linemen throughout their collegiate careers is advisable.

## Athlete Development Notes
Body composition profiles differ substantially by position in collegiate football. Linemen carry significantly higher body fat percentages and BMI — elevating long-term cardiometabolic risk — while skill-position players (DB, OB, R) maintain body compositions closer to athletic norms. Strength and conditioning staff should integrate body composition monitoring and individualized nutrition programming into athlete development plans, particularly for linemen who may need to prioritize body fat reduction alongside lean mass maintenance to protect long-term health.

## Return to Play Notes
This study does not directly address return to play from injury. However, inadequate carbohydrate and protein intake in linemen — the two most critical macronutrients for tissue repair and recovery — may impair recovery from injury or overuse. Nutritional optimization, particularly adequate carbohydrate availability and protein intake above the 1.6-1.7 g/kg/day recommendation for American football players, should be a component of any comprehensive injury recovery program.
