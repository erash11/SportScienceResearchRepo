# Stergiopoulos DC, Botonis PG, Miliotis P, Zavvos AX, Ntalapera SD, Papaleontіou GL, Pilatis GA, Soukara EK, Geladas ND. Effects of Simulated Short-Haul Flights on Sport-Specific Performance, Sleep, and Psychophysiological Responses in Football Players. Scandinavian Journal of Medicine & Science in Sports. 2025;35:e70169. doi:10.1111/sms.70169

## TLDR
Even short-haul air travel of 2–4 hours impairs football-specific performance the day after travel, primarily due to sleep loss the night before the flight rather than the physiological stressors of the flight itself. Both 4-h and 2-h simulated flights reduced total sleep time by over 100 minutes the night before departure (early morning flights), and next-day performance suffered across aerobic endurance, sprint ability, CMJ, and technical skill. The 4-h flight additionally suppressed HRV and sIgA on the day of and day after travel. Club staff should avoid booking early-morning flights to prevent pre-flight sleep disruption.

## Abstract
This randomised crossover study examined the effects of two simulated short-haul air travels (4-hour and 2-hour SHAT) compared to a control condition (no travel) on sport-specific performance, sleep, and psychophysiological responses in 19 highly trained male football players. Flights were simulated in a hypobaric chamber at 2438 m altitude equivalent and were completed at 08:00. Measures included football-specific aerobic capacity (Hoff test), sprint performance (10m, RAST), CMJ, technical skills (LSPT), sleep (actigraphy), HRV, salivary cortisol, and sIgA.

## Methods
Randomised crossover; 19 high-level male football players (age 21.3±4.2 y, VO2max Yo-Yo Level 1: 1823±325 m); two simulated short-haul flights (4-h and 2-h) in a hypobaric chamber (8,000 ft / 2438 m altitude equivalent) at 08:00 one week apart, followed by a control (CON); measures at baseline (D-1), day of flight (DF), and day after (D+1); Hoff test (total distance covered and blood lactate), 10-m sprint, RAST (4×35 m), CMJ, LSPT; actigraphy sleep monitoring; HRV (LnRMSSD, Polar V800); salivary sIgA and cortisol; Profile of Mood States; linear mixed models.

## Findings
Total sleep time was reduced by ~114 min (4-h) and ~107 min (2-h) the night before the flight (p<0.01). Hoff test distance decreased ~5–6% post-both flights vs. baseline (p=0.02 and p<0.01). RSA mean and total time increased ~3.5% after both flights (p<0.001). CMJ decreased ~8.5% at +3h and ~5.5% at D+1 for both flights (p<0.001). LSPT performance decreased only after the 4-h flight (p<0.05). 10-m sprint declined 6% after both flights (p<0.001). sIgA was reduced on DF and D+1 for both flights (p<0.05). HRV (LnRMSSD) decreased only on the day of the 4-h flight (p<0.05, d=-0.64). Mood states and salivary cortisol were unaffected.

## Limitations
Simulated flights in a hypobaric chamber may underestimate real-world travel stressors (transfers, psychological stress, nutrition variability). Both flights departed at 08:00, limiting generalisation to other departure times. Small sample (n=19). Study did not isolate sleep deprivation from hypoxic exposure or somatic inertia. No female athletes included.

## Practical Implications
Football club administrators and sports science staff should avoid scheduling early-morning flights before competition days, as the associated sleep loss is the primary driver of next-day performance impairment. Longer flights (≥4 h) additionally suppress autonomic (HRV) and immune (sIgA) function, increasing infection risk. Strategies to preserve pre-travel sleep — including travel timing adjustments and sleep hygiene protocols — are more impactful than managing in-flight physiological stressors.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football players who frequently travel for domestic or European competition, accrued sleep debt from repeated early-morning short-haul flights may compound within a congested schedule. Monitoring HRV and sIgA alongside sleep actigraphy can help identify players at elevated risk of immune suppression and performance decline after travel-heavy periods. Training load on post-travel days should be reduced.

## Return to Play Notes
Returning players who have recently travelled (particularly early-morning short-haul flights) should have RTP physical testing and high-intensity training sessions scheduled to allow for at least one full recovery night after travel. Reduced aerobic performance, sprint capacity, and neuromuscular output (CMJ) on the day following travel suggest that early post-travel assessments may under-represent true baseline function.
