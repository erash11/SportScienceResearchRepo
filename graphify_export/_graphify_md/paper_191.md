# Williams JZ, Singichetti B, Li H, Xiang H, Klingele KE, Yang J. Epidemiological patterns of initial and subsequent injuries in collegiate football athletes. Am J Sports Med. 2017. DOI: 10.1177/0363546516685317.

## TLDR
Once a collegiate football player sustains an initial injury, their risk of a subsequent injury increases approximately 40–50%, with the rate rising further with each successive injury. Head, neck, and face injuries—predominantly concussions—showed the highest rate escalation (2x) after an initial injury. Wide receivers and defensive linemen are at the greatest positional risk for subsequent injuries. This study provides a methodologically superior estimate of subsequent injury risk by using position-specific AEs, which prior studies did not adequately separate.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiological study examined rates of initial and subsequent injuries among collegiate Division I football players at two universities from 2007 to 2011, using a methodologically rigorous approach that calculated athlete-exposures (AEs) separately for initial and subsequent injuries. Of 365 enrolled players, 238 injuries occurred in 159 players. The overall injury rate was 35.2 per 10,000 AEs. Subsequent injuries occurred at a rate 1.4 times higher than initial injuries (45.3 vs. 31.7 per 10,000 AEs). The rate of subsequent head/neck/face injuries was double that of initial injuries, and wide receivers and defensive linemen had significantly elevated subsequent injury rates.

## Methods
Prospective cohort study using data from collegiate football players at two Division I universities (2007–2011). A total of 365 players were enrolled. Athletic trainers reported injuries via the Sports Injury Monitoring System (SIMS). Injury was defined as an event during games or practices requiring medical attention and resulting in at least one day of missed play. AEs for initial injuries were counted from enrollment to first injury; AEs for subsequent injuries were counted only from return to play after the prior injury. Poisson regression was used to calculate injury rate ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals, adjusting for covariates including age, grade, BMI, position, years of experience, and 12-month injury history.

## Findings
Total AEs: 67,564; overall injury rate: 35.2 per 10,000 AEs. Initial injury rate: 31.7; subsequent injury rate: 45.3 per 10,000 AEs (RR = 1.4; 95% CI 1.1–1.9). Game RR = 1.8 (95% CI 1.1–3.0). Rates for second through sixth injuries were 41.1, 77.5, 151.5, 116.7, and 64.9 per 10,000 AEs respectively. Subsequent head/neck/face injury rate was 10.9 vs. 5.6 per 10,000 AEs for initial injuries (RR = 2.0; 95% CI 1.1–3.5); 63% of these were concussions. Wide receivers had subsequent injury RR = 2.2 (95% CI 1.3–3.8); defensive linemen RR = 2.1 (95% CI 1.1–3.9). Prior 12-month injury history was strongly associated with subsequent injury (RR = 4.0 overall).

## Limitations
Only two Division I football programs were studied, limiting generalizability. Small sample sizes prevented detailed analysis of specific risk factors and how second injury characteristics predict third injury risk. AE recording did not allow separation of players who played from those who only attended competitions, likely underestimating game injury rates. Many athletes had pre-enrollment injury histories, meaning their 'initial' study injury may have been a higher-order injury in reality. Recurrent injuries (same type and site) were rare (n=6), precluding separate analysis.

## Practical Implications
Athletic trainers and team physicians should treat any injury as a significant marker for future injury risk, particularly for head/neck/face injuries and in wide receiver and defensive lineman positions. Prior 12-month injury history should be a key screening variable in pre-participation evaluations. Subsequent injury prevention protocols—including load management, biomechanical screening, and targeted rehabilitation—should be prioritized for previously injured athletes, especially those who missed more than 3 months due to an initial injury.

## Athlete Development Notes
Injury history is one of the strongest predictors of future injury in collegiate football. Development staff should factor prior injuries into training load decisions and return-to-play clearance standards. The finding that injury rate escalates with each successive injury argues for conservative return standards and structured reconditioning rather than rushing athletes back to full participation. Wide receivers and defensive linemen warrant close monitoring given their disproportionately elevated subsequent injury risk.

## Return to Play Notes
The study underscores that returning a player to play after an initial injury significantly elevates their risk of a subsequent injury—1.4x overall and 1.8x during games. Head/neck/face injuries, particularly concussions, showed a 2x subsequent injury rate, reinforcing the importance of conservative concussion RTP protocols. Players who lost 3 months of play following their first injury had the highest proportion of second injuries (50%), suggesting extended recovery timelines may reflect injury severity that persists as ongoing vulnerability.
