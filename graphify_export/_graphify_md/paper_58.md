# Rund JM, Christensen GV, Fleming JA, Wolf BR. Anterior Cruciate Ligament Tears among Football Players. Current Reviews in Musculoskeletal Medicine. 2025;doi:10.1007/s12178-025-09952-z.

## TLDR
ACL tears in football players require reconstruction (typically BPTB autograft) followed by milestone-driven rehabilitation; return-to-play rates range from 63–82% depending on level of play and position, with a recommended minimum 7–9 month timeline.

## Abstract
This narrative review synthesizes current literature on ACL tear epidemiology, mechanism of injury, diagnosis, treatment options, rehabilitation protocols, and return-to-play outcomes specifically in American football athletes across all levels of competition. ACL tears are one of the most common causes of lost playing time in football and require individualized, shared decision-making between athlete and surgeon. BPTB autograft remains the most common and preferred graft choice for elite football athletes. Return-to-play criteria should be milestone-based rather than time-based, with recent trends supporting a delayed return of at least 7–9 months post-operatively.

## Methods
Narrative review of published literature on ACL injuries in American football players at all levels (high school, collegiate, NFL). Topics covered include epidemiology, injury mechanism, diagnosis (radiograph, MRI), treatment (non-operative and operative), graft selection, rehabilitation phases (early, intermediate, late), and return-to-play criteria and outcomes. The MOON ACL Rehabilitation Guidelines served as a framework for rehabilitation discussion. No formal systematic search protocol or PRISMA methodology was described.

## Findings
• High school ACL incidence: ~11.1 per 100,000 athlete exposures; second only to girls' soccer
• NCAA ACL incidence: 14.4–18.0 per 100,000 athlete exposures
• NFL single-season ACL risk: 1.9%
• 70% of NFL ACL tears involve concomitant meniscal tears; 89% have associated meniscal, ligamentous, or cartilage injury on MRI
• Bone bruises in 95% of ACL tears; chondral pathology in 49%
• BPTB autograft is the gold standard for elite football athletes (97% of NFL team physicians as of 2016)
• LET/ALL augmentation increasingly added for athletes with hyperextension, high-grade laxity, or revision situations
• MOON group: weighted mean time to RTP = 11.6 months; 63% of high school and 69% of college football players return
• NFL RTP rates: 63–73%; running backs/wide receivers/tight ends have lower rates vs. defensive players/QBs/kickers
• Graft failure rate: BPTB 2.0% vs. hamstring 4.1% (meta-analysis); QT similar to BPTB
• Athletes returning before 9 months have 7x greater re-injury risk

## Limitations
As a narrative review, it is subject to selection bias in literature chosen for inclusion. No formal quality assessment of included studies was performed. Evidence base for some newer techniques (BEAR repair, LET/ALL augmentation) remains limited. Generalizability across all football levels and positions may vary. Specific NFL return-to-play data may not reflect current practice given rapidly evolving rehabilitation standards.

## Practical Implications
BPTB autograft should be the preferred graft for competitive football players given superior re-tear rates vs. soft-tissue grafts. Return-to-play decisions must be objective and milestone-driven, incorporating isokinetic strength testing, functional movement assessment, and psychological readiness. Delaying RTP to at least 9 months post-operatively is supported by data showing a 7-fold reduction in re-injury risk. LET augmentation should be considered for athletes with significant laxity, hyperextension, or pivot-shift findings.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football at all competitive levels. Position-specific RTP rates and re-injury risks should inform individualized ACL injury prevention and rehabilitation planning. Wide receivers, running backs, and tight ends — skill positions requiring cutting and pivoting — have the lowest RTP rates and highest re-injury risk, warranting more conservative return criteria and targeted neuromuscular training both pre- and post-injury.

## Return to Play Notes
NFL ACL RTP rates: 63–73%, with skill positions (RB, WR, TE) at lower rates than non-skill positions. Weighted mean time to RTP in NFL/NCAA is 11.6 months. Athletes who return before 9 months have 7x greater re-injury risk. Objective milestones (isokinetic quad/hamstring strength, functional testing, psychological readiness) should drive RTP clearance. BPTB autograft provides superior re-tear resistance. LET/ALL augmentation should be considered in high-risk athletes to reduce re-injury rates.
