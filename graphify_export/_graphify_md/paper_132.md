# Buchheit M, Hader K. Data everywhere, insight nowhere: a practical quadrant-based model for monitoring training load vs. response in elite football. Sport Performance & Science Reports. 2025;258(May):v1.

## TLDR
There is a fundamental imbalance in elite football athlete monitoring: load metrics (especially GPS) dominate while athlete response and adaptation monitoring is largely absent or misclassified. Buchheit and Hader present a four-quadrant model — metabolic load, neuromuscular load, metabolic adaptation, neuromuscular adaptation — that assigns each monitoring tool to its correct physiological function. GPS is valid for external neuromuscular load but cannot assess muscle/tendon strain. HRV monitors metabolic response, not load. sRPE captures global load effectively. The practical minimum monitoring setup requires GPS with HR (load), submaximal fitness testing (metabolic adaptation), force plates or isometric strength tests (neuromuscular response), and wellness/soreness questionnaires. Football programs should audit their monitoring systems against this quadrant model to identify where they have blind spots in tracking how athletes actually respond and adapt to training.

## Abstract
This conceptual paper presents a practical four-quadrant framework for monitoring elite football players, organizing tools and metrics along two physiological axes (metabolic vs. neuromuscular) and two functional dimensions (training load vs. training response and adaptation). The model defines four quadrants — metabolic load, neuromuscular load, metabolic response/adaptation, and neuromuscular response/adaptation — and maps specific monitoring technologies, protocols, and test metrics to each quadrant with ratings for practicality, cost, and evidence level. The paper argues that sport science 2.0 has created a publication ratio of 12.6:1 (training load vs. training response studies in 2020-2025) and that GPS metrics are systematically misapplied as neuromuscular load proxies despite providing no direct measure of muscle/tendon strain.

## Methods
Conceptual and narrative framework paper published in the Sports Science 3.0 Series (Sport Performance & Science Reports, May 2025). The authors conducted a PubMed-based analysis of publication ratios of training load vs. training response research from 1970 to 2025 to quantify the research imbalance. The four-quadrant model was structured around two axes: (1) load vs. response/adaptation, and (2) metabolic vs. neuromuscular physiological systems. Five detailed tables (Tables 2-5) catalogue available monitoring tools for each quadrant with ratings for type (internal/external), practicality, cost, evidence level, and clinical notes. Real-world implementation recommendations including a minimum viable monitoring setup are provided based on the authors' applied experience in elite European and international football environments.

## Findings
The ratio of PubMed publications on training load vs. training response has increased from 1.0 (1995-2000) to 12.6 (2020-2025), with 3,314 publications on load vs. 547 on response across all years examined. GPS is characterized as an external neuromuscular load tool that cannot infer muscle/tendon strain and has low evidence level for this purpose. Heart rate is a metabolic internal load proxy separate from GPS; HRV belongs to the metabolic response/adaptation quadrant. Session RPE (sRPE) is the single highest-evidence, most practical overall load marker covering both metabolic and neuromuscular systems. The response/adaptation quadrants are comparatively well-served: submaximal HR/lactate responses to standardized runs for metabolic adaptation; CPK, thermography, jump tests, isometric dynamometry, and muscle contractility (Myocene) for neuromuscular response. The recommended minimum practical monitoring system is GPS with HR capability plus sRPE for load, plus a system for standardized submaximal runs (metabolic adaptation tracking), force plates or isometric tests (neuromuscular response), and simple subjective wellness/soreness questionnaires.

## Limitations
Conceptual paper with no primary data collection; tool assessments reflect expert synthesis and explicitly selected (and acknowledged as potentially biased) reference sources. The framework intentionally defers in-depth discussion of individual technologies to follow-up papers. Some recommended tools (thermography, Myocene contractility devices, NIRS) are costly or not yet scalable for routine team use. The football context is association football (soccer), though the physiological framework is applicable across sports. Practical implementation within the football weekly microcycle is deferred to a follow-up manuscript.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football performance and sports science staff should use this quadrant model as an audit tool to evaluate whether their current monitoring systems address all four physiological domains. Many programs over-rely on GPS external load metrics while having no systematic assessment of metabolic adaptation or neuromuscular response — the quadrants with the strongest evidence base. The recommended minimum setup (GPS + HR + sRPE for load; standardized fitness testing, force plates/isometric tests, and wellness questionnaires for response) is feasible within professional football infrastructure. Differential sRPE ratings (neuromuscular vs. metabolic breathlessness) are a low-cost addition that provides meaningful load specificity without adding equipment burden.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athlete development programs, this framework provides a structured approach to building comprehensive, physiologically coherent monitoring systems from the ground up. Rather than collecting every available data stream, development programs should ensure at least one metric per quadrant: a GPS/load metric for external neuromuscular load, HR or sRPE for metabolic load, submaximal fitness tests for metabolic adaptation tracking, and jump tests or isometric strength assessments for neuromuscular response monitoring. The explicit distinction between training load and training response is especially valuable in developmental settings where understanding whether young athletes are positively adapting to imposed training stress is critical for long-term athletic development.

## Return to Play Notes
The four-quadrant framework has direct and substantial relevance for return-to-play. Effective RTP monitoring requires tracking both the load imposed on the athlete and how the athlete is actually responding and adapting. The neuromuscular response and adaptation quadrants — encompassing CPK, thermography, jump tests, isometric strength, and muscle contractility — provide the most targeted tools for monitoring RTP progression after musculoskeletal injury. The metabolic adaptation quadrant (submaximal HR responses, HRV) tracks cardiovascular reconditioning. The model explicitly notes that return-to-play is a unique opportunity for intensive individualized monitoring where the practical minimum setup should be applied without exception, and that more advanced tools such as CK, Myocene, and thermography are particularly valuable in the 1-on-1 RTP context where compliance barriers are reduced.
