# Lacki, J. F. (2021). High training/competition ratio, less incidence of injury? Professional football calendar exploration. Apunts Sports Medicine, 56(209), 100338.

## TLDR
Football teams competing in fewer matches per season with longer recovery-training cycles between games (5–8 days) show substantially lower injury incidence rates than European clubs playing in multiple concurrent competitions. This South American club's injury rate (4.2/1,000 h total; 2.3/1,000 h training; 20.5/1,000 h competition) was well below UEFA benchmarks (5.8 and 30.5/1,000 h respectively), and 65% of injuries resolved within one week—compared to only 27% in UEFA data. More training relative to competition may improve fitness, build structural resilience, and reduce severe injury burden.

## Abstract
This 12-season prospective analysis of an Argentine professional football club (Defensa y Justicia, 2003–2014) investigated whether a high training-to-competition ratio is associated with lower injury incidence. The team competed in a single tournament (38 rounds/season) at approximately 3.85 matches/month, yielding a training/competition ratio of 8.4 training hours per match hour. Over 12 seasons, 797 injuries were recorded across 4,710 training hours and 715 competition hours. Overall injury incidence was 4.2 per 1,000 hours, compared to 30.5 (competition) and 5.8 (training) per 1,000 hours in UEFA Champions League data. Sixty-five percent of injuries resolved within 7 days.

## Methods
Prospective longitudinal injury surveillance over 12 seasons (2003–2014) for one Argentine First Division club. Daily medical records documented all injuries (defined per Dvorak & Junge 2000 as missed training/match time with anatomical diagnosis). Injury severity classified using the Paus modification (Grade I: 1–7 days; II: 1–3 weeks; III: 3–8 weeks; IV: >8 weeks). Incidence calculated as injuries per 1,000 participation hours for training and competition separately. Data compared to UEFA Champions League and English FA Championship injury reports.

## Findings
Injury incidence rate: 4.2/1,000 h (combined), 2.3/1,000 h training, 20.5/1,000 h competition. Training/competition ratio: 8.4:1 (vs. 6.3:1 in UEFA 2016/17). Average absence per injury: 21.4 h. Injury burden: 90 days/1,000 h (vs. 130 days/1,000 h in UEFA Champions League). Grade I injuries (1–7 days): 65% of all injuries. Muscle injuries most common (44.5%), followed by knee (17.3%) and ankle (16%). Hamstrings comprised 53.5% of muscle injuries. Non-contact injuries: 57.1%. Surgical procedures required in only 1.24% of cases.

## Limitations
Single club and coaching staff across 12 seasons limits generalizability. Training load recorded by time only (no GPS), which reduces precision. Illness-related absences were not recorded as injuries, which may explain some of the lower incidence compared to European studies. The Argentine league structure (single tournament, two annual cycles) differs substantially from European multi-competition formats.

## Practical Implications
Sports calendars allowing 5–8 days between competitions and a training/competition ratio of approximately 8:1 may function as a protective factor for professional footballers. Administrators and governing bodies planning fixture schedules should consider injury burden data when designing competition formats. Clubs competing in congested schedules should proactively plan recovery, manage training load, and monitor injury surveillance metrics. Muscle injuries—especially hamstrings—remain the primary target for prevention regardless of schedule.

## Athlete Development Notes
Athletes in development programs who train more than they compete may benefit from greater structural adaptation and lower injury risk. The data support the idea that higher chronic training loads relative to competition can prepare athletes to tolerate the demands of matches. Periodization models that prioritize training volume and progressive loading during lower-competition periods may produce more robust and injury-resilient athletes.

## Return to Play Notes
The finding that 65% of injuries resolved within 7 days—compared to 27% in UEFA populations—suggests that lower accumulated fatigue from congested schedules may facilitate faster recovery and return to play. Adequate recovery-training cycles between competitions (5–8 days) may support more complete physiological restoration, reducing re-injury risk. Return-to-play protocols should account for the team's competition frequency as a contextual factor in managing return timelines.
