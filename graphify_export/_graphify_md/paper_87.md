# Coburn SL, Crossley KM, Kemp JL, Gassert F, Luitjens J, Warden SJ, Culvenor AG, Scholes MJ, King MG, Lawrenson P, Link T, Heerey JJ. Association between hip muscle strength/function and hip cartilage defects in sub-elite football players with hip/groin pain. Osteoarthritis and Cartilage. 2024. doi:10.1016/j.joca.2024.03.121

## TLDR
In young sub-elite football players with chronic hip/groin pain, overall isometric hip strength is not strongly linked to hip cartilage health on MRI. The most actionable finding for football medical staff is that better one-leg rise performance was associated with fewer superomedial cartilage defects, suggesting this functional test may serve as a clinically useful indicator of hip cartilage status. Greater hip extension strength was paradoxically associated with worse cartilage scores — likely reflecting that stronger athletes load the joint more during high-intensity activity — not that strengthening causes harm. Hip muscle strengthening should remain a rehabilitation priority for symptom management, but clinicians should not use strength measures alone to infer cartilage health.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional exploratory study used baseline data from the FORCe cohort to examine associations between isometric hip muscle strength, functional task performance, and MRI-assessed hip cartilage defects in 182 sub-elite soccer and Australian football players (37 women, mean age 26±7 years) with hip/groin pain lasting more than 6 months. Hip cartilage defects were assessed using the SHOMRI scoring tool across 10 femoral and acetabular subregions. Overall, few associations were found between hip strength or functional task performance and cartilage defects, with the notable exceptions that greater hip extension strength was associated with higher cartilage defect severity scores, higher one-leg rise performance was linked to lower odds of superomedial cartilage defects, and in females greater external rotation strength was associated with lateral cartilage defects.

## Methods
Cross-sectional study using baseline data from the FORCe (Femoroacetabular Impingement and Hip OsteoaRthritis Cohort) study. Participants: 182 sub-elite soccer and Australian football players (146 men, 38 women; age 26±7 years) with hip/groin pain >6 months recruited August 2015-October 2018 in Melbourne and Brisbane, Australia. Isometric hip strength assessed bilaterally in 6 directions using a hand-held dynamometer (JTECH Commander). Functional tasks: one-leg rise (repetitions), side-bridge endurance (seconds), hop for distance (cm). Hip cartilage defects assessed on 3.0T MRI (Phillips Ingenia) using SHOMRI scoring (10 subregions) by two blinded musculoskeletal radiologists with consensus read. Logistic regression and negative binomial regression with GEE controlled for BMI, age, test site, cam morphology, and sex. Sex-specific interaction terms tested.

## Findings
58% of symptomatic hips (n=288) had cartilage defects; most prevalent sites: superolateral (41%), lateral femoral (29%), superomedial (22%); 35% had defects in 2+ subregions. Greater hip extension strength (per 10 N) was associated with higher cartilage total score (aIRR 1.01, 95%CI 1.0-1.02, p=0.013) and superolateral defects (aOR 1.03, 95%CI 1.01-1.06, p<0.01). Greater one-leg rise repetitions were associated with lower odds of superomedial defects (aOR 0.96, 95%CI 0.94-0.99, p<0.01). In women only: greater external rotation strength was associated with lateral cartilage defects (aOR 1.61, 95%CI 1.05-2.48, p=0.03) and higher cartilage total score (aIRR 1.25, 95%CI 1.01-1.66, p=0.042). Intra- and inter-rater reliability for SHOMRI was almost perfect (kappas 0.86-0.94 intra-rater; 0.96 inter-rater).

## Limitations
Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference; the relationship between cartilage and strength could be interpreted in either direction. The proportion of women (38/182) limited power for sex-stratified analyses. Multiple regression models increase Type I error risk. Pain during strength testing may have influenced strength values. Acetabular morphology not fully characterized (acetabular retroversion excluded). Findings may not generalize to elite, asymptomatic, or non-football athletes.

## Practical Implications
Football medical and performance staff should not rely on isolated hip strength testing to assess cartilage health in players with chronic hip/groin pain. The one-leg rise test may offer added clinical value as a functional indicator of superomedial cartilage status and should be incorporated into hip/groin injury assessments. Hip muscle strengthening remains an important component of rehabilitation for symptom management and functional recovery even though its direct relationship to structural cartilage protection is unclear.

## Athlete Development Notes
Football players with persistent hip/groin pain have a high prevalence of underlying cartilage defects (58% of symptomatic hips), with half of these occurring in multiple subregions. Monitoring hip muscle strength and one-leg rise performance during pre-season screening may help identify athletes at higher risk for hip OA progression. Balancing hip extensor and external rotator strengthening with appropriate load management is important for players with cam morphology or persistent groin symptoms to avoid compounding joint loading.

## Return to Play Notes
Players returning from hip/groin injury should be assessed with functional tasks including the one-leg rise, as performance on this test is associated with reduced superomedial cartilage defect odds. Given the high prevalence of underlying cartilage changes in sub-elite football players with hip/groin pain, MRI evaluation should be considered for players with recurrent or persistent symptoms before full return to training. Return-to-play criteria should incorporate functional task benchmarks alongside pain resolution and strength symmetry.
