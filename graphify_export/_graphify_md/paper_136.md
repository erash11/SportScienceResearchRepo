# Grashow RG, Roberts AL, Zafonte R, Pascual-Leone A, Taylor H, Baggish A, Nadler L, Courtney TK, Connor A, Weisskopf MG. Defining Exposures in Professional Football: Professional American-Style Football Players as an Occupational Cohort. The Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine. 2019;7(2):2325967119829212. DOI 10.1177/2325967119829212

## TLDR
This review argues that studying NFL players through an occupational epidemiology lens — treating football like a job with measurable workplace hazards — could unlock deeper understanding of how specific football activities cause long-term health outcomes like CTE, arthritis, cardiovascular disease, and cognitive impairment. Most existing football health research uses overly broad 'ever played football' exposure measures, which cannot isolate what actually causes harm. The review proposes position-specific exposure matrices, better comparison groups (other elite athletes rather than the general population), and strategies to address the healthy worker effect and recall bias. For performance staff, the practical implication is that position, total snaps, full-contact practice load, and training regimen are all quantifiable exposures that should be documented and monitored.

## Abstract
This narrative review proposes that professional American football players should be studied as an occupational cohort using the established frameworks of occupational epidemiology. The authors conducted a review of existing football research, occupational epidemiology methods, and occupational medicine studies to describe how the four-step occupational epidemiology framework (surveillance, exposure assessment, hypothesis testing, prevention/evaluation) could be applied to NFL players. The review identifies key biases inherent to football health research — including the healthy worker effect and survivor bias — and maps potential exposures across the pre-professional, professional, and post-retirement life stages of a football player.

## Methods
Narrative review of existing football health research, occupational epidemiology methods papers, and occupational medicine studies. No primary data collected. Authors mapped a generalized occupational epidemiology workflow (Figure 1) and a hierarchy of hazard controls (Figure 2) onto professional football. Exposures were organized across life stages (pre-professional, professional, post-retirement) and categorized as game-related (total seasons, snaps, plays, position) or acute effects/mediators (concussions, joint injuries, medication use, sleep deprivation). Biases analyzed include healthy worker effect, volunteer/nonresponse bias, survivor bias, small-study bias, recall bias, and comparison group selection issues.

## Findings
Professional football players demonstrate a constellation of adverse health outcomes — cognitive dysfunction, neuroanatomical changes, joint injuries, cardiovascular disease, and neurodegenerative disease (CTE) — that are linked to football exposure but not yet fully causally mapped to specific activities. The healthy worker effect (HWE) inflates apparent longevity of NFL players compared to the general population, masking harmful effects; replacement-player comparison data suggest career players are ~38% more likely to have died during study follow-up than replacement players. Position is the most relevant subgroup variable and should be used in a position-exposure matrix (PEM). Current NFL safety interventions fall at the lower end of the hierarchy of controls (PPE and administrative controls), with no elimination or substitution strategies implemented. Only 62% of former players consistently recalled the same number of concussions at baseline and follow-up.

## Limitations
As a narrative review, no systematic search strategy or quantitative synthesis was performed. Many of the football health studies reviewed had small sample sizes subject to small-study bias. Historical exposure data for former players are largely unavailable or proprietary, making retrospective exposure reconstruction unreliable. The review does not provide new empirical data but rather a methodological framework. The influence of recall bias on published football health statistics is rarely explored systematically.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football organizations should begin systematically documenting and quantifying player exposures — snap counts, full-contact practice sessions, position changes, training load, medication use — using an occupational epidemiology framework. This data infrastructure would enable causal analysis linking specific football activities to health outcomes rather than relying on blunt 'ever played' measures. Safety interventions should progress up the hierarchy of controls beyond helmets and rule changes toward engineering and administrative controls (e.g., robotic tackling dummies, contact restrictions in practice). Position-specific health monitoring programs are justified by the evidence that injury and disease risk varies substantially by position.

## Athlete Development Notes
The occupational framework highlights that football exposure begins pre-professionally (age of first exposure, high school/collegiate play) and that early cumulative exposure may independently predict long-term neurocognitive and musculoskeletal outcomes. Developmental programs should document position history, snap counts, and full-contact practice exposure from early in a player's career. The review supports limiting high-intensity contact in practice, particularly for developmental-stage athletes, as this represents an administrative control that reduces exposure without eliminating participation. Monitoring weight gain — a documented risk factor for cardiovascular outcomes — throughout a player's developmental arc is also warranted.

## Return to Play Notes
The occupational epidemiology framework is directly applicable to return-to-play contexts: just as occupational medicine uses job-exposure matrices to determine safe return to work after injury, a football position-exposure matrix (PEM) could guide RTP decisions by quantifying the specific physical demands of each position. The review's discussion of acute injury mediators — including medication use, sleep deprivation, and cumulative sub-concussive blows — as contributors to long-term outcomes underscores that RTP protocols should address the full exposure environment, not just the presenting injury. Recall bias in concussion self-report (only 62% consistency) has direct implications for clinical history-taking during RTP evaluation.
