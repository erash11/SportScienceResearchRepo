# Desai SS, Dent CS, Hodgens BH, Rizzo MG, Barnhill SW, Allegra PR, Popkin CA, Aiyer AA. Epidemiology and outcomes of ankle injuries in the National Football League. Orthop J Sports Med. 2022;10(6):23259671221101056.

## TLDR
Ankle injuries in NFL players—despite a high 91% return-to-play rate—cause significant and lasting performance declines across multiple seasons. Players averaged a 22% drop in power rating in year one post-injury and a 27% drop in year two, with fewer games played and lower per-game output across all positions and experience levels. Running backs showed the largest performance decline (31–47%), while quarterbacks showed the smallest. These findings underscore that ankle injuries carry long-term career performance consequences beyond time missed.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiological study examined ankle injuries in NFL players across three seasons (2015–2017) using the Pro Football Reference database, and assessed the effect of those injuries on performance metrics over the following two seasons. Among 5,963 NFL players, 668 ankle injuries were identified (average cumulative incidence 11.2%). Return-to-play rate was 91%. A performance analysis cohort of 159 players showed a statistically significant mean decrease in power rating (PR) of 22% in the first post-injury season and 27% in the second, with significant declines in PR per game played as well as total games played. All position groups and experience categories showed performance decline.

## Methods
Retrospective epidemiological study using the Pro Football Reference database for NFL seasons 2015–2017. A total of 668 ankle injuries in 5,963 players were identified. Cumulative incidence, demographics, and RTP were recorded. For performance analysis, 159 players met inclusion criteria (started ≥8 games in preinjury season, returned to play, played in both post-injury seasons, excluded OL/ST positions). Power ratings (PRs) were calculated as: (offensive yards/10) + (touchdowns × 6) + (combined tackles) + (sacks × 2) + (interceptions × 2). PRs from preinjury season (Y-1) were compared to two postinjury seasons (Y+1, Y+2). Subgroup analyses by position, injury type, and experience category. Paired t-tests and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests were used.

## Findings
668 ankle injuries identified; cumulative incidence 11.2%; RTP 91%. Mean overall PR decreased from 96.95 (Y-1) to 76.10 (Y+1; -22%; P<.001) and 70.93 (Y+2; -27%; P<.001). Mean games played declined from 14.76 to 13.19 (Y+1; P<.001) and 12.50 (Y+2; P<.001). Mean PR per game decreased from 6.70 to 5.75 (Y+1; -14%; P<.001) and 5.54 (Y+2; -17%; P<.001). Running backs showed the largest PR decline (DPR2%=-47%); quarterbacks showed the smallest and non-significant decline. All confirmed ankle sprains (n=100) showed statistically significant PR declines. Performance declines were significant across all experience categories (early, intermediate, veteran). OL/ST were excluded from performance analysis.

## Limitations
Most ankle injuries were unspecified in type; only 51% were confirmed sprains and 4% confirmed fractures. Specific injury patterns, operative vs. nonoperative management, and number of games missed during the index season were not captured. Additional non-ankle injuries during post-index seasons may have contributed to performance decline. OL and ST positions were excluded from performance analysis. Small QB subgroup (n=5) was underpowered. The use of NFL injury reports may underestimate true cumulative incidence due to players playing through unreported injuries.

## Practical Implications
Ankle injuries in NFL players should not be viewed as minor events even when players successfully return to play. The sustained decline in per-game performance output over two seasons suggests incomplete functional recovery that current RTP protocols may not adequately address. Enhanced post-return rehabilitation targeting speed, agility, and proprioception—especially for running backs, wide receivers, linebackers, and defensive backs—may help reduce this performance gap. Medical staff should implement multi-season performance monitoring after ankle injuries.

## Athlete Development Notes
Ankle injury prevention and comprehensive rehabilitation should be cornerstones of NFL player development programs. The finding that even veteran players (≥7 years) show statistically significant performance decline after ankle injuries (DPR1=-20%, DPR2=-22%) suggests that experience and physical maturity do not confer protection from long-term functional impact. Development staff should integrate ankle stability training, proprioceptive conditioning, and load management into year-round programming for all skill positions.

## Return to Play Notes
91% of NFL players with ankle injuries returned to play (defined as playing at least 1 snap in a subsequent game), which compares favorably to other lower extremity injuries (ACL: 79%, Achilles rupture: 68%). However, RTP rates alone are misleading—players who returned still experienced 22–27% declines in total performance and 14–17% declines in per-game output across the following two seasons. RTP criteria should incorporate functional performance benchmarks beyond clinical healing, particularly speed and agility assessments, to better predict readiness for full competitive contribution.
