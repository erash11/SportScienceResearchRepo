# Fitzgerald CF, Jensen RL. A Comparison of the National Football League's Annual National Football League Combine 1999-2000 to 2015-2016. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research. 2020;34(3):771-781.

## TLDR
NFL Combine prospects measurably improved in speed (40-yard dash), lower-body power (vertical and broad jump), and agility (3-cone drill) from 1999-2000 to 2015-2016, suggesting that elite football player physical qualities have trended upward over nearly two decades. For strength and conditioning coaches and scouts, the longitudinal normative benchmarks by position provide a historical baseline for combine preparation targets. The finding that broad jump improved at virtually every position suggests that lower-body explosive training should be a universal priority across all NFL positional development programs.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study compared NFL Combine performance data from players across the 1999-2000 and 2015-2016 draft cycles, analyzing seven combine tests (40-yard dash, pro-agility shuttle, 225-lb bench press repetitions, vertical jump, broad jump, 3-cone drill) and anthropometric measurements across eight position groups (QB, RB, WR, TE, DL, OL, LB, DB). Two-way ANOVA revealed statistically significant improvements from 1999-2000 to 2015-2016 in 40-yard dash speed, vertical jump, broad jump, and 3-cone drill times, with no significant changes in height or overall body mass, suggesting elite prospects have become faster and more explosive without significant size increases.

## Methods
Cross-sectional retrospective analysis comparing NFL Combine performance data from the 1999-2000 draft class (n=635) and the 2015-2016 draft class (n=628) using a two-way non-repeated ANOVA. Data were obtained from public NFL historical databases. Performance variables included the 40-yard dash, pro-agility shuttle, 225-lb bench press repetitions, vertical jump, broad jump, 3-cone drill, height, and body mass. Participants were separated by position group: QB, RB, WR, TE, DL, OL, LB, and DB. Punters, kickers, and long snappers were excluded. Statistical significance set at p<0.05.

## Findings
Significant improvements from 1999-2000 to 2015-2016 were found for: 40-yard dash (4.85 to 4.80 s, p=0.002), vertical jump (82.04 to 83.46 cm, p=0.028), broad jump (282.88 to 292.18 cm, p<0.001), and 3-cone drill (7.41 to 7.29 s, p<0.001). No significant differences were found for height or overall body mass. Position-specific significant improvements in 40-yard dash were found for RBs, WRs, OLs, and DBs. QB body mass and WR body mass increased significantly. Broad jump showed significant improvement at nearly every position group. Bench press repetitions did not differ significantly between time points.

## Limitations
Data obtained from public online databases, which may contain entry errors. Not all combine invitees complete every test, creating variable sample sizes across measures. Punters, kickers, and long snappers excluded, limiting generalizability to all roster positions. Body composition (body fat percentage) was not directly measured. The two time-point comparison cannot establish causal mechanisms for observed physical changes (e.g., training advances, nutrition science, population changes).

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football strength and conditioning staffs can use these longitudinal normative benchmarks to set position-specific combine preparation targets and evaluate where current athletes stand relative to historically draftable prospects. The consistent improvement in broad jump across positions reinforces the value of prioritizing lower-body explosive training in all positional development programs. Coaches can identify position-specific physical qualities where gaps between current athletes and combine norms are largest.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football athlete development and NFL Draft preparation. Speed and lower-body power improvements across positions from 1999-2000 to 2015-2016 indicate that multi-year explosive training programs can produce measurable gains at the elite scouting level. Programs can use position-specific normative data to set precise development targets for the 40-yard dash, broad jump, vertical jump, and agility drills, and track longitudinal progress toward draftable benchmarks.

## Return to Play Notes
Not the primary focus of this study. However, position-specific combine performance benchmarks could inform return-to-play physical readiness standards following injury by providing normative values for speed, agility, and power metrics used during NFL pre-participation evaluations, helping clinicians and coaches determine when a rehabilitating athlete has returned to pre-injury or draft-standard physical capacity.
