# Zhao Y, Wei X, Zhao K. Analysis of Z-Score and total score of athleticism on drafted and undrafted players from the NFL scouting combine. Sci Rep. 2025;15:21742.

## TLDR
Drafted NFL players significantly outperform undrafted players on z-scores and TSA across all positions. The 40-yard dash z-score is the single best discriminator (AUC 0.637–0.719), while bench press is the weakest. Position-specific z-score calculations outperform overall-sample calculations for talent identification — coaches should use position-normed combine scores rather than league-wide benchmarks.

## Abstract
This study evaluated the effectiveness of z-scores and Total Score of Athleticism (TSA) in distinguishing drafted from undrafted players in the NFL Scouting Combine (SC). Data from 3,446 players who participated in the NFL SC between 2000 and 2024 were analyzed, grouped into skill players (SP, n=1,318), big skill players (BSP, n=1,067), and linemen (LM, n=1,061). Z-scores were calculated for seven combine tests (40-yard sprint, vertical jump, bench press, broad jump, 3-cone drill, shuttle run) and summed to produce TSA. ROC analysis and binary logistic regression assessed discriminatory accuracy between drafted and undrafted players across positions.

## Methods
Secondary data analysis of NFL SC data (2000–2024) from Pro Football Reference; 3,446 players included after excluding QBs, kickers, punters, long snappers, and players missing tests; grouped into SP (n=1,318), BSP (n=1,067), LM (n=1,061); z-scores calculated as (score − group mean) / SD; TSA = sum of all z-scores; independent t-tests and Mann-Whitney U for drafted vs. undrafted comparisons; ANOVA and Kruskal-Wallis H for positional group comparisons; ROC analysis (AUC) and binary logistic regression; Spearman correlation; SPSS 27 and Origin Pro 2024 used.

## Findings
• Drafted players significantly outperformed undrafted players on all z-scores and TSA (p<0.001) across all positions
• 40-yard sprint z-score achieved the highest AUC (0.637–0.719) across all positional groups
• Bench press had the lowest AUC (0.556–0.631), poorest discriminator between drafted/undrafted
• Position-specific z-score and TSA calculations showed better discriminatory accuracy than overall-sample calculations
• SP showed best sprint performance; BSP and LM showed greater upper body strength emphasis
• TSA AUC: ALL = 0.604; SP = 0.704; BSP = 0.709; LM = 0.667 — position-normed TSA substantially better
• For every 1-point increase in 40-yd sprint z-score, probability of being drafted increased 1.7–2.4x depending on position
• Cohen's d for TSA: SP = 1.94, BSP = 2.42, LM = 3.03 — large effect sizes across all groups

## Limitations
Findings specific to NFL SC participants; may not generalize to athletes in other leagues or development pathways; position groupings (SP, BSP, LM) simplify nuanced positional differences; confounding factors such as training background, collegiate performance, and anthropometric data not included; QBs and specialists excluded; prediction of draft selection is only partially explained by physical metrics alone.

## Practical Implications
Strength and conditioning coaches should use position-normed z-scores (not raw scores or league-wide averages) when benchmarking athletes against combine standards. The 40-yard sprint is the most discriminating combine metric and should receive the greatest emphasis in speed development programs targeting NFL draft preparation. TSA provides a useful holistic athleticism index, but its predictive value is enhanced substantially when calculated within positional groups.

## Athlete Development Notes
In American football, position-specific z-scores and TSA from the NFL SC are effective tools for distinguishing elite athletes from sub-elite in talent identification. For collegiate S&C staff, using combine-normed positional benchmarks allows coaches to identify specific physical deficits (e.g., a lineman weak in bench press z-score but strong in sprint) and tailor development programs accordingly. The large Cohen's d values (1.94–3.03) confirm that physical test performance is a major separating factor between drafted and undrafted players.

## Return to Play Notes
The NFL SC physical testing battery — including 40-yd sprint, vertical jump, broad jump, bench press, 3-cone drill, and shuttle run — provides position-normed benchmarks that can be adapted as RTP performance targets following injury. Achieving position-group z-score thresholds for speed and power tests (particularly 40-yd dash and broad jump, which showed the strongest discriminatory power) can serve as objective RTP milestones for high-level football players recovering from lower extremity injuries.
