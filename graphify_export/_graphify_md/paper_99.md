# Green B, Lin M, Schache AG, McClelland JA, Semciw AI, Rotstein A, Cook J, Pizzari T. Calf muscle strain injuries in elite Australian Football players: A descriptive epidemiological evaluation. Scand J Med Sci Sports. 2020;30:174-184. https://doi.org/10.1111/sms.13552

## TLDR
Calf muscle strains in elite football are predominantly soleus injuries, and re-injuries almost exclusively involve soleus and older players — requiring about 19 extra days to RTP versus first-time injuries. Running-related injury mechanisms (acceleration, high-intensity running, steady-state running) add approximately 12 days to recovery compared to non-running mechanisms. Football performance staff should implement progressive running reloading protocols after all calf strains, establish high-speed running clearance tests before full RTP, and heighten re-injury surveillance for at least 8 weeks post-return, particularly in veteran players.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiological study analyzed 184 calf muscle strain injuries (CMSI) reported by 16 AFL clubs from 2014 to 2017 using the Soft Tissue Injury Registry. Data included player demographics, MRI findings, and time to reach four recovery milestones including return to play (RTP). Soleus injuries were far more prevalent than gastrocnemius injuries (84.6% vs 11.4%), and CMSI sustained during running-related activities required approximately 12 days longer to recover than those from non-running activities. Re-injuries involved significantly older players and required substantially longer recovery periods compared to index injuries.

## Methods
Descriptive epidemiological study using data from the Soft Tissue Injury Registry of the Australian Football League (STRAFL). Data from 16 of 18 AFL clubs across 4 consecutive seasons (2014-2017). Data included player characteristics, training and match history at time of injury, MRI findings, and time to reach four recovery milestones: pain-free walking, running at >90% of maximum speed, return to full training, and return to play. Index injuries were first-time injuries at a location; re-injuries were same-location injuries within two seasons of the index injury. MRI was available for 81% of cases. Mann-Whitney U tests assessed between-group differences in recovery times; effect size calculated as r = z/sqrtn. Significance set at P < .05.

## Findings
184 total CMSI occurred across 4 seasons (41-51 per year); 149 index injuries (81%) and 35 re-injuries (19%). Soleus was the primary muscle in 126/149 MRI-available cases (84.6%); gastrocnemius in 17 (11.4%). Median player chronological age was 25 years. Re-injuries involved significantly older players by playing age (133.3 vs 87.3 games, P=.006). Soleus injuries took 25.4 ± 16.2 days to RTP vs 19.1 ± 14.1 for gastrocnemius (P=.097). Running-related CMSI took 33.4 ± 21.6 days to RTP vs 21.3 ± 15.1 days for non-running-related CMSI (P=.001, r=0.28). Re-injuries required an extra 18.9 days to RTP compared to index injuries (P<.001, r=0.30) and an extra 16.2 days to return to full training (P=.001). Over half (54.3%) of re-injuries occurred within 2 months of the index injury. Almost all re-injuries involved the soleus (91.4%).

## Limitations
Comparison of injured versus uninjured AFL players was not possible since the STRAFL only collects data on injured players. Reporting of CMSI to the STRAFL was voluntary, potentially underestimating true incidence, particularly for preseason injuries. MRI was unavailable for 14.7% of injuries, excluding them from muscle-specific analyses. Detailed MRI injury features (edema length, tissue disruption) were not compared between groups. The study did not include a control cohort of uninjured players.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football staff should recognize that calf strains carry high re-injury risk, especially within 2 months of initial RTP. Running-related injury mechanisms substantially prolong recovery — athletes who strain their calf during high-speed running or acceleration need approximately 12 additional days beyond those injured through non-running mechanisms. Formal high-speed running clearance testing (>90% maximum speed) should be completed before full practice return. Older players carry significantly greater re-injury risk and should be managed with more conservative RTP thresholds and mandatory eccentric calf loading programs during rehabilitation and return to sport.

## Athlete Development Notes
Younger football players sustaining a first calf strain have a relatively favorable prognosis with median RTP around 19-25 days. Development programs should incorporate progressive calf loading, eccentric calf strengthening (soleus-specific loading at end range), and running volume monitoring to reduce both initial injury occurrence and re-injury risk. Preseason calf conditioning that emphasizes tendon-loading capacity is particularly important given that running-related mechanisms (acceleration, high-intensity sprint efforts) dominate the injury profile in field sports.

## Return to Play Notes
Re-injuries are almost exclusively soleus-based and require a median of approximately 42 days to RTP versus ~23 days for index injuries. RTP criteria should include pain-free walking, ability to run at >90% maximum speed, and completion of full-training sessions. Approximately 20% of re-injuries occurred before the median RTP time for index injuries, underscoring that premature clearance is a key recurrence driver. Medical staff should maintain heightened monitoring for at least 8 weeks post-RTP in older athletes and those with a history of soleus injury, and any subsequent training spike should trigger a re-evaluation of calf load tolerance.
