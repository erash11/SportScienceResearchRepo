# Ekstrand J, Van Zoest W, Gauffin H. Changes in head staff members in male elite-level football teams are associated with increased hamstring injury burden for that season: the UEFA Elite Club Injury Study. BMJ Open Sport & Exercise Medicine. 2023;9:e001640. https://doi.org/10.1136/bmjsem-2023-001640

## TLDR
Staff turnover in the fitness/performance coaching and sports medicine departments at elite football clubs is directly associated with dramatically elevated hamstring injury burdens — up to a 276% increase when a new head coach brings his own fitness staff. The head coach replacement alone does not significantly increase injury rates; it is the disruption of the performance and medical infrastructure that drives injury risk. For NFL and college football organizations, this research makes a compelling evidence-based case for maintaining continuity within strength and conditioning and sports medicine departments, even when coaching changes occur. When staff transitions are unavoidable, thorough handover protocols, gradual training philosophy introductions, and early-season load monitoring are essential injury mitigation strategies.

## Abstract
This observational cohort study from the UEFA Elite Club Injury Study (ECIS) evaluated whether changes in head coaching or medical staff before or during a season were associated with increased hamstring injury (HI) burden in 14 male elite-level European football clubs across three seasons (2019/2020, 2020/2021, 2021/2022). Staff changes occurred in 25 of 42 team-seasons (60%), with head coach replacement being the most frequent change. Replacing the head fitness/performance coach was associated with a statistically significant 63% increase in HI burden (p=0.017), and replacing the team doctor was associated with an 81% increase in HI burden (p=0.011). When a new head coach introduced his own fitness/performance coach, HI burden increased by 276% (p<0.001). An isolated head coach replacement was not significantly associated with increased HI burden.

## Methods
Observational cohort study with prospectively collected injury data and retrospectively collected staff change data. Fourteen clubs participating in the ECIS that qualified for the UEFA Champions League or Europa League in three latest seasons (2019/2020, 2020/2021, 2021/2022) were included. All 14 CMOs completed a SurveyMonkey questionnaire reporting changes in four head staff positions (head coach, head fitness/performance coach, head team doctor, head physiotherapist). Staff change data were cross-referenced with the Transfermarkt database for head coach changes. HI burden was calculated as days lost per 1000 hours of exposure. Student's t-test used for between-group comparisons with 95% CIs; two-sided tests; significance level p<0.05. 100% questionnaire response rate.

## Findings
49 total head staff changes occurred across 42 team-seasons; 60% of seasons included at least one change. Head coach replacement occurred in 48% of team-seasons and was not significantly associated with HI burden change alone (±0 days, 95% CI: −16 to 14). New head fitness/performance coach before or during season associated with +63% HI burden increase (24 to 39 days lost/1000h, 95% CI: 3 to 28, p=0.017). New head team doctor associated with +81% HI burden (26 to 47 days lost/1000h, 95% CI: 5 to 37, p=0.011). When a new head coach started the season bringing his own fitness/performance coach, HI burden increased by 175% compared to seasons without such changes. When a new head coach replaced during the season brought his own fitness coach, HI burden increased by 300%. Combined, a new head coach (before or during the season) bringing his own fitness/performance coach was associated with +276% HI burden (95% CI: 14 to 45, p<0.001). Head physiotherapist change was associated with a non-significant +10% HI burden change.

## Limitations
Relatively small sample size (14 clubs, 42 team-seasons) limits statistical power. Staff change data were collected retrospectively, introducing potential recall bias, although the main responsible team doctor was the data provider and was in position for all 3 seasons for 8 of 14 clubs. Causality cannot be inferred from this descriptive design. Study conducted on male professional football (soccer) clubs only; a women's ECIS was launched in 2017 but data were not yet available. Other staff roles (assistant coaches, assistant fitness coaches) were not tracked. Possible uncontrolled confounders include transfer activity and changes in player roster composition.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football organizations should treat continuity in strength and conditioning and sports medicine departments as a core injury prevention priority. When head coaching changes occur, retaining the existing performance staff significantly mitigates injury risk. When performance staff transitions are unavoidable, organizations should mandate formal handover protocols transferring player load history, injury history, and individualized training profiles to incoming staff. New performance coaches should implement a 'minimal intervention' strategy — gradually introducing their training philosophy rather than making immediate sweeping changes to periodization or training demands. The 276% increase in HI burden when a new coach brings his own fitness staff represents one of the strongest organizational injury risk signals in the sports medicine literature.

## Athlete Development Notes
Player development continuity is directly linked to stability in the performance and medical staff. Abrupt changes in training philosophy or periodization imposed by a new fitness coach without adequate knowledge of individual athletes' load histories and capacities can result in overloading or underloading that manifests as significantly elevated hamstring injury rates. Long-term athlete development programs should build institutional knowledge systems — load monitoring databases, individualized training profiles, injury history documentation — that survive staff transitions and allow incoming coaches to maintain appropriate loading for each athlete from day one of the new relationship.

## Return to Play Notes
Staff instability in the fitness/performance coaching and medical roles has direct RTP implications. When a new fitness coach or team doctor inherits athletes currently in hamstring injury rehabilitation, disrupted communication continuity and reduced individual athlete familiarity can compromise RTP decision quality. Organizations should ensure that RTP criteria, rehabilitation progression data, and individual athlete load histories are formally documented and explicitly transferred during all staff transitions. A new team doctor or fitness coach should receive comprehensive medical and performance briefings on all athletes currently in rehabilitation before assuming clinical or programming decision-making responsibility.
