# McCormick WF, Lomis MJ, Yeager MT, Tsavaris NJ, Rogers CD. Field Surface Type and Season-Ending Lower Extremity Injury in NFL Players. Transl Sports Med. 2024;2024:6832213.

## TLDR
Despite widespread perception that artificial turf causes more serious injuries, this three-season NFL study found no statistically significant difference in season-ending lower extremity injury rates between artificial and natural turf when injury counts are normalized for games played on each surface. Artificial turf did account for 60% of injuries, but it also hosted 56% of games. The apparent elevated raw injury count on turf reflects greater utilization, not inherently greater risk. Surface hardness, player preference, and specific injury subtypes may still warrant continued monitoring.

## Abstract
This study compared the incidence of in-game season-ending lower extremity injuries on natural and artificial playing surfaces in the NFL during the 2020, 2021, and 2022 seasons. Using publicly available data from multiple sports reference databases, researchers identified 199 season-ending lower extremity injuries across 891 games. Injury categories included knee ligament tears (ACL, PCL, LCL, MCL), knee tendon tears, leg/ankle fractures, and Achilles tendon ruptures. Injury rates were calculated per game played on each surface and compared using two-sample T-tests. No statistically significant difference in season-ending lower extremity injury rates was found between artificial turf (0.242/game) and natural turf (0.199/game) when accounting for the greater number of games played on artificial surfaces.

## Methods
Retrospective epidemiological study using publicly available NFL injury data from the 2020, 2021, and 2022 regular seasons and preseason games. Season-ending injuries involving the knee, lower leg, or ankle were identified through fantasydata.com, pro-football-reference.com, spotrac.com, ESPN.com, and NFL.com. Field surface at each stadium was classified as artificial (any percentage of artificial fiber) or natural grass (100% natural). Only in-game injuries were included; practice, off-season, and postseason injuries were excluded. Two-sample T-tests compared injury rates per game between surfaces for each season and the combined three-year period.

## Findings
Over three seasons, 199 season-ending lower extremity injuries occurred across 891 games. Of these, 79 (39.7%) occurred on natural turf and 120 (60.3%) on artificial turf. Games were distributed as 396 on natural turf (44.4%) and 495 on artificial turf (55.6%). Injury rate per game: natural turf 0.199 (95% CI 0.155–0.241), artificial turf 0.242 (95% CI 0.191–0.275). Two-tailed T-tests produced p-values of 0.72 (2020), 0.90 (2021), 0.19 (2022), and 0.25 for the full three-year period—none statistically significant. Knee ligament tears were the most common season-ending injury on both surfaces each year.

## Limitations
Only three seasons of data were analyzed, limiting statistical power for subcategorization by injury type. The study used exclusively publicly available data, preventing contact vs. non-contact injury classification. Hybrid surfaces were classified as artificial, which may not fully reflect playing characteristics. T-tests do not control for confounding variables such as team, weather, game phase, or player position. Season-ending injuries represent only a small fraction of total NFL injuries, so findings do not generalize to all injury types.

## Practical Implications
The oft-cited narrative that playing on artificial turf inherently causes more serious injuries is not supported when accounting for the volume of play on each surface. Organizations making field surface decisions should not rely solely on raw injury counts. However, monitoring for surface hardness and specific injury subcategories (e.g., ACL tears, non-contact injuries) should continue, as subgroup differences may exist. Player-reported preferences regarding surface feel and playability should also factor into surface selection even when not quantifiable in injury data.

## Athlete Development Notes
For NFL athletes training and competing on artificial turf, the overall risk of a season-ending lower extremity injury does not appear statistically higher than on natural grass when exposure is equated. Strength and conditioning staff can use this data to contextualize player concerns without dismissing them. Individual surface variation (hardness, drainage, fiber type) may affect injury risk more than surface classification alone, suggesting ongoing monitoring at the facility level is warranted.

## Return to Play Notes
No surface-specific return-to-play protocols are indicated by this data, as rates of season-ending injuries (ACL tears, Achilles ruptures, fractures) did not significantly differ between surfaces. Clinicians should not modify RTP timelines based solely on the surface on which an injury occurred. Future research incorporating contact mechanism, surface subtype, and non-contact injury classification would provide more actionable RTP guidance.
