# Askling CM, Tengvar M, Thorstensson A. Acute hamstring injuries in Swedish elite football: a prospective randomised controlled clinical trial comparing two rehabilitation protocols. Br J Sports Med. 2013;00:1–8.

## TLDR
A hamstring rehabilitation protocol emphasising lengthening exercises at long muscle-tendon lengths returned elite football players to play ~23 days faster than a conventional protocol, supporting specificity of training as a key rehabilitation principle.

## Abstract
This prospective randomised controlled trial compared two rehabilitation protocols for acute hamstring injuries in 75 Swedish elite football players verified by MRI. Players were assigned to a lengthening-focused protocol (L-protocol, n=37) or a conventional exercise protocol (C-protocol, n=38), with the primary outcome of days to return to full team training and match availability. Time to return was significantly shorter in the L-protocol (mean 28 days) compared to the C-protocol (mean 51 days), across both sprinting-type and stretching-type injuries. Only one reinjury was recorded across the 12-month follow-up, occurring in the C-protocol group. MRI and palpation measures confirmed that injury proximity to the ischial tuberosity and lesion length were independently correlated with longer return times.

## Methods
Prospective RCT in Swedish elite football (top two divisions); 75 MRI-confirmed acute hamstring injuries; stratified randomisation by gender and injury type (sprinting vs. stretching); L-protocol: three lengthening exercises (The Extender, The Diver, The Glider); C-protocol: three conventional exercises (contract-relax stretching, cable-pendulum, pelvic lift); supervised weekly, pain-free progression; Askling H-test as return-to-play criterion; 12-month reinjury follow-up; Mann-Whitney U test and Spearman correlations; SPSS v.19.

## Findings
• L-protocol mean return: 28 ± 15 days (range 8–58); C-protocol mean: 51 ± 21 days (range 12–94); p < 0.001.
• L-protocol faster for both sprinting-type (43 vs. 74 days) and stretching-type injuries (23 vs. 41 days).
• 54/75 injuries (72%) were sprinting-type; primary muscle was biceps femoris long head in 69% of cases.
• One reinjury recorded (C-protocol, sprinting-type, BFlh) vs. zero in L-protocol over 12 months.
• Shorter distance to ischial tuberosity and longer oedema length on MRI both correlated with longer return time (Spearman r = −0.736 to −0.832).
• MRI-negative players (n=11, all sprinting-type, all L-protocol) returned mean 6 days, significantly faster than MRI-positive sprinting injuries.

## Limitations
Single test leader (CMA) performed all clinical examinations and supervised protocols, increasing potential for bias; no independent blinding of clinical assessors; performance-motivated elite players may have returned faster than recreational populations; Askling H-test added ~7 days to rehabilitation period on average; no direct comparator data from prior RCTs in football.

## Practical Implications
Hamstring rehabilitation should prioritise exercises that load the muscle at long lengths under eccentric conditions (e.g., Nordic-style, hip-dominant lengthening movements). Establishing injury type (sprinting vs. stretching) and MRI characteristics at presentation provides early prognostic information and can guide expected return timelines. A functional criterion test (equivalent to the Askling H-test) should gate return-to-play decisions.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football: sprinting-type hamstring injuries dominate in skill positions (WR, DB, RB) due to high-speed running demands. Incorporating lengthening-based hamstring exercises (RDLs, Nordic curls, single-leg hip hinges at full range) into off-season and in-season programming addresses both primary prevention and rehabilitation specificity. Paper is from soccer/association football context.

## Return to Play Notes
Return to full team training and match availability occurred ~23 days sooner with the lengthening protocol (mean 28 vs. 51 days). Proximal free tendon involvement, injury closer to the ischial tuberosity, and longer MRI oedema length each independently predicted longer return times. A validated dynamic stability test (Askling H-test) should be passed before clearance to avoid premature return and reinjury.
