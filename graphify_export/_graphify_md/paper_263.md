# Walton, S. R., Carneiro, K. A., Smith-Ryan, A. E., Stoner, L., Kerr, Z. Y., Brett, B. L., Chandran, A., DeFreese, J. D., Mannix, R., Lempke, L. B., Echemendia, R. J., McCrea, M. A., Guskiewicz, K. M., & Meehan, W. P. III. (2025). Health-promoting behaviors and their associations with factors related to well-being among former National Football League players: An NFL-LONG study. Journal of Athletic Training, 60(2), 185–197. https://doi.org/10.4085/1062-6050-0537.23

## TLDR
In 1,784 former NFL players, lower sleep disturbance and more frequent moderate-to-vigorous exercise were the most consistent predictors of better subjective well-being across the lifespan. These two modifiable behaviors represent key clinical targets for improving overall health and well-being in former professional football players as they age.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional study from the NFL-LONG cohort examined associations between health-promoting behaviors (exercise frequency, diet quality, sleep duration, sleep disturbance) and well-being outcomes (Meaning and Purpose, Self-Efficacy, Ability to Participate in Social Roles and Activities) in 1,784 former NFL players (mean age 52.3 years). Multivariable linear regression models were fit for the full sample and for six age subgroups. Less sleep disturbance was associated with better outcomes on all three well-being measures across nearly all age groups. More frequent moderate-to-vigorous exercise was associated with better Meaning and Purpose and Social Role Participation. Diet quality and resistance training were not broadly associated with well-being outcomes.

## Methods
Cross-sectional survey (online via Qualtrics or paper-based) from the NFL-LONG study, conducted January 2019–April 2020. Former NFL players (n=1,784; mean age 52.3±16.3 years) completed the survey. Health-promoting behaviors assessed: moderate-to-vigorous exercise frequency, resistance training frequency, other wellness activities, REAPS diet quality score, nightly sleep duration, and PROMIS-29 Sleep Disturbance T-score. Well-being outcomes: PROMIS Meaning and Purpose SF-4a, PROMIS Adult Self-Efficacy SF-4a, PROMIS Ability to Participate in Social Roles and Activities 4-item scale. Multivariable linear regression models adjusted for demographic, behavioral, and functional covariates; separate models fit for full sample and six age subgroups (<30, 30–39, 40–49, 50–59, 60–69, 70+ years). Multiple imputation used for missing data.

## Findings
Sleep disturbance was negatively associated with Meaning and Purpose (β=−0.196), Self-Efficacy (β=−0.185), and Social Role Participation (β=−0.137) in the full sample and across almost all age subgroups. More frequent moderate-to-vigorous exercise was associated with better Meaning and Purpose (β=0.068) and Social Role Participation (β=0.151). Resistance training, diet quality, sleep duration, and other wellness activities were not broadly significant in the full-group models. Age-group specific patterns emerged, with exercise associations particularly pronounced among players aged 40–69 years. Diet quality was associated with better Self-Efficacy only among players <30 and 30–39 years.

## Limitations
Cross-sectional design limits causal inference. Survey response frequency was relatively low (1,784/15,025 = ~11.9%), limiting generalizability. Self-reported behaviors may be under- or over-estimated. Exercise session duration was not captured, so meeting aerobic guidelines cannot be confirmed. Data collection overlapped with the onset of COVID-19 in spring 2020, which may have affected exercise and sleep behaviors. Mild cognitive impairment and dementia—more prevalent in this population—were not directly measured and could affect self-reported outcomes.

## Practical Implications
Former NFL players represent a population at elevated risk for chronic health conditions and well-being decline as they age. Clinicians treating former players should prioritize sleep quality assessment and intervention alongside exercise prescription. Encouraging moderate-to-vigorous aerobic exercise—particularly in the 40–69 age range—appears to be an especially high-impact strategy for improving multiple dimensions of well-being.

## Athlete Development Notes
For active players approaching the end of their careers, establishing sustainable exercise habits and healthy sleep routines during their playing years may support long-term well-being post-retirement. Organizations should integrate lifestyle education and behavior-change support into career transition programs.

## Return to Play Notes
Sleep disturbance is both a common post-concussion symptom and a primary predictor of poor well-being in former players. This study reinforces the importance of targeting sleep quality as part of comprehensive concussion management and long-term athlete health monitoring, particularly as players age and accumulate career exposure.
