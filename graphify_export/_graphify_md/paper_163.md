# Cairns JT, Kolstad AT, Galarneau J-M, van den Berg CA, Jordan MJ, Schneider KJ, Pasanen K, Emery CA. Down, set, record: Assessing warm-up structure and use of neuromuscular training exercises in Canadian adolescent tackle football. International Journal of Sports Science & Coaching. 2025;1–11. https://doi.org/10.1177/17479541251373073

## TLDR
Canadian adolescent tackle football coaches are not implementing evidence-based neuromuscular training (NMT) warm-up programs, which have been shown to reduce musculoskeletal injuries by >35% and concussions by 59% in adolescent sports. Warm-ups are dominated by aerobic running and dynamic stretching, while strength, agility, balance/coordination, and head-and-neck control exercises — critical NMT components — collectively account for less than 10% of all exercises. Head-and-neck control exercises, which are especially important for concussion prevention in a collision sport, were used by only one team. Football programs should restructure warm-ups to include all five NMT components and extend active exercise time to the recommended 10–15 minutes.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional video-analysis study examined warm-up structure and neuromuscular training (NMT) component use across 46 warm-up sessions (23 practice, 23 game) from nine adolescent (ages 14–17) tackle football teams in Calgary, Canada during a nine-week 2022 season. Researchers coded warm-up footage for duration and exercise type across NMT components (aerobic, strength, agility, balance/coordination, head-and-neck control) and non-NMT components (dynamic and static stretching). Teams spent a median of 456.9 seconds total in warm-up with 266.7 seconds actively exercising, and 80% of exercises were aerobic (51%) or dynamic stretching (29%), while NMT-specific components (strength 9%, agility 3%, balance/coordination 1%, head-and-neck control 1%) were substantially underutilized.

## Methods
Cross-sectional video-analysis sub-study within the SHRed Concussions research program. Nine tackle football teams (Calgary Spring Football Association, April–June 2022) were filmed across 46 warm-ups (23 practice, 23 game) over a 9-week season. Dartfish software (v10.1) was used to code exercise components and durations. A full factorial mixed effects linear regression model compared warm-up structure by session type (game vs practice) and season period (weeks 1–3, 4–6, 7–9), using restricted maximum likelihood estimation and Kenward-Roger degrees of freedom.

## Findings
Median total warm-up time was 456.9 s (25th–75th percentile: 276.8–532.3 s); median active exercise time was 266.7 s (193.5–326.9 s). Aerobic exercises comprised 51% (283/550) and dynamic stretching 29% (158/550) of all exercises. NMT components: strength 9% (51 exercises), agility 3% (15), balance/coordination 1% (3), head-and-neck control 1% (6). No significant differences in warm-up structure between game and practice sessions. The only statistically significant seasonal change was that aerobic exercise time was 42.2 s longer in the middle season period (weeks 4–6) than the beginning (p=0.02). Only one team consistently used head-and-neck control exercises.

## Limitations
Minimum of 4 warm-ups filmed per team due to feasibility constraints limited precision of estimates. Presence of the research team may have altered coach behavior. Only team-structured warm-ups were examined, excluding individual player warm-ups. Exercise fidelity was not assessed. Some exercises were classified into a single component category despite potential overlap. Sample limited to Calgary adolescent male teams, limiting generalizability.

## Practical Implications
Tackle football programs must substantially restructure their warm-up protocols to incorporate all NMT components — particularly strength, agility, balance/coordination, and head-and-neck control — which are currently near-absent. Coaches should target 10–15 minutes of active NMT-based warm-up per session, 2–3 times per week, consistent with dose-response evidence. Head-and-neck control exercises should be standard given football's second-highest sport concussion rate. The video-analysis methodology used in this study provides a practical tool for coaching staff to audit their own warm-up practices.

## Athlete Development Notes
Adolescent tackle football players (ages 14–17) are not receiving evidence-based NMT warm-up preparation that develops strength, agility, balance/coordination, and head-and-neck control — all critical athletic attributes in a collision sport. Coaches should replace excess aerobic running time with structured NMT exercises targeting these components. Implementation of a tackle football-specific NMT program (analogous to FIFA 11+ or Rugby Activate) would provide a structured framework for coaches without specialized strength and conditioning knowledge.

## Return to Play Notes
This study does not directly address return-to-play populations. However, the near-absence of head-and-neck control and neuromuscular strength exercises in current warm-up practice has direct implications for concussion and musculoskeletal injury prevention, both of which affect RTP timelines. Programs that implement NMT warm-ups with head-and-neck control components and strength exercises may reduce primary concussion incidence and lower-limb injury rates, thereby reducing the frequency of RTP scenarios that football medical staff must manage.
