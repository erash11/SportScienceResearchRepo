# Hunzinger KJ, Caccese JB, Law CA, Wittmer RM, Buckley TA, Broglio SP, McAllister TW, McCrea MA, Pasquina PF, Schneider ALC, the CARE Consortium Investigators. Associations of Collegiate Football Career and Incident Concussion with Players' Health: A Longitudinal Study from the CARE Consortium. Sports Medicine. 2025. https://doi.org/10.1007/s40279-025-02234-1

## TLDR
Over a median 3.3-year collegiate football career, players showed no clinically meaningful negative changes in neurocognitive function, postural stability, or psychological health, suggesting that multiple seasons of repetitive head impacts and current concussion protocols do not produce significant performance declines on standard assessment tools.

## Abstract
This longitudinal study from the CARE Consortium examined associations between collegiate football careers and/or incident concussion and changes in neurocognitive function, postural stability, and physical/psychological health. A total of 574 collegiate football players (median age 18.0 years; 26% with incident concussion) completed baseline and exit evaluations using standardized concussion assessment tools including ImPACT, BESS, SCAT-5, and BSI-18. Adjusted linear regression with inverse probability of attrition weighting was used to compare changes between initial and exit assessments. Overall, athletes showed small, non-clinically meaningful improvements in neurocognitive functioning and postural stability, with the incident concussion group reporting fewer and less severe symptoms over time than the non-concussion group.

## Methods
Longitudinal cohort study using data from the NCAA-DOD CARE Consortium (30 universities). 574 football players completed both initial baseline and exit assessments. Measures included ImPACT (cognitive), BESS (postural stability), SCAT-5 (symptoms), and BSI-18 (psychological health). Adjusted linear regression models with stabilized inverse probability of attrition weighting were used. Participants categorized as incident concussion (n=149) vs. no incident concussion (n=425). Covariates included years of contact sport exposure, race, prior concussion history, primary position, and time between assessments.

## Findings
- Athletes overall showed statistically significant improvements in ImPACT Visual Memory (+4.54), ImPACT Visual Motor (+2.86), and BESS total score (-2.40) from baseline to exit
- No statistically significant changes in ImPACT Verbal Memory, Reaction Time, SAC, or BSI-18 scores in the overall population
- Incident concussion group reported fewer SCAT symptoms (difference -1.22; 95% CI -1.89 to -0.54) and lower symptom severity (-2.46; 95% CI -4.06 to -0.86) over time vs. non-concussion group
- BSI-18 somatization improved more in the incident concussion group (-0.55; 95% CI -0.93 to -0.17)
- All observed changes were small and did not meet thresholds for clinically meaningful difference

## Limitations
Large proportion of athletes did not complete exit assessments (heavily disrupted by COVID-19); differential participation may bias results despite attrition weighting. Repeated test exposure and practice effects likely contributed to observed improvements. Unable to determine testing condition stability at baseline and exit. Some athletes with unreported concussions may have been misclassified. Effect of post-concussion rehabilitation programs on outcomes is unknown.

## Practical Implications
Collegiate football programs and medical staff can be reassured that standard concussion assessment battery outcomes do not show clinically meaningful decline over a full college career. Current concussion management protocols appear sufficient for protecting short- to medium-term neurological and psychological health. However, longer follow-up into mid- and late-adulthood is needed to understand chronic effects of repetitive head impacts.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly relevant to American football athlete development. Data from 574 collegiate football players across Division I, II, and III programs indicate that participation through a full collegiate career (median 3.3 years) does not measurably harm neurocognitive function, postural stability, or psychological health as assessed by common clinical tools. Supports continued participation in football when paired with proper concussion surveillance.

## Return to Play Notes
The finding that athletes with incident concussion showed greater improvement in symptom measures over time (rather than worse outcomes) supports the effectiveness of current collegiate concussion management protocols. No clinically meaningful declines in ImPACT, BESS, or psychological health measures were observed at exit assessments, providing baseline evidence that return-to-play outcomes are not being compromised over multi-year careers. Future work should investigate resilience and rehabilitation contributions to these outcomes.
