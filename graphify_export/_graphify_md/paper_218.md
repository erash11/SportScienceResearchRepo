# Brophy RH, Wojtys EM, Mack CD, Hawaldar K, Herzog MM, Owens BD. Factors associated with the mechanism of ACL tears in the National Football League: a video-based analysis. Orthop J Sports Med. 2021;9(11):23259671211053301. doi:10.1177/23259671211053301

## TLDR
Most ACL tears in NFL games occur via noncontact or indirect contact mechanisms, but position matters enormously: offensive linemen sustain the majority of their ACL tears via direct contact at the line of scrimmage, while skill positions like wide receivers experience almost exclusively noncontact ACL tears. This means that injury prevention strategies cannot be one-size-fits-all in American football — linemen may benefit from bracing interventions while skill players need neuromuscular training programs targeting noncontact ACL risk. Understanding mechanism by position is essential for designing targeted prevention programs.

## Abstract
This descriptive epidemiology study used video analysis to characterize the mechanism of ACL tears in NFL games over six consecutive seasons (2014–2019). Two fellowship-trained sports medicine orthopaedic surgeons independently reviewed 140 video-confirmed ACL tears from the NFL's centralized electronic health record, classifying each as direct contact (to injured lower extremity), indirect contact (contact elsewhere), or no contact. The study found that 70% of NFL ACL tears occurred via noncontact mechanism (44% indirect contact, 26% no contact), with only 30% involving direct contact to the knee. However, injury mechanism varied substantially by position: 70% of offensive linemen's ACL tears were from direct contact, while wide receivers had zero direct contact ACL tears. Elevated BMI (≥35 kg/m²) was associated with greater likelihood of direct contact injury (53% vs 24%; P<.01), largely explained by the high BMI of linemen. Rookies had the fewest direct contact tears. Timing effects were observed — direct contact was most common in the third quarter (44%) and least common in the fourth quarter (13%; P<.01). Surface type (synthetic vs. natural grass) did not significantly affect the distribution of injury mechanism.

## Methods
Descriptive epidemiology study using prospective NFL injury surveillance data (2014–2019 regular and postseason games). ACL tears identified via the NFL centralized electronic health record (EHR). Film archives reviewed to identify 142 video-confirmed injuries; 140 were included after exclusions (2 events not visualizable). Two orthopaedic surgeons with sports medicine fellowship training independently reviewed videos and classified mechanism as direct contact, indirect contact, or no contact. Additional abstraction: player engaged with opponent, limb position, cleat-turf engagement, contact location. Interrater reliability evaluated via Cohen kappa (n=31 double-reviewed; kappa=0.80). Additional variables (BMI, years of service, surface type, game timing) extracted from NFL EHR and Game Statistics and Information System. Fisher exact chi-square tests for group comparisons; significance at P<.05.

## Findings
Of 140 ACL tears: 30% direct contact, 44% indirect contact, 26% no contact. Offensive linemen: 70% direct contact. Wide receivers: 0% direct contact. Defensive secondary: 8% direct contact. BMI ≥35 kg/m²: 53% direct contact vs 24% for BMI <35 kg/m² (P<.01); this finding confounded by position. Among non-linemen with BMI ≥35, only 1 of 9 tears was direct contact. Rookies: 18% direct contact vs 32% non-rookies (P=.22, NS). Third quarter: highest direct contact rate (44%); fourth quarter: lowest (13%; P<.01). Middle 8 weeks (weeks 5–12) of regular season: higher direct contact rate (38%) vs first/last 4 weeks (22%; P=.06). No significant difference in noncontact mechanism by surface type (synthetic 72% vs natural grass 69%).

## Limitations
Excludes practice (n=132) and preseason game (n=81) ACL tears, limiting generalizability to all ACL injuries. Cannot calculate injury rates or relative risks — descriptive study only. Small positional subgroup sizes limit ability to fully disentangle BMI vs position effects. Video quality varied; exact moment of injury could not always be determined with certainty. No practice-related injuries captured, although game ACLs exceed practice ACLs in frequency (>60% occur in games). Interrater reliability was high (kappa=0.80) but 4 injuries required adjudication. Study funded through NFL research relationship, which may introduce institutional bias.

## Practical Implications
ACL injury prevention in American football must be position-stratified. Offensive linemen, who sustain the majority of direct contact ACL tears at the line of scrimmage, represent a population that may benefit from protective knee bracing. Skill position players — particularly wide receivers, defensive backs, and linebackers — sustain predominantly noncontact ACL tears and are the appropriate target for neuromuscular and movement-quality-based prevention programs (e.g., FIFA 11+ equivalents). Teams should not apply a single prevention protocol uniformly across all positions. Performance staff should also be aware that fatigue-related noncontact mechanisms may be elevated in the fourth quarter, supporting the importance of conditioning and rotation management late in games.

## Athlete Development Notes
For football athlete development, this study confirms that noncontact ACL risk is the dominant concern for skill positions throughout their careers, while linemen face a distinct contact-mediated risk profile. Developmental programs for skill position players should prioritize landing mechanics, deceleration technique, single-leg stability, hip and hamstring strength, and knee valgus control as injury prevention foundations. For linemen at all levels, technique coaching in blocking and controlled engagement — alongside appropriate bracing consideration — should be integrated into position-specific development curricula.

## Return to Play Notes
While this study characterizes injury mechanism rather than RTP outcomes, the findings have important RTP implications. Understanding that a player's ACL tear was contact-driven (especially for linemen) versus noncontact has ramifications for rehabilitation emphasis and re-injury prevention. A lineman returning from a direct-contact ACL tear may benefit from bracing post-RTP, while a skill player returning from a noncontact ACL tear requires neuromuscular readiness benchmarks to address the movement deficits that predisposed to the original injury. Position-specific mechanism awareness should be incorporated into RTP decision frameworks for NFL and elite football players.
