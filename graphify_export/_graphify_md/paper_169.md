# Jildeh TR, Okoroha KR, Taylor KA, Buckley P, Mehta S, Mehran N, Moutzouros V. Effect of Concussions on the Performance of Running Backs and Wide Receivers in the National Football League. Am J Sports Med. 2019. DOI: 10.1177/0363546519864581

## TLDR
NFL running backs and wide receivers who sustained concussions returned to play at a very high rate (89% within the same season, missing ~1.5 games on average) and maintained performance levels comparable to uninjured controls both acutely and up to 3 years post-injury. Despite common concern, concussions did not measurably degrade on-field offensive production as measured by power rating. This is reassuring for staff managing return-to-play timelines but does not diminish the importance of rigorous concussion protocols given documented long-term neurological risks.

## Abstract
This cohort study examined the effect of concussions on the performance of NFL running backs and wide receivers over 4 seasons (2012-2015). A total of 38 concussed players were compared to 139 matched controls using an offensive power rating metric (total yards/10 + touchdowns x 6). Player performance was tracked up to 3 seasons before and after the injury season to assess acute and longitudinal changes.

## Methods
Cohort study (Level 3 evidence). NFL injury reports, player profiles, and game summaries were used to identify 38 running backs/wide receivers who sustained concussions during 2012-2015 seasons. A matched control group of 139 players without concussion history was assembled. Performance was quantified using an offensive power rating (total yards/10 + 6 x touchdowns) calculated for 3 seasons before and after the index injury season. Repeated measures analyses controlled for age and NFL experience. Statistical significance set at p < .05.

## Findings
89% of concussed players (34/38) returned to play within the same season, missing a mean of 1.5 ± 0.9 games; average time to RTP was 18.9 ± 7.6 days. Power ratings were statistically similar between concussed and control players at all time points. No significant difference in 1-year power rating delta (-1.2 ± 4.8 vs -1.1 ± 3.9, P=.199) or 3-year delta (-3.6 ± 8.0 vs -3.0 ± 4.5, P=.219). Within-season pre- vs. post-injury performance did not significantly differ (8.1 ± 4.3 vs 7.8 ± 3.7, P=.41). Two retirements were directly attributed to concussion.

## Limitations
Injury data were collected from publicly available sources and may be subject to inaccuracies; concussions may be underreported due to athlete self-reporting and organizational variance. Study was limited to running backs and wide receivers only, limiting generalizability. History of prior concussion was not controlled. The power rating metric may oversimplify multifactorial human performance. A time-period bias existed between concussion and control groups.

## Practical Implications
NFL staff can have reasonable confidence that running backs and wide receivers who follow proper concussion protocols will return to comparable performance levels. The high RTP rate (~89% same season) supports aggressive but protocol-driven rehabilitation. The longer RTP timeline (~19 days) vs. earlier eras likely reflects improved medical standards and should be maintained. Staff should not compress return timelines based on performance pressure, given documented long-term neurological risks even when short-term performance is preserved.

## Athlete Development Notes
Young skill position players who sustain concussions should not be expected to suffer long-term performance decline if managed properly. Development pipelines should include standardized concussion protocols; tracking offensive metrics (yards, touchdowns, power rating) over multiple seasons is a useful tool for objectively monitoring post-concussion recovery in skill players.

## Return to Play Notes
This study directly informs RTP decisions for running backs and wide receivers post-concussion. The ~19-day average RTP and 1.5-game absence is a useful benchmark for NFL medical staffs. All 38 players eventually returned to NFL competition, confirming that high-level return is achievable. RTP criteria should prioritize neurological clearance over performance metrics, as performance did not decline upon return when proper protocols were followed.
