# Walton SR, Kerr ZY, Powell JR, Giovanello KS, McCrea MA, Guskiewicz KM, Brett BL. An 18-Year Study of Changes in Neurocognitive Function and Associations with Repetitive Head Trauma among Former Collegiate American Football Players: A Case Series. Archives of Clinical Neuropsychology. 2025;00:1–10.

## TLDR
Group-level neurocognitive test performance did not significantly decline over 18 years among former collegiate football players now in early midlife, and neither concussion history nor repetitive head impact exposure was related to cognitive change scores, though the high individual variability underscores the need for larger longitudinal studies.

## Abstract
This preliminary longitudinal case series examined neurocognitive test performance changes over approximately 18 years in 31 former collegiate American football players (mean age at follow-up 38.4±1.3 years), assessed at baseline during college (T1; c.1999–2001) and at 18-year follow-up (T2; c.2016–2018). A battery of paper-and-pencil neurocognitive tests was administered at both time points: HVLT-R Immediate and Delayed Recall, FAS Verbal Fluency, Symbol Digit Modalities Test (SDMT), and Trail Making Test-B (TMT-B). Neither sport-related concussion (SR-CHx) history nor the Head Impact Exposure Estimate (HIEE) was significantly associated with changes in neurocognitive test scores over the study period. Group-level cognitive performance did not significantly change over 18 years, though individual-level variability was extremely high.

## Methods
Longitudinal case series; n=31 former collegiate American football players who participated in the original NCAA Concussion Study (T1: August 1999–January 2002) and a 15-year follow-up study (T2: July 2016–August 2018). Neurocognitive battery: HVLT-R Immediate Recall, HVLT-R Delayed Recall, FAS Verbal Fluency, SDMT, and TMT-B. Raw change scores (T2–T1) were computed alongside Cohen's d effect sizes and coefficients of variation (CoV). Repeated-measures ANCOVA models controlled for estimated premorbid ability (WTAR). Multivariable linear regression models tested associations of SR-CHx (categorical and continuous) and HIEE with change scores. Exploratory Spearman correlations examined associations between cognitive change and BSI-18 symptom domains, PSQI sleep quality, AUDIT alcohol use, DAST drug abuse, and total years of football play.

## Findings
- No significant group-level changes in any neurocognitive test over 18 years (all p-values ≥ 0.06); small-to-medium effect sizes (Cohen's d: 0.18–0.41).
- Individual-level variability was very high across all measures (CoV: 244%–560%), with both improvements and declines observed across participants.
- Neither SR-CHx nor HIEE was significantly associated with change in any neurocognitive test outcome (all ps > 0.05); SR-CHx as a continuous variable approached significance for HVLT-R Immediate Recall (p=0.054).
- Higher BSI-18 Somatic symptom scores at T2 were significantly correlated with greater declines in HVLT-R Immediate Recall (ρ=−0.395, p=0.028) and SDMT (ρ=−0.380, p=0.035).
- Higher DAST scores (drug abuse screening) were significantly correlated with greater declines in HVLT-R Immediate Recall (ρ=−0.370, p=0.040).
- Over 40% of participants had HVLT-R scores ≥1.5 SD below normative means at T1, and far fewer at T2, suggesting potential practice/learning effects or true improvement.

## Limitations
Very small sample (n=31) substantially limits statistical power. No non-athlete control group for comparison. Self-reported concussion history and HIEE reliance introduces recall and estimation error. HIEE does not capture pre-high school participation. Cross-sectional exploratory factors (BSI-18, DAST) measured only at T2, precluding examination of concurrent changes. Performance validity measures not employed. Limited racial/ethnic and socioeconomic diversity reduces generalizability.

## Practical Implications
Former collegiate football players at approximately 15+ years post-sport do not show uniform neurocognitive decline as a group, which provides some reassurance for players and staff concerned about long-term brain health. However, the wide individual variability means that population-level reassurances do not preclude meaningful decline in specific individuals. Somatic symptom burden and substance use emerged as potentially meaningful correlates of cognitive change and should be monitored and addressed in former player health programs.

## Athlete Development Notes
Relevant to long-term athlete health monitoring in American football. The lack of a clear group-level cognitive decline trajectory over 18 years among former collegiate players in early midlife provides longitudinal evidence that competitive collegiate football does not universally result in measurable neurocognitive impairment by middle age. High individual variability suggests that identifying who is most at risk requires larger prospective studies accounting for lifestyle, health behaviors, and biological factors.

## Return to Play Notes
Not a return-to-play study; participants were 15+ years removed from collegiate play. However, the finding that somatic symptoms and substance use — rather than head trauma history alone — correlate with cognitive change scores suggests that comprehensive health monitoring protocols for retiring players should extend beyond concussion history to include mental and physical health screening as part of long-term athlete care.
