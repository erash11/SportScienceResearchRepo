# Park, G., & Kent, R. (2020). Foot shape analysis of professional American Football players. Footwear Science. https://doi.org/10.1080/19424280.2020.1769203

## TLDR
NFL players' feet vary most in overall size, arch shape, and curvature—factors that conventional Brannock devices do not fully capture. Linemen and hybrid players are larger in all dimensions and more likely to have flat feet than skill players. This data should inform position-specific last design and footwear selection to optimize fit, performance, and injury prevention at the elite level.

## Abstract
This study quantified the foot shape of NFL players to identify characteristics that inform position-specific footwear design. Statistical shape analysis (SSA) was performed on 3D scans of 290 NFL players' feet from 4 teams during preseason training camps. Eight foot shape measurements were defined to reflect the principal components (PCs) of shape variability. The first three PCs explained 82.3% of foot shape variation: foot volume/size (34.4%), medial longitudinal arch shape with foot length (26.2%), and foot curvature (21.7%). Mean foot length was 293 ± 13.7 mm; width 110.6 ± 6.8 mm. Significant between-position differences were observed primarily in size-related measures. Flat foot prevalence (zero arch depth) was higher in linemen and hybrid groups than skill players.

## Methods
A subset of 290 left-foot 3D scans (from 1,451 total scanned across all 32 NFL teams) was extracted from 4 teams during preseason training camps. Scans were collected with a laser foot scanner (INFOOT USB IFU-S-01) at 1 mm resolution, processed in Meshmixer v3.5. Correlation-based PCA was performed on 240 pseudo-landmarks from 8 transverse cross-sectional contours. Eight foot measurements (foot length, width, arch length, heel width, mid-foot width, ball girth, arch depth, flare angle) were defined and compared across three position groups (linemen, n = 83; hybrid, n = 68; skill, n = 135) using statistical comparisons.

## Findings
First three PCs explained 82.3% of shape variation: PC1 = foot volume/size (34.4%), PC2 = arch shape with foot length (26.2%), PC3 = foot curvature (21.7%). All measurements except arch depth and flare angle differed significantly between position groups (p < .05). Linemen had the largest feet overall (length 303.7 mm, width 117.0 mm, ball girth 277.0 mm). Flat foot prevalence (arch depth = 0) was greater in linemen and hybrid groups than skill players. Longer feet associated with lower arch height, consistent with prior literature.

## Limitations
Only left-foot scans were analyzed. Sample was a subset of 4 teams from the full 32-team database, though weight and height distributions were comparable to the full NFL population. Kickers and punters were excluded due to insufficient scan numbers. Scans captured static unloaded foot shape; dynamic loading during play may alter effective foot dimensions.

## Practical Implications
NFL footwear design should be position-specific, accounting for differences in foot size and arch shape between linemen, hybrid, and skill players. Flat-foot prevalence in linemen and hybrid players supports dedicated arch support and last modifications for those groups. The 8 measurements identified here provide a practical framework for footwear fitting systems beyond the traditional Brannock device.

## Athlete Development Notes
Understanding position-specific foot morphology in American football players can guide footwear selection during player development pipelines. Proper footwear fit improves comfort, performance, and reduces injury risk from conditions such as fifth metatarsal fractures, turf toe, Lisfranc injuries, and blisters, which are relevant across developmental and elite levels.

## Return to Play Notes
Foot shape data can inform orthotics and footwear modifications for athletes returning from foot and ankle injuries. Position-specific arch and curvature profiles should be considered when prescribing return-to-play footwear to ensure mechanical support is appropriate for the demands of the player's position.
