# Rosbrook P, Looney DP, Margolis LM, Perez Yudin S, Hostler D, Pryor RR, Pryor JL. Dietary Carbohydrates Influence the Performance Outcomes of Short-Term Heat Acclimation. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise. 2025 (Published ahead of print). DOI: 10.1249/MSS.0000000000003726

## TLDR
Carbohydrate intake during and after an intense, exercise-based heat acclimation protocol is a critical determinant of whether performance actually improves after HA. Despite both groups achieving equivalent thermoregulatory adaptations, only athletes consuming ~6 g/kg/day of carbohydrate (70% kcal) showed meaningful improvements in a 3.22 km heat-based run time trial—improving ~7-8% versus essentially no change in the lower-CHO group. For football strength and conditioning coaches running summer camp or two-a-day training blocks in heat, this means that failing to meet carbohydrate targets during high-exertion heat exposure can nullify expected performance gains from heat adaptation, even when physiological markers of acclimation look normal.

## Abstract
This RCT examined whether a high-carbohydrate (HC) diet enhances 3.22 km run time trial (TT) performance in the heat following a 6-day, exercise-intensive short-term heat acclimation (STHA) protocol. Fourteen healthy military-age males were pair-matched by fitness and randomized to either a HC diet (70% kcal from CHO, ~6 g/kg/day, n=7) or a lower-CHO comparison diet (COMP; 35% kcal from CHO, ~3 g/kg/day, n=7) during and for one week after HA. Both groups achieved equivalent physiological heat adaptations (reduced HR, rectal and skin temperature, increased sweat rate), but only the HC group improved TT performance at one day (p=0.01, g=0.59) and five days post-HA (p=0.04, g=0.59), with HC running 7.0±4.9% faster than COMP at one day post-HA (p=0.03, g=1.23).

## Methods
Randomized controlled trial; N=14 healthy males of military age (18-40 years), paired by VO2peak and training volume; 6-day STHA protocol (6 sessions in a hot room: 39.3°C, 38.3% RH) with high-intensity intervals until Tre reached 38.5°C, then maintained Tre 38.5-39.0°C for ≥60 min; HC diet: 70% kcal CHO (~6 g/kg/day); COMP diet: 35% kcal CHO (~3 g/kg/day); isonitrogenous (1.4 g/kg/day protein); 3.22 km self-paced run TT in heat (37.2°C, 41.7% RH) assessed pre-HA (HST1), 1 day post-HA (HST2), and 5 days post-HA (HST3); VO2peak assessed in hot room; mixed-model ANCOVA with HST1 as covariate; Bonferroni post-hoc corrections.

## Findings
Both groups acclimated equally, showing significant reductions in end-walk HR, rectal temperature (Tre), skin temperature (Tsk), and increased sweat rate at HST2 and HST3 (all p<0.01). HC group ran significantly faster at HST2 vs. COMP (959±103 s vs. 1067±172 s, p=0.02, g=0.71). HC improved TT from baseline at HST2 (p=0.01, g=0.59) and HST3 (p=0.04, g=0.59); COMP did not improve at either time point. HC showed 7.0±4.9% TT reduction at HST2 vs. 0.7±4.7% for COMP (p=0.03, g=1.23) and 8.4±4.9% vs. 2.8±3.9% improvement at HST3 (p=0.01, g=1.18). No between-group differences in any physiological strain marker during TTs.

## Limitations
Small sample size (n=7 per group) limits statistical power. Only male participants; findings may not generalize to female athletes. Participants could not be blinded to diet assignment. The HA exercise protocol was not pair-matched or randomized across sessions due to individual capacity differences. The 35% kcal CHO comparison diet, while representing the lowest recommended CHO floor for active individuals, is below the habitual intake of young active males (~48% kcal), potentially exaggerating the contrast. No muscle glycogen measurements were taken to directly confirm glycogen status differences between groups.

## Practical Implications
For NFL and college football coaching and performance staffs, this study directly challenges the assumption that completing a heat acclimation block guarantees performance improvements. If athletes are not meeting carbohydrate targets (~6 g/kg/day) during pre-season two-a-days or training camp in the heat, they may undergo the physiological stress of HA without realizing the performance benefits. Nutrition staffs should actively ensure CHO targets are met during any heat-intensive training block, particularly when athletes face high energy demands, limited meal access, or selective eating patterns.

## Athlete Development Notes
During pre-season training camp and summer conditioning—periods of heavy heat exposure in football—athletes' carbohydrate availability may be the limiting factor determining whether the training stimulus translates to performance adaptation. Programs should prioritize nutrition education and intake monitoring during heat-intensive developmental periods, particularly for younger athletes who may under-eat due to fatigue, unfamiliar environments, or poor nutrition habits. Meeting ~6 g/kg/day CHO during HA blocks appears to be the threshold for converting heat stress into performance gains.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes returning to practice in hot conditions after injury-related absence may face an unacclimatized state combined with potential nutritional deficits from their recovery period. This study suggests that ensuring adequate carbohydrate intake during the re-acclimation phase is critical for safely restoring performance capacity in the heat. RTP protocols for summer and pre-season returns should include a nutritional assessment component to confirm CHO availability supports the heat re-acclimation process.
