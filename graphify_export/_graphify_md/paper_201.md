# Hoge C, Sabbagh R, Morgan M, Grawe BM. Epidemiology of Youth and High School American Football-Related Injuries Presenting to United States Emergency Departments: 2010-2019. Physician Sportsmed. 2021. doi:10.1080/00913847.2021.1931980

## TLDR
Across a decade of national ED surveillance data, football injury risk for both youth and high school athletes significantly declined — suggesting that rule changes, equipment improvements, and coaching technique initiatives since 2010 are showing measurable impact. However, high school athletes carry a substantially higher overall injury burden, with nearly double the concussion risk and over 5 times the dislocation risk compared to youth players. Injury prevention efforts must be age-group-specific, targeting knee and shoulder injuries in high school athletes and fractures in youth players.

## Abstract
This cross-sectional retrospective study queried the NEISS database (2010-2019) to compare football-related ED injury characteristics and rates in youth (ages 6-12) and high school (ages 14-18) athletes. A total of 2,827,751 weighted national estimates were identified from 89,236 unique cases. High school athletes accounted for 59.87% of injuries. The overall ED injury risk was 16.17 per 1,000 participants/year for high school athletes versus 11.97 for youth. Both groups showed significant decreases in injury risk over the decade (p < 0.0001). Head/neck and hand/wrist injuries were most common in both groups. High school athletes were almost twice as likely to present with knee injuries and 2.37 times more likely with shoulder injuries. Youth athletes were 2.5 times more likely to sustain shoulder fractures while high school athletes had over 5 times the risk of dislocations.

## Methods
Cross-sectional retrospective study querying the Consumer Product Safety Commission's NEISS database from 2010-2019 for football-related injuries (Product Code 1211) in ages 6-12 (youth) and 14-18 (high school). Age 13 was excluded to minimize overlap. National weighted estimates and 95% CIs were calculated using IBM SPSS v27. Chi-square with odds ratios assessed bivariate associations. Poisson regression evaluated trends in injury risk, participation counts, and weighted estimates over time. Injury risk was calculated as injuries per 1,000 participants/year using SFIA (youth) and NFHS (high school) participation data.

## Findings
2,827,751 weighted national estimates of football ED injuries (2010-2019); high school athletes comprised 59.87%. Overall injury risk: 16.17/1,000/year (high school) vs 11.97/1,000/year (youth). Both groups showed significant annual decreases (5.27%/year youth; 4.92%/year high school; p < 0.0001). Head/neck and hand/wrist were the most common injury sites in both groups. High school athletes were ~2x more likely to present with knee injuries and 2.37x more likely with shoulder injuries per 1,000/year. Youth athletes were 2.5x more likely to sustain shoulder fractures; high school athletes were ~10x more likely to sustain shoulder dislocations. High school athletes had almost double concussion risk and over 5x the dislocation risk compared to youth per year.

## Limitations
NEISS only captures injuries presenting to EDs, underrepresenting total injury burden and emphasizing more severe injuries. The database codes only the most severe injury per encounter; multiple injuries in one visit are not captured. Participation data (SFIA/NFHS) account only for core or team participants, not recreational players, potentially exaggerating injury rates. NFHS participation data were unavailable for 2019-2020 due to COVID-19. The study is limited to ages 6-12 and 14-18, excluding age 13.

## Practical Implications
The declining injury risk trend since 2010 provides support for continued investment in rule changes, improved equipment, and coaching education as effective public health interventions. However, the persistent age-related differences in injury patterns — particularly the high school athletes' elevated concussion and dislocation risk — indicate that prevention programming must be age-differentiated. High school programs should emphasize shoulder and knee stability, while youth programs should focus on bone health and fracture-risk awareness.

## Athlete Development Notes
Youth football athletes are at higher relative risk for skeletal fractures (including shoulder fractures) due to open physes and immature neuromuscular control, while high school athletes have greater risk for ligamentous and soft tissue injuries such as dislocations and sprains consistent with skeletally maturing bodies. Developmental programming should account for these physiological differences, incorporating age-appropriate contact progressions, movement skill development, and load management.

## Return to Play Notes
This study does not directly address RTP timelines, but the high rates of concussion, knee, and shoulder injuries in high school football players — conditions associated with significant recovery periods — underscore the importance of structured RTP protocols at the youth and scholastic levels. Concussion rates were nearly double in high school compared to youth athletes, making standardized concussion assessment and graduated RTP protocols especially critical at the high school level.
