# Adlou B, Grace J, Wilburn C, Weimar W. College-to-NFL Stadium Turf Transitions as a Risk Factor for Lower Extremity Non-Contact Injuries in Rookie Players: A 13-Year Cohort Analysis. Healthcare. 2025;13(14):1415. doi: 10.3390/healthcare13121415

## TLDR
College-to-NFL surface transitions do not independently drive lower extremity non-contact injury risk in rookie players — but position does. Skill players (WR, RB, DB, CB, S) had 88% higher adjusted odds of LE-NCI compared to hybrid position players (AOR=1.88; 95% CI: 1.20-2.97; p=0.006) regardless of turf type. Cumulative LE-NCI incidence reached 33.1% by season end, with injury acceleration clustering between weeks 4 and 10. NFL medical staff should concentrate prevention resources on skill position neuromuscular training and heightened monitoring during the weeks 4-10 high-risk window, rather than adjusting surface-transition protocols.

## Abstract
This retrospective cohort study examined 826 first- and second-round NFL draft picks (2012-2024) to determine whether the transition in home stadium turf type from the final college season to the rookie NFL season influenced lower extremity non-contact injury (LE-NCI) risk. Six transition categories were defined (Grass-to-Grass, Grass-to-Artificial, Artificial-to-Grass, Artificial-to-Artificial, Grass-to-Hybrid, Artificial-to-Hybrid). Competing risk analysis (Aalen-Johansen) and multivariable logistic regression assessed LE-NCI associations with turf transition, position group, college multi-surface training access, and draft cohort.

## Methods
Retrospective cohort study; 826 NFL first- and second-round draft picks (2012-2024) from Auburn University Sport Biomechanics Lab (one kicker excluded). Players categorized into four position groups: Linemen (n=305, 36.9%), Skill (n=291, 35.2%), Hybrid (n=177, 21.4%), Quarterback (n=53, 6.4%). Home stadium surface classified as natural grass, artificial turf, or hybrid, yielding six college-to-NFL transition types. College multi-surface training access coded as binary variable. Primary outcome: first LE-NCI during rookie season (preseason + regular season), defined as lower extremity injuries occurring without direct player or object contact. Data sourced from Pro-Football-Reference, NFL.com, ESPN.com, CSB Sports. Aalen-Johansen estimator for cumulative incidence; multivariable logistic regression (two models); sensitivity analysis.

## Findings
21.2% (175/826) of players sustained a LE-NCI. No surface transition category was significantly associated with LE-NCI versus Grass-to-Grass reference (p=0.175 bivariate). Skill position players had significantly higher LE-NCI odds vs. hybrid players (AOR=1.88; 95% CI: 1.20-2.97; p=0.006). College multi-surface training access was not protective (AOR=0.97; p=0.874). Cumulative LE-NCI incidence reached 33.1% (95% CI: 28.7-37.5%) by week 18, with steepest acceleration between weeks 4 and 10. Skill players had the highest raw LE-NCI rate (30.2%; 88/291); quarterbacks the lowest (13.2%; 7/53). Grass-to-Grass skill players had the highest absolute LE-NCI count (27/70; 38.6%).

## Limitations
Retrospective design with 32% of injured players lacking definitive injury type documentation; home stadium surface used as proxy for actual playing surface, not capturing practice or away-game surfaces; footwear-surface interactions not measured; climate and surface temperature not captured; hybrid surface transition groups had small sample sizes limiting power; position-by-transition interaction terms could not be tested due to sparse strata; limited to rounds 1-2 draft picks.

## Practical Implications
NFL teams should not prioritize surface-transition-based protocols for rookie injury prevention. Instead, resources should concentrate on skill position players through enhanced neuromuscular training targeting cutting mechanics, landing strategies, and proprioceptive conditioning. The weeks 4-10 window represents the highest-risk period and warrants intensified soft tissue screening, modified training loads, and heightened monitoring specifically for skill positions entering regular season play.

## Athlete Development Notes
The 88% higher LE-NCI risk for skill positions points to the movement demands of those roles — high-speed cutting, acceleration, and deceleration — as primary injury drivers in the NFL rookie year. Rookie development programs should incorporate position-specific neuromuscular training well before and throughout the professional transition, regardless of the home stadium surface. Early-career load management for skill positions should be prioritized.

## Return to Play Notes
The temporal clustering of LE-NCIs between weeks 4 and 10 provides a data-driven window for heightened RTP caution. Rookies returning from early-season LE-NCIs during this high-risk window should be managed conservatively, with objective neuromuscular readiness assessments before full return to practice and play. Cumulative incidence reaching 33.1% by season end underscores the ongoing injury burden throughout the rookie year.
