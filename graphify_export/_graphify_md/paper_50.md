# Altarriba-Bartes A, Drobnic F, Til L, Malliaropoulos N, Montoro JB, Cos F. Analysis of Recovery Methods' Efficacy Applied up to 72 Hours Postmatch in Professional Football: A Systematic Review With Graded Recommendations. J Strength Cond Res. 2020;34(3):829-848.

## TLDR
No single recovery modality has strong Level A evidence in professional football post-match. Cold-water immersion and sleep/nutrition carry the best current evidence, while most other modalities receive only C-grade recommendations due to low-quality or inconsistent data. Practitioners should prioritize sleep and nutrition as the foundation of any post-match recovery protocol.

## Abstract
This systematic review evaluated the efficacy of recovery methods applied within 72 hours of competitive match play in professional football (soccer). Thirty studies meeting inclusion criteria were analyzed, covering modalities including cold-water immersion, contrast water therapy, active recovery, compression garments, sleep, nutrition, and massage. Recovery outcomes assessed included muscle damage biomarkers, perceived muscle soreness, neuromuscular function, and perceptual wellness. Graded recommendations were assigned to each modality based on the level and quality of evidence.

## Methods
Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines; electronic database search (PubMed, SPORTDiscus, CINAHL, Cochrane) through 2018; 30 studies included after screening; recovery modalities categorized and evidence graded using a modified Oxford Centre for Evidence-Based Medicine framework (Level A–D); outcomes included CK, LDH, muscle soreness (VAS/DOMS), jump performance, sprint performance, and perceived recovery.

## Findings
• Cold-water immersion (CWI): moderate evidence for reducing perceived muscle soreness and CK; best supported post-match modality
• Contrast water therapy: some evidence for perceptual recovery but limited objective performance data
• Active recovery: inconsistent evidence; may benefit low-intensity clearance sessions
• Compression garments: limited evidence in football-specific populations
• Sleep and nutrition: foundational to recovery; indirect but strong mechanistic support
• Massage: limited and low-quality evidence in professional football context
• No modality achieved Level A evidence in professional football-specific populations
• Most interventions studied at ≤24h post-match; 48–72h window is understudied
• Overall evidence base is limited by heterogeneous study designs, small samples, and lack of ecological validity

## Limitations
High heterogeneity in study designs, populations, intervention protocols, and outcome measures; most studies conducted on amateur or semi-professional players; ecological validity of laboratory-based recovery studies to actual match-play scenarios is limited; publication bias likely; recovery outcomes often assessed at a single post-match time point rather than the full 72-hour window; competition schedule and prior training load not consistently controlled.

## Practical Implications
Practitioners should implement a tiered post-match recovery protocol with sleep and nutrition as non-negotiable foundations, followed by CWI (10–15 min at 10–15°C) as the most evidence-supported active modality. Decisions about additional modalities (contrast water therapy, compression, massage) should be guided by individual player response, logistical feasibility, and match schedule. Research gaps at 48–72 hours post-match are significant and limit confident prescription for congested fixture periods.

## Athlete Development Notes
Note: This paper examines professional football (soccer), not American football. The post-match recovery modalities reviewed — CWI, contrast therapy, active recovery, nutrition, sleep — are broadly applicable to American football, particularly in the 24–72 hours following games. The lack of Level A evidence for any single modality supports the use of individualized, multi-modal protocols rather than prescriptive team-wide approaches.

## Return to Play Notes
The 72-hour post-match recovery window is critical for neuromuscular restoration before the next training session. CWI remains the most evidence-supported active recovery tool, with the strongest effects on perceived soreness and inflammatory markers. For RTP planning, the understudied 48–72h window is particularly relevant in congested schedules; practitioners should monitor neuromuscular readiness (e.g., CMJ force-time metrics) alongside perceptual data throughout this window.
