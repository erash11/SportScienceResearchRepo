# Silva H, Nakamura FY, Serpiello FR, Ribeiro J, Roriz P, Marcelino R. Adapting the percentage intensity method to assess accelerations and decelerations in football: moving beyond absolute and arbitrary thresholds. Sports Biomechanics. 2024;(online ahead of print).

## TLDR
A GPS-based individualised method for classifying acceleration and deceleration intensities in football showed that acceleration capacity is highly dependent on starting speed whereas deceleration is less so, enabling practitioners to monitor training loads without arbitrary absolute thresholds or field tests.

## Abstract
This study adapted the Sonderegger et al. (2016) percentage intensity approach to individualise the assessment of accelerations and decelerations in professional football using GPS data from 42 male players across four training microcycles. Raw velocity-time data from 10-Hz Catapult GPS devices were used to calculate acceleration and deceleration magnitudes across seven starting speed bandwidth intervals (<5 to >30 km/h), and effort intensities were classified as very low (<25%), low (25–50%), moderate (50–75%), and high (>75%) of each player's maximal observed value per speed band. Acceleration capacity decreased strongly with increasing starting speed (r = −0.97), while deceleration capacity was less starting-speed dependent (r = −0.94). The adaptation allows practitioners to classify individual efforts during real-life training scenarios without requiring dedicated field tests.

## Methods
42 male professional footballers from two Portuguese first-division teams; 10-Hz Catapult GPS (Vector S7 and X7); one full mesocycle (4 weeks) per team across 2020/21 and 2021/22 seasons (812 training files total); maximal acceleration/deceleration per starting speed bandwidth interval (<5, 5–10, 10–15, 15–20, 20–25, 25–30, >30 km/h) extracted from proprietary OpenField software; percentage intensity = (action value / maximal value for that speed band) × 100; intensity zones: very low <25%, low 25–50%, moderate 50–75%, high >75%; Pearson r and linear regressions analysed maximal effort vs. starting speed; mean paired differences with Cohen's d (90% CI) compared adjacent speed bands; Linear Mixed Model with player individuality and position as random factors; jamovi with ESCI package.

## Findings
• 86% of very low intensity accelerations and 79% of very low intensity decelerations started from <5 km/h.
• Correlation between maximal acceleration and starting speed: r = −0.97 (p < 0.001); deceleration: r = −0.94 (p < 0.01).
• Maximal acceleration decreased significantly at each successive starting speed band (all very large effect sizes, Cohen's d range −1.57 to −3.34).
• Deceleration differences between adjacent starting speed bands ranged from unclear to very large effect sizes, confirming weaker starting-speed dependence.
• Linear Mixed Model: acceleration F = 1085.81, R² = 0.96; deceleration F = 35.08, R² = 0.42—initial velocity explained nearly all variance in acceleration but not deceleration.
• Very low intensity efforts (<25%) dominated the training distribution and likely represent noise or negligible mechanical load.

## Limitations
Only training sessions analysed; competition data excluded; maximal values derived from training may underestimate true maximal capacity compared to isolated field tests; only two teams from one country; goalkeepers excluded due to positional specificity; >30 km/h starting speed band contained very few efforts, potentially inflating maximal values for that interval.

## Practical Implications
Load monitoring systems using absolute acceleration thresholds (e.g., >3 m/s²) misclassify individual effort intensity because the same magnitude is harder to achieve from low speeds. Practitioners should collect maximal acceleration per starting speed band from each training session (using the week's highest value) to build a rolling individual reference without dedicated testing days. Very low intensity efforts (<25% of max) can be excluded from analysis as they represent minimal mechanical stimulus. For decelerations, starting speed matters less—a single threshold approach may be more acceptable.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football GPS/GNSS monitoring programs. Skill positions (WR, DB, RB) who accelerate frequently from standing starts are misrepresented by absolute m/s² thresholds. Adopting a relative, individualised classification system would allow strength and conditioning staff to accurately quantify high-intensity acceleration work across different positions and training phases. Paper is from soccer/association football context.

## Return to Play Notes
Although not a return-to-play study, the individualised threshold method can be applied during rehabilitation to verify that an athlete is producing high-intensity accelerations and decelerations relative to their own maximal capacity—not just crossing arbitrary absolute values—before being cleared for full competition loads.
