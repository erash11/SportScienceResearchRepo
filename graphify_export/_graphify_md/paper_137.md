# DeFroda SF, Bodendorfer BM, Hartnett DA, Milner JD, Yang DS, Silber ZS, Forsythe B. Defining the Contemporary Epidemiology and Return to Play for High Ankle Sprains in the National Football League. The Physician and Sportsmedicine. 2021. DOI 10.1080/00913847.2021.1924046

## TLDR
High ankle sprains are rising in frequency in the NFL, now occurring at nearly 2.5 per 10,000 player exposures — up from 1.75 a decade ago — and represent a significant source of missed playing time at approximately 11 weeks per injury. Nine in 10 players successfully return to play, but offensive linemen and wide receivers carry the greatest positional burden. Older players at the time of injury are meaningfully more likely to fail RTP (OR=1.24 per year of age), while more experienced players are more likely to return. Defensive players return faster than offensive players. Performance staff should plan for ~11-week absences when a high ankle sprain occurs and recognize that younger/more experienced players have a clear RTP advantage.

## Abstract
This retrospective epidemiological study examined 533 high ankle sprains (syndesmotic injuries) in 498 NFL players across 11 seasons (2009-10 to 2019-20) using publicly available data. The annual incidence increased linearly from 1.75 to 2.49 per 10,000 player exposures over the study period. Offensive players accounted for 57% of injuries, primarily offensive linemen (36.5%) and wide receivers (24%). Overall, 89.7% of players returned to play with an average return time of 80.5 ± 132.9 days; defensive players returned faster (68.1 days) than offensive players (90.1 days), and higher age at injury significantly increased failure-to-RTP risk (OR=1.24, p=0.0088).

## Methods
Retrospective review of publicly available NFL injury data from the 2009-10 through 2019-20 regular seasons using Pro-Football-Reference.com and ESPN. Injuries identified as 'high ankle sprain,' 'syndesmosis,' or 'syndesmotic injury.' Player characteristics recorded: age (mean 25.8 ± 3.1 yr), BMI (mean 31.8 ± 4.6), years of experience, and position. Injury setting (preseason/regular season/postseason) and timing documented. RTP defined as first date activated and played a preseason/training camp or regular season game. Incidence calculated per 10,000 athlete exposures (53 players/team, 105 practices/season, 20 games/season, 32 teams). Linear regression assessed incidence trends; multivariate logistic regression identified RTP failure risk factors (controlling for year of injury, position, BMI, age, experience). Kaplan-Meier survivorship plots computed for RTP.

## Findings
533 high ankle sprains identified in 498 players. Incidence increased linearly from 1.75 to 2.49 per 10,000 player exposures (R²=0.3357). Offensive players: 304/533 injuries (57.0%); offensive linemen most affected (111/304, 36.5%), followed by wide receivers (73/304, 24%). Defensive secondary accounted for 86/228 (37.7%) of defensive injuries. 89.7% (478/533) of players returned to play; average RTP 80.5 ± 132.9 days. Defensive players returned faster (68.1 ± 114.6 days) than offensive players (90.1 ± 144.8 days, p=0.084). Higher age at injury increased failure-to-RTP risk (OR=1.24, 95% CI 1.05-1.46, p=0.0088). More years of experience decreased failure-to-RTP risk (OR=0.71, 95% CI 0.59-0.86, p=0.0004). Injuries occurred predominantly in the regular season (422/533, 79.2%).

## Limitations
Data sourced from public databases, which may result in over- or under-reporting; high-profile players may be overrepresented. Minor high ankle injuries with rapid recovery may be underreported, potentially inflating the average RTP time. Injury severity, imaging findings, and treatment type (operative vs. nonoperative) were not available, preventing severity-stratified analysis. Player injury history was unavailable, precluding analysis of re-injury risk. Variables such as playing surface and contact mechanism could not be assessed. Team rehabilitation protocols and personnel decisions influencing RTP could not be controlled.

## Practical Implications
With an average RTP of ~11 weeks and a linearly increasing incidence rate, high ankle sprains represent one of the most significant sources of offensive player unavailability in the NFL. Programs should have standardized high ankle sprain management protocols that account for the full expected timeline rather than the 2-3 week timelines reported in older NCAA literature. Age at injury is an independent predictor of RTP failure — players 28+ years old warrant closer monitoring and more conservative RTP criteria. Offensive linemen and wide receivers are the highest-risk positional groups and should receive targeted preventive attention including ankle stability training and appropriate taping/bracing protocols.

## Athlete Development Notes
High ankle sprains have a documented rising incidence in the NFL, likely related to increasing athlete size, speed, and strength — all of which are also developmental targets. This creates a tension where optimizing physical development may increase syndesmotic injury risk, particularly for offensive linemen who bear the highest absolute injury burden. Developmental programs should incorporate ankle dorsiflexion mobility, syndesmotic stability exercises, and progressive contact load management to prepare linemen and wide receivers for the high-force environments that cause these injuries. The injury peak in the regular season (79.2%) rather than preseason suggests that load and collision intensity — not conditioning — are primary drivers.

## Return to Play Notes
High ankle sprains require an average of 80.5 days for RTP in the NFL, approximately 3-4x longer than historically reported in NCAA studies, likely reflecting more severe injury profiles or higher return-to-performance standards at the professional level. The 90% RTP rate is encouraging, but the 10% failure rate — concentrated in older, less-experienced players — should inform realistic counseling at time of injury. Defensive players return approximately 22 days faster than offensive players (68.1 vs. 90.1 days), which may reflect positional demand differences or different operative management rates. Age at injury is the strongest modifiable risk predictor: each additional year of age increases failure-to-RTP odds by 24%. Conservative criteria and extended timelines should be applied for veteran players sustaining these injuries.
