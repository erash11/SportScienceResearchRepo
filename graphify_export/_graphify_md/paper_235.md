# Eichner, E. R. (2018). Football Team Rhabdomyolysis: The Pain Beats the Gain and the Coach Is to Blame. Current Sports Medicine Reports, 17(5), 142–143.

## TLDR
Team exertional rhabdomyolysis (ER) in football is a preventable, coach-driven phenomenon. Every documented outbreak shares the same profile: novel overexertion (too much, too soon, too fast) after a deconditioning break, often as punishment or to assert a new coaching philosophy. Strength and conditioning staff must resist pressure to implement extreme novel loading in the first sessions after any break, as the combination of deconditioning and unfamiliar high-volume exercise reliably precipitates ER requiring hospitalization.

## Abstract
This clinical commentary reviews 17 outbreaks of team exertional rhabdomyolysis (ER) across multiple sports over recent years, with particular focus on seven football outbreaks at college and high school levels. Documented outbreaks occurred at Iowa (2011, 13 hospitalized), Maine (2012), Oregon (2017), Nebraska (2018), and multiple high school programs. The paper identifies a consistent causal pattern: novel, high-volume exercise performed immediately after a period of deconditioning (typically winter or summer break), directed by coaches motivated to 'toughen up' players following a losing season. The author argues that in each case, institutional and coaching decisions—not individual player failure—were the proximate cause of ER outbreaks.

## Methods
Narrative clinical review describing 17 team ER outbreaks across college and high school sports. The author synthesizes published case reports, conference presentations, and media-documented incidents. Cases span football (7), swimming (2), lacrosse (2), soccer (1), track (1), basketball (1), softball (1), volleyball (1), and golf (1). Epidemiological patterns across outbreaks are described qualitatively.

## Findings
All 17 outbreaks shared the same etiology: novel overexertion following a break from training. Football outbreaks predominantly involved squat-based or weightlifting protocols administered in the first 1-3 days after winter break. Multiple outbreaks required fasciotomies for compartment syndrome. Coaches consistently blamed athletes rather than accepting institutional responsibility. Programs with new coaches or following losing seasons were disproportionately represented. The Iowa outbreak (100 timed back squats at 50% 1RM on day 2 of return) produced 13 hospitalizations—the largest single football ER event documented.

## Limitations
Narrative review without systematic search methodology. Reliance on published case reports and media accounts introduces selection bias toward more severe or publicized outbreaks. Causality is inferred from temporal and contextual patterns rather than prospective study design. Individual player-level physiological risk factors are not analyzed.

## Practical Implications
The first training session after any break (winter, summer, postseason) should never introduce novel, high-volume exercises at near-maximal intensity. Strength and conditioning staff must follow evidence-based return-to-training progressions regardless of coaching pressure. Institutions should have explicit rhabdomyolysis prevention protocols including graduated loading after deconditioning periods. Early recognition of ER (severe muscle pain and weakness 12-48 hours post-exercise, dark urine) and immediate medical referral is essential. Culture change is needed so coaches view ER outbreaks as institutional failures, not player weakness.

## Athlete Development Notes
Young football athletes are particularly vulnerable to ER because they may push through pain to avoid social stigma or dismissal from the team. Strength and conditioning programs must implement standardized return-to-training ramp protocols after all breaks exceeding one week. Novel movements (e.g., back squats, plyometrics) should be introduced at low volumes before increasing load or repetitions, regardless of prior training history.

## Return to Play Notes
Athletes who suffer ER require medical clearance before returning to full training. Severe cases involving compartment syndrome and fasciotomy have prolonged RTP timelines. Recurrence risk is elevated in players with prior ER history, and graduated loading should be especially conservative on subsequent return-to-play after breaks. Institutions should document all ER cases to identify repeat patterns and modify programming accordingly.
