# Svirsky SE, Wood CC, Raymond O, McIntyre P, Appleton H, Wagner C, Gill J, Puccio AM. Detection of Injury Biomarkers in Sweat of Collegiate Athletes Pre- and Post-Football Season: A Pilot Study. Neurotrauma Reports. 2025;6(1):700-705. DOI: 10.1177/08977151251367345

## TLDR
Sweat may provide a practical, non-invasive window into cumulative brain stress in football athletes. This study is the first to detect TBI-related proteins (tau, GFAP, UCH-L1, Nf-L) in sweat and found that pre-season levels of GFAP and UCH-L1 were elevated in athletes with prior TBI history—suggesting sensitivity to historical injury—while tau and UCH-L1 rose across a full season even without reported concussions. For sports medicine and performance staff, this points toward potential wearable biomarker monitoring tools that could flag subconcussive accumulation before symptoms appear, supporting smarter return-to-play and long-term athlete health decisions.

## Abstract
This pilot study investigated whether sweat could serve as a non-invasive biofluid to detect traumatic brain injury (TBI) biomarkers in collegiate football players across a season. Thirty-four NCAA Division III football players wore non-occlusive sweat patches (~24 h) at pre-season and 18 returned for post-season collection. Four hallmark TBI proteins—total-tau, neurofilament light (Nf-L), glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP), and ubiquitin carboxyl-terminal hydrolase L1 (UCH-L1)—were quantified via Simoa immunoarray. GFAP and UCH-L1 were significantly elevated pre-season in athletes with a prior TBI history compared to those without, and total-tau and UCH-L1 increased significantly from pre- to post-season despite no reported concussions during the season.

## Methods
Prospective observational pilot study; N=34 pre-season, N=18 post-season; NCAA Division III football players (2023 season); non-occlusive PharmChem sweat patch worn ~24 h on the upper arm at pre-season (August) and post-season (December); Simoa Neurology 4-Plex A Advantage kit (Quanterix) used to measure total-tau, Nf-L, GFAP, and UCH-L1 at 4x dilution in duplicate; OSU-TBI questionnaire and Rivermead Post Concussion Symptoms Questionnaire used to assess TBI history and symptoms; Mann-Whitney and Wilcoxon matched-pairs signed-rank tests used for unpaired and paired comparisons.

## Findings
Athletes with a prior TBI history showed significantly higher pre-season GFAP (p=0.0036) and UCH-L1 (p=0.0169) compared to those without TBI history. Paired pre-to-post-season analysis (N=17) showed significant increases in total-tau (p=0.0046; mean 0.89 to 3.18 pg/mL) and UCH-L1 (p=0.0032; mean 40.74 to 125.30 pg/mL). Nf-L and GFAP did not differ significantly between pre- and post-season. No concussions were reported during the season, suggesting these changes reflect subconcussive exposure.

## Limitations
No non-contact sport comparison group and no female athletes included, limiting specificity to head impacts. No standardized method to normalize sweat volume or rate across participants. Small sample size with high pre-season attrition (6 patches fell off, 2 not returned). Self-reported concussion history and symptom data are subject to recall and reporting bias. Blood was not collected concurrently, preventing direct comparison of sweat vs. serum biomarker profiles.

## Practical Implications
Sweat-based TBI biomarker monitoring could offer a scalable, non-invasive tool for NFL and college football medical staffs to track cumulative neurological stress across a season without venipuncture. Elevated GFAP and UCH-L1 at pre-season in players with prior TBI history suggest these markers may serve as baseline risk indicators. Rising tau and UCH-L1 levels across the season—even absent reported concussions—support the concept of sub-threshold cumulative load monitoring, which could inform individualized load management and return-to-play thresholds.

## Athlete Development Notes
For athlete development programs, this research supports the idea that repetitive sub-concussive exposure across a season produces measurable neurological stress even in asymptomatic athletes. Monitoring sweat biomarkers longitudinally could help development staff understand individual neurological resilience and adapt training and contact loads accordingly, particularly for younger players with prior TBI histories.

## Return to Play Notes
This study is directly relevant to return-to-play decision-making. Athletes with prior TBI history showed elevated GFAP and UCH-L1 at the start of the season—biomarkers that are already used in clinical TBI settings. Elevated post-season tau and UCH-L1 without reported concussions suggests that current symptom-based RTP protocols may miss biologically meaningful injury accumulation. Sweat biomarker panels could eventually supplement existing RTP protocols as an objective, non-invasive physiological check.
