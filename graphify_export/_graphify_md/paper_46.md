# Lundberg TR, Howatson G. Analgesic and anti-inflammatory drugs in sports: Implications for exercise performance and training adaptations. Scand J Med Sci Sports. 2018;1-11.

## TLDR
Paracetamol may acutely improve endurance and neuromuscular performance through increased pain tolerance, but both NSAIDs and paracetamol can attenuate anabolic responses to resistance exercise and may blunt long-term strength and hypertrophy gains in young individuals—a critical risk-benefit consideration for athletes using these drugs routinely.

## Abstract
This narrative review synthesizes evidence on the effects of NSAIDs and paracetamol (acetaminophen) on exercise performance and long-term training adaptations in athletes. NSAIDs and paracetamol are widely consumed by athletes to manage pain and inflammation, yet both inhibit cyclooxygenase (COX) activity and have the potential to modulate tissue protein turnover. The review covers acute performance effects, exercise-induced protein synthesis, satellite cell activity, translational signaling, lipid mediator responses, training adaptations from chronic use, and safety and ethical considerations.

## Methods
Narrative review; literature search covering acute performance trials (time trials, cycling, sprint, neuromuscular), acute cellular response studies (protein synthesis, satellite cells, translational signaling, lipid mediators), chronic training adaptation studies (resistance training in young and older adults), and safety/ethical literature; studies in both recreational and elite athletes reviewed.

## Findings
• Paracetamol improved cycling time-trial performance, heat exercise capacity, repeated sprint performance, and maximal voluntary contractions in several studies. • Both ibuprofen and paracetamol suppressed post-exercise muscle protein synthesis in young individuals via COX inhibition. • NSAIDs attenuated satellite cell proliferation response to resistance exercise. • High-dose ibuprofen (1200 mg/day) attenuated muscle hypertrophy and strength gains after 8 weeks of resistance training in young adults; low-dose ibuprofen showed no effect. • NSAIDs may positively influence training adaptations in older individuals. • No performance benefit of analgesics was shown for marathon running pain outcomes. • NSAIDs carry risks of GI complications, renal impairment, thrombocyte dysfunction, and increased mortality risk at higher doses.

## Limitations
Most acute adaptation studies used extreme eccentric protocols not representative of typical training; few studies in elite athletes; no studies examining paracetamol's effect on training adaptations in young individuals; lack of dose-response data; cross-sectional designs limit causal inference; potential ethical issues with analgesic use for competitive advantage.

## Practical Implications
Athletes and support staff should conduct a careful risk-benefit analysis before routinely using NSAIDs or paracetamol during training. Routine NSAID use during resistance training may blunt hypertrophy and strength gains in younger athletes, while chronic use may mask pain signals and increase injury risk. A professional guidance and individualized approach is strongly advised.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly relevant to American football athlete development: frequent use of NSAIDs by football players (documented extensively at the FIFA World Cup and collegiate levels) during preseason and in-season resistance training may attenuate the anabolic adaptations critical for linemen and skill positions. Strength and conditioning staff should be aware that high-dose NSAID use during hypertrophy-focused training blocks could compromise gains.

## Return to Play Notes
Chronic analgesic use that dampens pain perception may cause athletes to return to play prematurely, increasing re-injury risk. NSAIDs' GI and cardiovascular risks are particularly relevant in collision sports where acute trauma is frequent. Staff should consider that NSAID-mediated blunting of the inflammatory repair response could delay true tissue healing despite pain resolution.
