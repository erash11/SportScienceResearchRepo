# Meyers, M. C., Sterling, J. C., & Robinson, S. K. (2024). Imaging and surgical procedures after surface-related collegiate football injuries on artificial turf versus natural grass: Prevalence and trends over 15 seasons. The Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine, 12(9), 23259671241274144.

## TLDR
Collegiate football played on heavyweight artificial turf (≥9.0 lb/ft²) resulted in significantly fewer imaging and surgical procedures, and fewer diagnoses of serious foot and ankle injuries (syndesmosis sprains, Lisfranc trauma), compared with natural grass over 15 seasons. Contrary to widespread perception, this generation of artificial turf was associated with lower medical intervention rates. However, both surfaces showed a significant upward trend in combined medical procedures and severe injuries over the study period — suggesting increasing player size, speed, and aggression are overriding any surface-related safety improvements. ACL and associated tissue injuries comprised 53% of all knee surgeries, with no significant surface effect on knee injury rates.

## Abstract
This prospective cohort study (Level 2 evidence) evaluated imaging and surgical procedures following surface-related collegiate football injuries at 39 FBS universities over 15 competitive seasons (2006–2020). A total of 2224 games were documented (1106 on heavyweight artificial turf [≥9.0 lb/ft²], 1118 on natural grass). Of 9137 total injuries, 4010 (44%) were surface-related. MANOVA revealed significant main effects between surfaces for imaging procedures (F2,1738=4.718; P=.009), surgical procedures (F1,539=5.974; P=.003), and medical diagnosis (F2,456=2.643; P=.040). Artificial turf was associated with significantly lower incidences of imaging after player-to-surface and shoe-to-surface trauma, lower body surgeries, ligament tear surgeries, and fewer diagnoses of syndesmosis sprains and Lisfranc injuries. Significant rising trends in combined medical procedures (P=.005) and combined substantial/severe injuries (P=.0007) were observed irrespective of surface over the 15-year period.

## Methods
Prospective cohort study across 39 FBS universities (2006–2020). Participating universities played on both heavyweight artificial turf (≥9.0 lb/ft²) and natural grass during the same seasons. Lightweight infill games (n=376) were excluded. Certified athletic trainers (ATCs) completed standardized injury surveillance forms within 7 days of each game; follow-up via telephone ensured completeness. Outcomes included imaging (radiograph, CT, MRI), surgical procedures, and specific diagnoses categorized by injury type, anatomical location, and surface. MANOVA with Wilks λ criteria using general linear model procedures; incidence rates (IRs) expressed per 10-game season. Simple linear regression assessed 15-season trends. Statistical power: 1−β=0.732–1.000.

## Findings
Artificial turf vs. natural grass incidence rates (IR per 10 games): total imaging 7.1 vs. 8.6; player-to-surface impact imaging 1.5 vs. 2.1; shoe-to-surface contact imaging 4.4 vs. 5.2; foot imaging 0.6 vs. 1.1; ligament sprain/tear imaging 4.6 vs. 5.3; muscle strain/tear imaging 0.2 vs. 0.7; shoe-to-surface contact surgeries 1.1 vs. 1.6; lower body surgeries 1.8 vs. 2.3; ligament tear surgeries 1.0 vs. 1.5; syndesmosis sprain/tear diagnoses 0.7 vs. 1.0; Lisfranc trauma diagnoses 0.3 vs. 0.5. All differences significant (P<.05). No significant surface effect on knee injury rates. Mean surgical procedures per season: artificial turf 15.4, natural grass 20.5. Significant positive trends over 15 seasons for natural grass combined procedures (r=0.612), mean combined procedures (r=0.684), and combined substantial/severe injuries (r=0.776).

## Limitations
Inability to control for inherent random variation in high-collision sports injury. Could not account for athlete strength/conditioning status, precompetition workloads, injury history, weather conditions, field maintenance quality, coaching style, foul play, or individual biomechanical differences. Orthotic support information was not obtained. Time between trauma and advanced medical procedures and incidence of multiple operations over time were not tracked. Findings generalizable only to FBS collegiate football competition. Research support was received from FieldTurf USA for the lead author — a potential conflict of interest. Inherent challenges of multi-team, multi-index data collection over 15 years.

## Practical Implications
Decisions about playing surface installations at the collegiate level should be informed by longitudinal, multi-team data rather than perception or anecdote. Heavyweight artificial turf systems (≥9.0 lb/ft²) are associated with fewer imaging and surgical interventions than natural grass for surface-related injuries, particularly for foot, ankle, and lower extremity ligament trauma. However, the secular rise in severe injuries and medical procedures irrespective of surface suggests that playing surface alone is insufficient to address growing injury burden — player size, speed, rule changes, and play intensity require broader intervention. Future injury surveillance should routinely include post-trauma medical procedure tracking beyond simple time-loss metrics.

## Athlete Development Notes
The significant upward trend in substantial and severe injuries over 15 seasons (r=0.776, P=.0007) indicates that collegiate football athletes are facing increasing injury burden regardless of surface. Programs should invest in comprehensive injury surveillance systems that capture imaging and surgical outcomes — not just time loss — to accurately assess severity trends. Surface choice alone cannot offset the injury risk associated with increasing athleticism and play intensity; strength, conditioning, and load management remain primary modifiable factors for athlete development programs.

## Return to Play Notes
The finding that natural grass was associated with significantly higher rates of lower extremity ligament surgeries, syndesmosis sprains, and Lisfranc injuries has direct implications for RTP timelines. Syndesmotic sprains and Lisfranc injuries are associated with prolonged recovery and high reinjury risk if not fully healed. The rising trend of severe injuries over 15 seasons underscores the importance of rigorous, criterion-based RTP protocols that account for injury severity — particularly for lower extremity ligament and foot injuries — rather than simple time-loss thresholds. Facilities choosing playing surfaces should understand the downstream medical procedure burden associated with each surface type.
