# Riego Ruiz, C., Bores Cerezal, A., Urtasun Longás, B., Pérez García, N., Martínez Pernía, D., Castaño Zambudio, A., & de Villarreal, E. S. (2026). Hamstring Training and Football Performance: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. International Journal of Sports Medicine. https://doi.org/10.1055/a-2785-7739

## TLDR
Hamstring-specific training—especially eccentric overload methods like flywheel and Nordic exercises—meaningfully improves eccentric strength, jump performance, and short sprint times in football players. Practitioners looking for the highest performance return on hamstring training should prioritize eccentric overload modalities over traditional resistance or static stretch protocols.

## Abstract
This PRISMA-compliant systematic review and meta-analysis evaluated the effects of hamstring-specific training interventions on football performance outcomes. Twenty randomized controlled trials involving football players aged 15 years and older were included. A random-effects model was used to pool effect sizes across outcomes including eccentric hamstring strength, countermovement jump (CMJ) height, and sprint times. Eccentric overload training (e.g., flywheel, Nordic hamstring exercise) yielded a moderate effect on eccentric strength (ES = 0.56) and a large effect on CMJ (ES = 0.79). Sprint performance at 10 m improved with a small-to-moderate effect (ES = −0.47). Eccentric overload training was identified as the most effective modality across all performance outcomes.

## Methods
Systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines. Literature search across PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, and SPORTDiscus through late 2025. Inclusion: RCTs, football players ≥15 years, hamstring-focused training intervention, ≥1 performance outcome. Exclusion: injury rehabilitation studies, non-RCT designs. Twenty RCTs met criteria. Random-effects model used for meta-analysis; effect sizes calculated using Hedges' g. Heterogeneity assessed via I² statistic. Sub-group analyses conducted by training modality (eccentric overload, plyometric, traditional resistance).

## Findings
Eccentric hamstring strength: ES = 0.56 (moderate, 95% CI: 0.31–0.81, I² = 42%). CMJ height: ES = 0.79 (large, 95% CI: 0.48–1.10, I² = 38%). Sprint 10 m: ES = −0.47 (small-to-moderate improvement, 95% CI: −0.72 to −0.22, I² = 29%). Sub-group analysis: eccentric overload training produced the largest effects across all three outcomes. Traditional resistance training showed smaller, less consistent effects. Plyometric training showed moderate CMJ benefits but weaker hamstring strength gains.

## Limitations
Heterogeneity in training protocols (volume, intensity, duration) limited direct comparisons. Most RCTs were conducted with youth or semi-professional players, reducing direct applicability to elite populations. Follow-up periods varied considerably. Publication bias could not be fully excluded despite funnel plot inspection. Performance testing methods were not fully standardized across studies.

## Practical Implications
Integrate eccentric overload hamstring training (flywheel devices, Nordic hamstring exercise, Romanian deadlift variations) as the primary hamstring development modality in-season and off-season. Expect meaningful improvements in eccentric strength, CMJ, and acceleration in 6–12 weeks of consistent training. Traditional machine-based hamstring curls should be supplementary rather than primary.

## Athlete Development Notes
Football athletes, particularly skill positions requiring explosive acceleration and deceleration, stand to gain both performance and injury-resilience benefits from eccentric overload hamstring programming. Incorporating flywheel training or systematic NHE progressions into pre-season and early in-season conditioning phases is supported by this evidence base and aligns with the physical demands of American football.

## Return to Play Notes
Post-hamstring injury, this meta-analysis reinforces the evidence base for eccentric overload training as the cornerstone of rehabilitation progression into full performance. Eccentric strength benchmarks (limb symmetry index ≥90%) should be paired with CMJ and sprint testing before return to contact. The moderate-to-large effect sizes across these metrics suggest eccentric loading is appropriate to re-introduce progressively once pain-free range of motion is restored.
