# Murphy CP, Frangiamore SJ, Mannava S, Sanchez A, Beiter ER, Whalen JM, Price MD, LaPrade RF, Provencher MT. Effect of Anterior Glenoid Labral Tears and Glenoid Bone Loss at the NFL Combine on Future NFL Performance. Orthop J Sports Med. 2018;6(7):2325967118784884. doi: 10.1177/2325967118784884

## TLDR
Having an anterior labral tear (Bankart lesion) confirmed at the NFL Combine does not independently reduce games played, games started, or snap percentage in a player's first two NFL seasons. However, labral tears occurring alongside other shoulder injuries (Hill-Sachs, SLAP, glenoid bone loss, rotator cuff tear) significantly drop a player's draft position — meaning teams penalize these athletes in the draft even though the combined injuries don't appear to hurt on-field performance. Glenoid bone loss specifically lowered draft position but did not affect playing time. Surgical versus non-surgical management showed no performance differences. This data is relevant for evaluating college athletes at the NFL Combine and making clearance and draft decisions.

## Abstract
This retrospective cohort study reviewed all 2285 NFL Combine participants from 2009 to 2015 to determine whether MRI-confirmed anterior glenoid labral tears and associated injuries affected future NFL performance. Of 2285 players, 206 (9%) had anterior labral tears confirmed by MRI; 908 players without surgery history or significant missed time served as controls. NFL draft position, games played, games started, and snap percentage across the first two NFL seasons were compared between injured players and controls.

## Methods
Retrospective cohort study of NFL Combine participants 2009-2015. MRI scans reviewed independently by two fellowship-trained orthopaedic surgeons for anterior labral tears, associated injuries (SLAP, glenoid bone loss, Hill-Sachs, rotator cuff tear, HAGL, combined posterior tears), and surgical evidence. Control group: players with no surgery history and no more than 2 missed collegiate games. Performance metrics (draft position, games played, games started, year 1 and year 2 snap %) collected from NFL.com and Pro Football Focus. Mann-Whitney U tests, chi-square, and Fisher exact tests used for comparisons.

## Findings
206 of 2285 players (9%) had MRI-confirmed anterior labral tears; 20 were bilateral (226 total affected shoulders). Players with anterior labral tears did not perform significantly worse than controls in draft position (P=0.259), games played (P=0.391), games started (P=0.486), or snap percentage year 1 (P=0.268) or year 2 (P=0.757). Concomitant injuries significantly worsened draft position vs isolated labral tears (170.1 vs 95.8 pick, P=0.003). Glenoid bone loss reduced draft position (175.5 vs 95.8, P=0.001) but not playing metrics. Severity of bone loss did not significantly affect any metric. Operative vs nonoperative management showed no significant performance differences.

## Limitations
Retrospective design; not all players underwent shoulder MRI at the Combine, potentially missing asymptomatic tears. MR angiography and 3D CT were not consistently available, limiting sensitivity for certain lesions and bone loss quantification. Physical examination findings were not correlated to imaging. Confounding variables (collegiate performance, combine testing, interview, team needs) could affect draft position. Follow-up limited to first 2 NFL seasons; some players had only 2-year data available.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college medical staffs should recognize that an anterior labral tear — even with associated injuries — does not independently predict reduced on-field performance in the first two years of professional play. Teams may be over-penalizing athletes in the draft for labral findings that do not translate to playing time differences. For collegiate athletes being evaluated for professional opportunities, appropriate surgical management of instability before the Combine does not appear to disadvantage performance outcomes relative to nonoperative care.

## Athlete Development Notes
Athletes with anterior labral tears or a history of shoulder instability can be developed for professional play with appropriate management. The 9% prevalence of MRI-confirmed labral tears at the Combine reflects the high contact demands of collegiate football. Strength and conditioning programs emphasizing rotator cuff and scapular stabilizer conditioning may reduce recurrence risk for athletes returning from labral injury. Positions with highest prevalence (defensive backs, defensive linemen, linebackers) warrant proactive shoulder screening.

## Return to Play Notes
The lack of significant performance differences between operatively and nonoperatively managed labral tears suggests both pathways can lead to successful return to elite competition. Players returning from anterior labral repair should follow validated progressive RTP protocols with attention to end-range external rotation loading before clearing for full-contact practice. The presence of concomitant injuries (Hill-Sachs, glenoid bone loss) warrants more conservative RTP timelines and may require surgical stabilization to reduce recurrent dislocation risk in contact sport athletes.
