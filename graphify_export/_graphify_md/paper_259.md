# Walia, B., Owora, A., Kmush, B., Ehrlich, J., & Sanders, S. (2024). Head impact, concussion, and salary in the NFL: Is there a compensating wage-risk premium? American Behavioral Scientist. https://doi.org/10.1177/00027642241260774

## TLDR
Contrary to standard labor economic theory predicting a wage premium for hazardous work, NFL players at higher-risk head-impact positions actually earn less on their next contracts. Teams appear to discount salaries to offset projected future healthcare liabilities and roster depth costs associated with high-concussion positions. Net effect is an estimated $2.54 million per-season dispremium for head impact risk exposure.

## Abstract
This study examined whether NFL players in position groups with higher estimated cumulative head impact or concussion risk receive a compensating wage premium in their next contract, controlling for productivity (Wins Above Average), experience, and contract year. Using 1,162 fully observed player contracts and 290 non-rookie contracts from the 2006–2017 NFL seasons, frequency-weighted fixed-effects linear regressions were specified. Results showed players are compensated for productivity and experience, but position groups with higher head impact or concussion risk receive a significant salary dispremium rather than a premium, consistent with an augmented compensating salary-differential theory in which both firm and employee share risk.

## Methods
Fixed-effects linear regression with frequency weighting by contract length. Dependent variable: next-contract average annual salary. Independent variables: player productivity (WAA from Pro Football Focus), years of NFL experience, experience squared, and three alternative measures of head impact risk (cumulative Head Impact Risk Factor, Concussions Per 100 Games Played, Concussions Per 1,000 Athletic Events) by position cluster. Twelve model specifications total, including six using lagged productivity for the non-rookie sub-sample (n=290). Data span 2006–2017 NFL seasons.

## Findings
Across all 12 model specifications, higher head impact risk and higher concussion rate were associated with significantly lower next-contract salaries (dispremium). At mean concussion rate and salary, the estimated salary decrease was approximately $5.25 million/season for concussion risk; the net overall head-impact dispremium was approximately $2.54 million/season. Players are compensated for sub-concussive head impacts (a premium), but penalized more strongly for concussive head impact risk, resulting in a net dispremium. Productivity (WAA) and experience are positively and significantly associated with salary.

## Limitations
Study limited to skill positions (QB, WR, RB/TE) where WAA data were available. Does not cover all NFL positions. Head impact risk measures are position-cluster estimates, not individual player measurements. Salary cap dynamics and collective bargaining effects may confound results. Does not account for positional depth chart differences or strategic roster construction beyond salary modeling.

## Practical Implications
NFL teams appear to be financially responding to head impact risk by reducing salaries at high-risk positions rather than paying a risk premium. This has implications for player contract negotiations, union advocacy, and long-term healthcare liability planning. Organizations should consider the financial and ethical dimensions of how head injury risk is factored into compensation structures.

## Athlete Development Notes
Findings highlight that positions with higher cumulative head impact exposure (e.g., running backs, wide receivers) face market-level salary penalties, which may influence athlete position decisions and career longevity considerations. Development staff should be aware that players at high-impact positions face compounded risks—both neurological and financial.

## Return to Play Notes
The study's evidence that concussive head impact risk reduces team demand for individual players has indirect RTP implications: teams may have financial incentives to limit playing time for high-concussion-rate positions, which could informally influence RTP timelines. Staff should advocate for RTP decisions based purely on clinical criteria rather than financial considerations.
