# Vlahoyiannis A, Aphamis G, Bogdanis GC, Sakkas GK, Andreou E, Giannaki CD. Deconstructing athletes' sleep: A systematic review of the influence of age, sex, athletic expertise, sport type, and season on sleep characteristics. Journal of Sport and Health Science. 2020. doi:10.1016/j.jshs.2020.03.006

## TLDR
Athletes consistently get less sleep and lower sleep efficiency than recommended, averaging only 7.2 h/night versus the 8+ hours optimal for recovery. Young athletes are the most sleep-deprived subgroup (SE ~80.3%), and sleep duration and quality decline meaningfully during heavy training phases compared to pre-season or competition phases. Sports performance coaches should prioritize sleep monitoring and optimization — especially during high-load training blocks — and should recognize that heavy training periods predictably worsen both sleep duration (by ~36 min vs. pre-season) and efficiency. Position-specific and sport-type differences in sleep architecture exist, highlighting the need for individualized sleep interventions.

## Abstract
This systematic review aimed to describe objective sleep parameters for athletes under different conditions using only polysomnography, EEG, or actigraphy measures. PubMed and Scopus were searched from inception to April 2019, yielding 81 included studies monitoring 1,830 athletes over 18,958 nights. Athletes slept on average 7.2 ± 1.1 h/night with 86.3% ± 6.8% sleep efficiency (SE), below the AASM consensus for healthy non-athlete adults. Notable sleep deficiencies were found in young/adolescent athletes and during heavy training phases.

## Methods
Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines; PubMed and Scopus searched from inception to April 2, 2019. Included studies measured sleep exclusively via objective tools (polysomnography/EEG and/or actigraphy). 81 studies included, covering 1,830 athletes (230 females, 1,040 males, 560 mixed) across 18,958 recorded nights. Data were extracted for total sleep time (TST), sleep efficiency (SE), sleep onset latency (SOL), wake after sleep onset (WASO), REM, and sleep stages N1–N3. Athletes were categorized by sex, age group (children/adolescents 6–17 yr; young adults 18–24 yr; middle-aged adults 25–40 yr), athletic expertise (elite vs. semi-elite), training season (pre-season, in-season/competition, heavy training), and sport type (aerobic, anaerobic, mixed; individual vs. team). Quality assessed with modified Newcastle-Ottawa Scale.

## Findings
Athletes slept 7.2 ± 1.1 h/night with 86.3% ± 6.8% SE; all dataset means were <8 h TST. Young athletes had SE of 80.3% ± 8.8% with elevated WASO (74 min). During heavy training phases, sleep duration was 36 min less than pre-season and 42 min less than in-season; SE was 3.0% lower than in-season. Elite and semi-elite athletes had similar TST but differed in SOL (semi-elite: 9.5 vs. elite: 18.4 min) and SE (elite 1.5% lower). Female athletes slept slightly longer (7.5 vs. 7.2 h) with higher SE (89.1% vs. 85.2%). Sleep architecture showed athletes spent 23% in REM, 9.7% N1, 48.7% N2, 18.5% N3. Power/explosiveness sport athletes had longer SOL and less N3 sleep compared to aerobic athletes.

## Limitations
Only objectively measured data were included, limiting sample sizes for certain subgroups (e.g., female athletes, masters athletes, middle-aged adults). Differences in SE calculation methods across studies could artificially inflate some SE values. No sleep architecture data were available for female athletes at elite or semi-elite levels, or for middle-aged adult athletes. Wearable consumer devices were excluded due to validation concerns. Generalizations about sleep stages should be made with caution given the limited number of PSG/EEG studies.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football programs should treat sleep as a trainable and monitorable component of recovery. Given that heavy training phases predictably reduce sleep duration by 36–42 min and efficiency by up to 3%, load management and sleep hygiene interventions (e.g., consistent sleep schedules, sleep environment optimization, napping protocols) should be proactively deployed during high-volume training blocks. Actigraphy monitoring is a validated, practical tool for ongoing sleep surveillance in squad settings. Young players are the highest-risk group for insufficient sleep and warrant dedicated sleep education.

## Athlete Development Notes
Young football athletes (high school and early college) are particularly vulnerable to sleep deficiency, with SE averaging only 80.3% and WASO of ~74 min — well below adult norms. Insufficient sleep impairs motor learning, psychomotor vigilance, hormone regulation, and body composition, all critical for athlete development. Development programs should integrate sleep education, limit early-morning training sessions that cut sleep short, and monitor sleep as a recovery metric alongside HRV and soreness. Sleep extension interventions (targeting >8 h for developing athletes) have demonstrated improvements in reaction time and sport-specific performance in adolescent cohorts.

## Return to Play Notes
Sleep disruption during rehabilitation should be monitored as a recovery metric, as low SE and reduced TST are associated with impaired physiological restoration and hormonal imbalances that suppress muscle growth and tissue repair. The finding that heavy training load reduces sleep quantity and quality suggests that injured players undergoing intensive rehab are at heightened risk for sleep deficiency. Ensuring adequate sleep (>8 h with high SE) during the return-to-play process may accelerate tissue healing and reduce risk of re-injury. Coaches and medical staff should screen for sleep disorders (e.g., sleep apnea) in high-BMI players, as obstructive sleep apnea is prevalent in linemen-type athletes.
