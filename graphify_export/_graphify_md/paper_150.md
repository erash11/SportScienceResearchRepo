# Pimenta R, Antunes H, Lopes T, Veloso A. Do Repeated Sprints Affect the Biceps Femoris Long Head Architecture in Football Players with and without an Injury History? — A Retrospective Study. Biology. 2023;12:96. 10.3390/biology12010096

## TLDR
This study demonstrates that previously injured football players maintain shorter BFlh fascicle lengths and greater pennation angles compared to healthy controls and their own contralateral limbs, even after returning to full training and competition. Intriguingly, the previously injured players were actually faster during repeated sprints. These architectural differences — likely a consequence of the original injury rather than a pre-existing risk factor — mean the injured limb may generate less force at the long muscle lengths required during sprinting, potentially increasing re-injury risk. Repeated sprint exercise itself had limited impact on BFlh architecture in both groups. For football staff, this reinforces the importance of long-term monitoring of BFlh fascicle length after hamstring injury, even in fully 'cleared' players.

## Abstract

This retrospective study compared biceps femoris long head (BFlh) muscle architecture — fascicle length (FL), pennation angle (PA), and muscle thickness (MT) — between 12 professional football players with a history of BFlh strain injury and 20 healthy controls, before and after a 10 × 30 m repeated sprint protocol. Ultrasound B-mode imaging was used to assess architecture at rest and during active contraction (20% MVIC) pre- and post-sprint task, and knee flexor peak torque was measured via dynamometry.

## Methods
Retrospective study; 32 male professional football players (age 25.03 ± 3.72 years, height 1.79 ± 0.07 m, mass 74.69 ± 8.67 kg). Injury group: 12 players with unilateral BFlh strain history within last 11.7 ± 7.4 months (range 2–23 months). Control group: 20 players with no BFlh history. All returned to pre-injury training and competition levels. BFlh architecture (FL, PA, MT) assessed via two Aixplorer v11 ultrasound scanners at ~50% femur length. Protocol: 30-min rest, warm-up, 2 resting videos per limb, active videos at 20% MVIC, then 10 × 30 m repeated sprint task (30 s rest between sprints), followed by repeat measurements and 2 MVIC. Dynamometry measured knee flexor peak torque (1000 Hz). Data analysed with IBM SPSS 27.0; two-way mixed repeated measures ANOVA.

## Findings
Injured players had significantly shorter FL at rest (p = 0.014; η²p = 0.196) and in active state (p < 0.001; η²p = 0.413), and greater PA at rest (p = 0.002; η²p = 0.307) and active state (p < 0.001; η²p = 0.368) vs. controls. Intra-individual: injured limbs had shorter FL at rest (p = 0.012; η²p = 0.519) and active state (p = 0.039; η²p = 0.332) and greater PA in passive (p < 0.001; η²p = 0.732) and active states (p = 0.018; η²p = 0.412) vs. contralateral limbs. Injured players were significantly faster during sprints (7.00 ± 0.25 vs. 6.82 ± 0.19 m/s; p = 0.026). No significant differences in peak torque between groups. Sprint task had limited impact on BFlh architectural parameters; contralateral FL decreased and PA increased post-sprint in resting conditions for both groups.

## Limitations
Results apply only to senior professional male football players. The static ultrasound technique may overestimate FL by ~0.5 cm (especially for FL >7 cm) due to the transducer width and linear extrapolation method. Cannot determine whether shorter BFlh FL is a consequence of injury or a pre-existing risk factor due to retrospective design. Muscle and tendon stiffness were not measured. Small sample sizes.

## Practical Implications
Football staff should not assume BFlh architecture has normalised after a player returns to full training following hamstring injury. Shorter fascicle lengths in the previously injured limb persist and may reduce force-generating capacity at the long muscle lengths encountered during sprinting. Regular ultrasound monitoring of BFlh FL after hamstring injury — including comparison to the contralateral limb — is recommended. The absence of between-group differences in peak torque suggests isometric strength testing alone is insufficient to detect residual structural deficits post-injury.

## Athlete Development Notes
Developmental football players who sustain hamstring strain injuries may carry residual BFlh architectural changes that predispose to re-injury. Programmes targeting fascicle length development — such as long-length eccentric training (e.g., Nordic curls, Romanian deadlifts at full range) — should be incorporated into post-injury development plans even after medical clearance. Monitoring BFlh FL via ultrasound longitudinally may identify players at elevated re-injury risk.

## Return to Play Notes
Return-to-play decision-making after BFlh strain should include assessment of BFlh fascicle length via ultrasound, not just strength or functional performance testing. The finding that injured players had shorter FL and greater PA than their contralateral limb even months after RTP suggests structural normalisation is not guaranteed by standard RTP protocols. Consider bilateral ultrasound assessment as a supplementary RTP criterion, with the goal of reducing the FL asymmetry between limbs before clearing for full competition exposure.
