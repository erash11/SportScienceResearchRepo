# Zuckerman SL, Elbin RJ, Sills AK, Crandall JR, Lessley DJ, Moran CM, Moran CD, Solomon GS. Concussions in the National Football League: the evolution of video review for assessing the frequency and reliability of visible signs. The Physician and Sportsmedicine. 2020. DOI: 10.1080/00913847.2020.1731379

## TLDR
Video review of NFL concussions using a standardized visible signs checklist shows moderate-to-substantial reliability among expert raters, with 'slow to get up,' 'motor incoordination,' and 'no behavior observed' being the most commonly identified signs. Expert raters outperform non-experts, and using multiple visible signs simultaneously is superior to recording only one. For football staff, this research supports investing in trained expert spotters with multi-angle video access, and underscores that helmet and facemask design in American football creates unique challenges for some visible signs (e.g., blank/vacant look) compared to other sports.

## Abstract
This cohort study examined the frequency and inter-rater reliability of visible signs (VS) of sport-related concussion (SRC) in NFL players using the Australian Football League (AFL) checklist across the 2015 and 2017 NFL seasons. Non-expert raters reviewed 96 SRC cases from the 2015 season using a single VS from the AFL checklist; expert biomechanical engineers reviewed 211 SRC cases from the 2017 season using all AFL checklist VS. The frequency, total percent agreement (TPA), and kappa reliability coefficients were calculated for each VS across both groups. The most frequent VS were slow to get up, motor incoordination, and no behavior observed; least frequent were impact seizure, blank/vacant look, and facial injury. Expert raters demonstrated higher reliability (kappa .56–.86) compared to non-experts (kappa .52–.68).

## Methods
Retrospective observational pilot study (2015 NFL season) and prospective observational study (2017 NFL season). Non-expert raters (NFL Player Health and Safety Department employees, bachelor's education, football familiarity) reviewed 96 SRC videos from the 2015 season and recorded a single primary VS per case using the AFL checklist. Expert raters (advanced degree biomechanical engineers with concussion research experience) reviewed 211 SRC videos from the 2017 season and independently recorded all VS present per case (up to 8). Video footage obtained from four sources: wide-angle end zone/sideline, network broadcast, untelevised NFL Films footage, and unaired network footage; edited in Adobe Premiere Pro. Kappa coefficients and TPA calculated for each VS; kappa classifications: fair (.41–.60), moderate (.61–.80), substantial (.81–1.00). Significance: p<.05.

## Findings
Most frequent VS across both seasons: slow to get up, motor incoordination, and no behavior observed. Least frequent: impact seizure, blank/vacant look, and facial injury. For non-expert raters (2015): TPA ranged 84–100%; kappa ranged .52–.68; moderate agreement on 4/6 VS; no substantial agreement on any VS; complete agreement achieved for 74% of cases. For expert raters (2017): TPA ranged 83–100%; kappa ranged .56–.86; substantial agreement on 1/9 VS (loss of responsiveness, κ=.86); moderate agreement on 6/9 VS; complete agreement on 53% of cases. Blank/vacant look had lowest kappa for experts (κ=.56), likely attributable to helmet/facemask obstruction. Use of multiple VS was a superior methodology to a single VS.

## Limitations
Inconsistency in data collection methods between non-expert (2015, single VS) and expert (2017, all VS) groups limits direct between-group comparison. Non-experts and experts reviewed different video sets with different sample sizes, further limiting statistical comparison. Expert raters were biomechanical engineers, not the same medical professionals used in current NFL gameday practice. Non-experts recorded only one VS per case, which may have artificially lowered their agreement scores. Sample sizes may limit generalizability to non-professional American football.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football programs should use trained expert spotters with access to multi-angle video replay to monitor for concussion visible signs in real time during games. No single visible sign is sufficient; protocols requiring assessment of multiple VS simultaneously (as in the current NFL gameday system with Unaffiliated Neurotrauma Consultants and ATC spotters) are superior. Non-expert spotters can be trained to achieve acceptable agreement if given structured education and standardized checklists. The helmet and facemask design of American football limits reliable detection of some VS, necessitating alternative identification strategies.

## Athlete Development Notes
Understanding visible signs of concussion is essential for sports medicine and performance staff at all levels of American football. Slow to get up, motor incoordination, and no protective action are the most observable and reliable indicators of a potential head injury. Coaches and strength staff should be educated on these VS to assist medical personnel in identifying at-risk players, particularly in situations where medical coverage is limited (youth, high school).

## Return to Play Notes
Reliable identification of visible signs at the time of injury is foundational to appropriate removal from play and initiation of the concussion management protocol. Improving VS recognition reliability — through expert rater training and multi-VS methodologies — reduces the risk of concussed athletes returning to play prematurely. The presence of any 'No-Go' sign (LOC, confusion, amnesia, impact seizure) per NFL protocol requires immediate removal. Video footage should be retained for all suspected concussion events to support RTP decision-making and protocol compliance review.
