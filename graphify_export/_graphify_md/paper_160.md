# Oleksy Ł, Mika A, Kuchciak M, Bril G, Kielnar R, Adamska O, Wolański P, Deszczyński M. Does Restricted Ankle Joint Mobility Influence Hamstring Muscle Strength, Work and Power in Football Players after ACL Reconstruction and Non-Injured Players?. J. Clin. Med. 2023;12(19):6330. https://doi.org/10.3390/jcm12196330

## TLDR
Ankle dorsiflexion restriction does not weaken hamstring muscles in football players, whether or not they have undergone ACL reconstruction. Post-ACL reconstruction players showed significantly lower hamstring Peak Torque/BW and Average Power compared to uninjured players, but within both groups, restricted ankle mobility did not further impair hamstring strength, power, or total work. Coaches and sports scientists should not assume limited ankle dorsiflexion is a primary driver of hamstring weakness; prevention programs should target a broad spectrum of physical techniques beyond ankle mobility interventions. ## Abstract
This study investigated how ankle dorsiflexion ROM limitation affects hamstring muscle Peak Torque/BW (%), Average Power (W), and Total Work (J) in 47 professional football players divided into a post-ACL reconstruction group (n=24) and an uninjured group (n=23). Players were sub-classified by the Weight-Bearing Lunge Test (WBLT) into normal (≥12 cm) and restricted (<12 cm) ankle dorsiflexion subgroups, and concentric isokinetic testing at 60°/s was performed on both limbs. Results showed that ankle dorsiflexion restriction had no significant effect on hamstring strength variables within either group, with poor and non-significant correlations between WBLT and all strength measures. AUC values below 0.5 across all variables confirmed that ankle dorsiflexion ROM has no diagnostic accuracy for hamstring muscle strength.

## Methods
Cross-sectional study of 47 professional football players (Group 1: n=24 post-ACL reconstruction 2-3 years prior, cleared for RTS; Group 2: n=23 injury-free for 3 years; mean age ~22.6 years). Ankle dorsiflexion was measured with the WBLT (cut-off 12 cm). Hamstring Peak Torque/BW (%), Average Power (W), and Total Work (J) were assessed via concentric isokinetic dynamometry (Biodex System 4) at 60°/s, 10 repetitions per leg. MANOVA, Pearson correlations, and ROC/AUC analyses were used.

## Findings
Post-ACL players had significantly lower Peak Torque/BW (Group 1 injured leg: 121 ± ~25% vs Group 2 right leg: 164 ± ~30%, p<0.006) and Average Power compared to uninjured players. Within both groups, no significant differences were found between normal and restricted ankle dorsiflexion subgroups for any strength variable (all p>0.05). Pearson correlations between WBLT and all strength variables were poor (r range: -0.18 to 0.26, all non-significant). AUC values ranged from 0.40 to 0.65, all near or below 0.5, indicating no diagnostic utility.

## Limitations
Cross-sectional design prevents causal inference. Longitudinal monitoring of both muscle strength and ankle dorsiflexion ROM over a season would provide more robust conclusions. The sample was limited to professional regional-level players, limiting generalizability to elite or other competitive levels.

## Practical Implications
Football programs should not rely solely on ankle dorsiflexion assessments to identify athletes at risk for hamstring weakness. Post-ACL reconstruction players exhibit persistent bilateral hamstring strength deficits that require targeted rehabilitation regardless of ankle mobility status. Injury prevention programming should include a broad spectrum of physical training techniques addressing multiple risk factors beyond ankle joint mobility.

## Athlete Development Notes
Hamstring strength development programs for football players should be prioritized independently of ankle dorsiflexion status. Athletes returning from ACL reconstruction demonstrate lasting hamstring strength deficits bilaterally, reinforcing the need for comprehensive lower-limb conditioning protocols throughout development. Screening should include isokinetic hamstring strength testing in addition to ankle mobility assessments.

## Return to Play Notes
Football players cleared for RTS after ACL reconstruction continue to show significantly reduced hamstring Peak Torque/BW and Average Power compared to uninjured peers, indicating that RTS clearance does not equate to full hamstring strength recovery. RTP protocols should include objective hamstring strength benchmarks and should not be conflated with ankle mobility restoration. Bilateral hamstring strength deficits persisting 2-3 years post-reconstruction highlight the need for extended post-clearance monitoring.
