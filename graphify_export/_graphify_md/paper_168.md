# Reams N, Hayward RA, Kutcher JS, Burke JF. Effect of Concussion on Performance of National Football League Players. Int J Sports Physiol Perform. 2017;12(8):1100-1106. doi: 10.1123/ijspp.2016-0508

## TLDR
NFL skill-position players who were cleared to return to play after concussion performed at their pre-injury baseline level — there was no measurable decline in game performance upon return. However, players showed significantly reduced DYAR performance in the one and two weeks prior to their concussion appearing on the official injury report, which may indicate delayed diagnosis, underreporting, or a multi-hit cumulative injury phenomenon. For performance and medical staff, this suggests current return-to-play criteria are capturing functional recovery adequately, but earlier detection of subclinical concussion effects may be an important opportunity to improve athlete protection.

## Abstract
This retrospective cohort study examined 1,882 NFL skill-position players (running backs, quarterbacks, wide receivers, tight ends) from 2007-2010 to determine whether concussion affects on-field performance as measured by Defense-Adjusted Yards Above Replacement (DYAR). Players reported on the weekly NFL injury report with concussion were compared to controls with other head/neck injuries on performance changes relative to each player's baseline. Fixed-effects regression models estimated performance before and after injury.

## Methods
Retrospective cohort study of 1,882 NFL skill-position players over 2007-2010 regular seasons. Concussion identified via weekly NFL injury report. Controls: players with other head/neck injuries. Primary outcome: Defense-Adjusted Yards Above Replacement (DYAR) from Football Outsiders, a context-adjusted performance metric. Multi-level fixed-effects linear regression estimated DYAR changes at weeks -4 through +4 relative to the injury report. Sensitivity analyses adjusted for plays per game, season week, player age, years in the league, and weeks missed.

## Findings
140 concussions and 57 control injuries identified. Upon return to play (week 0 and subsequent weeks), concussed players performed at baseline DYAR — no significant decline post-injury. However, DYAR was significantly lower 2 weeks (-7.12, 95% CI -13.0 to -1.31) and 1 week (-12.2, 95% CI -17.9 to -6.5) before the injury report, suggesting pre-report performance decrement. In the week of the injury report, concussed players performed slightly better than controls (DYAR difference +11.2, p=0.06). Results were robust to sensitivity analyses.

## Limitations
Performance is not a guaranteed sensitive marker of neurological recovery; lingering effects may exist that do not manifest as DYAR changes. DYAR is a team-context-dependent metric that cannot fully isolate individual performance. NFL injury report diagnoses are non-specific and subject to underreporting (a 2012 poll found 56% of NFL players would hide concussion symptoms). Control group contamination (misclassified concussions) is possible. Study predates modern concussion awareness and management advances.

## Practical Implications
NFL performance and medical staff can take some reassurance that the formal return-to-play protocol, as practiced during 2007-2010, was associated with players returning to baseline performance. However, the decline in performance detected 1-2 weeks before official concussion diagnosis should prompt earlier recognition efforts — sideline performance monitoring, weekly tracking of objective metrics, and low-threshold reporting culture. Performance-based metrics may complement traditional symptom-based concussion screening as an adjunct monitoring tool.

## Athlete Development Notes
The multi-hit hypothesis suggested by pre-diagnosis performance declines has implications for practice design: reducing cumulative head impact exposure in practice reduces the probability of subclinical injury that may compound into diagnosed concussion. Tracking weekly objective performance metrics for skill-position players (yards per route run, completion percentage, etc.) may help identify players experiencing subclinical neurological effects before a formal concussion manifests.

## Return to Play Notes
Players cleared through formal concussion protocols returned to baseline DYAR performance, supporting the validity of symptom-based and neurocognitive RTP criteria for NFL skill-position players. Staff should not assume a cleared player will underperform — but should continue to monitor performance metrics and symptom recurrence in the weeks following RTP. The finding that performance declines precede official diagnosis underscores the importance of a low-threshold reporting environment and proactive monitoring throughout the season to enable earlier intervention.
