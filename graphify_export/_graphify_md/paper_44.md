# Funk JR, Cormier JM, Lessley DJ, Bailey AM, Sánchez EJ, Sherwood CP, Crandall JR, Arbogast KB, Myers BS, Mayer T, Sills AK. An Analysis of Guardian Cap Use and Changes in the Concussion Rate in National Football League Preseason Practices From 2018 to 2023. Am J Sports Med. 2025;1-8.

## TLDR
Requiring the Guardian Cap NXT was associated with a 54–62% reduction in NFL practice concussion rates for mandated positions, but the effect was no longer significant when only helmet-shell-involved concussions were analyzed, suggesting mechanisms beyond padding compression contributed to the reduction.

## Abstract
This cohort study examined the effect of the Guardian Cap NXT requirement on concussion incidence in NFL preseason practices from 2018 to 2023 (excluding 2020). Data from the NFL Injury Surveillance System were used to compare concussion rates before (2018, 2019, 2021) and after (2022, 2023) the Guardian Cap NXT mandate for offensive linemen, defensive linemen, tight ends, and linebackers. A total of 139 concussions occurred during the study period. Two Poisson regression models were run to assess overall concussion rates and helmet-shell-specific concussion rates while controlling for preseason, position, practice intensity, and running back/fullback status.

## Methods
Cohort study using NFL Injury Surveillance System data; concussion rates normalized to player practice exposures; univariate chi-square comparisons; two Poisson regression models (all concussions and helmet-shell concussions only); qualitative video review for impact source and helmet shell involvement; multiple imputation for missing shell impact data.

## Findings
• Guardian Cap NXT requirement associated with 54% reduction in concussion rate (univariate, P=.001) and 62% reduction in regression model (P=.004) for mandated positions. • No statistically significant association between Guardian Cap use and concussion rate when only helmet-shell concussions were analyzed (P=.122). • Concussions in GC group decreased from 23.3/year pre-requirement to 11.0/year post-requirement. • Non-GC group showed a 42% increase in concussions after the requirement (not attributable to Guardian Cap). • Padded practices had 3.97× higher concussion odds than unpadded practices. • Reduction was shared across all four required positions.

## Limitations
Small sample sizes in some subgroups; incremental rollout of Guardian Cap requirement; no data on Guardian Cap use by non-required players; inability to account for all factors affecting concussion risk including changes in practice style; video review unavailable for 18% of concussion cases; assumptions about universal compliance in required positions.

## Practical Implications
Requiring padded helmet covers such as the Guardian Cap NXT for linemen, tight ends, and linebackers during practices may significantly reduce concussion incidence. Staff should note the benefit appears partially independent of shell padding compression (i.e., mass/inertia effects may contribute), and risk compensation behavior remains a theoretical concern that warrants monitoring.

## Athlete Development Notes
Directly applicable to American football programs considering Guardian Cap NXT policies in preseason and regular-season contact practices. The mandate covering offensive/defensive linemen, tight ends, and linebackers reflects the highest-contact positions; programs at all levels may benefit from adopting similar policies during padded contact sessions.

## Return to Play Notes
This study does not directly address return-to-play protocols; however, reducing practice concussion incidence by 54–62% has downstream implications for athlete availability and recovery burden. Teams with lower practice concussion rates will face fewer RTP decisions and associated disruptions to development.
