# Sadowska-Krepa E, Bankowski S, Kargul A, Iskra J. Changes in blood antioxidant status in American football players and soccer players over a training macrocycle. Journal of Exercise Science & Fitness. 2021;19:229-233. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jesf.2021.08.001

## TLDR
Blood antioxidant biomarkers track meaningfully across a football training macrocycle and can serve as objective indicators of training adaptation quality. American football and soccer players both showed improved antioxidant defense and reduced muscle damage markers entering the competition phase following preparatory training — a biochemical signature of appropriate adaptation. The transition period showed the highest oxidative stress markers (MDA, UA), suggesting accumulated fatigue. For NFL and college football programs, periodic blood panels (SOD, GSH, MDA, LDH, CK) can confirm that preseason training is producing positive adaptation rather than overreaching, and the consistently elevated MDA in American footballers relative to soccer players suggests heavier contact-sport athletes may benefit from proactive dietary antioxidant support.

## Abstract
This study assessed and compared changes in blood antioxidant defense in American football players (AF, n=11) and soccer players (SP, n=11) across three periods of a training macrocycle: preparatory (PP), competition (CP), and transition (TP). Resting venous blood samples measured antioxidant enzyme activity (SOD, CAT, GPx, GR), non-enzymatic antioxidants (glutathione, uric acid), the lipid peroxidation marker MDA, and muscle damage enzymes CK and LDH. Both groups showed elevated SOD, CAT, and GSH entering the competition period, with lower CK and LDH, suggesting positive physiological adaptation to preparatory training. The transition period was characterized by the highest UA, GPx, and MDA values, reflecting accumulated oxidative stress from competition.

## Methods
Cross-sectional repeated-measures design with two groups: American football players (AF, n=11, age 24.0±3.7 years, body mass 98.2±15.5 kg, VO2max 52.4±3.7 ml/kg/min) and first-league soccer players (SP, n=11, age 26.5±3.8 years, body mass 81.1±5.4 kg, VO2max 56.7±4.3 ml/kg/min) from Poland's first leagues. Resting venous blood samples were collected at the start of preparatory, competition, and transition periods. Variables measured: SOD and CAT activity in erythrocytes (Randox RANSOD and Aebi methods), GPx (Randox RANSEL), GR, GSH (colorimetric, DTNB method), uric acid (Randox UA230), CK (Randox CK522), LDH (Randox LD3818), and MDA (thiobarbituric acid reaction). Two-way mixed-design ANOVA with HSD Tukey post-hoc tests; effect sizes as eta squared (η²); Shapiro-Wilk normality and Levene homogeneity tests; Statistica 10.0.

## Findings
Period effect significant for SOD (p<0.001, η²=0.31), CAT (p<0.05, η²=0.24), GPx (p<0.001, η²=0.30), GSH (p<0.0001, η²=0.60), and UA (p<0.0001, η²=0.48). Group × period interaction significant for SOD, CAT, GPx (p<0.05), GSH (p<0.001), and UA (p<0.01). SOD activity was highest at CP onset in both groups: AF +12%, SP +33% vs PP. CAT also elevated at CP. GSH concentrations increased significantly in SP at CP and TP vs PP (p<0.01); lowest in both groups at PP. UA rose progressively across the macrocycle in both groups, peaking at TP. MDA group effect was significant (p<0.001, η²=0.45): higher in AF than SP throughout the macrocycle. LDH group effect was significant (p<0.0001, η²=0.51); LDH was significantly higher at TP than PP and CP in SP (p<0.05). CK activity was not significantly different by group, period, or interaction.

## Limitations
The study only examined endogenous antioxidant mechanisms without controlling for dietary antioxidant intake (vitamins A, C, E, selenium, polyphenols), which could confound the findings. Small sample size (n=11 per group) limits statistical power and generalizability. Players were from Polish semi-professional leagues, limiting applicability to elite NFL or international-level athletes. Blood samples were collected only at resting baseline at three time points, not during or immediately post-exercise, so acute within-session dynamics are not captured.

## Practical Implications
NFL and college football performance staff can use periodic blood antioxidant panels (SOD, GSH, MDA, LDH, CK) as objective indicators of training adaptation quality. Improvements in SOD, CAT, and GSH alongside reductions in CK and LDH from preseason to early in-season confirm that the preparatory block is driving positive physiological adaptation. Persistently elevated MDA or rising LDH entering the transition period may signal accumulated oxidative damage requiring active recovery intervention. The markedly higher MDA in American football players versus soccer players — likely attributable to greater body mass and training intensity — suggests that heavier contact-sport athletes may require more aggressive antioxidant-supportive nutrition. Dietary antioxidant supplementation (vitamins C and E, polyphenols) during the preseason may augment endogenous defense mechanisms.

## Athlete Development Notes
Progressive preparatory training in American football drives meaningful upregulation of the endogenous antioxidant defense system (SOD, CAT, GSH), reflecting RONS-mediated gene signaling through MAPKs and NF-κB pathways. Development programs should structure preseason training to apply sufficient load stimulus to drive these adaptations while monitoring for excessive oxidative stress via MDA and LDH. Younger developing athletes with lower training status may show more pronounced initial oxidative stress markers before adaptation occurs, warranting individualized monitoring protocols across the training year.

## Return to Play Notes
The transition period — representing post-competition offseason entry — was associated with the highest UA, GPx, LDH, and MDA values in both groups, reflecting cumulative oxidative stress and muscle damage from competition. Athletes returning from injury late in the competition period may present with elevated baseline oxidative stress and muscle damage markers that could impair tissue healing. Blood antioxidant profiling (particularly GSH and MDA) at the time of injury may help individualize recovery timelines, and nutritional antioxidant support should be considered as part of early-phase injury management for athletes with high oxidative stress indicators.
